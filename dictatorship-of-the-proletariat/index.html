THE DICTATORSHIP OF THE PROLETARIAT

The working class organized as the ruling class: the revolutionary bridge between capitalism and communism.

BY ELEANOR ASTER

grown from long talks with a discord friend

READ THE WHOLE MOVEMENT
PROLOGUE
The thesis

The dictatorship of the proletariat is the working class organized as the ruling class. Workers seize political power, break the ready-made capitalist state machinery, defeat attempts by the bourgeoisie to restore its rule, take the means of production out of private hands, and reorganize society for collective human need.

Its positive content is Proletarian Self-Governance: workers directly managing social life through communes, councils, cooperatives, unions, assemblies, planning bodies and popular defensive organizations.

It is not the final society. It is the revolutionary bridge between capitalism and communism.

The transition also produces a Commonalty. It begins wherever people directly organize their common life. Communal self-government develops alongside the proletarian state: the state suppresses counterrevolution while the Commonalty absorbs administration into society. As class antagonisms disappear and communal organization grows, the state loses its coercive work and dissolves into the Commonalty.

Its historic work is to abolish:

  1. The economic relations that create classes.
  2. The social relations built upon those economic relations.
  3. The ideas, habits and institutions produced by class society.
  4. The separation of administrative power from society.
  5. The need for a coercive state itself.
  6. The proletariat’s own existence as a class.

Once this work is complete, neither the bourgeoisie nor the proletariat exists as a class. With no exploiting class to defend and no exploited class to restrain, political state power loses its purpose. Government over people gives way to the communal administration of resources, production and common affairs.

The whole movement is therefore:

I

Class
Power

§ 1 – 5 · SEIZURE & FORCE

What “dictatorship” means, why the transition is necessary, and why the old machinery cannot simply be inherited.

01
§ 1

What “Dictatorship” Means

“Dictatorship” means class power.

Every class society is governed according to the interests of a ruling class. The particular constitutional form can change without changing the class holding real power.

Under capitalism, the bourgeoisie controls the means of production. Because workers must sell their labor power to survive, capital commands production, employment and investment. Capitalist ownership also shapes the state, law, education, media and political system.

The dictatorship of the bourgeoisie can therefore operate through:

  • a monarchy;
  • a parliamentary republic;
  • a presidential system;
  • military government;
  • fascism;
  • formal multiparty democracy.

These forms differ greatly, but capitalist property remains protected and production remains governed by capital.

The dictatorship of the proletariat reverses this class relationship. Workers cease being a subordinate class and become the organized ruling power. They use political and economic force to prevent the bourgeoisie from restoring capitalism and to eliminate the conditions that reproduce both classes.

Proletarian dictatorship is exercised by the working class itself. If a party, military council or revolutionary elite governs while workers remain politically passive, that is the dictatorship of that organization, not the self-government of the proletariat.

The dictatorship exists only where workers themselves possess and exercise power through their councils, communes, unions, cooperatives, assemblies and armed organizations.

02
§ 2

Why the Transition Is Necessary

Capitalist society cannot become communist through a single decree.

Immediately after a revolution:

  • capitalists and landlords still exist;
  • capitalist habits and expectations still exist;
  • economic inequity remains;
  • the old administrative knowledge remains concentrated among specialists;
  • production still depends upon inherited supply chains;
  • money and production for exchange still operate;
  • hostile states still surround the revolution;
  • sections of the old military and bureaucracy may resist;
  • the working class has not yet learned to administer every social function;
  • divisions between intellectual and physical labor remain;
  • differences between urban and rural development remain;
  • nationalism, racism, patriarchy and other inherited hierarchies remain.

The working class therefore needs an organized political power capable of defending the revolution and directing the transformation.

Without that power, separate workplaces and communities would act without sufficient coordination. Capitalists could use their remaining wealth, expertise, media influence, international connections and control over supplies to reorganize themselves. Foreign states could intervene. Production could collapse before new relations had time to develop.

The dictatorship provides the organized force needed to pass through each stage consciously rather than leaving the transition to chance.

Economic inequity means that people and regions enter the revolution with unequal resources, capacities, needs and living conditions. Equity directs different resources toward different needs instead of applying one formally equal standard to materially unequal situations.

The movement from money and production for exchange toward labor accounting can begin within roughly five to fifteen years after the revolution. Its speed depends upon productive capacity, technical preparation, international conditions and the degree to which production and distribution have already been socialized.

Every transitional form prepares the conditions for its own disappearance.
03
§ 3

The Capitalist State Must Be Dismantled

The existing state is not an empty machine that any class can operate for any purpose. Its structure grew alongside capitalist property and embodies the separation between rulers and ruled.

Its central organs include:

  • a standing army separated from the population;
  • professional police;
  • permanent bureaucracies;
  • unelected administrative hierarchies;
  • courts organized around property and contract;
  • professional politicians;
  • secret executive institutions;
  • officials enjoying special salaries and privileges;
  • centralized power insulated from daily popular control.

Winning elections does not automatically transform this machinery. A workers’ government attempting to operate through the old structure remains dependent upon officials, generals, judges, financiers and administrators formed by capitalist society.

The working class must therefore dismantle the old machinery and construct new organs of power.

Necessary technical functions remain. Society still needs:

  • public transportation;
  • sanitation;
  • healthcare;
  • quality housing;
  • socialized childcare and care services;
  • recordkeeping;
  • production statistics;
  • infrastructure;
  • conflict resolution;
  • disaster response;
  • scientific coordination;
  • public meeting spaces and other forms of social architecture;
  • other activities required for common life.

But these functions cease belonging to a permanent caste standing above society. They are placed under direct public control, simplified, made transparent and gradually integrated into ordinary social activity.

04
§ 4

Revolutionary Force and Direct Action

The bourgeoisie will not surrender its power merely because workers pass a resolution.

Capitalist resistance can take many forms:

  • military attack;
  • sabotage;
  • capital flight;
  • factory closures;
  • refusal to invest;
  • withholding wages;
  • destruction of records, software or technical systems;
  • removal of machinery;
  • support for counterrevolutionary groups;
  • economic blockade;
  • foreign intervention;
  • propaganda intended to divide the population.
Workers must be capable of meeting organized force with organized force.

The revolution therefore requires the direct physical power of the proletariat. Workers disarm the old repressive institutions, seize strategic infrastructure, occupy workplaces and establish control over the territory upon which production and social life depend.

Violence is not treated as a ritual or moral virtue. It is the force required to defeat the organized violence of the old ruling class. Wanton cruelty toward the population weakens the revolution, destroys solidarity and reproduces the barbarism of the old order. Revolutionary force must remain directed toward defeating restoration and transferring social power to the working population.

Clear public rules govern coercive activity. Accountability, proportionality, appeal and communal oversight distinguish material counterrevolution from criticism, disagreement and ordinary error. The humane character of the revolution is expressed through the liberation it creates and the restraint with which it exercises necessary force.

05
§ 5

Reabsorbing Social Power

Under capitalism, society’s own powers confront people as external authorities.

Workers produce wealth, but capital controls it. Communities create social life, but bureaucracies administer it. People collectively possess the capacity for defense, yet a professional military monopolizes weapons. Citizens generate knowledge, but private institutions and state hierarchies control its use.

The dictatorship reabsorbs these alienated powers into society.

This means:

  • workers manage production;
  • communities govern local affairs;
  • the population participates in defense;
  • delegates remain subordinate to their constituents;
  • knowledge becomes publicly accessible;
  • administration becomes an ordinary social duty;
  • public officials cease being a separate political class.

The working class breaks the ready-made state machinery and transforms state power from an external force into its own organized social activity.

Every council, cooperative, neighborhood assembly, communal service and popular defensive organization forms an element of the developing Commonalty. Social power grows in the hands of society as the state’s independent power contracts.

§ 6 – 12 · COMMUNES, DEMOCRACY, JUSTICE

The commune as the basic political form: delegates on workers’ pay, democracy as daily life, the end of bureaucracy.

06
§ 6

Communes as the Foundation of Self-Government

The commune is the basic political form of the new society.

A commune is a self-governing association of people who directly manage the common affairs of their locality. The organized population acts for itself rather than surrendering administration to professional politicians.

Communes oversee matters such as:

  • housing;
  • food distribution;
  • local transportation;
  • public safety;
  • education;
  • healthcare;
  • cultural activity;
  • environmental management;
  • communal justice;
  • local production.

Workplaces, neighborhoods and social organizations select delegates to communal bodies. Communes then send mandated delegates to regional and central bodies.

Selection can take several forms according to the character of the institution:

  • direct election;
  • sortition or random selection by lot;
  • workplace or assembly designation;
  • selection from experienced organizational leadership;
  • mixed systems combining election, lot and technical qualification.
  • serve short terms;
  • remain subject to immediate recall;
  • receive ordinary workers’ pay;
  • follow binding instructions;
  • publicly report their decisions;
  • return regularly to ordinary productive or social work.

They do not receive a free political mandate to rule according to their private judgment. Their authority comes from an organized body whose will they are required to express.

Recall remains an active power through public reports, scheduled confidence reviews, visible recall procedures and automatic mandate expiration. Constituents do not need to wait for a major scandal before reviewing a delegate.

Delegation coordinates self-government without transferring sovereignty away from the organized population.
07
§ 7

Central Coordination and Local Execution

Socialism requires both centralization and decentralization.

Large systems cannot be managed by isolated localities. Energy networks, railways, river systems, industrial supply chains and national healthcare require coordination across regions.

Central organs therefore establish common plans, standards and priorities.

Execution remains decentralized. Local workers and communities possess direct knowledge of:

  • their productive capacity;
  • environmental conditions;
  • available skills;
  • cultural practices;
  • urgent needs;
  • technical difficulties.
Central planning provides unity. Local self-management provides responsiveness and initiative.

The central body does not replace local organization. Local bodies do not ignore the needs of the entire society. Each level becomes part of one system of continual information, consultation, planning, implementation and correction.

Communication moves upward, downward and horizontally. Workers can contact immediate coordinators, higher planning bodies and related workplaces without waiting for permission from administrative gatekeepers. Direct communication prevents functionaries from controlling the flow of information between organizational levels.

The right of refusal protects communities against destructive or irrational commands. But refusal carries responsibility: a locality cannot demand full access to the common product while rejecting every common obligation. Conflicts between local autonomy and general necessity must be resolved through transparent negotiation, mediation and recallable representation.

08
§ 8

Complete Suffrage and Continuous Democracy

Political participation ceases being an occasional vote among professional candidates.

Workers govern through:

  • workplace councils;
  • trade unions;
  • neighborhood assemblies;
  • communes;
  • cooperatives;
  • planning committees;
  • consumer councils;
  • local militias;
  • cultural organizations;
  • educational bodies.
Democracy becomes part of daily life.

Real political liberty requires material conditions. A person cannot participate fully while working exhausting hours, fearing unemployment, lacking education or depending upon a landlord or employer.

The dictatorship therefore expands democracy through:

  • shorter working hours;
  • universal education;
  • free access to information;
  • social guarantees;
  • public meeting spaces;
  • quality housing;
  • socialized childcare;
  • public transportation;
  • communication systems;
  • direct access to decision-making bodies.

Rights become real capacities rather than words written in statutes.

Social architecture gives these capacities a physical form. Neighborhoods, workplaces and public buildings provide spaces for meetings, childcare, collective meals, recreation and cultural activity. Political space becomes part of ordinary social space.

09
§ 9

Abolition of the Standing Army

The standing army is a specialized armed organization separated from society. Its chain of command allows it to be turned against the population.

The proletarian state replaces it with a people’s army rooted in local militias.

The people’s army is composed of workers who:

  • remain connected to their communities;
  • elect or supervise commanders;
  • rotate duties where possible;
  • receive political and technical education;
  • participate voluntarily under ordinary conditions;
  • defend the revolution against internal and external attack.

Commanders are elected, selected or directly supervised by the people they organize. Officers receive ordinary workers’ wages. Rank expresses technical responsibility within a particular task, never social superiority or permanent material privilege.

A central military organ coordinates:

  • national defense;
  • logistics;
  • intelligence;
  • training;
  • modern technology;
  • international military cooperation.

Military expertise remains necessary, but experts remain subordinate to communal and proletarian authority. The officer corps cannot become an independent caste.

As counterrevolutionary threats disappear, military functions contract. The population’s defensive organization becomes part of ordinary communal self-management rather than a separate state power.

10
§ 10

Communal Justice

Capitalist law gives priority to property, contract, debt and state order. Communal justice gives priority to human needs, collective safety, social responsibility and the defense of the revolution.

Judges and legal officials become:

  • elected or communally appointed;
  • recallable;
  • publicly accountable;
  • paid ordinary wages;
  • accessible to the population.

Community mediation resolves conflicts wherever reconciliation is possible. Formal punishment becomes a final measure rather than the default response.

The purpose of justice changes from protecting property and imposing obedience to:

  • repairing harm;
  • protecting the community;
  • resolving conflict;
  • restoring social relationships;
  • preventing exploitation;
  • defending the revolution.

Armed conspiracies, sabotage and organized attempts to restore capitalist domination remain subject to coercive suppression. Clear procedures must distinguish material counterrevolution from criticism, disagreement or ordinary error. If every disagreement becomes “counterrevolution,” class justice degenerates into arbitrary rule and destroys proletarian self-government.

Neighborhood public-safety organizations draw their members from the community. An elected and recallable sheriff, or equivalent communal coordinator, organizes training, emergency response, search-and-rescue, investigation and cooperation between neighborhoods. This coordinator remains accountable to communal courts and assemblies.

Secret-police institutions disappear.

Investigative functions operate under defined mandates, judicial supervision and communal review. Temporary confidentiality may protect an active investigation or vulnerable person, but permanent secrecy cannot become an independent source of political power.

11
§ 11

Ending Bureaucracy

Bureaucracy develops when administration becomes the permanent occupation and private domain of a specialized group.

Its starting point is a real need for coordination. Different parts of a large organization develop specialized knowledge, terminology and procedures. Functionaries arise to translate between them, standardize information and prepare decisions. Bureaucratic power develops when these functionaries gain control over communication, resources, rules and access to decision-making. Procedures then displace outcomes, and administration begins justifying and expanding itself.

It is broken down through:

  • short terms of service;
  • immediate recall;
  • ordinary workers’ pay;
  • rotation of responsibilities;
  • public records;
  • open meetings;
  • reduction of administrative layers;
  • simplification of procedures;
  • broad technical education;
  • direct participation by workers;
  • continual public inspection;
  • direct lines of communication between organizational levels;
  • systematic and active reduction of functionaries;
  • local autonomy wherever common coordination does not require central intervention;
  • continuous internal feedback loops.

Executive and legislative work are combined wherever their separation merely produces delay and evasion. Those who decide are responsible for implementation, and those who implement remain involved in decision-making.

Administrative mistakes are publicly admitted and corrected. Officials cannot hide behind obscure language, secret procedures or claims of infallibility.

Roles become technical rather than political. Coordination still exists, but it no longer grants command over a subordinate population.

Workers possess the right to remove or replace administrators who obstruct production or communal activity. Performance is judged through publicly defined results: useful output, product quality, safety, ecological effects, fulfillment of the plan, reduction of labor time, working conditions and responsiveness to workers and consumers.

Audits identify problems and spread successful methods. They remain simple, transparent and directed toward outcomes so that inspection does not itself become a new bureaucracy.

12
§ 12

Expertise Without a Ruling Caste

Complex societies require expertise. Engineering, medicine, economics, agriculture, computing and military defense cannot be operated through enthusiasm alone.

Technical experts therefore remain essential.

Their knowledge is integrated into collective government through:

  • public advisory bodies;
  • transparent models;
  • accessible explanations;
  • democratic review;
  • merit-based appointment;
  • continual education;
  • collaboration with workers;
  • accountability for results.
Experts determine what is technically possible. Society determines what is socially desirable.

Technical authority remains attached to the task. It does not become a general right to govern. Experts establish technical possibilities, requirements and consequences; society determines the ends toward which that knowledge is applied.

A planner may explain the resources required to build a railway, but cannot privately decide that the railway matters more than hospitals. An engineer may identify safe designs, but cannot use technical knowledge to escape public accountability.

The aim is not to abolish knowledge differences instantly. It is to prevent knowledge from becoming a basis of class rule while expanding education until specialized knowledge is shared as widely as possible.

III

Expropriating the
Expropriators

§ 13 – 16 · PROPERTY CHANGES HANDS

Factories, banks, land and infrastructure return to the people who collectively created and operate them.

13
§ 13

Expropriating the Expropriators

Capitalist private property in the means of production is abolished.

This does not mean taking personal belongings. The target is property that allows one group to command the labor of another:

  • large factories;
  • commercial land;
  • banks;
  • mines;
  • energy systems;
  • transport networks;
  • communication infrastructure;
  • major agricultural holdings;
  • natural resources.

Capitalists accumulated these assets through the appropriation of surplus labor. Revolutionary expropriation returns society’s productive capacities to the people who collectively created and operate them.

The process begins with:

  • capitalists who sabotage production;
  • owners who abandon enterprises;
  • major monopolies;
  • banks;
  • strategic infrastructure;
  • absentee landlords;
  • rentiers and speculators.

It expands until private ownership of social production disappears completely.

The sequence follows the concentration of capital. Highly concentrated banks, energy systems, transportation networks, communications and major industries can move directly into public ownership. Smaller enterprises pass through cooperative, municipal or communal forms and federate as their production becomes more interconnected.

14
§ 14

Defeating the Financial Strike

Capitalists exercise power by deciding whether production and investment will continue.

When threatened, they can:

  • stop investing;
  • close workplaces;
  • refuse wages;
  • move funds;
  • create shortages;
  • produce unemployment;
  • demand political concessions.
The proletarian state ignores this veto.

Workers continue production. Councils take control of abandoned workplaces. Managers are elected from experienced workers or replaced by accountable technical teams. Central institutions provide credit, resources and coordination.

Workers possess the right to manage production directly and remove administrators who obstruct productive capacity. Continued authority depends upon demonstrated contribution rather than ownership, title or political connection.

Production no longer depends upon whether a private owner expects profit.

15
§ 15

Nationalization and Socialization

Nationalization places an enterprise under state ownership.

Socialization places it under conscious social control.

Nationalization alone is not socialism. A state-owned corporation can still contain wage labor, managerial domination, commodity exchange and profit-centered production.

Nationalized enterprises therefore pass under the control of:

  • workers’ councils;
  • communes;
  • planning bodies;
  • consumer organizations;
  • the organized working class as a whole.

Large and concentrated enterprises are socialized first because their production is already interconnected and internally planned. Smaller enterprises form cooperatives, municipal units and communal enterprises. These organizations federate as production becomes more integrated. Large cooperative associations then enter communal control and central coordination while management remains rooted in workers and local communities.

Ownership, management and purpose are all transformed.

State ownership without worker control preserves managerial domination. Cooperative ownership without social planning can reproduce competition.

Socialization unites worker management, communal ownership and production for common need.

16
§ 16

Land and Agriculture

Land ceases being an object of speculation and rent.

Large landowners, absentee owners and rentiers lose land they do not personally cultivate. Working farmers initially retain direct use of the land they work.

Absentee landlords, rentiers and speculators are expropriated without compensation. A person who personally works land initially retains only the land they actually cultivate.

Cooperative agriculture develops through:

  • access to machinery;
  • shared infrastructure;
  • irrigation;
  • agronomic knowledge;
  • social insurance;
  • coordinated distribution;
  • reduced risk;
  • relief from debt and rent.

Small holdings combine as cooperation becomes materially superior to isolated production. Large cooperative units eventually enter communal and social ownership.

The purpose is not to replace landlords with distant bureaucrats. Agricultural workers and communities manage land within a common ecological and economic plan.

Public transportation, shared storage and processing facilities connect rural producers to the broader social plan without subordinating them to commercial middlemen. Rational agronomy restores soil fertility, water cycles, biodiversity and the metabolic interchange between humanity and nature.

§ 17 – 22 · PLANNING, CYBERNETICS, DISTRIBUTION

From the Mass Line to labor certificates to free goods: the conscious coordination of the whole economy.

17
§ 17

Central Economic Planning

Capitalist production is coordinated after the fact through competition, prices, crises, bankruptcies and unemployment.

Socialist production is coordinated beforehand.

Planning determines:

  • what society needs;
  • what resources exist;
  • how much labor is available;
  • what ecological limits must be respected;
  • which infrastructure must be maintained;
  • what reserves are necessary;
  • how production should expand or contract.

The planning process continuously gathers information from:

  • consumers;
  • workplaces;
  • communities;
  • scientists;
  • distribution centers;
  • environmental systems.

Plans remain adjustable. Production results return as feedback, allowing corrections.

Planning also changes social relations. Workers cease responding to commands from owners and begin coordinating their activity through common information, negotiated priorities and direct knowledge of social need.

Planning does not mean one office issuing fixed orders for every object. It is a layered system of democratic coordination operating at local, regional, national and international levels.

18
§ 18

Cybernetic Governance

Cybernetics organizes continual feedback.

The process is:

  1. Workers and communities report needs and capacities.
  2. Technical systems organize the information.
  3. Planners produce feasible alternatives.
  4. The population evaluates priorities.
  5. Councils adopt a plan.
  6. Local bodies implement it.
  7. Outcomes are measured.
  8. Errors and shortages are corrected.
Computers calculate possibilities. They do not determine social values.

Algorithms, statistics and planning models remain publicly inspectable. If only a technical caste understands the system, cybernetic planning becomes bureaucratic domination. Its liberating purpose depends upon mass education and transparent control.

Cybernetic governance also acts against bureaucracy. It keeps information moving between levels, exposes obstruction, measures actual outcomes and prevents functionaries from monopolizing communication. Workers can challenge the aims, measurements and categories used by the planning system.

19
§ 19

The Mass Line

The Mass Line turns popular experience into organized policy.

It moves through a continuous cycle:

  • gather ideas from the people;
  • study the real conditions behind them;
  • unite scattered experiences into a coherent program;
  • return that program to the people;
  • test it through practice;
  • gather new feedback;
  • revise the program.

This connects central leadership with mass initiative.

Leadership does not manufacture policy in isolation. The masses do not remain an uncoordinated collection of opinions. Their experience becomes conscious collective action.

20
§ 20

Social Distribution

The total social product is divided into several parts.

Before individual consumption, society deducts resources for:

  • replacement of machinery;
  • expansion of production;
  • administration;
  • healthcare;
  • education;
  • public transport;
  • ecological restoration;
  • emergency reserves;
  • people unable to work;
  • common cultural needs.

The remainder is distributed for personal consumption.

These proportions change throughout the transition. Administrative costs fall as bureaucracy contracts, procedures simplify and communities govern themselves directly. Common provision grows as healthcare, education, housing, transportation, food, energy and cultural goods become freely accessible. Emergency reserves remain because uncertainty cannot be abolished, although planning and resilient infrastructure reduce their relative burden.

An equity fund directs additional resources toward people and regions burdened by disability, oppression, colonial underdevelopment, ecological damage or unequal access to productive capacity.

At first, individual remuneration remains connected to labor contributed. Over time, more necessities become freely available, reducing the portion that requires individual accounting.

Distribution moves through three broad stages:

1Money under controlMoney and markets still operate under proletarian control.
2Labor certificatesLabor certificates replace money in individual consumption.
3According to needFree distribution according to need replaces individualized exchange.
21
§ 21

Labor Certificates

Labor certificates record an individual’s contribution of socially recognized labor.

They differ from money because they cannot be:

  • transferred;
  • invested;
  • lent for interest;
  • accumulated as capital;
  • used to hire labor;
  • inherited as productive power.

A worker contributes labor and receives access to an equivalent portion of the consumable social product after common deductions.

This establishes equal labor for equal consumption during the lower phase.

But equal measurement still treats unequal people by one standard. People have different health conditions, dependents and needs. Therefore, labor certificates remain a transitional form.

Equal right is an unequal right when it applies one standard to unequal people. Equity corrects the unequal results of equal labor accounting through common provision and differentiated support.

As abundance and common provision expand, distribution moves from contribution toward need.

Labor certificates can begin replacing money and production for exchange within roughly five to fifteen years of the revolution, as social ownership, planning and distribution become sufficiently developed. They eventually cease regulating personal consumption. Labor-time calculation remains useful for planning, comparing techniques and allocating productive resources, while individual access proceeds according to need. The certificate disappears as a personal claim and survives only as social accounting.

22
§ 22

Free Goods and Decommodification

Goods and services become free where social accounting makes individual payment unnecessary.

The process begins with common services such as:

  • healthcare;
  • education;
  • public transportation;
  • libraries;
  • digital knowledge;
  • parks;
  • basic utilities;
  • social housing.
A train travels whether every seat is occupied or not. Charging for an unused seat does not create the train’s capacity; it merely excludes people from it.

Free access expands as productivity rises and planning reduces waste. Scarce goods still require social allocation, but access no longer depends upon wealth.

Money steadily loses its function until production and distribution operate directly as conscious social processes.

This expansion of common provision is Commonification. Privately controlled wealth becomes common wealth, and individualized payment loses its purpose wherever society can already provide abundance directly.

Commonification reverses primitive accumulation. Capitalism produced private wealth by separating people from common resources. Communism produces the Commonwealth by abolishing private command over accumulated social wealth.

Common wealth does not mean unlimited consumption of every scarce object. Scarce goods remain consciously allocated according to need, ecological limits and available supply. Access depends upon social need rather than purchasing power.

V

Labor &
Earth

§ 23 – 28 · WORK, MACHINES, ECOLOGY

Shorter hours, living labor over dead labor, production within the reproductive limits of the biosphere.

23
§ 23

Full Employment and Shorter Work

Everyone capable of contributing participates in socially necessary activity.

Full employment ends the reserve army of unemployed workers used to discipline wages and working conditions.

At the same time, working hours fall.

Available labor is distributed across the whole population rather than forcing some people to work excessively while others are excluded.

Automation, efficient organization and elimination of waste reduce necessary labor further.

Wasteful labor includes functions resembling:

  • flunkies, whose presence mainly displays another person’s status;
  • goons, whose work advances one organization against competitors, including much lobbying, telemarketing and competitive advertising;
  • box-tickers, who produce reports and paperwork that create the appearance of action;
  • taskmasters, whose role consists mainly of supervising people already capable of managing themselves;
  • duct-tapers, who repeatedly repair problems created by defective organization.

These categories describe wasteful functions rather than condemning whole occupations. Reception remains useful where people require guidance. Assistants remain useful where they perform necessary coordination or accessibility work. Legal labor remains useful in substantive negotiation and conflict resolution. Auditors identify inefficiency and spread successful production methods. Repair workers address immediate problems while structural redesign removes their causes.

The first reorganization can target a reduction of roughly 20 percent of present working time even before major new automation.

Later reductions become more gradual as the remaining activity becomes increasingly necessary.

The aim is:

  • less compulsory work;
  • more leisure;
  • greater variety of activity;
  • more participation in administration;
  • more intellectual and artistic development;
  • greater dignity in labor.
24
§ 24

Living Labor Over Dead Labor

Dead labor is past labor embodied in machinery, buildings and accumulated capital.

Living labor is present human activity.

Under capitalism, dead labor dominates living labor. Workers serve machinery, profitability and capital accumulation.

Socialism reverses this relationship. Machinery serves people.

Productive equipment is evaluated according to whether it:

  • reduces necessary labor;
  • improves safety;
  • protects the environment;
  • satisfies human needs;
  • increases free time;
  • supports human development.

Excess productive capacity maintained only for competition and profit is decommissioned. Useful capacity is shared, improved and rationally coordinated.

25
§ 25

Ending the Boom-and-Bust Cycle

Capitalist enterprises produce independently for an uncertain market. This creates:

  • overproduction;
  • shortages;
  • speculation;
  • unemployment;
  • bankruptcies;
  • unused factories;
  • destroyed goods;
  • periodic crises.
Planning matches production with anticipated needs.

Society no longer faces “overproduction” merely because people lack money. Products are socially accessible. Economic information becomes direct rather than passing through prices, profits and commercial failure.

Reserves remain necessary because prediction is imperfect. But errors produce plan corrections rather than mass unemployment and destruction.

Preordering gives producers direct knowledge of demand before production begins. Durable goods can be configured and manufactured for their users rather than mass-produced in speculative quantities.

A vehicle can be ordered with selected features, colors and specifications. Flexible and increasingly automated factories then produce it through modular methods. A short planned waiting period replaces years of storage, speculative dealer inventories and wasteful model variation.

Additive manufacturing extends the same principle. People request specific objects, and production begins against registered demand. Inventories shift from masses of finished commodities toward versatile machinery, standard components and feedstock. Immediate necessities and goods with predictable use remain stocked, while reserves protect against forecasting errors and emergencies.

26
§ 26

Ecological Planning

Production is placed within the reproductive limits of the biosphere.

Economic calculation includes:

  • resource depletion;
  • energy use;
  • soil fertility;
  • water cycles;
  • pollution;
  • biodiversity;
  • climate effects;
  • long-term restoration.

Industries expand or contract according to human and ecological need.

Production for profit gives way to production for durable use. Planned obsolescence, wasteful advertising, unnecessary transport and destructive luxury consumption decline.

Ecological socialism does not reduce every kind of production equally. It contracts destructive activity while expanding:

  • renewable energy;
  • healthcare;
  • housing;
  • public transport;
  • ecological restoration;
  • durable consumer goods;
  • resilient infrastructure.
Degrowth is the planned contraction of ecologically destructive and socially wasteful production: fossil-fuel dependence, planned obsolescence, competitive advertising, destructive luxury consumption, redundant transportation and excessive military production.

This transformation develops unevenly across the world. Imperial centers reduce wasteful material throughput. Formerly colonized and underdeveloped regions expand necessary productive forces, infrastructure, healthcare, housing and technical capacity. Useful global production rises while unnecessary throughput falls.

27
§ 27

Rational Agronomy and Uneven Urban–Rural Development

Capitalism separates cities from the natural systems supporting them. Nutrients, waste, labor and resources move through society without rational ecological return.

Rational agronomy restores these cycles.

Agriculture and industry are coordinated so that:

  • soil is renewed;
  • waste is reused safely;
  • water is managed collectively;
  • production fits local ecosystems;
  • communities regain control over food systems.

The distinction between urban and rural life also loses its class character.

Rural residents gain equal access to:

  • education;
  • healthcare;
  • culture;
  • communication;
  • infrastructure;
  • technical development;
  • frequent and reliable public transportation.

Cities become more ecologically integrated, while rural life ceases being associated with deprivation and isolation.

Expanded rail, bus and other public-transportation networks connect rural communities with one another and with urban centers. Mobility ceases depending upon private vehicles or profitable routes.

Cities become ecologically integrated through mixed land use, green infrastructure, urban agriculture, restored waterways, collective waste systems, reduced sprawl and social architecture. Agriculture, industry and residential life become geographically and socially coordinated.

Geographical differences remain; their class character disappears.
28
§ 28

Universal Basic Services

All necessities of a dignified life become available without charge.

These include:

  • food;
  • quality housing;
  • healthcare;
  • education;
  • public transportation;
  • communication;
  • water;
  • energy;
  • socialized childcare and care services;
  • cultural participation.

This removes the economic compulsion forcing people to accept exploitation merely to survive.

Freedom becomes material. A person can refuse abusive work, leave a harmful household, study, participate politically and develop their abilities without losing access to life’s necessities.

§ 29

Eliminating Social Hierarchy

Class society gives material support to social hierarchies based on:

  • sex;
  • gender;
  • gender identity;
  • sexual orientation;
  • race;
  • nationality;
  • religion;
  • ethnicity;
  • disability;
  • colonial status.

The dictatorship attacks both discriminatory practices and their economic foundations.

Equity requires more than declaring everyone equal. Historically oppressed groups receive the resources and institutional support needed to overcome inherited disadvantages.

The revolution abolishes:

  • discriminatory exclusion;
  • unequal access to work and education;
  • material dependence reproducing patriarchal control;
  • racial and colonial divisions of labor;
  • nationalist privilege;
  • compulsory gender roles;
  • persecution based upon gender identity or sexuality.

Care and reproductive labor become social responsibilities through childcare, healthcare, communal services, parental support and shorter working hours. Reproductive freedom becomes materially real.

Coercive force protects people against organized discrimination and violence. Social development removes the conditions that continually reproduce these hierarchies.

30
§ 30

The Transformation of the Intelligentsia

Scientists, teachers, engineers, artists and professionals cease serving private capital and elite institutions.

They receive guaranteed access to socially useful work.

Research is directed toward:

  • public health;
  • ecological stability;
  • reduced labor time;
  • improved production;
  • education;
  • human development.

Intellectual workers participate in community and practical activity. Manual workers gain broad access to scientific and intellectual education.

The division between those who think and those who execute gradually disappears.

Science becomes a popular force rather than an instrument of profit, war or class domination.

Society also supports fundamental and speculative research whose immediate use remains unknown: mathematics, particle physics, astronomy, space research, theoretical biology and foundational computing. Research in one field regularly produces unforeseen advances elsewhere.

Scientific freedom extends beyond formal academia through public laboratories, open data, shared equipment, public funding, open publishing and collaborative research networks. Knowledge becomes the General Intellect: society’s shared capacity to understand and direct natural and productive processes.

31
§ 31

Revolutionary Education

Education prepares everyone to participate in social life.

Students learn:

  • critical thinking;
  • scientific knowledge;
  • practical skills;
  • social history;
  • cooperation;
  • administration;
  • political participation;
  • ecological responsibility;
  • emotional self-regulation;
  • independence;
  • autonomous learning.

Learning combines classrooms with real activity.

Students participate in:

  • community projects;
  • workshops;
  • agriculture;
  • research;
  • cultural production;
  • democratic management.

They help govern their schools and shape their own education.

Students learn how to investigate a subject without waiting for an authority to assign every step. They acquire the confidence and capacity to govern themselves while cooperating with others.

Syntegrity supplies structured, non-hierarchical deliberation through interconnected discussion groups. Participants examine a common problem from several positions and continually exchange information across the whole system.

Tektology develops general principles of organization. Students learn how systems combine, coordinate, adapt, encounter disruption and reorganize themselves.

Revolutionary education teaches people to identify domination and transform society. It must also preserve the right to criticize revolutionary institutions. Without that right, “critical consciousness” becomes obedience taught in revolutionary language.

32
§ 32

Cultural Revolution

Economic change alone does not immediately erase capitalist habits.

Competition, hierarchy, nationalism, consumerism and prejudice continue through everyday life.

Cultural revolution transforms:

  • family relations;
  • education;
  • media;
  • art;
  • social prestige;
  • patterns of consumption;
  • attitudes toward work;
  • relations between people and nature.
Workers become active cultural producers rather than passive consumers.

Society provides:

  • public art spaces;
  • cultural education;
  • publishing platforms;
  • community studios;
  • international exchanges;
  • citizen journalism;
  • access to music, literature and performance.

Cultural change develops through participation and criticism. Humiliation, mob shaming and arbitrary persecution reproduce domination rather than overcoming it.

33
§ 33

Communication and Media

Communication infrastructure passes out of capitalist ownership and into social control.

Private media owners can no longer purchase public opinion or decide which voices possess a platform.

Media becomes:

  • publicly accessible;
  • transparently governed;
  • open to workers and communities;
  • connected to social action;
  • internationalist;
  • protected from commercial manipulation.

Citizen journalism expands. Cultural workers receive material support without depending upon advertisers or wealthy patrons.

Counterpropaganda answers capitalist narratives with education and evidence.

But proletarian control cannot mean unaccountable party control. If one organization monopolizes information and suppresses working-class criticism, it places itself above the proletariat. Social ownership requires democratic access, public oversight and room for disagreement within the revolution.

§ 34

International Revolution

Capitalism is a world system. Socialism cannot remain secure as an isolated national island.

A proletarian state faces:

  • military encirclement;
  • economic blockade;
  • dependence on the world market;
  • technological isolation;
  • foreign sabotage;
  • pressure to accumulate competitively.

The revolution therefore advances internally and internationally at the same time.

It supports:

  • workers’ movements;
  • strikes;
  • anti-imperialist struggles;
  • revolutionary organizations;
  • socialist states;
  • international political education.

National liberation reaches completion through international working-class liberation. Workers cannot emancipate themselves while other peoples remain sources of cheap labor, extracted resources or imperial domination.

35
§ 35

The World Republic

Proletarian states federate into an international social republic.

The federation coordinates:

  • production;
  • resources;
  • technology;
  • ecological policy;
  • development;
  • transportation;
  • defense;
  • disaster response.

It reduces uneven development created by colonialism and imperialism.

Resources flow according to common development rather than national profit. Poorer regions receive infrastructure and technology without becoming dependent colonies of richer ones.

National borders lose political significance as the material basis for antagonistic national interests disappears.

The world market gives way to global social planning by freely associated communities and producers.
36
§ 36

The Communist International

The International unifies worker organizations across borders.

It:

  • coordinates strategy;
  • connects revolutionary movements;
  • shares knowledge;
  • organizes material support;
  • develops common programs;
  • assists mutual defense;
  • prevents socialist states from becoming nationalist powers.

No single state can treat its temporary national interests as superior to the world revolution.

The International harmonizes different revolutionary movements without erasing their local conditions. Its own delegates must remain accountable and recallable, or international coordination will become authority standing above the workers it claims to unite.

§ 37

The Communist Party

The Party is the most conscious and organized section of the proletariat.

It develops because workers do not acquire identical political understanding at the same time. Some have greater experience in:

  • class struggle;
  • political theory;
  • organization;
  • international coordination;
  • revolutionary strategy.

The Party preserves the full communist objective during moments when immediate pressures encourage retreat.

Its work includes:

  • education;
  • propaganda;
  • organization;
  • strategic advice;
  • international coordination;
  • combating opportunism;
  • assisting councils and communes;
  • defending the revolution’s final aim.
It acts as the general staff of the revolution, but the proletariat itself remains the army and the ruling class.
38
§ 38

The Party Cannot Substitute Itself for the Class

A small revolutionary minority cannot seize power and then declare its own dictatorship to be proletarian rule.

The masses must:

  • understand what they are fighting for;
  • participate in the revolution;
  • create their own institutions;
  • remain armed and organized;
  • directly govern afterward.
The Party places its organization and knowledge in the hands of the workers. It does not place the workers beneath itself.

Workers remain independently organized and armed through their own councils, communes, unions and militias. Their institutions do not become administrative branches of the Party.

It leads through:

  • persuasion;
  • clarity;
  • example;
  • successful organization;
  • earned trust.

It cannot establish proletarian rule through commands issued to a passive population.

39
§ 39

The Party and State Must Remain Distinct

Party members will often be elected because of their experience and commitment. This creates a danger that Party and state will merge.

Once Party membership becomes a route to:

  • employment;
  • promotion;
  • prestige;
  • administrative power;
  • material privilege;

careerists enter the Party and loyalty replaces revolutionary consciousness.

The Party then becomes a bureaucracy, and the state becomes its institutional possession.

This is the progression by which a revolutionary organization turns into a jobs program: superficial loyalty becomes a qualification for office, administrative position becomes a new division of labor, and the preservation of the apparatus replaces the final aim.

A clear separation must remain between:

  • political education and public administration;
  • party discipline and communal decision-making;
  • theoretical leadership and coercive authority.

The Party may criticize the state and organize workers against bureaucratic degeneration. It cannot possess a legal monopoly that allows it to overrule workers automatically.

If the Party controls the councils, military, appointments and media, the Party rules. Calling that arrangement proletarian self-government does not make it so.
40
§ 40

The Party’s Own Disappearance

The Party exists because revolutionary consciousness and organizational ability remain unevenly developed.

As socialist society matures:

  • everyone participates politically;
  • administration becomes common knowledge;
  • class enemies disappear;
  • communist consciousness becomes ordinary social consciousness;
  • communal institutions perform the Party’s organizational functions.

The Party’s direct intervention decreases.

It first becomes primarily educational and advisory. Its remaining functions are then absorbed into communal life.

When all of society performs the conscious work once concentrated in the Party, the Party ceases to exist as a separate and exclusionary organization.

§ 41

The Withering Away of the State

The proletarian state begins to wither from the moment it is created.

Every successful act reduces the conditions requiring state power:

  • expropriation weakens the bourgeoisie;
  • socialization eliminates capitalist ownership;
  • communal administration replaces bureaucracy;
  • equality removes class antagonisms;
  • international revolution reduces military danger;
  • education expands self-government;
  • free provision reduces conflict over survival.

The Commonalty grows throughout this process. Communes, councils, cooperatives, neighborhood assemblies and popular organizations absorb resource accounting, infrastructure management, ecological coordination, production planning, scientific administration, public safety, conflict mediation and cultural organization.

As classes disappear, repression loses its social object.

The state’s remaining functions become:

  • resource accounting;
  • infrastructure management;
  • ecological coordination;
  • production planning;
  • scientific administration;
  • conflict mediation.
Government over people becomes administration of things.

The state is not simply abolished by decree. It dies out because its coercive functions become unnecessary and its useful functions are absorbed by society.

42
§ 42

The Commonalty

The Commonalty develops wherever people directly organize their common life. It appears immediately in communes, workers’ councils, cooperatives, neighborhood assemblies, communal services and freely associated cultural and scientific organizations.

Its early forms coexist with the proletarian state. The state suppresses counterrevolution while the Commonalty administers the expanding sphere of associated life. The Commonalty grows unevenly, absorbs the state’s useful functions, removes intermediaries and generalizes free association.

The Commonalty progressively sublates the state: it preserves necessary social coordination, transforms that coordination into direct communal activity and discards its coercive political form.

This movement continues until free association becomes universal.

It consists of freely associated producers who collectively manage:

  • production;
  • distribution;
  • culture;
  • science;
  • nature;
  • communal affairs.

Participation is voluntary in the sense that no alien class force stands above society. People follow common arrangements because those arrangements express their own associated needs.

The Commonalty still has organization, rules, responsibilities and methods of resolving conflict. What it lacks is a separate class apparatus governing a subordinated population.

Commonification supplies its economic substance. Accumulated social wealth becomes the common property of freely associated producers, while labor accounting becomes a tool of planning rather than an individual claim over consumption.

43
§ 43

Ending Alienation

Under capitalism, people’s own products and institutions dominate them.

Workers create capital, but capital commands workers. People create markets, but market forces appear as uncontrollable laws. Society creates the state, but the state stands above society.

Communism ends this inversion.

People consciously control:

  • their products;
  • their workplaces;
  • their institutions;
  • their relations with nature;
  • their collective future.

Labor ceases being merely a means to survive. With shorter hours, free access to necessities and democratic production, creative activity becomes a field of human development.

People recognize social power as their own shared power.

The abolition of class society does not end history. It removes the ruling class’s power to obstruct development in order to preserve its position. Productive capacities and social organization enter a continuous feedback spiral: new capacities produce new forms of association, and new forms of association consciously guide further productive development.

Liberation remains an active movement through which people continually transform inherited conditions. New needs, discoveries and contradictions continue to arise, but no class possesses the material power to freeze society around its privileges.

44
§ 44

The Unity of Individual and Collective Freedom

Human beings develop through social relationships.

Language, science, art, knowledge and productive capacity are collective achievements. Individual freedom depends upon access to them.

Communism does not erase individuality. It creates the material conditions for individuality to flourish.

Each person gains:

  • education;
  • security;
  • free time;
  • cultural access;
  • meaningful work;
  • freedom of association;
  • the ability to develop many capacities.

The development of one person enriches the community, while the community provides the conditions for that development.

The abolition of rigid divisions of labor releases humanity’s adaptability. People cease being confined to one lifelong function and participate in intellectual, practical, artistic, administrative and scientific activity. Human species-being, the capacity to consciously create a shared social world, becomes concrete through free association.

Freedom becomes both:

  • freedom from exploitation, poverty and domination;
  • freedom to create, learn, associate and develop fully.

The Complete
Historical Movement

THIRTY-FOUR STEPS · A → G

The whole transition unfolds as follows:

  1. Capitalism develops social production while preserving private control.
  2. Its productive forces collide with capitalist relations.
  3. Workers organize through unions, councils, cooperatives and revolutionary movements.
  4. The working class seizes political power.
  5. The ready-made capitalist state machinery is broken.
  6. Communes, councils and militias become the organs of proletarian self-government.
  7. The Commonalty begins developing within these organs.
  8. Counterrevolution is defeated.
  9. Capitalists, landlords, rentiers and speculators are expropriated.
  10. Banks, infrastructure and concentrated industry enter public ownership.
  11. Smaller enterprises become cooperatives and communal units.
  12. Cooperatives federate and enter broader communal coordination.
  13. Workers’ management and social planning replace capitalist command.
  14. Money and production for exchange steadily contract.
  15. Labor accounting replaces monetary exchange.
  16. Common provision expands according to equity and need.
  17. Labor certificates cease regulating personal consumption and remain only as units of social planning.
  18. Commonification transforms accumulated private wealth into the Commonwealth.
  19. Production on demand replaces speculative overproduction.
  20. Wasteful labor disappears and working hours fall.
  21. Public transportation and balanced development overcome the class division between urban and rural life.
  22. Universal services remove dependence upon wages and markets.
  23. Education, science and cultural revolution transform inherited social relations.
  24. Gender, racial, national, colonial and other social hierarchies lose their material foundations.
  25. The division between intellectual and physical labor contracts.
  26. Ecological planning repairs the metabolic rift between society and nature.
  27. International revolution replaces the world market with global federation.
  28. Class distinctions disappear.
  29. The proletariat abolishes itself because everyone has become a freely associated producer.
  30. Repressive state functions vanish.
  31. Administrative functions are absorbed by society.
  32. The Party dissolves into universal communist consciousness and organization.
  33. The Commonalty becomes the direct self-government of humanity.
  34. Social organization, productive capacity, human need and nature enter a continuous process of conscious development.

The dictatorship of the proletariat therefore exists to make itself unnecessary. It concentrates the revolutionary power of the working class in order to destroy every material condition that separates rulers from ruled. Proletarian Self-Governance transforms that power into the direct activity of the working population. The Commonalty grows within that transformation, absorbs its social functions and dissolves its coercive form.

Its victory is complete only when no class remains to dictate, no separate state remains to enforce class domination, common wealth belongs to freely associated producers, and humanity consciously governs its common life through universal free association.
