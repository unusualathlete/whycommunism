The Real World Is Not a Dead Thing

An idea cannot lift a brick.

No bridge has ever risen because a design remained beautiful inside an engineer’s head. Stone has weight. Steel bends. Bodies tire. Rain enters every gap left open to it.

Yet no bridge rises without thought either. Someone imagines a crossing, surveys the ground, calculates a load, coordinates labour, corrects an error, and teaches other people how the structure works. Thought moves no material by magic. It moves material when embodied people organize activity through it.

Both facts matter. Lose the first and ideas become spirits ruling the world from outside it. Lose the second and human beings become objects knocked about by circumstances they can never consciously transform.

Dialectical materialism begins with the world independent of us, then places us fully inside it. Human beings are nature becoming capable of producing tools, language, institutions, knowledge, and purposes. We inherit a reality we did not choose. Through our life-activity we reproduce it, resist it, misunderstand it, and sometimes remake it. The changed world then acts back upon us.

That movement is the subject of this Study.

The double refusal

Materialism is often introduced as the opposite of idealism. That is correct, but incomplete.

Idealism makes thought, spirit, consciousness, or conceptual development primary. The world is explained from the movement of ideas. Hegel’s great achievement was to present reality as process rather than a warehouse of finished things. His dialectic made relation, contradiction, emergence, and historical movement intelligible. Its limit was that material life appeared as the expression of a movement whose ultimate subject was thought.

Feuerbach restored nature and embodied humanity against that idealism. Religion was no longer divine truth descending into history. It was humanity’s own power projected away from itself. But Feuerbach’s human being still mostly contemplated the world. Circumstances formed people while the activity through which people formed circumstances remained weakly developed.

Marx’s break in 1845 was therefore not a return to inert matter. It was a break with two incomplete pictures at once:

  1. Ideas do not create the material world from themselves.
  2. Material conditions do not act upon passive human clay.

The first of the Theses on Feuerbach attacks materialism that understands reality only as an object of contemplation. The third insists that “the educator must himself be educated.” The point is not a clever paradox. Whoever proposes to change other people is already a product of the same society. There is no teacher standing outside history with clean hands and a complete map.

People change through changing their conditions, and they change those conditions through powers they have developed within them. Marx calls this unity of self-change and the change of circumstances revolutionary practice.

This is why the word material cannot mean only hard, heavy, or visible. A language is material because bodies learn and speak it, institutions preserve it, and meanings carried through it organize conduct. A legal title is material because offices record it, courts recognize it, police enforce it, and people arrange their lives around it. A memory is material because a living brain reconstructs it, a body reacts through it, and relationships can be altered by it. None of these things floats outside nature.

Materialism gives reality priority over our account of it. Dialectics refuses to treat that reality as dead.

A totality is not a sack containing everything

The word totality can sound like a demand to know the whole universe before saying anything useful. It means nearly the opposite.

A totality is a structured whole whose parts become intelligible through their relations. The relations are not decorative lines drawn between things that were already complete. They help make each thing what it is.

A number on a payslip becomes a wage through the relation among labour-power, an employer, money, a working day, a labour market, property, law, household reproduction, and a whole history of class struggle. Remove enough of those relations and the wage ceases to be a wage. It becomes some other transfer of goods or claim upon production.

But we cannot begin by naming every relation at once. Marx describes the difficulty in the 1857 Introduction to the Grundrisse. If we begin with “the population,” we begin with what appears to be a concrete whole. Without class, wage-labour, capital, division of labour, exchange, and the state, however, population is still a blurred image. Analysis moves from that image toward simpler determinations. Thought then makes the return journey, reconstructing the concrete as a “concentration of many determinations, hence unity of the diverse.”

The method is closer to assembling a puzzle than tossing every piece from the box and declaring the picture complete.

The pieces are not equal clues. A corner tells us more about the boundary than a patch of sky. A repeated pattern may disclose how distant regions connect. We may first sort by colour, then discover that colour hid a more important shape. The completed picture was real before we understood it, but knowledge of it had to be produced.

This ascent from the abstract to the concrete explains why Capital begins with the commodity. Marx does not believe the whole of capitalism is secretly contained inside one coat. The commodity is a simple, recurrent social form through which use, exchange, private production, abstract labour, value, and money can be unfolded. It is a reproductive cell of a larger order.

Abstraction is the disciplined act of isolating a relation or determination so we can study it. A useful abstraction reveals movement in the real whole. A bad abstraction quietly removes the very relation that produces the result.

An unemployment rate may show a crisis while hiding unpaid care, discouraged workers, shortened hours, migration, and the difference between losing a job and never being admitted to one. Numbers remain useful once we learn what each number can see, what it cannot see, and how the missing relations alter its meaning.

Totality therefore requires boundaries, scales, and levels. Stafford Beer’s viable-system model gives this problem an operational language. In a socialist planning system, a national planner does not need to know every motion of every hand inside every workshop. A productive unit can operate as a black box at one level, communicating its inputs, outputs, capacities, limits, and failures, while containing its own rich internal organization. Open that box when persistent discrepancies or an exceptional success require investigation. Do not confuse knowledge of the whole with continuous command over every part.

A viable whole is differentiated. Its parts possess real powers, internal processes, and degrees of autonomy. Their relation to the whole is neither isolation nor absorption.

Reciprocal action has direction

Marx and Engels rejected a one-way history in which production presses a button and law, culture, religion, science, and politics emerge in a neat row.

Their phrase reciprocal action, Wechselwirkung, names the way several sides of social life act upon one another. In The German Ideology, after distinguishing production, needs, family, cooperation, and consciousness, they say that history must be shown in its totality, including “the reciprocal action of these various sides on one another.”

This does not mean everything causes everything equally.

A hurricane and a badly written newspaper column may both affect an election. It would be absurd to give them equal causal weight merely because both belong to the totality. A landlord, a tenant, a housing law, a bank, and a neighborhood organization act upon one another, but they do not begin with the same property, information, authority, or capacity to survive a conflict.

Reciprocal action has:

  • direction, because an order sent downward is not the same as information reported upward;
  • strength, because some forces dominate while others barely register;
  • timing, because a weak influence repeated for years can outlast a spectacular event;
  • mediation, because an economic pressure may act through law, family, race, religion, or political organization;
  • scale, because a force decisive in one workplace may be negligible across a continent; and
  • feedback, because an effect can return to alter the condition that produced it.

The same precision matters in organization. “Centralized” and “decentralized” tell us little by themselves. A flat organization can be ruled through control of information. A technically hierarchical process can remain politically subordinate to the people carrying it out.

What matters is whether vertical, horizontal, lateral, environmental, and crisis communication actually work. Can an operational unit report a limit upward? Can peers coordinate without waiting for permission? Can different sections preserve a common purpose? Can the organization learn from its environment? Can people closest to a crisis act before a distant office understands it?

Feedback is not merely a complaint box. Suppose one workshop produces more with the same inputs than every comparable workshop. An audit that only hunts misconduct will miss the most valuable discrepancy in the system. A learning audit enters the process, finds the labour-saving method, and makes that gain available elsewhere. The effect returns to reorganize the whole that made it possible.

This is reciprocal action made concrete: an account of the channels through which a relation moves, rather than the foggy statement that everything is connected.

Engels later had to defend this point against readers who reduced historical materialism to economic fatalism. In his 1890 letter to Joseph Bloch, he called the claim that the economic factor is the only determining one an “abstract, absurd phrase.” Production and reproduction set decisive conditions, but political forms, law, philosophy, inherited traditions, and struggles exert their own influence upon the course and form of events.

The materialist question is never only, “What economic interest is underneath this?” It is also, “Through which institutions, habits, conflicts, and forms of consciousness does this interest become effective, fail, or turn into something its first agents did not intend?”

Contradiction is not two words with “versus” between them

A contradiction is not any disagreement, contrast, or pair of opposites.

Night and a bicycle are different. They do not form a dialectical contradiction. A worker and an employer can disagree over the colour of a wall without disclosing the central contradiction of wage-labour.

A dialectical contradiction exists where connected sides presuppose one another while their relation also drives them against one another, or where a process reproduces conditions that undermine its own continuation.

Capital needs living labour. It can expand only by commanding labour and appropriating the surplus produced through it. Yet each capital is driven to cheapen labour, discipline it, replace it where profitable, and weaken the collective power on which production depends. The sides are not strangers. Capital and wage-labour are constituted through their antagonistic relation.

Lenin called the “splitting of a single whole and the cognition of its contradictory parts” the core of dialectics. The phrase is useful because it blocks an easy mistake. We do not take two finished things and ceremonially oppose them. We investigate a whole whose own organization generates distinct and conflicting tendencies.

This also explains why the schoolroom formula thesis, antithesis, synthesis is a poor map of Marx’s method. Marx does not place an idea on the table, invent its opposite, and fuse them by verbal skill. He investigates a material relation, follows its movement, and shows how its connected sides reproduce and transform one another. No sequence of three words can do that work for us.

Three examples show how much can be lost when contradiction becomes a slogan.

A productive power can become a threat

Imagine that a machine as capable as a Star Trek replicator is invented under capitalism. It could make wide classes of goods nearly effortless to reproduce. That capacity would not automatically deliver abundance. It would also threaten the property relations through which a ruling class controls production.

The likely struggle would concern special inputs, patents, licences, sealed hardware, software locks, and the right to inspect or reproduce the machine. The device could be crippled so that abundance remained dependent upon a proprietary gatekeeper. The familiar world of Apple offers a smaller image: enormous productive intelligence joined to deliberate restrictions on repair, modification, and access.

The contradiction is not “technology versus society.” Technology already has a social form. Productive power develops inside relations that may first nourish it and later confine it.

Ecological ruin need not rescue us by ruining profit

One current of ecosocialism, especially James O’Connor’s theory of capitalism’s second contradiction, emphasized how capital undermines its conditions of production and raises its own costs. That process is real. Ecological damage can interrupt supply, destroy infrastructure, exhaust workers, and devalue capital.

But ecological crisis does not have to become an economic crisis severe enough to stop accumulation. Firms can remain profitable while costs are pushed onto households, colonized regions, future generations, and the rest of nature. New fields of accumulation can grow around disaster. A system can destroy its long-term conditions of life while rewarding each successful act of destruction in the present.

This is why the metabolic rift reaches deeper than the hope that nature will force capital to correct itself. Contradiction creates pressure and motion. It does not promise a humane resolution.

Consciousness can know and not yet know

A person may understand intellectually that a boss depends upon the workforce and still feel authority as something natural and untouchable. Someone may reject a prejudice in words while their attention, fear, and reflexes continue to reproduce it. Human memory is reconstructive, not a file pulled unchanged from storage. Schemas guide perception by predicting what a situation means, and heavily weighted schemas can resist evidence that a person can state perfectly well.

The contradiction between explicit knowledge and embodied expectation is not proof that mind floats beyond matter. It shows that mind is a layered material process with a history. A sentence can change quickly. A nervous system, habit, institution, and relationship may not.

Motion is not a magic word

Dialectics is a theory of motion, but calling something in motion explains nothing by itself.

Useful dialectical categories name recurrent forms of development:

  • quantitative changes can accumulate until a relation changes quality;
  • a form can generate the pressures and capacities that negate it;
  • a transformation can preserve real achievements while overcoming the form that confined them; and
  • a temporary equilibrium can be the active balance of opposed forces rather than the absence of motion.

Water changing phase is the familiar example of quantity becoming quality. It is useful because the same substance behaves differently once definite relations of temperature and pressure cross a threshold. It becomes useless when treated as proof that every social accumulation must produce a revolutionary boiling point.

There is no universal political temperature of one hundred degrees.

In society, thresholds depend upon organization, memory, institutions, international conditions, opposing forces, and conscious decisions. Ten strikes may exhaust a movement. One strike may open a crisis. Counting cannot replace investigation.

The same caution applies to negation. Dialectical negation is not simple erasure. A form is transformed through relations that have developed within and against it. Sublation names a transformation that both overcomes and preserves.

If negation breaks down a wall, sublation does not restore the wall or celebrate the rubble. It uses the bricks to build a house whose form and purpose are new. The material is retained. The confining relation is not.

Capitalism socializes labour across the world while placing that cooperation under private command. Communism does not negate large-scale cooperation by scattering production back into isolated households. It preserves and develops the social power while abolishing its existence as capital. The content survives through a qualitative change of form.

These categories are instruments of attention, not laws that allow us to deduce history before studying it.

The discovery of Neptune offers a useful limit. When Uranus did not fit existing orbital calculations, astronomers did not immediately abandon celestial mechanics. They proposed another body whose gravity could account for the deviation, then looked for evidence and found it. The hypothesis earned its life by directing investigation toward something that could prove it wrong.

“Dialectics says so” earns nothing.

A method should not be discarded at the first anomaly, but neither may it convert every failure into secret confirmation. If a dialectical claim cannot tell us what relation to investigate, what evidence would count against it, or how its account differs from a rival explanation, it has become a protective vocabulary rather than a scientific method.

Mind is matter organized into a point of view

Mechanical materialism pictures causation as a row of falling blocks:

economy -> institution -> idea -> behaviour

This model preserves the priority of material conditions by removing the activity of every material being inside them.

Human consciousness is not a mirror waiting for reality to print an image upon it. A living mind selects, predicts, interprets, remembers, and acts. It is constructive without creating the world from nothing. What we sense underdetermines what we may conclude, but the independent world continues to test those conclusions through consequences.

Social life enters this construction from the beginning. Nobody invents a language alone. Nobody privately creates money, citizenship, a workweek, or the shame attached to unemployment. These forms are learned through repeated activity and organized relations. Once formed, they become real causal powers.

Money is the clearest example. It is not divine, yet it operates as a “real God” because the mediating activity of human beings has been alienated into it. A number in an account opens a home, buys medicine, commands labour, or leaves a person hungry. No banker has to believe money is sacred. The relation is reproduced whenever access to social wealth must pass through it.

An idea becomes powerful when it is carried by:

  • a trained body or settled habit;
  • a shared language;
  • an organized group;
  • records and instruments;
  • property and resources;
  • rules, offices, and sanctions; or
  • a repeated practice that makes one response feel natural and another almost unthinkable.

This is why changing property can outrun consciousness, and why changing consciousness without changing property can leave the old world standing. Productive relations, institutions, and organized thought act through one another without becoming causes of equal weight. History Does Not Keep Appointments develops that historical feedback in full. The methodological point here is narrower: once thought lives in bodies and institutions, it is among the causal powers materialism must explain.

Reality has priority. Ideas have efficacy. There is no contradiction between those claims once mind is understood as an activity within nature rather than a visitor from elsewhere.

Praxis is where a theory risks itself

Praxis is often weakened into a polite combination of theory and action. The word is sharper than that.

Praxis is purposeful activity through which people test an understanding, alter a real relation, confront consequences they did not foresee, and develop new capacities for the next action. The people who act are part of what changes.

flowchart LR
    A["Inherited conditions"] --> B["Perception, memory, and theory"]
    B --> C["Organized activity"]
    C --> D["Changed relations and institutions"]
    C --> E["Changed powers of the people acting"]
    D --> F["New consequences and evidence"]
    E --> B
    F --> B

The arrows do not form a smooth circle. A defeat can destroy an organization before it learns. A victory can reward a false explanation. People with power can suppress the evidence that would correct them. Praxis requires institutions capable of memory, criticism, and response.

Action alone does not make an idea true. A crowd can act upon a lie. A policy can meet its target by damaging everything the target failed to measure. “It worked” is incomplete until we ask: for whom, for how long, by what mechanism, at whose cost, and according to which purpose?

Theory alone does not produce communist consciousness either.

A person can internalize philosophical materialism and still enter a shop seeing every need through the money in their pocket. The material practice of exchange has already taught the reflex. Workers can recognize exploitation and reasonably pursue higher wages, safer work, and social-democratic protection without arriving automatically at the abolition of wage-labour. Immediate material interest does not contain a hidden communist programme waiting to be read correctly.

This is where a passive version of Marxism turns idealist while calling itself materialist. It waits for conditions to become severe enough that the working class will finally receive the correct revelation. Historical agency is alienated back onto capital. Capital must ripen the moment, strip away reform, expose itself completely, and awaken everyone at once.

Conditions matter. Timing matters. No organization can command a revolutionary situation into existence. But consciousness comes from life-activity guided by theory, not from waiting for capital to think on our behalf.

Workers develop political capacities before and between crises. They learn to investigate, coordinate, speak, trust, refuse, decide, remember, and act as a class by exercising those powers. A crisis can then accelerate capacities that already possess some organization and memory. Without them, suffering may produce resignation, flight, nationalism, religious revival, mutual aid, riot, fascism, or nothing coherent at all.

Praxis refuses both voluntarism and fatalism. We do not make history under conditions of our choosing. The conditions do not make history without us.

The dispute over nature

Does dialectics belong only to human history, or does it describe nature as well?

The argument has never been settled by repeating a party formula.

Engels tried to show that nature is not a collection of fixed substances but a history of transformations, interdependence, emergence, and qualitative change. In Anti-Dühring and the unfinished notes later published as Dialectics of Nature, he treated human society as one development within nature rather than a kingdom standing above it. He also gave the essential warning against turning dialectics into an a priori scheme: principles are not the starting point imposed upon nature and history. They are abstractions drawn from them.

The warning was often lost.

In the Soviet tradition, a small set of laws increasingly became a universal key, a tendency fixed in Stalin’s 1938 Dialectical and Historical Materialism. Stock examples from physics, biology, and politics were lined up beneath each law. Written the year before, Mao’s On Contradiction pressed in a more concrete direction by distinguishing principal and secondary contradictions, antagonistic and non-antagonistic forms, and changing dominant aspects. Yet the danger remained whenever categories were used to certify a line instead of investigate a relation.

A major current of Western Marxism moved in the opposite direction. Lukács’s History and Class Consciousness became a touchstone for restricting the dialectical method to social and historical reality. Later traditions often treated a dialectics of nature as a category mistake or as the philosophical cover for Soviet mechanism.

Both reactions can miss what matters.

Nature does not need Marxist vocabulary in order to move. A biologist does not improve an experiment by renaming every interaction a contradiction. But the natural sciences do need concepts adequate to open systems, reciprocal causation, emergence, historical development, and relations in which organism and environment transform one another.

Richard Levins and Richard Lewontin made this concrete in biology. An organism adapts to an environment, but it also selects, alters, and partly produces that environment. The inside and outside remain distinct without becoming independent. Cause travels both ways through a structured relation.

John Bellamy Foster and Paul Burkett recover the same strength in Marx’s ecology. Human labour is a metabolism within the larger metabolism of nature. Capitalist accounting treats the economy as if it were a closed circuit of value while drawing matter and energy from an earth system and returning waste to it. A dialectical, open-system account can hold value flows, material flows, class power, colonial displacement, and ecological thresholds within one analysis without reducing any of them to another.

Philosophy does not replace science here. It refuses the abstractions that make a destructive system look complete.

Three disciplines for using the method

Dialectical language is easy to imitate. A real analysis has to survive three disciplines.

Name the relation

Do not announce that two things contradict each other until the relation that joins them is clear. What does each side require from the other? Through which practice or institution are they made parts of one whole? What would have to change for the contradiction to take another form?

Follow the asymmetry

Reciprocal action does not erase domination. Identify which side controls property, time, communication, force, or the definition of success. Track the channels through which weaker forces can accumulate, combine, or be blocked. A loop drawn without power is not yet a social explanation.

Build correction into action

Ask what evidence could revise the analysis and who is allowed to produce it. Treat repeated failure as information about the plan, not automatically as sabotage. Investigate exceptional success as seriously as breakdown. Redesign positions and channels when they systematically hide error.

These disciplines join materialism, dialectics, and praxis. They keep the method answerable to a reality that no doctrine owns.

What the method can and cannot promise

Dialectical materialism can help us:

  • begin from a world that does not depend upon our belief;
  • understand mind as an active development within that world;
  • reconstruct concrete wholes from determinate relations;
  • distinguish reciprocal action from equal causation;
  • locate contradictions inside the processes that reproduce a system;
  • recognize qualitative transformation without inventing automatic thresholds; and
  • organize practice so that action produces knowledge and new human powers.

It cannot supply a universal list of contradictions before inquiry. It cannot prove that every crisis ends progressively. It cannot turn an analogy from boiling water into a strategy. It cannot guarantee that a class recognizes its historical possibility. It cannot exempt a cherished theory from correction. It cannot substitute a philosophy of motion for the work of moving.

Reality exists independently of us. That is why our mistakes have consequences.

We exist within reality as thinking, organized, transforming beings. That is why consequences can become knowledge rather than fate.

The real world beneath theory remains a moving totality in which thought takes a risk, acquires hands, and becomes one force among others.

Source notes

From Hegel’s movement to Marx’s practical materialism

  • G. W. F. Hegel, Science of Logic, published from 1812 to 1816. Hegel’s account of determinate negation, contradiction, and conceptual development is the inheritance Marx transforms rather than simply discards.
  • Ludwig Feuerbach, Principles of the Philosophy of the Future, 1843. Feuerbach turns philosophy toward embodied humanity and nature while leaving practical, historical activity underdeveloped.
  • Karl Marx, “Critique of the Hegelian Dialectic and Philosophy as a Whole”, in the 1844 Manuscripts. This is an early confrontation with Hegelian negation, alienation, and the need to ground human powers materially.
  • Karl Marx, Comments on James Mill, 1844, in the linked Clemens Dutt translation. Marx describes money as alienated human mediation that becomes a “real God,” the precise basis for the example of an idea acquiring causal power through a social relation.
  • Karl Marx, Theses on Feuerbach, 1845, in the linked 2002 translation by Cyril Smith, based on work with Don Cuckson. Theses I, II, III, and VIII establish sensuous human activity, practice as the testing of truth, self-change, and social life as practical.
  • Karl Marx and Friedrich Engels, The German Ideology, “Feuerbach” and the section on production, consciousness, and reciprocal action, written in 1845 to 1846, in the linked English text corresponding to Marx-Engels Collected Works, Volume 5. These sections place consciousness within the production of life without reducing historical movement to a single cause.

How an inquiry reconstructs a concrete totality

  • Karl Marx, The Poverty of Philosophy, Chapter II, “The Metaphysics of Political Economy”, 1847. Marx attacks Proudhon’s conversion of historical economic relations into self-moving logical categories and ridicules the thesis-antithesis-synthesis machinery attributed to dialectics.
  • Karl Marx, “Introduction” to the Grundrisse, 1857, in Martin Nicolaus’s 1973 translation. The section on the method of political economy distinguishes the represented whole from the concrete reconstructed in thought as a unity of many determinations.
  • Karl Marx, afterword to the second German edition of Capital, Volume I, 1873. Marx separates the method of inquiry from the method of presentation and describes the dialectic as critical and revolutionary because it grasps social forms in movement and transience.

Reciprocal determination against economic fatalism

  • Friedrich Engels, letter to Joseph Bloch, 21 to 22 September 1890, in the linked translation by J. I. Cheskis. Engels rejects the reduction of historical materialism to an economic factor acting alone and describes the interaction of political, legal, ideological, and inherited conditions.
  • Friedrich Engels, letter to Conrad Schmidt, 27 October 1890. The letter develops the relative autonomy and reciprocal effects of law, philosophy, state power, and economic development.
  • Friedrich Engels, letter to W. Borgius, 25 January 1894. Engels clarifies historical conditions, human action, and the distance between material determination and a mechanical prediction of individual events.
  • Stafford Beer, Brain of the Firm, second edition, first published in 1972 and substantially expanded in 1981. Beer’s recursive viable-system model provides the organizational language adapted here for differentiated levels, operational autonomy, communication, monitoring, and the relation of parts to a viable whole.
  • Stafford Beer, “The Viable System Model: Its Provenance, Development, Methodology and Pathology”, 1984. This concise retrospective is useful for separating the model’s actual recursive and methodological claims from a generic defence of hierarchy.

Contradiction as inquiry, not ceremonial opposition

  • Vladimir Lenin, “On the Question of Dialectics”, written in 1915 and published posthumously, in the linked Clemence Dutt translation from Collected Works, Volume 38. Lenin treats contradiction as the cognition of divided tendencies within a single whole, criticizes stock examples, and points to the commodity in Capital as a living beginning for analysis.
  • Mao Zedong, “On Contradiction”, 1937. Mao distinguishes principal from secondary contradictions, dominant aspects from subordinate ones, and antagonistic from non-antagonistic forms, insisting that contradictions change with concrete conditions.
  • Joseph Stalin, Dialectical and Historical Materialism, 1938. This influential codification presents dialectics through a compact set of universal features and shows both the pedagogical appeal and the danger of turning a method into a ready-made key.

Nature, open systems, and the argument over scope

  • Friedrich Engels, Anti-Dühring, 1877 to 1878, especially Part I on principles, motion, contradiction, freedom, and quantity and quality. Engels insists that principles must be abstracted from nature and history rather than imposed upon them.
  • Georg Lukács, History and Class Consciousness, 1923. Its restriction of dialectical method to historical and social reality became one influential Western Marxist answer to Engels.
  • Friedrich Engels, Dialectics of Nature, notes composed mainly in the 1870s and 1880s and first published in 1925. The unfinished manuscripts revealed Engels’s fuller attempt to extend dialectical analysis into natural science and became a central object of later dispute.
  • Richard Levins and Richard Lewontin, The Dialectical Biologist, 1985. Their essays develop reciprocal causation among organism and environment, part and whole, structure and process, while keeping the argument inside concrete biological research.
  • James O’Connor, “Capitalism, Nature, Socialism: A Theoretical Introduction”, 1988. O’Connor formulates the second contradiction through capital’s degradation and underproduction of its conditions of production, making explicit the position challenged in the ecological example above.
  • Bertell Ollman, Dance of the Dialectic: Steps in Marx’s Method, 2003. Ollman makes relations and levels of abstraction explicit, illuminating why totality is a method of determinate inquiry rather than an order to mention everything.
  • John Bellamy Foster and Paul Burkett, Marx and the Earth: An Anti-Critique, 2016. Foster and Burkett reconstruct Marx’s open-system materialism through thermodynamics, social metabolism, value, ecology, and the history of Marxist natural science.
