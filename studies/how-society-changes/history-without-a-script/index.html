History Does Not Keep Appointments

Marx left us two sentences that are often made to fight.

In 1852:

“Men make their own history, but they do not make it as they please.”

In 1859:

“No social order ever perishes before all the productive forces for which there is room in it have developed.”

The first sentence sounds open. People act, improvise, organize, fail, and begin again.

The second sounds closed. A mode of production seems to remain until history has completed a hidden checklist.

If we keep only the first, history can become a story about courage and betrayal. Material conditions fade behind will. If we keep only the second, people become passengers waiting for the productive forces to announce the next station.

Historical materialism begins by refusing that choice.

People make history through powers and relations they inherit. Those powers and relations exist only because people keep reproducing, contesting, repairing, and transforming them. Conditions act through human beings, and human activity becomes part of the conditions faced by everyone else.

There is structure without a script.

The map that became a timetable

The 1859 preface to A Contribution to the Critique of Political Economy offers one of Marx’s most compressed maps of historical change.

People enter relations of production that do not depend on any one person’s wishes. These relations form an economic structure. Law, politics, institutions, and forms of consciousness arise upon that basis. As productive forces develop, they can come into conflict with the relations through which they have operated. Relations that once helped organize production become fetters. An epoch of social revolution begins.

Every word in that chain matters.

So does what the chain does not say.

It does not say that a machine overthrows a government. It does not say that every crisis produces a revolution. It does not say that every society climbs the same staircase. It does not say that people can identify a date when a mode of production has exhausted every last possibility.

Marx was giving the guiding result of an investigation, not publishing a railway schedule.

A compressed map becomes mechanical when its arrows are treated as one-way commands:

  1. technology develops;
  2. property becomes outdated;
  3. the old society collapses; and
  4. a new society takes its place.

Actual history has never moved with that obedience. Relations of production influence which technologies are funded, hidden, discarded, militarized, or made profitable. States reorganize infrastructure. Colonial power destroys one productive system and forces another into export. Workers defend skills, resist machinery used against them, and sometimes seize the machine for a different purpose. Ecological damage removes possibilities that a simple progress chart assumes will remain available.

The map has to move.

The base is not a basement

The familiar picture of base and superstructure can make society look like a house.

The economy sits below. Politics and culture sit above. Causes travel upward. Effects never return.

That is not a materialist model of a living society.

The base is the way people produce and reproduce life. It includes labour, land, tools, technical knowledge, bodies, care, energy, transport, cooperation, ecological conditions, and the relations through which these powers are organized. The superstructure includes political institutions, law, education, religion, media, family forms, and the ideas through which people understand and fight over their world.

These are distinguishable. They are not sealed.

Text alternative: Productive forces operate through relations of production. Those relations help shape institutions and culture. Institutions form habits, knowledge, obedience, and resistance. Organized human activity then changes both the productive forces and the relations that govern them.

Engels later described this as reciprocal action among unequal forces. The economic movement remains decisive because every society must produce and reproduce life. Yet political power develops movement of its own. Law changes investment. War redirects industry. A school system expands technical knowledge. A racist order divides labour. A strike interrupts production. A revolution changes property.

The “last instance” never arrives as an hour in which every other force politely leaves the room.

The ruling class builds dams

A ruling class does more than own.

It organizes law, state power, education, administration, knowledge, borders, and ordinary expectations around the reproduction of its relations. When a productive capacity threatens those relations, the ruling class can block it, redirect it, or allow only the form that preserves command.

The image of a dam is useful here because a dam does not abolish movement. It contains and channels movement through a structure built for a purpose.

Labour-saving machinery can free time. Under capital, it is directed toward reducing labour costs, removing workers, intensifying the remaining work, and expanding accumulation. Digital communication can place the world’s knowledge within reach. Private ownership turns access into subscriptions, patents, surveillance, and rent. A global transport system can distribute necessities according to need. Production for exchange sends them toward profitable demand.

The capacity remains real. Its social form bends it.

The dam is reinforced through more than police power. An economics curriculum can present private accumulation as a natural law. A workplace can make managerial knowledge appear mysterious. A legal system can turn an inherited claim into an owner’s obvious right. A media system can describe resistance as disorder and the daily force of property as peace.

These institutions are not decorations on the economy. They help reproduce the relation. They also contain workers, students, officials, scientists, artists, and communities whose activity can open cracks in it.

The river is not destiny. The dam is not eternal.

Four meanings hidden inside “necessary”

Political arguments become confused when several kinds of necessity are treated as one.

Kind of necessity What it means Example What it does not mean
Material necessity A process has conditions that cannot be wished away. People need food, water, care, energy, and an inhabitable ecology. One political institution has been selected by nature.
Relational necessity A social relation compels recurring conduct. Capital must seek accumulation or lose ground to competing capitals. Every firm succeeds or every event follows one schedule.
Conditional necessity A chosen purpose creates requirements. If everyone is to have safe water, sources, pipes, testing, repair, and public reporting are necessary. Only one design can meet those requirements.
Historical necessity Existing contradictions make a transformation necessary for a stated human end. Class domination must be abolished if associated producers are to govern their common powers. The transformation will occur without organization, judgment, and struggle.

Inevitability is different. It claims that an outcome will happen regardless of the action that should produce it.

Communism can be necessary without being inevitable.

It is necessary in the same strong sense that ending fossil dependence is necessary for a livable climate. Necessity tells us what the problem demands. It does not promise that society will do it.

This distinction protects both sides of revolutionary thought. Communism is not one pleasant option beside endless capitalism. Capitalist accumulation collides with human development, democratic control, and the ecological conditions of life. Yet contradiction does not secretly vote for the better outcome.

Socialism or extinction names an alternative, not a prophecy that socialism wins.

A contradiction opens a field of action

A contradiction is not a trapdoor through which one society falls into another.

It is a relation whose connected sides produce opposing pressures.

Capital depends on labour and seeks to reduce its dependence on workers. Production becomes more social while control remains private. Each capital must raise productivity, yet the resulting expansion can deepen overproduction and crisis. Accumulation develops productive capacities while exhausting workers and nature.

These contradictions generate tendencies. They do not act alone.

Consider automation at a port. The same technical change can lead toward:

  • shorter working time with no loss of access;
  • layoffs and intensified labour;
  • deskilling under centralized software;
  • safer work with stronger worker control;
  • relocation to a less organized workforce;
  • lower transport costs that expand wasteful circulation; or
  • common logistical capacity used to guarantee necessities.

The machine does not contain a preferred politics.

The relation among owners, workers, states, communities, supply chains, ecological limits, and organized power gives the change its historical direction.

This is why a tendency must always be studied with its counteracting influences. Capital’s drive can be delayed, displaced, or transformed. A reform can stabilize a system and enlarge the capacity to fight it. A crisis can produce solidarity, fascism, apathy, war, or revolution. Each result changes the terrain on which the next contradiction develops.

History rhymes because relations leave patterns.

It does not repeat because every repetition inherits the consequences of the last one.

Class struggle is inside the mechanism

The phrase “class struggle is the engine of history” is sometimes read as if struggle were a motor added to an otherwise complete machine.

Class struggle is already inside the mode of production.

Workers and owners struggle over the length and intensity of the working day, the organization of the labour process, the use of machinery, access to knowledge, wages, safety, housing, land, taxation, public provision, and state power. These conflicts influence accumulation itself. They alter costs, skills, institutions, technologies, family life, migration, and political organization.

When workers shorten the day, capital does not simply lose several hours. It may reorganize production, introduce new machinery, seek another workforce, or move elsewhere. Workers then confront a changed process. A victory becomes part of the next condition.

Consciousness enters the same circuit. People do not first receive a complete understanding and then act. They learn through action, bring inherited ideas into struggle, discover connections, remember defeats, build organizations, and return to the world with greater or lesser power.

Praxis is not the escape of free will from material determination.

It is material determination becoming conscious, organized, and capable of answering back.

This also explains why an organization cannot emancipate a class from outside. If working people remain objects receiving commands from a body that claims to know history’s law, the relation of rule survives inside the attempted liberation. The educator must be educated. The party must learn from the class whose dispersed experience it helps connect. Strategy must be corrected by results.

Will matters because organized will is a material force.

It cannot make impossible conditions disappear. It can discover possibilities that passive observation never reveals.

Russia broke the staircase

The Russian Revolution forced this problem into the open.

A rigid reading of historical stages said Russia required a long bourgeois development before socialist revolution could become a serious question. In 1917, workers, soldiers, and peasants acted inside a society that combined concentrated modern industry, a vast peasantry, imperial war, autocracy, national oppression, land hunger, and revolutionary organization.

Young Gramsci called October a “revolution against Capital.” His phrase captured the shock, but it risked turning Marx into the mechanical reading being challenged. Marx had already warned against converting a history of Western European capitalism into a universal path. In his drafts to Vera Zasulich, he examined whether the Russian rural commune might develop through a different route if joined to revolution and the achievements of world production.

Will did not defeat material conditions. The conditions were more numerous than the staircase allowed.

Althusser later used overdetermination for the way a contradiction is shaped by the whole social formation: state structure, inherited institutions, national history, ideology, international war, uneven development, and other conflicts all act within the decisive relation. His Russian example showed why the capital-labour contradiction never appears in a pure laboratory form.

That correction matters. So does its limit. Historical actors cannot disappear into “structures” that act like persons. Workers, peasants, soldiers, parties, colonial peoples, and rulers made decisions. Their organizations and inherited ideas were among the material forces composing the crisis.

There was no choice between structure and agency.

The revolution was their relation at a breaking point.

There is no universal staircase

Capitalism created a world market by connecting unequal histories through conquest, slavery, colonial extraction, migration, debt, and trade.

That world does not contain separate countries waiting at different steps of one ladder. Development in an imperial center can depend on the enforced underdevelopment of another region. A railway may represent technical expansion and colonial seizure at the same time. A mine may connect advanced machinery to coerced labour, poisoned water, and a community stripped of land.

Productive forces therefore cannot be measured by counting factories alone.

A system that raises output while destroying soil, knowledge, health, care, or climatic stability may be expanding one capacity by devouring others. A supposedly advanced society can possess immense machinery and remain unable to meet basic human needs because production answers profit.

This changes the meaning of communist development.

The imperial core must contract destructive throughput. Formerly colonized regions need expanded housing, sanitation, electricity, cooling, medicine, transport, education, food security, and independent technical capacity. Global useful production can rise while the richest regions undergo material degrowth.

One command to “produce less” would freeze colonial inequality.

One command to “develop the productive forces” without asking which forces, for whom, and at whose ecological cost would reproduce it.

Historical materialism studies the whole relation.

A form can win and later become a fetter

Marx’s image of the fetter applies beyond capitalism.

A relation is not progressive forever because it once broke an older barrier. Its historical character depends on what it now allows people to do and what it prevents.

Private property once displaced earlier forms and helped concentrate production. It became a fetter on social powers that could be commonly directed. Nationalization can break the private command of major capital, then become a fetter if administrators rule producers through a state title. Labour certificates can displace money and make contribution visible, then become a fetter when common provision makes individual payment pointless. A party can preserve programme and connect struggle, then become a fetter if it substitutes its own apparatus for working-class self-government. Emergency command can defend a revolution, then become a fetter if danger gives a permanent caste its reason to exist.

The potato explains the principle better than a ceremonial formula.

When society has more potatoes than it knows what to do with, demanding an individual certificate for each one obstructs access and wastes administration. The certificate did useful work at an earlier moment. Its success helped create the condition in which it should disappear.

This is historical materialism turned inward.

Communist institutions are not exempt from history. They must be judged by whether they develop common capacity, equity, free association, and conscious control. A form that prepares its own disappearance is not weak. It has understood its purpose.

Even socialist relations can become fetters.

Classless society is not the end of history

The abolition of class society removes a specific brake on development: a ruling class with the material power to preserve relations that secure its rule.

It does not abolish change, necessity, disagreement, ecology, error, aging, grief, technical limits, or the arrival of problems no one can currently imagine.

The difference is who can act on them.

Under class rule, collective powers return as alien forces: market compulsion, private property, administrative command, state coercion, and institutions protected from the people who reproduce them. In a society of associated producers, social organization and productive capacity can enter a conscious feedback spiral.

New capacities permit new forms of association. New forms of association guide the development of capacities. People can still be conservative. They can still make mistakes. What disappears is the economic foundation that lets one class impose its interest as society’s permanent limit.

This is why communism is not the last page of a book.

It is the end of human prehistory in Marx’s precise sense: the end of social development ruled by alien class powers that people produce but do not consciously govern.

What lies beyond cannot be specified honestly from here. New humans, formed through different labour, education, relationships, technologies, and ways of living with nature, will possess needs and capacities that our present society cannot predict. They will meet necessities we do not know and make freedoms we lack the words to describe.

A theory that claims to write their history in advance would restore the script it has just rejected.

Direction without destiny

Communism remains a direction grounded in the social character of modern labour, accumulated knowledge, planetary cooperation, and the working class’s position within production and reproduction. Its necessity grows from the collision between those powers and capitalist command.

Direction also contains judgment.

No tendency in the economy can decide that hunger, domination, ecological ruin, and lives consumed by alienated labour should end. Human beings make that judgment from within material life. They turn it into programme, organization, struggle, and institutions.

The final aim then creates requirements:

  • productive wealth must become common;
  • the working class must govern itself rather than receive rule from a substitute;
  • public power must be reabsorbed into society;
  • planning must remain answerable to the people living inside it;
  • imperial inequality must be overcome rather than frozen; and
  • transitional forms must surrender functions when common capacity makes them unnecessary.

These commitments follow from the relation being abolished and the freedom being built. They do not guess at the furniture of a future society.

History does not guarantee this movement.

History has produced the powers, contradictions, dangers, and people through which it can be made.

The appointment no one will keep for us

There is no date on which capitalism becomes mature enough to fall.

There is no crisis severe enough to organize its own answer.

There is no productive force that can choose what it will serve.

There is no final stage in which people stop inheriting problems and making history under conditions they did not select.

Historical materialism gives neither passive optimism nor dignified despair. It gives a harder freedom: the ability to understand necessities, discover openings, organize powers, test a direction, and change the conditions from inside them.

Communism is not waiting at the end of history.

It exists as the real movement through which people abolish class society and bring their common powers under conscious control. If that movement succeeds, history will not be over.

For the first time, it will fully belong to the people making it.

Source notes

The argument as it developed

  • Karl Marx, Theses on Feuerbach, written in 1845, especially the first and third theses on practical-critical activity, changed circumstances, and self-change.
  • Karl Marx and Friedrich Engels, The German Ideology, chapter I, written in 1845 to 1846, on the production of life, social relations, consciousness, division of labour, and communism as the real movement abolishing the present state of things.
  • Karl Marx, The Poverty of Philosophy, chapter II, 1847, on historical economic categories and the rejection of an abstract dialectical formula imposed on them.
  • Karl Marx and Friedrich Engels, Manifesto of the Communist Party, 1848, on class struggle, world-market development, crisis, productive powers, and bourgeois relations becoming too narrow for the wealth they created.
  • Karl Marx, The Eighteenth Brumaire of Louis Bonaparte, 1852, a concrete political history of inherited conditions, class fractions, representation, organization, contingency, and state power.
  • Karl Marx, preface to A Contribution to the Critique of Political Economy, 1859, the compressed statement of productive forces, relations of production, superstructure, consciousness, fetters, and social revolution examined throughout this Study.
  • Karl Marx, Capital, Volume I, chapter 32, 1867, on capitalist private property, socialized labour, expropriation, and the negation of the negation.
  • Friedrich Engels, Anti-Dühring, 1877 to 1878, on freedom and necessity, historical development, force, class, productive relations, and the transformation rather than restoration of earlier forms.

Corrections to the mechanical reading

  • Karl Marx, drafts and final reply to Vera Zasulich, 1881, on the Russian rural commune and the refusal to turn Western European capitalist development into a universal historical route.
  • Friedrich Engels, letter to Joseph Bloch, September 21, 1890, on determination in the last instance, reciprocal influence, inherited conditions, and the resultant of many acting wills.
  • Friedrich Engels, letter to Conrad Schmidt, October 27, 1890, on the reciprocal relation between economic movement and political power.
  • Georgi Plekhanov, The Development of the Monist View of History, 1895, a major systematic defense of historical materialism that also became part of the later dispute over necessity, stages, and political passivity.
  • Antonio Gramsci, “The Revolution against Capital”, 1917, an early response to the Russian Revolution and to a stage-bound reading of Marx.
  • Louis Althusser, “Contradiction and Overdetermination”, 1962, on the specificity of historical contradictions, the Russian conjuncture, superstructural effects, and the absence of a pure economic contradiction.
