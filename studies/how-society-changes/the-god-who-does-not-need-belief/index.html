The God Who Does Not Need Belief

A person can stop believing in God and still organize every need around an altar.

Rent, food, medicine, transport, education, free time, and the power to begin almost anything must pass through money. Nobody has to think money is holy. The relation works whether anyone believes in it or not.

This is the disturbing center of the argument.

Traditional religion gives human powers an imagined life outside humanity. Capitalism goes further. It places those powers in social forms that really do command us. Money does not merely symbolize access to the work of others. Under capitalist relations, it controls that access. Capital does not merely represent accumulated social power. It hires, closes, builds, removes, and directs.

An atheist can reject every god in heaven while obeying this earthly one without hesitation.

That does not make religious belief foolish. It makes the criticism of religion more difficult. Religion can console people, justify rulers, preserve memory, organize care, sanctify hierarchy, teach endurance, and carry rebellion. Its contradictions come from the world that produces it.

Materialism does not begin by asking how to defeat a belief.

It asks why human beings need the belief, which real powers appear inside it, and why those powers do not yet belong to them.

The pain inside the halo

Marx’s most famous sentence about religion is usually cut in half.

Religion is called “the opium of the people,” and the reader is invited to imagine a fraud imposed upon the ignorant. But the passage first calls religion an expression of suffering and a protest against suffering. It is “the heart of a heartless world.”

Opium in Marx’s time was a medicine as well as a narcotic. It dulled pain it could not cure.

This changes the question. If a person reaches for consolation because the world is unbearable, taking away the consolation does not make the world bearable. It leaves the wound exposed.

Marx therefore writes about imaginary flowers on a chain. Criticism should not strip away the flowers so that a person bears an undecorated chain. It should help them throw off the chain and reach the living flower.

Religion can be illusion without being meaningless.

It may carry:

  • grief that has nowhere else to speak;
  • a demand that the humiliated possess dignity;
  • a memory of common life;
  • hope that present injustice is not final;
  • rules of care and mutual obligation;
  • obedience to an authority declared sacred; and
  • protest expressed in the only language a people has been allowed to share.

A materialist account must explain the consolation, the protest, and the command. Reducing the whole formation to priestly deception explains too little. Treating it as a private mystery beyond social criticism explains too little as well.

The halo belongs to a real vale of tears.

Human powers leave and return as commands

Alienation is often described as a feeling of distance. Here it means something more exact.

People create a social power. That power becomes separated from them, takes on an independent form, and returns as an authority over its creators.

Religious alienation gives this movement a visible shape. Human imagination, love, judgment, terror, law, memory, and collective possibility are gathered into gods, sacred texts, priesthoods, places, rituals, and institutions. What humanity can do appears as the gift or command of something beyond humanity.

This process is never identical in every religion or period. A village rite, an imperial church, a liberation theology, a millenarian revolt, and a private practice of mourning do not perform one political function. Religion can bless a throne and give the oppressed a language with which to condemn it.

The common material question is not, “Are religious people progressive or reactionary?”

It is:

  1. Which powers have people placed outside themselves?
  2. Which institutions interpret and administer those powers?
  3. Which suffering, dependence, or hope keeps the relation alive?
  4. Does the form teach people to act together, to wait, to obey, to endure, or to revolt?
  5. What would it take for people to recognize the power as their own?

The fifth question cannot be answered by explanation alone.

Money becomes a real God

In his 1844 notes on James Mill, Marx turns from heavenly mediation to economic mediation.

People make different products that must complement one another. The social act joining those products could be a direct relation among people. Under private exchange, that mediating activity becomes the attribute of money, an object outside them.

Marx calls the mediator a “real God.”

Marx is not decorating an economic argument with a religious insult. Calling the mediator a god identifies a power that is real.

Money stands between a person and the social world:

Text alternative: Human labour, care, knowledge, and production acquire social form through money, value, and capital. Access to social life then depends on that mediator. People arrange their conduct around the gate, reproducing the human powers that confront them as accumulated command.

A god interpreted by priests still needs people to speak in its name. Money can enforce the relation through ordinary transactions carried out by people who disagree about everything else.

A landlord may be kind. The mortgage still falls due.

A company director may care about the climate. Competition still punishes a firm that accepts costs its rivals displace.

A worker may understand commodity fetishism. At the shop, needs still appear as prices compared with the money available.

The relation is strongest where it feels least religious. It requires no hymn, no creed, and no moment of conscious submission. Its ritual is the normal day.

This is why simple unbelief cannot complete the criticism of religion.

Atheism can leave the altar standing

Atheism negates theism. It says there is no divine being ruling the world.

That can be true and liberating. It can break the authority of a church, defend scientific inquiry, and free a person from fear. But a negative answer remains tied to the question it rejects.

Anton Pannekoek drew the political distinction sharply. Atheism treats religion as a major cause of ignorance and oppression. Materialism treats religion as a product of social relations and asks which real forces govern human life.

The difference can be stated simply:

Approach First question Main danger
Theism What does divine authority require? Human judgment may be surrendered to a sacred mediator.
Atheism Does divine authority exist? Refuting the symbol may leave the social relation untouched.
Materialism Which real relations produce the symbol and govern life? Explanation can become passive if it never develops organized action.

Materialism is neither a compromise between belief and unbelief nor a polite refusal to decide. It changes the object of inquiry.

If religion grows from insecurity, alienation, class rule, incomprehensible social forces, and the inability to govern a common fate, then anti-religious propaganda attacks the effect while preserving the cause.

Worse, imposed atheism can reproduce the structure it claims to destroy. An authority declares the correct belief, punishes dissent, and commands a transformation of consciousness from above.

Engels mocked this in 1874 when Blanquist refugees proposed abolishing God by decree and forbidding religious organization. Persecution, he argued, is an excellent way to strengthen a disliked conviction.

A free society cannot be produced by a state examination in unbelief.

Knowing is not yet being able to act

Materialist explanation meets its own limit.

A person can understand that money is a social relation and still need money. A worker can know that production depends on the workforce and still experience the boss’s authority as immovable. A communist can explain that crises create openings and spend years waiting for history to act first.

Correct knowledge does not automatically become an instinct, a capacity, or a factor of will.

Consciousness is formed through life-activity. It grows through acting, testing, remembering, teaching, coordinating, failing, repairing, and discovering what people can do together.

This point cuts against two easy stories.

The first says that workers become communist simply by suffering exploitation. They do not. A person may respond to exploitation through solidarity, private escape, reform, prejudice, religion, nationalism, resignation, or revolt.

The second reduces the missing element to communist information. Yet a person can repeat a complete theory while the reflexes of everyday life remain organized by value, competition, hierarchy, and fear.

Praxis joins knowledge to developed power.

It changes the world and the people acting within it. A meeting teaches only part of what a strike committee learns by coordinating food, communication, risk, disagreement, and time. A text can explain self-government. An institution people actually govern teaches them that collective judgment can have consequences.

The factor of will grows when experience and organization support the belief that people possess the right and capacity to act as historical subjects. Heroic confidence added from nowhere cannot supply it.

Capital continually teaches the opposite lesson. Social power belongs to the market. Feasibility belongs to the budget. Initiative belongs to management. History belongs to forces too large for anyone to direct.

Revolutionary consciousness must become more than a fact a person agrees with. It must become a power they know how to exercise.

A crisis is not a school with automatic lessons

A hot point of struggle can create astonishing organization. People learn in days what ordinary life concealed for years.

But a crisis can end before the new capacity becomes durable.

Organizations formed inside one conflict may lose membership, memory, and confidence when capital stabilizes. A later crisis then meets people who remember the event but no longer possess its living connections. The Russian sequence from 1905 through war and 1917 shows the exceptional force of organization and memory carried across renewed upheaval. That exceptional sequence cannot become a timetable other countries copy.

This is the problem behind the unfinished notes on dual revolutions and prolonged consciousness. How does a movement preserve active knowledge between moments when immediate conditions reward it?

Trade-union action can win real gains. Social democracy can secure protections. Workers may achieve many direct interests without concluding that wage-labour itself must end. None of this proves that they have been bribed away from an essence hidden inside them. It shows that immediate interest and communist purpose are not identical.

A revolutionary organization exists partly to hold the longer relation in view. It connects a present struggle to the total class relation without dismissing the present need. It preserves memory, develops judgment, and makes capacities available before the next emergency.

If it does this only through doctrine, it becomes a reading circle standing outside life.

If it does this only through immediate struggle, it may disappear into the movement of the day.

The hard problem is how knowledge becomes a durable form of collective life.

What early Christianity can and cannot teach

Engels approached early Christianity as a historian of movements, not as a theologian looking for a communist scripture.

He saw a movement of enslaved, dispossessed, and conquered people confronting an empire vastly stronger than any isolated group. It spread a shared promise across populations divided by local origin and condition. It created communities, correspondence, discipline, sacrifice, mutual obligation, courage under persecution, and confidence that the existing world would not have the final word.

It also placed liberation beyond this world.

That limit belonged to its historical conditions. The oppressed of the Roman Empire did not possess the productive powers or class position from which to reorganize society through associated production. Salvation became a transformation after death because a material path to universal emancipation was not yet available.

The comparison illuminates four organizational powers:

  • a movement can make distant people part of one moral world;
  • shared practices can preserve commitment when immediate evidence offers defeat;
  • a promise can make sacrifice intelligible across long periods; and
  • mass movements develop through disagreement, competing interpretations, and forms no founder fully controls.

It also gives four warnings:

  • a world beyond can replace the transformation of this one;
  • certainty can harden into dogma;
  • interpreters can become a specialized authority over ordinary members; and
  • a movement of the oppressed can become an institution reconciled with ruling power.

Communism should not imitate religion. The comparison instead breaks the pretense that an organization can survive on correct propositions alone.

Orthopraxy enters as a question

Orthopraxy means right practice. The emphasis falls on what people repeatedly do, not only on what they profess to believe.

The proposal grows from a real problem. Capitalist relations acquire strength through repetition. Buying, selling, competing, obeying, measuring time, guarding property, and treating other people as costs become ordinary before anyone builds a theory to justify them.

Could communist organization develop practices through which cooperation, courage, honesty, study, criticism, care, and collective responsibility become equally ordinary?

The question is worth preserving. The answer is not finished.

Any serious answer has to survive several dangers:

  1. Ritual can preserve memory, or replace understanding.
  2. A named virtue can guide conduct, or become a weapon used by a clique.
  3. Collective standards can protect an organization, or invade private life.
  4. Discipline can make action dependable, or train obedience to leaders.
  5. Moral language can resist capitalist calculation, or conceal material disagreement beneath accusations of vice.

No organization is value-neutral. A group that claims to have no moral commitments still rewards some conduct and punishes other conduct. It decides, openly or silently, whether ambition, truthfulness, cruelty, care, loyalty, dissent, and opportunism have consequences.

The mature institutional question concerns who defines the values, how conduct is judged, which evidence counts, whether criticism can reach leaders, and how an unjust decision is repaired.

Communist practice cannot be a private priesthood of virtue. Its standards must be public, connected to the organization’s purpose, open to criticism, and enforced through procedures that do not turn confession, humiliation, or personal loyalty into proof.

This is where orthopraxy must meet self-government or be rejected.

Religion in a society becoming free

Communism does not need to force people into atheism.

It separates religious authority from public command. It ends compulsory funding, religious tests for office, clerical control of education, and every legal privilege that lets one belief govern another person’s life. It protects scientific inquiry, criticism, private conviction, communal worship, and the freedom to leave.

More importantly, it changes the conditions from which religious alienation draws its necessity.

People gain conscious power over production. Care does not depend on charity. Death is no longer made needlessly early by poverty. Institutions become intelligible and answerable. Social forces cease appearing as markets, offices, or rulers whose decisions cannot be challenged.

Religion may change, diminish, persist, divide, or take forms no present theory predicts.

The state cannot settle that future by decree. If people cease needing a sacred mediator, it will be because their common powers have become earthly, visible, and their own.

The goal is not a population that passes an atheism test.

It is humanity no longer kneeling before powers humanity made.

What is settled, and what remains open

The developed argument establishes several strong conclusions:

  • religion must be explained through the real life that produces it;
  • consolation and protest cannot be reduced to stupidity;
  • criticism of belief cannot replace transformation of the condition that needs belief;
  • money and capital are alienated human powers with real causal force;
  • atheist consciousness can coexist comfortably with commodity fetishism;
  • communist consciousness develops through organized life-activity guided by theory;
  • crises do not reliably create or preserve that consciousness; and
  • compulsory atheism reproduces alienated authority.

The next step remains open.

The notes propose orthopraxy, explicit virtue, shared ritual, and a revolutionary teleology as possible supports for durable communist consciousness. They also recognize that these forms can reproduce dogma, hierarchy, moral policing, and displaced power.

A finished answer would need to show:

  • which practices develop real capacity rather than symbolic purity;
  • how an organization names destructive conduct without pathologizing dissent;
  • how shared purpose survives without a sacred center;
  • how standards remain revisable without becoming empty;
  • how private life is protected from organizational reach; and
  • how the whole structure prepares people for self-government instead of dependence upon it.

That inquiry belongs with pedagogy, party life, criticism, institutional design, and the factor of will. It should not be smuggled into a final paragraph as if the bridge had already been built.

The unresolved problem is the most valuable one.

Capitalism does not rule through argument alone. It forms reflexes, incentives, habits, institutions, and a world in which its categories feel natural.

A communist movement must become materially capable of producing another consciousness without becoming another church above the people.

Religion as suffering, protest, and alienated power

  • Karl Marx, “Introduction” to A Contribution to the Critique of Hegel’s Philosophy of Right, written in 1843 to 1844. This is the source of religion as real suffering and protest, the heart of a heartless world, the imaginary flowers on the chain, and criticism turning from heaven toward law and politics.
  • Karl Marx, Theses on Feuerbach, 1845, especially Theses III, VI, and VIII. These locate the human essence in social relations and join changed circumstances to human self-change.
  • Friedrich Engels, Anti-Dühring, Part III, Chapter V, 1878. Engels explains religion as a fantastic reflection of natural and social powers dominating everyday life and argues that its basis disappears only when society consciously governs those powers.

Money, fetishism, and the real God

  • Karl Marx, Comments on James Mill, 1844. Marx describes human social mediation becoming an attribute of money, an alien mediator and “real God” with power over the objects and people it connects.
  • Karl Marx, Capital, Volume I, Chapter 1, Section 4, 1867. Commodity fetishism gives relations among people the appearance of relations among things because private labours acquire social validity through exchange.

Atheism, materialism, and religious freedom

  • Anton Pannekoek, “Socialism and Religion”, 1907. Pannekoek connects the disappearance of religion to transformed social relations and defends religion as a private matter rather than a target of socialist coercion.
  • Anton Pannekoek, “Communism and Religion”, 1936. This essay distinguishes materialism from dogmatic atheism and treats both religious and anti-religious consciousness as historically produced.
  • Anton Pannekoek, “Religion”, 1947, appended to Workers’ Councils. This is the immediate source for the distinction between atheism attacking religion and materialism explaining it as a product of social relations.
  • Friedrich Engels, “The Program of the Blanquist Fugitives from the Paris Commune”, 1874. Engels attacks the attempt to abolish God and religious organization by decree and warns that persecution strengthens the convictions it targets.

Early Christianity and the organizational question

  • Friedrich Engels, “On the History of Early Christianity”, 1894 to 1895. Engels compares early Christianity and the modern workers’ movement as persecuted mass movements of oppressed people while preserving the decisive difference between salvation beyond this world and social transformation within it.
