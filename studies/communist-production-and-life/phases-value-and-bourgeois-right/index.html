When Equal Becomes Unequal

Two people receive the same-sized pair of shoes. One pair fits. The other cuts into the wearer’s feet.

The rule was equal. The result was not.

This is the small, ordinary shape of a difficult communist problem. Capitalism measures people constantly: hours, grades, prices, output, rent, debt, credit, eligibility. A revolution can overthrow the class that rules through those measures without immediately acquiring the abundance, knowledge, habits, and institutions needed to leave every old measure behind. An early communist society will still have to decide how much grain to store, how many nurses to train, which damaged bridge to repair first, and how scarce goods should be shared.

The question is not whether to count. It is what our counts mean, what social relations they express, and whether one abstract standard is being allowed to rule lives that are concretely different.

Marx called the persistence of such a standard bourgeois right. The phrase does not mean that capitalists must still own the factories. It means that a society born from capitalism may continue to judge unequal people by an equal measure. That principle is an advance over exploitation, but it is not yet human emancipation.

Gotha was an argument, not a constitution

The famous distinction between a lower and higher phase of communism appears in Marx’s 1875 Critique of the Gotha Programme. It is often presented as if Marx sat down to write the constitution of all future societies: first labour certificates, then abundance, then the formula “to each according to his needs.”

That is not what happened.

Marx was intervening in a concrete political dispute. The Gotha Programme was intended to unite two German socialist organizations. Its language carried the influence of Ferdinand Lassalle, including the demand for the “undiminished proceeds of labour” and the claim that distribution should be “fair.” Marx objected because these phrases made distribution look like a separate moral puzzle. They hid the social relations of production that determine what can be distributed, by whom, and under which conditions.

There can be no undiminished personal proceeds. Before any individual receives a claim, society has to replace worn-out tools, expand useful production, build reserves, support administration, and provide for education, health, disability, age, childhood, and other common needs. Some deductions may shrink as social organization becomes simpler. Others should grow as more of life becomes a common right.

Marx’s argument begins there: not with a perfect distribution rule, but with the total social product and the kind of society producing it.

Friedrich Engels returned to the point in his 1890 letter to Conrad Schmidt. The distribution of consumer goods, he argued, changes with the organization of production. A society built on wage-labour and private control cannot distribute as though its productive relations were already communist. A society of associated producers will develop new forms of distribution because the people, resources, and purpose of production have changed.

Distribution follows production, though it also acts back upon it. If access to childcare, transport, food, and medicine depends entirely on an individual’s labour claim, then those rules shape who can work, participate, learn, and care. The relation moves both ways. Production remains decisive because it continually recreates the capacities and limits within which distribution takes place.

What bourgeois right measures

Imagine an early communist society in which each person receives a certificate recording the amount of labour they contributed after deductions for common needs. The certificate can be exchanged for an equivalent amount of goods from the social stores. It cannot circulate between people, earn interest, hire labour, or become capital.

Exploitation by an owning class has ended. The producer receives back from society what they gave, in a different form.

Yet the standard remains unequal in its effects.

People differ in strength, health, skill, age, household responsibility, disability, and the amount of labour required to perform different tasks. One person may contribute more in six hours than another can in eight. One may support children. Another may need expensive medicine. If everyone is measured only as a contributor of labour, the rule abstracts from every difference that makes them a whole person.

Marx therefore calls equal right “a right of inequality, in its content, like every right.” To apply one standard, law has to see people from one side and ignore the rest. That is the defect carried over from bourgeois society.

The criticism is sharper than “perfect equality is impossible.” Equal treatment is sometimes essential. Equal political standing, equal freedom from domination, and equal protection are not mistakes. The mistake is using formal equality as an excuse not to see unequal circumstances.

Equity begins where an equal measure stops. It asks what a person actually needs to live, participate, and develop. It does not assign worth by identity or create a new hereditary rank. It refuses to let hours worked settle questions that hours worked cannot answer.

A wheelchair, insulin, childcare, interpretation, and safe housing are not prizes awarded for superior performance. They are conditions of equal participation.

Labour certificates are not labour money

The phrase labour certificate can create confusion because a certificate seems to name labour time and purchase goods. Is this merely money with a new face?

Not necessarily.

Money circulates. It can be saved, transferred, loaned, accumulated, used to buy labour-power, and converted into capital. It connects private producers through exchange after they have produced separately. Its movement belongs to a system in which products must prove themselves socially useful through sale.

A non-transferable labour certificate has a narrower function. It records a personal claim on a portion of goods already produced by an associated society. Once used, it is cancelled. It cannot hire another person or command a workshop. Production is not waiting for the certificate-holder’s purchase to become social.

Tadayuki Tsushima develops this distinction carefully in “Understanding ‘Labor Certificates’ on the Basis of the Theory of Value”. Labour certificates can measure a distributive claim without turning products into commodities, provided they do not circulate and production is already directly social.

That distinction does not make certificates automatically desirable. They may demand more administration than they save. They may reward the easiest labour to record and undervalue difficult care. They may encourage people to guard personal claims rather than expand common provision. They may also be a practical transitional device under scarcity.

The decision depends on the relation they serve.

This is why labour-time calculation must not be confused with value. Every society must know something about how long work takes. A hospital needs to schedule shifts. A community deciding between two rail routes should know their construction and maintenance demands. A plan has to compare material, ecological, and human costs.

Value is not the mere existence of a number. In commodity production, value is a social relation in which privately undertaken labour is reduced to abstract social labour through exchange. Sale confirms, or refuses to confirm, that the labour counted as part of society’s total labour. The market performs this validation behind people’s backs.

Associated producers may calculate labour time consciously before production begins without reproducing that relation. Counting becomes capitalist when the count belongs to capitalist social relations, not because numbers carry capitalism inside them.

The phases describe a movement

The lower and higher phases are not two sealed rooms. Society does not step through a door one morning and discover that every rule has changed at once.

The transformation will be uneven.

Medicine may become freely available while rare minerals remain strictly allocated. Public transport may be universal while housing construction is constrained by damage, climate, or a shortage of skilled labour. One region may possess mature infrastructure while another must devote enormous effort to clean water and electrification. Some activities may be automated rapidly. Others will continue to demand attentive human care.

The phase distinction names a direction within those uneven changes:

Developing relation Earlier communist form Movement of transformation More developed form
individual access personal claim tied partly to contribution more goods enter direct common provision access follows need
necessary labour long, unequal, and inherited division of tasks automation, education, rotation, and redesign reduce burdens necessary labour occupies less of life
common deductions limited services and large inherited repair needs care, reserves, infrastructure, and free provision expand much of individual consumption is already social
human capacity people remain confined by old training and roles knowledge and decision-making spread many-sided activity becomes ordinary
measurement personal labour measures remain prominent measures become planning tools rather than claims of worth no general link between contribution and access

The higher phase is not defined by a warehouse containing an infinite quantity of everything. Nature remains finite. Human time remains finite. Some goods will always be rare because they depend on place, season, craft, or ecological restraint. Need itself develops historically, and no sane society promises every possible whim regardless of its effects on others.

Abundance means that the ordinary conditions of a good life cease to be prizes. Food, shelter, care, education, mobility, communication, culture, and the means of development become reliable common capacities. Scarcity loses its role as the general organizer of social obedience.

Common deductions are already a different principle

Even where individual labour claims survive, common deductions open another logic.

Suppose society produces one hundred units of usable social product. It does not divide all one hundred among current workers. Part goes to replacing equipment. Part goes to reserves. Part supports people who cannot work. Part supports children, schools, clinics, research, transport, ecosystems, and care.

The portion distributed according to individual contribution is therefore only one part of social consumption. A person receiving surgery does not pay its full labour cost from a private certificate. A child does not incur a debt for education. A flooded town does not have to prove that it contributed enough hours before receiving emergency supplies.

These are not exceptions to communism. They are communist relations developing inside an incomplete form.

The goal is not to preserve a narrow contribution rule forever and decorate it with compassion. Common provision should grow wherever it is materially possible and socially useful. Administration devoted to checking personal entitlement should shrink as direct access becomes easier. The result is not an “undiminished” individual share. It is a larger life made possible through common wealth.

This changes the meaning of wealth itself. A free clinic, a shaded public square, a library, a flood reserve, clean air, and time released from necessary labour may never appear inside a person’s individual claim. They nevertheless enlarge what that person can be and do.

When a claim becomes a fetter

A rule can be necessary at one stage and obstructive later.

Imagine a community where potatoes are scarce after crop failure. Careful allocation is unavoidable. It may make sense to record personal shares, protect reserves, and give priority according to need.

Years later, growing capacity and better storage make potatoes plentiful. The community can provide them freely without threatening other needs. If an office still demands that each person surrender a labour certificate for every potato, the old claim has lost its function. Clerks spend time policing an abundance. People learn to regard common food as a private equivalent they have individually earned. A measure designed for scarcity now blocks common use.

The personal claim has become a fetter.

This is a qualitative change produced through quantitative development, but it is never automatic. Greater productive capacity can be enclosed. Software can impose artificial scarcity. A public system can preserve forms of eligibility because an administrative group derives authority from them. A society has to recognize when the material reason for an old rule has disappeared, then consciously remove it.

Commonification names that movement. A good, service, space, tool, or body of knowledge ceases to confront people as private exclusion and becomes an accessible common capacity. The change is not merely a transfer of legal title. People gain reliable use, shared responsibility, and power to shape the conditions of provision.

Commonification does not mean that everyone can occupy the same room at once or consume without ecological limit. It means that exclusion is justified by concrete limits and common purposes, not by money or inherited property.

No measure should become a theory of the person

Communist planning will use many measures: hours, tonnes, temperatures, distances, energy, toxicity, durability, accessibility, urgency, and ecological recovery. No single unit can decide a social question by itself.

Labour time can disclose a hidden burden. It cannot tell us whether a medicine should be produced for a small number of people. Carbon accounting can reveal damage. It cannot decide which community must surrender land for a project. A vote can register preference. It cannot make an inaccessible meeting equally usable.

Measures become emancipatory when people understand them, challenge them, combine them with qualitative judgment, and revise the institutions built around them. They become alien powers when their social origin disappears and their results are obeyed as if the numbers themselves had decided.

The movement beyond bourgeois right is therefore also a movement beyond the rule of one abstract side of humanity. It develops common capacity until personal contribution no longer governs access. It makes concrete difference visible without turning difference into hierarchy. It preserves calculation while ending the fiction that calculation can measure a person’s worth.

The pair of shoes was never equal in the only sense that mattered: whether a person could walk in it.
