The Machine Contains a Society

Place a modern machine on an empty island.

Without electricity, spare parts, manuals, standards, trained hands, mines, factories, software, roads, and communication, it soon becomes a silent object. Even the simplest repair may require a screw thread agreed upon by people who never met its present user.

The machine was never only a machine.

It contains mathematics, failed experiments, craft, extraction, language, education, coordination, and the experience of generations. It is social knowledge made material. Marx’s phrase general intellect names this accumulated power of a society: knowledge and cooperation embodied in people, institutions, methods, instruments, and systems.

Under capitalism, the general intellect appears as private power. A company owns the patent, platform, laboratory, data, and machine. Workers confront their own collective knowledge as a speed they must follow, a system that watches them, a qualification they lack, or a threat that their wage will disappear.

Communism does not smash the machine to free the worker. It returns the knowledge, purpose, and benefit of the machine to associated people.

Knowledge never begins inside one head

A scientist may make a singular discovery. The singularity is real. It is also socially possible.

The researcher depends on language, prior results, instruments, technicians, public infrastructure, food, care, education, criticism, and communities that preserve a question long enough for someone to answer it. A mathematical proof written by one person can contain conceptual tools produced over thousands of years.

This is not an argument against recognition. It is an argument against enclosure.

Private ownership allows an institution to claim the common inheritance as its exclusive asset. Patents, paywalls, proprietary formats, sealed devices, and secret data can prevent society from using knowledge it collectively made possible. Researchers may spend years pursuing a funder’s market rather than the question they judge important.

Open science reverses that relation. Methods, results, failures, code, designs, and data become available for criticism and development wherever safety and privacy permit. Instruments and laboratories become common infrastructure rather than gates surrounding a professional caste.

Knowledge grows because it circulates.

The general intellect is not the internet

The general intellect is sometimes reduced to information stored in networks. That is too narrow.

It includes:

  • tacit skill in a nurse’s hands;
  • the shared timing of a work crew;
  • a community’s knowledge of a watershed;
  • standards that let parts fit together;
  • the maintenance routine that prevents catastrophe;
  • scientific concepts and mathematical methods;
  • institutions capable of preserving and testing memory;
  • languages through which knowledge can be taught;
  • machines embodying operations; and
  • the capacity to create new knowledge from all of these.

Some knowledge can be written. Some has to be practiced beside another person. Some belongs to a place and changes when removed from it. A plan that collects only formal data loses much of the intelligence it hopes to coordinate.

Common ownership of knowledge must therefore include conditions for people to exercise and transmit it. Publishing a manual does not give everyone a laboratory. Opening a dataset does not create time to study it. Education, equipment, translation, disability access, and mentorship are parts of the commons.

Machinery develops the contradiction

In Capital, Volume I, Chapter 15, machinery appears as a productive power of social labour appropriated by capital.

A tool in a worker’s hand extends activity. A system of machinery reorganizes the labour process around its own connected movement. Under capitalist command, the worker can become an appendage who feeds, monitors, or obeys the system. Knowledge is removed from the collective worker and returned in the form of management, engineering authority, and fixed capital owned by another class.

The machine can save labour. Capital uses labour saved in one place to intensify exploitation, expand production, displace workers, or lengthen insecurity elsewhere. The technical possibility of freedom appears as a social threat.

Marx’s Grundrisse brings the contradiction toward its limit. As science and general social knowledge become direct productive forces, wealth depends less on the immediate labour time applied to each unit. Yet value continues to measure wealth through labour time. Society develops the power to reduce necessary labour while maintaining a relation that treats labour time as the source and measure of social claims.

Automation does not abolish capitalism. It sharpens the question of who owns the time it saves.

The purpose of automation is free time

Under wage-labour, an automated task can mean unemployment, lost income, deskilling, surveillance, or a faster pace for the workers who remain.

Under associated production, the direct gain can be time.

If a machine safely performs half of a dangerous process, the working day can contract. If software removes repetitive administration, people can devote attention to judgment and care. If a shared tool makes fabrication easier, more people can design and repair without years of narrow training.

Maximum automation is not the goal.

Some activities are valuable partly because a person performs them. A child may learn from a teacher’s attention rather than a perfectly optimized lesson. A patient may need a caregiver who can understand fear and respond beyond a protocol. A craft may be worth doing even when a machine can copy its object.

The decision should ask:

Question Why it matters
Does the technology reduce danger or drudgery? Labour-saving should first release people from harm and compulsion.
Does it preserve or enlarge human skill? A tool can support judgment or make its user helpless without the vendor.
Who controls its purpose and operation? Social knowledge should not return as managerial command.
Is it repairable and understandable? Dependence on sealed systems creates technical hierarchy and fragility.
What energy, materials, and care does it require? Time saved in one place may hide extraction and labour elsewhere.
Does a human relationship belong to the activity? Efficiency can destroy the use if the use is care, trust, play, or shared creation.
What happens to the time saved? The gain is not emancipatory if another task immediately consumes it.

Automation becomes communist when people collectively acquire the time it releases.

Appropriate technology is not lesser technology

The most complex available device is not always the most developed choice.

A repairable pump made from common parts may serve a rural water system better than a sealed networked machine. A passive cooling design may outperform an energy-intensive system in a particular climate. A local tool may be more resilient than a universal platform requiring continuous remote permission.

Appropriate technology fits its human, ecological, and practical setting. It should be judged by durability, accessibility, safety, repair, energy, material demand, privacy, resilience, skill, and control.

This principle does not romanticize simplicity. A magnetic resonance scanner, particle detector, semiconductor plant, or space telescope cannot be built from local materials in a neighborhood workshop. Humanity needs large, delicate, globally coordinated systems.

Scale should follow the object.

The mistake is making complexity a badge of progress. A technology is advanced when it enlarges human capacity within real ecological relations, not when it contains the greatest number of inaccessible parts.

Research needs room beyond immediate use

A society planning for use may be tempted to ask every researcher, “What use will this have?”

The question is legitimate when common labour and resources are involved. It becomes destructive when “use” means an immediate product.

Pure mathematics may develop structures whose applications appear decades later, or never appear at all. Astronomy changes humanity’s knowledge of its place without promising a household device. Particle physics investigates matter at scales remote from daily need. Space research, evolutionary biology, archaeology, and speculative engineering can matter because knowing the world is itself a human power.

Research freedom means that people can pursue such questions without first proving profitability.

It also means research can occur beyond universities:

  • public laboratories with shared equipment;
  • community observatories and ecological stations;
  • workshops joining craft and science;
  • open libraries, journals, datasets, and software;
  • long-term institutes protected from yearly fashion;
  • independent teams able to challenge established fields; and
  • education that lets people enter research throughout life.

Freedom is not exemption from accountability. A particle collider, launch system, genome project, or large computational facility uses labour, energy, materials, land, and time that could serve other purposes. Its advocates must make the scientific case intelligible. The public must be able to compare opportunity costs. Safety, ecological limits, and the rights of affected communities remain binding.

The answer may still be yes. A communist society should be capable of saying yes to knowledge whose practical return is unknown.

Education ends the monopoly, not expertise

The division between mental and physical labour is not simply a difference between tasks. It is a social division in which some people monopolize conception and direction while others execute.

Communist education must overcome that monopoly.

People should encounter science through investigation, production through understanding, and governance through real participation. Engineers should know something of maintenance. Planners should encounter the places their measures describe. Workers should have access to theory, design, data, and the time required to master them.

No one will become equally expert in everything. A surgeon needs training. A theoretical physicist may spend years on a narrow problem. A bridge should not be approved through a popularity contest over stress calculations.

Expertise remains necessary. Its social relation changes.

An expert should explain assumptions, disclose uncertainty, teach others, submit to peer review, and hold authority only where the relevant competence justifies it. Political decisions about acceptable risk and common purpose remain public. Technical truth is not decided by vote, but the power to impose a technical project is not granted by expertise alone.

This relation lets specialization deepen without creating a permanent class of directors.

Artificial intelligence can advise but cannot rule

Artificial intelligence can help compare scenarios, detect patterns, translate information, model systems, search large bodies of evidence, and reveal inconsistencies in a plan.

These are real powers. None gives a model the right to determine communal purpose.

Every model has boundaries. It receives selected data, represents some relations, excludes others, and optimizes according to an objective someone has specified. A result can be mathematically correct inside the model and socially disastrous outside it. Historical data can preserve inequality. A confidence score can conceal ignorance behind precision.

No increase in computational ability removes the political question: what should society value, risk, protect, or become?

Human rule requires:

  1. people choose purposes before models optimize means;
  2. affected communities can challenge the data and assumptions;
  3. uncertainty and disagreement remain visible;
  4. independent people and institutions can test the system;
  5. local bodies retain enough knowledge to refuse or operate without it;
  6. privacy is protected without concentrating secret informational power;
  7. no single system becomes the only channel through which society can understand itself; and
  8. final responsibility remains with accountable human institutions.

A computational system should inform judgment, not become the organ that supplies society with its will.

The same caution applies to more capable future systems. Intelligence alone does not produce solidarity, human need, or legitimate authority. A brilliant instrument remains an instrument unless people freely choose another relation, and no such choice can be assumed in advance.

Open knowledge still has boundaries

Common science favors openness, but openness is not an absolute detached from consequences.

Medical records, personal communications, locations of threatened species, and knowledge easily turned toward mass harm may require protection. A community holding sensitive ecological knowledge may have good reason to refuse universal extraction. Workers reporting a safety failure need protection from retaliation.

The communist alternative to proprietary secrecy is democratically justified stewardship.

Information should be open by default where openness expands common power. Restrictions should identify the harm they prevent, the people responsible, the duration, the scope, and the means of review. “Security” must not become a word that creates an unanswerable technical caste.

The general intellect belongs to society, but society is composed of people who retain privacy, dignity, and rights against the whole.

A machine can return as a human power

The machine on the island fell silent because its real body extended across society.

Under capital, that social body is hidden. The owner appears to animate the machine. The worker appears as a replaceable input. Knowledge appears as property. Time saved appears as profit.

Associated production makes the relation visible. The machine becomes a common instrument in a consciously organized metabolism among people and nature. Its purpose can be argued about. Its burden can be traced. Its knowledge can circulate. Its gain can return as free time.

The general intellect then ceases to be an alien intelligence standing above society. It becomes what it was all along: the accumulated power of human beings making one another capable of more.

Source notes

Primary texts

  • Karl Marx, Grundrisse, “The Chapter on Capital”, on machinery, the general intellect, necessary labour, disposable time, and social wealth.
  • Karl Marx, Capital, Volume I, Chapter 15, on machinery, large-scale industry, cooperation, and the power of science within the capitalist labour process.
  • Karl Marx and Friedrich Engels, The German Ideology, Chapter I, on production, human development, and the division of labour.
  • Friedrich Engels, Anti-Dühring, on conscious social production, education, and the overcoming of fixed divisions of labour.

Further study

  • Eden Medina, Cybernetic Revolutionaries: Technology and Politics in Allende’s Chile, a history of Project Cybersyn that shows how technical design, political purpose, worker participation, and institutional power become inseparable.
