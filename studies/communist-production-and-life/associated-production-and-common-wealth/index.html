Before the Work Begins

A tomato in a supermarket carries a world inside it.

Someone bred and planted the seed. Other people made fertilizer, repaired irrigation, drove a truck, built a road, stocked a shelf, cooled a warehouse, wrote routing software, maintained a power grid, cared for the workers’ children, treated their injuries, and taught each new worker skills accumulated across generations.

No one produced the tomato alone.

Yet the connected labour usually meets itself only after the work is done. Separate firms decide what to produce. Each tries to sell. Prices move. Unsold food spoils beside hunger. A good harvest can bankrupt a farmer because success lowers the price. A supermarket knows demand through purchases, but people without money disappear from its signal.

Labour is social in fact and private in form.

Communist production begins by bringing those two sides together. Producers do not wait for a market to tell them afterward whether their work belonged to society. They recognize their interdependence before the work begins, decide common purposes, and organize the connected process accordingly.

That is associated production.

More than a new owner

The phrase can sound mild. It names a change deeper than nationalization, employee ownership, or a ministry replacing a board of directors.

A nationalized factory can still produce for sale, obey managers appointed from above, hide information from workers, and treat wages as the only reason people enter. An isolated cooperative can be democratically governed inside while competing in a market that forces it to cut costs, intensify work, or fail. State ownership can gather productive property into one legal hand while leaving producers as subordinates.

Associated production changes the relation itself.

Free and equal producers consciously direct:

  • the purposes of production;
  • the organization and conditions of work;
  • the distribution of labour among activities;
  • the use of land, energy, knowledge, and materials;
  • the care and education that reproduce society;
  • the reserves needed for uncertainty;
  • the ecological limits no unit may push onto another; and
  • the way products and services reach the people who need them.

“Producer” here does not mean only the person standing beside a machine. Society is reproduced through farms, hospitals, kitchens, workshops, laboratories, schools, transport, cleaning, maintenance, administration, art, and care. People affected by production also possess knowledge and claims that a workplace alone cannot represent. A river belongs in the plan even though it sends no delegate. A disabled passenger knows something about transport that its engineers may miss. A neighborhood breathing refinery smoke has standing in decisions about the refinery.

Association therefore joins workplace control to communal control. Neither side may swallow the other.

Social before it starts

In commodity production, private labour becomes social through exchange. A firm may devote thousands of hours to a product, but those hours count as socially necessary only if the product sells under competitive conditions. The market validates the labour after it has been spent.

In associated production, labour is directly social because a recognized social purpose precedes the activity.

That does not mean every plan succeeds. People can misjudge demand, overlook a constraint, build badly, or change their minds. Directly social labour is not infallible labour. It means that error appears inside a consciously organized relation and can be corrected as such. Society does not need a failed sale to discover that two hospitals ordered the same scarce machine while a third lacked basic equipment.

The order of reasoning changes:

    A["Needs, desires, and limits are expressed"] --> B["Capacities and alternatives are made visible"]
    B --> C["People choose purposes and priorities"]
    C --> D["Connected units organize the work"]
    D --> E["Goods and services enter use"]
    E --> F["Results, failures, and changed needs return as feedback"]
    F --> A

Text alternative: Needs, desires, and limits are expressed. Available capacities and alternatives are made visible. People choose priorities, connected units organize the work, and goods enter use. Results and failures then return as information for the next decisions.

Nothing in this movement requires a single office to issue every instruction. A common purpose can be binding while methods remain local. A rail network needs coordinated timetables and technical standards. It does not need a national committee to decide how each maintenance crew arranges its tools.

The historical dispute over who plans

The workers’ movement has repeatedly confronted a false choice: market competition or administrative command.

The Group of International Communists attacked “state communism” in 1930 because transferring production to the state could leave workers without control over the social product. Their proposed accounting in labour time tried to give associated producers a transparent basis for coordinating production and consumption without a market or an administrative caste.

Paul Mattick argued in “What Is Communism?” that communism could not be reduced to state ownership. Workers’ councils had to become organs through which producers governed the economy. Cornelius Castoriadis later made workers’ management central to “The Meaning of Socialism”, insisting that socialism required the abolition of the split between order-givers and order-takers.

These arguments do not hand us a finished system. Labour-time accounting can become too narrow. A council can reproduce local egoism. A workplace may know its process while ignoring people outside its gates. A central body may know national constraints while losing the living detail on which every feasible plan depends.

The lasting question is better than any single institutional answer:

Can producers and communities understand, govern, challenge, and transform the system that connects their activity?

If the answer is no, the plan confronts them as another alien power, however public its legal owner.

Planning is a relation among capable parts

A complex society requires binding decisions. A power grid cannot be coordinated through voluntary suggestions issued independently by every station. Vaccination, flood defense, rail construction, watershed protection, and emergency reserves cross local boundaries. Some tasks need technical command in real time.

But coordination is not identical to domination.

A surgeon directs a procedure because the work demands trained judgment and clear responsibility. That does not grant surgeons authority over hospital budgets, neighborhood health priorities, or the lives of nurses outside the operating room. Technical authority should be specific to the function that justifies it, open to review, and subordinate to common purposes.

The same principle applies across a plan.

Relation What must be secured What must be prevented
common purpose ecological limits, universal provision, major priorities a center treating every local unit as a passive instrument
local autonomy practical knowledge, initiative, adaptation shifting burdens outward or withholding necessary information
technical authority competence, safety, rapid coordination expertise becoming a permanent right to rule
refusal and appeal protection against unsafe or unjust orders every connected obligation becoming optional
audit truth, learning, correction, shared improvement ritual inspection devoted only to blame
feedback changed needs and failures altering the plan reports disappearing into a hierarchy that cannot be challenged

A productive unit can be partly opaque at one scale. A regional food body may need to know a bakery’s inputs, outputs, capacity, working conditions, and failures without directing every movement inside it. Persistent discrepancies, serious harms, or exceptional successes justify opening the process to deeper review.

Audit should search for gains as seriously as it searches for misconduct. If one workshop cuts dangerous labour in half, the point is not merely to congratulate it. The method should become common knowledge.

Production for use is not production without choice

“Production for use” is sometimes imagined as a stern society issuing everyone a list of necessities. That confuses use with austerity.

People need beauty, pleasure, novelty, play, privacy, travel, music, clothes they enjoy, and tools for activities no planner could invent in advance. A use is not limited to biological survival. A guitar and a telescope have uses even when neither keeps a body alive.

The communist difference is that production answers to human purposes rather than profitability. People can choose among alternatives while seeing their real costs. They can decide that a rare material belongs in medical equipment rather than disposable decoration. They can accept a longer wait for a durable custom product instead of filling warehouses with standardized goods that may never be wanted.

Production on demand can reduce waste where the technology and time allow it. A person chooses a vehicle configuration, garment, furniture design, or replacement part before final assembly. Modular systems and flexible tools can create variety without forcing competing firms to guess demand and destroy unsold stock.

This does not fit every good. Emergency medicine must be ready before anyone orders it. Grain reserves must exist before a failed harvest. Public transit cannot be assembled only when a passenger arrives. Planning distinguishes between predictable needs, uncertain risks, durable stocks, and goods that can wait.

A society able to wait can often produce better. Capital races to circulate value. Associated producers can choose durability, repairability, and thoughtful design because no investor demands the fastest return.

Common deductions make the whole visible

No associated producer receives the “full product” of individual labour because there is no purely individual product to receive.

Part of the social product must replace equipment and infrastructure. Part expands useful capacity. Part forms reserves against fire, crop failure, epidemic, breakdown, and disaster. Part supports administration. Part becomes schools, clinics, childcare, disability support, elder care, science, culture, transport, and provision for people unable to contribute labour.

Marx describes these deductions in the Critique of the Gotha Programme. Their importance is larger than bookkeeping. They make visible the labour that reproduces the whole and the people whose lives cannot be reduced to current contribution.

Common deductions also change over time.

Administrative costs devoted to money, sales, insurance billing, competing brands, proprietary barriers, and surveillance of eligibility can contract. Provision that satisfies needs in common can expand. What first appears as a deduction from an individual’s share becomes a larger field of free access.

A public library makes every reader wealthier without placing a private library’s replacement cost in each pocket. A reliable bus expands everyone’s reach without requiring each person to own a vehicle. A clinic, a shared workshop, open scientific knowledge, safe streets, clean water, and a flood reserve are portions of social wealth that cannot be represented by personal income.

Wealth is capacity and time

Capitalism teaches people to see wealth as a pile of objects or a command over money. Common wealth is broader.

It includes:

  • healthy ecosystems capable of continuing life;
  • infrastructure available without private tolls;
  • knowledge no owner can lock away;
  • tools and spaces people can use together;
  • reserves that make society resilient;
  • institutions people can understand and govern;
  • developed human capacities; and
  • free time in which life is not subordinated to necessity.

Marx’s Grundrisse pushes toward this richer measure. As social knowledge and productivity reduce necessary labour, disposable time becomes a central form of real wealth. The purpose is not universal idleness. It is the release of human activity from the compulsion to earn access to life.

Free time can become study, friendship, care, sport, art, invention, politics, rest, or simply a quiet afternoon. The activity matters because it is a person’s own.

This also changes work. Necessary labour can be redesigned, shortened, shared, and made more meaningful. Society can eliminate tasks whose only purpose was competition or property enforcement while preserving occupations that people actually value. The name of a job tells us little. Advertising that informs people about a new service may remain useful even when manipulative competition disappears. Accounting that tracks real resources remains necessary even when private profit vanishes.

The test is concrete: what human or ecological purpose does this activity serve, and can that purpose be met better?

Association does not dissolve the individual

A common plan is not a single collective person swallowing everyone inside it.

Association is valuable because distinct people gain powers they could not exercise alone. A person can refuse a relationship, change an occupation, pursue an unusual interest, form a new group, or spend time in solitude because housing, care, education, tools, and access do not depend on obedience to a private owner.

Communal provision should enlarge exit.

The same is true of productive units. A neighborhood, laboratory, workshop, or cultural group needs room to initiate projects no central plan anticipated. A society that can only reproduce approved purposes has not achieved conscious association. It has mistaken coordination for sameness.

Connected freedom always carries obligations. A factory cannot declare autonomy while poisoning a river. A region cannot hoard medicine produced through a continental network. A research team cannot demand unlimited materials without explaining the opportunity cost. Autonomy is real within relations that make other people’s autonomy possible.

The plan must remain ours

Associated production cannot be installed once and then left to run.

Needs change. Knowledge changes. Ecological limits become clearer. Institutions develop habits. Specialists may protect their authority. Local units may hide difficulties. Majorities may overlook minorities. A technically elegant plan may make daily life miserable.

People must therefore be able to inspect the plan, contest its measures, learn its language, recall its delegates, create alternatives, and revise its purposes. Institutions have to carry disagreement without treating every objection as sabotage. They also have to reach decisions and coordinate action.

The plan succeeds when it develops the capacity to plan.

That capacity belongs neither to a market nor to a machine nor to a ministry. It belongs to associated people whose labour was connected all along, but who can finally recognize and govern the connection before the work begins.

Source notes

Primary texts

  • Karl Marx, Capital, Volume I, Chapter 1, on commodity production, private labour, abstract labour, and value.
  • Karl Marx, Critique of the Gotha Programme (1875), on the total social product, common deductions, and communist distribution.

Historical debates

  • Group of International Communists, Fundamental Principles of Communist Production and Distribution (1930), on workers’ control, labour accounting, and the critique of state ownership.

Further study
