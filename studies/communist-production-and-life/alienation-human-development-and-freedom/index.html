The Powers We Lose and Take Back

At six in the morning, a city begins moving.

Lights enter windows. Water climbs towers. Trains leave depots. Bread arrives at shops. A nurse finishes a night shift while another person cleans the room she used. Millions of separate movements fit together well enough for the city to wake.

No one designed the whole thing. No one can stop working for long without losing access to it. The power of everyone’s cooperation is everywhere, but it belongs to no one in common. It appears as the authority of an employer, the price of rent, the movement of a market, the schedule of a machine, the threat of debt, and the impersonal statement that “the economy requires it.”

Human beings made these relations. Then the relations confront human beings as an independent world.

This is alienation.

Alienation can be felt as boredom, distance, anxiety, or powerlessness. Its foundation is not a feeling. A worker can enjoy a job and remain alienated from the purpose, product, and conditions of the work. Another can hate a task while exercising real control over why and how it is done. The decisive question is whether our activity belongs to us through free association or returns as the command of an alien social power.

Objectification is how humanity becomes real

Human life constantly moves outward.

A carpenter makes skill visible in a table. A musician gives a passing feeling a repeatable form. A child stacks blocks and discovers balance. A community cuts a path, names a place, preserves a story, plants a garden, and changes what later generations can perceive and do.

Marx calls this movement objectification. Human powers take objective form in a product, practice, institution, or transformed part of nature. Objectification is not a fall from pure inward freedom. It is how freedom acquires a world.

The problem begins when the objectified power separates from its makers.

The table enters a commodity chain the carpenter does not control. The song becomes an asset owned by a platform. The path becomes private property. The machine embodies generations of knowledge but confronts its operator as an instruction, a speed, and a threat of replacement. The institution created to coordinate common work becomes a career structure with interests of its own.

Alienation is objectification under relations in which people lose control of what their objectified power becomes.

This distinction matters. If every object or institution were alienating merely because it stands outside an individual, emancipation would require abolishing civilization. No one person can contain a railway, a language, or centuries of mathematics inside a private mind. Our dependence upon a shared world is a source of human power. It becomes domination when access and direction belong to separate owners or authorities.

Four separations in the labour process

The Economic and Philosophic Manuscripts of 1844 follows alienated labour through four connected separations.

The product

The worker’s product belongs to another. The more wealth the worker creates, the more objective power capital possesses. A building may remain for a century while its builders struggle to pay rent. A software system may coordinate millions of lives while the people who wrote and maintain it can be dismissed by a distant owner.

The worker’s activity becomes someone else’s property.

The activity

The work itself is external. Its purpose, pace, organization, and duration answer to the need to earn a wage and the employer’s need to accumulate. Activity becomes a means to remain alive outside the activity.

This does not mean every future task must be effortless delight. Surgery can be exhausting. Cleaning a sewer can remain unpleasant. Chosen necessity differs from imposed necessity. Associated people can decide that a task must be done, reduce its danger, share its burden, and recognize its purpose. Under alienated labour, that social necessity appears through the command of a separate power.

Human life-activity

Humans can make their activity an object of conscious will. We compare possible futures, inherit the powers of people long dead, and create for needs beyond the immediate pressure of hunger. Marx names this developed, social power species-being.

Alienated labour reduces that open life-activity to a means of individual survival. The person is most fully themselves in the hours left after work, while the activity through which society is reproduced belongs to someone else.

Species-being is not a mystical group soul. Nor is it a fixed list of traits that every person must display. It is the historically developed human capacity to live universally: to learn from the species, take many objects and relations as our concern, consciously shape life, and develop powers through one another.

Other people

When our common activity takes an alien form, other people confront us through that form. One person appears as boss, competitor, customer, debtor, landlord, employee, or obstacle. The social relation does not erase real personality, but it organizes encounter before two people have spoken.

Alienation from the product, activity, human power, and other people is one movement seen from four sides.

Money carries the powers of society

In Comments on James Mill, Marx studies exchange as an estranged form of social connection.

People need one another’s products. But instead of directly recognizing and organizing that interdependence, they meet through exchange value. Money becomes the mediator that confirms whether a need can count. Human social power gathers in a thing.

This creates a peculiar reversal.

A person who cannot sing can command a singer’s labour through money. A person capable of healing may be unable to practice because a clinic is not profitable. A vacant apartment can have exchange value while a person without money sleeps outside it. Real powers fail to count, while the possession of money grants access to powers its owner never developed.

The criticism is not that buying is morally cold. Money performs necessary coordinating functions inside commodity society. The problem is that our social connection takes an independent form and governs us through access or exclusion.

Marx’s later analysis of commodity fetishism deepens the same reversal. In Capital, Volume I, relations among producers appear as relations among things. Prices seem to move by their own nature. Capital seems to possess the power to create more value. The cooperative intelligence of workers appears as the productive power of the firm.

Fetishism is not a false story pasted onto an otherwise transparent relation. Commodity society really does make people’s lives depend on the movement of things. The appearance has objective force because the social relation is organized through it.

Our senses have a history

Alienation reaches beyond the factory because labour produces more than goods. It produces a human sensorium, a way of noticing, wanting, judging, and imagining.

An ear trained through music hears a structure where an untrained ear hears only sound. A mechanic can feel a fault through vibration. A gardener reads soil, leaf, shade, and season. A person raised among several languages hears distinctions another language may not mark. These powers are personal, but none was produced by an isolated person.

Each capacity carries accumulated social labour.

Someone made the instrument, named the part, preserved the technique, taught the gesture, and built the environment in which practice became possible. Even solitude is socially furnished. A person reading alone depends upon language, printing, education, light, shelter, and generations of thought.

This does not make an individual less remarkable. It makes individuality more remarkable. A common inheritance does not enter everyone in the same way. Each person selects, resists, combines, forgets, and transforms it through a singular body and life.

The social explanation of personality is not its cancellation. It explains how a finite person can contain a world.

Capital develops powers by confining them

Capitalism has enlarged human productive powers enormously. It has joined continents, built sciences, multiplied communication, and placed the accumulated knowledge of society inside machines and institutions.

It develops these powers in an alien form.

Cooperation appears as the organization of capital. Science appears as proprietary technology. A machine that could shorten labour appears as unemployment or intensified work. A global supply chain reveals human interdependence while hiding the people and ecosystems that sustain it. Education develops capacities, then sorts them according to labour-market demand and ability to pay.

The contradiction is real. The productive relations that organize social labour also confine the capacities they produce.

Communism cannot answer by rejecting large-scale cooperation, machines, science, or developed need. It has to preserve their human content while transforming their social form. The aim is not to return to a world where each household produces everything it uses. It is to make the powers of social humanity openly available to the people who create and depend upon them.

Freedom beyond money

Freedom is often reduced to choice among items for sale. A person with more money appears to possess more freedom because more doors will open.

This is a real power inside capitalist society. Money can purchase privacy, movement, education, care, time, and escape from a harmful relationship. Denouncing it does not make those needs disappear.

Freedom beyond money means placing the conditions behind those choices into common reach.

It means:

  • secure housing that makes refusal possible;
  • healthcare that does not depend on an employer;
  • education and tools available throughout life;
  • transport that opens the city to everyone;
  • care that does not trap a person inside dependence;
  • access to culture, nature, and communication;
  • participation in the institutions that shape daily life; and
  • free time in which capacities can develop without having to justify a return.

These are not goods handed to a passive recipient. They are the material conditions of active self-development.

Marx’s Grundrisse identifies free time as both leisure and time for higher activity. The reduction of necessary labour allows artistic, scientific, social, physical, and political capacities to grow. Those capacities then return to transform production and the people carrying it out.

Freedom is not the absence of every dependence. No human being is independent of nature, other people, childhood, care, or inherited knowledge. Freedom is conscious, non-dominating interdependence. It is the power to enter, shape, refuse, and leave relations without losing the conditions of life.

A free association needs distinct people

Communism is sometimes pictured as the individual dissolving into a collective command. That would preserve alienation in another form.

The collective has no life apart from the people and relations composing it. A common institution becomes alien when it treats those people as materials for its own survival. A plan becomes alien when its measures cannot be questioned. A community becomes alien when intimacy is compulsory and exit is punished.

Free association has to expand individuality:

  1. Common provision reduces obedience imposed by property.
  2. Shared knowledge reduces dependence upon a permanent directing caste.
  3. Shorter necessary labour expands time for self-chosen development.
  4. Many forms of association let people cooperate without one institution defining the whole person.
  5. Privacy and exit protect the difference through which association remains free.

The old opposition between individual and society is itself a product of alienated relations. Capital celebrates private independence while making survival depend on forces no individual controls. Common ownership can create more personal independence because the means of living no longer belong to a boss, landlord, spouse, or market.

The free development of each and the free development of all are not competing promises. Each is the condition of the other.

Communism is not the end of becoming

No social order abolishes grief, conflict, error, unreturned love, difficult effort, or death. Communism does not perfect humanity and freeze history in place.

It abolishes definite relations that make human powers alien: private command over social production, wage dependence, production for exchange, class rule, and institutions separated from the people whose capacities they organize.

New tensions will emerge because developed people will want different things. New technologies will create unforeseen problems. Common decisions will sometimes fail. Freedom requires institutions able to receive conflict without converting difference into domination.

Communism is therefore both a determinate transformation and an open horizon. It takes back powers that already belong to social humanity, then makes possible forms of individuality we cannot completely describe from inside the old world.

At six in the morning, the city will still wake through millions of connected acts. The change is that the connection no longer needs to hide inside prices and commands. People can recognize their power in the world they made, and recognize the world as something they can make again.

Source notes

Primary texts

  • Karl Marx, Comments on James Mill (1844), on exchange, money, estranged social mediation, and human production.
  • Karl Marx, Economic and Philosophic Manuscripts of 1844, especially “Estranged Labour” and “Private Property and Communism.”
  • Karl Marx and Friedrich Engels, The German Ideology, Chapter I, on human beings, production, social relations, and the division of labour.
