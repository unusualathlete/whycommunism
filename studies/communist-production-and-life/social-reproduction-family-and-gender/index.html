Who Makes Tomorrow Possible?

The working day does not begin at the factory gate.

Before a bus driver starts a route, someone may have prepared food, washed clothes, calmed a frightened child, found a missing schoolbook, cleaned a bathroom, ordered medicine, or sat awake with an ill parent. After the paid shift ends, those tasks continue. Bodies have to be fed, healed, rested, taught, and loved before any economy can begin again tomorrow.

This work is easy to miss because much of it is done inside homes and without a wage.

Social reproduction names the activity through which people and social life are maintained across days and generations. It includes pregnancy and birth, childcare, cooking, cleaning, healthcare, education, emotional support, disability assistance, elder care, and the countless acts that make a human being able to enter the next day.

Capital depends upon this work. It does not automatically provide the time, security, and resources required to do it well.

A private home performs public work

Capital purchases labour-power for a limited period. The employer does not ordinarily feed the worker at night, raise the next generation, repair a relationship after a punishing shift, or care for an injured relative. Those tasks are pushed toward households, public services, markets, and communities.

The household is therefore both intimate and economic.

People form families because they love one another, want companionship, raise children, share culture, or choose a common life. The same household may also pool wages, transfer property, organize inheritance, divide unpaid work, shelter people from unemployment, and make one person’s survival depend on another.

There is no contradiction in saying both things. Love can be real inside a relation shaped by economic pressure. A person may freely choose to care for a partner while having no free choice about the amount of care, the absence of public support, or the financial cost of leaving.

Calling the household “private” hides how much of society’s necessary work it performs.

The burden has a gender

Women have historically carried a disproportionate share of domestic labour and care. The form changes across class, place, and time, but the unequal burden remains material.

An employed woman may finish paid work and begin a second shift at home. A wealthier household may purchase relief by hiring a poorer woman. Migrant care workers may support children or elders far from their own families. The burden is not abolished when it moves. It is displaced through class, race, citizenship, and international inequality.

This is why formal equality at work cannot be the whole of liberation.

If childcare is unavailable, housing insecure, healthcare expensive, and domestic work treated as an individual problem, the legal right to compete for wages leaves the structure of dependence largely intact. One person can still be forced into part-time work, financial reliance, or exhaustion because somebody must keep life going.

Patriarchy is not one attitude held by individual men. It is a history of male dominance reproduced through property, law, violence, family roles, culture, work, and institutions. Capitalism did not invent every patriarchal relation, but it has repeatedly reorganized and used them.

Communist transformation has to reach the organization of daily life, not only the title deed of the factory.

Engels opened a question he could not finish

Friedrich Engels’s The Origin of the Family, Private Property and the State made a powerful break with the idea that the family had one eternal natural form. He connected changes in kinship, marriage, inheritance, property, and women’s position to material history.

The argument’s enduring strength is historical. Family forms change. Property and inheritance shape intimate institutions. Women’s economic dependence can survive beneath declarations of equality.

Its limitations also matter. Engels relied on nineteenth-century anthropology, including schemes of social evolution that later research has overturned or complicated. Some claims about early societies were too confident. Predictions about future relationships cannot settle choices that belong to people living under conditions Engels never knew.

August Bebel’s Woman and Socialism likewise joined women’s emancipation to a transformation of economic life. He understood that equality could not rest on legal reform alone while property and dependence continued to organize marriage and work.

These texts opened a materialist field. They did not close it.

Later social reproduction theory asks more precisely how the production of goods depends upon the production and maintenance of people. Lise Vogel’s Marxism and the Oppression of Women centers the reproduction of labour-power within the capitalist totality. The essays collected by Tithi Bhattacharya in Social Reproduction Theory extend the analysis across households, schools, hospitals, migration, race, sexuality, and struggle.

These works disagree in important ways. Social reproduction is not a phrase that dissolves every form of oppression into one mechanism. It is a way to see that the place where life is maintained is part of the organization of society.

Socializing care is not nationalizing affection

To make care a common responsibility does not mean turning every intimate act into a public service.

A person may want to cook for someone they love. Parents may want to spend long hours with children. Friends may care for one another through illness. Communist society should enlarge the time and resources available for such relationships, not replace them with compulsory institutions.

Socialized care means that no household faces its responsibilities alone and no person’s survival depends on private sacrifice.

It can include:

  • universally available childcare with trained and trusted caregivers;
  • healthcare and reproductive care as rights;
  • disability support directed by disabled people;
  • dignified elder care in varied settings;
  • communal kitchens and laundries people may use without being forced to;
  • paid, respected, well-supported care work;
  • shorter necessary labour for everyone;
  • accessible housing and transport;
  • meals, education, play, and safe public spaces for children; and
  • emergency support that does not require a family to collapse before society responds.

The point is not to move unpaid women into poorly paid public jobs. Care has to be shared, resourced, redesigned, and given real authority in social planning.

Some work can be reduced through better buildings and tools. A laundry shared by several households may save equipment, space, maintenance, water, and time. Some care cannot be accelerated without harming the relation itself. Listening to a distressed person is not made efficient by cutting the conversation in half.

A communist measure of productivity has to know the difference.

Material independence changes love

If housing, food, healthcare, education, childcare, and income security depend on remaining with a partner, consent is compromised by material fear.

Common provision cannot guarantee good relationships. It can remove some of the conditions that keep harmful ones intact.

The real ability to leave requires more than a legal right. It requires somewhere safe to go, access to resources, support for children, protection from violence, and confidence that the future is livable. Material independence is therefore not opposed to intimacy. It lets intimacy become more fully voluntary.

Common ownership also weakens the pressure to organize relationships around inheritance. When access to land, education, housing, and productive wealth no longer passes through a private family line, control over marriage and parentage loses part of its economic force.

What relationships will people form under those conditions?

We cannot know, and we should not pretend to know.

They may preserve familiar family forms, create new ones, or combine kinship, friendship, partnership, and communal care in ways that do not yet have settled names. A communist programme can establish the conditions of freedom without legislating the shape of love.

This is the defensible meaning of abolishing the family: abolishing its compulsory role as a unit of property, inheritance, unpaid obligation, and economic survival. It does not mean abolishing affection, kinship, lasting bonds, private homes, or the right to raise children. The relation people are free to choose should not be confused with the institution they are forced to need.

Children are not household property

Children need protection because adults possess greater physical, social, and economic power. They also need privacy, trust, play, voice, and growing participation in the decisions that shape them.

A purely private household can make abuse invisible. A purely administrative model can treat children as cases belonging to an institution. Communal responsibility has to avoid both.

Children should have access to several trusted adults, safe places beyond the home, independent channels for asking for help, and meaningful participation appropriate to their development. Parents and close caregivers can remain central without becoming the child’s only connection to society.

The same principle applies to education. A school should not train obedience for future employment. It should develop the practical, emotional, scientific, artistic, and political capacities through which young people gain increasing command of their lives.

Protection and autonomy are not opposites. Good care changes their relation as a child grows.

Gender hierarchy will not vanish by decree

Common ownership removes major material supports of domination. It does not automatically erase habits, expectations, violence, or exclusion.

A society can provide childcare while still treating care as women’s natural duty. It can proclaim equality while leadership remains masculine, medical needs are ignored, or people are punished for departing from inherited gender roles. Ideas survive through institutions and daily relationships as well as property.

The communist commitment is definite even where future forms remain open:

  • no one should be forced into dependence through gender;
  • no one should be confined to a social role by sex or identity;
  • violence and coercion must be confronted rather than hidden as private matters;
  • bodily autonomy and reproductive freedom must be materially real;
  • care must not be assigned as a natural debt;
  • queer and trans people must possess the same security, dignity, healthcare, participation, and freedom of association as everyone else; and
  • people must be able to form or leave relationships without losing the conditions of life.

These commitments do not require us to claim that one economic change settles every question. They require institutions capable of hearing harms that the majority has learned not to see.

Care is part of the plan

Planning usually makes factories, energy, transport, and materials visible. Care enters as an afterthought, then a shortage of nurses, teachers, time, or domestic support makes every other plan fail.

Social reproduction belongs at the beginning.

A shorter working day has to account for care already being performed outside the wage. A housing plan has to ask how a disabled person moves through a room and how a caregiver rests. A transport plan has to consider children, strollers, shift workers, hospital visits, and people who cannot walk far. A food plan has to include preparation time, dietary need, pleasure, culture, and the person expected to clean afterward.

The question “Who makes tomorrow possible?” should be asked of every institution.

The answer must no longer be an exhausted person hidden inside a private home.

What can be decided, and what must remain free

We can decide that survival will not depend on marriage, property, or obedience. We can decide that care is necessary social labour, that children are members of society, and that the material conditions of consent belong among common rights.

We cannot decide in advance whom future people will love, how they will name their households, or which voluntary forms of intimacy will feel most human to them.

Communism opens that future by removing coercive necessities. Its task is not to prescribe private life. It is to give people enough security, time, care, space, and equality that private life can finally become their own.

Source notes

Primary texts

  • Friedrich Engels, The Origin of the Family, Private Property and the State (1884), on the historical development of family, property, inheritance, and the state. Its anthropology and predictions require critical treatment.
  • August Bebel, Woman and Socialism (1879, revised through 1910), on women’s emancipation, economic dependence, and social transformation.
  • Karl Marx and Friedrich Engels, The German Ideology, Chapter I, on production, social relations, and the reproduction of life.

Further study

  • Lise Vogel, Marxism and the Oppression of Women: Toward a Unitary Theory, on the reproduction of labour-power and women’s oppression within capitalist society.
  • Tithi Bhattacharya, ed., Social Reproduction Theory: Remapping Class, Recentering Oppression, a collection developing social reproduction theory through care, race, gender, sexuality, migration, and class struggle.
