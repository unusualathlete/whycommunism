No Number Chooses the Future

A hospital stands beside a river that rises every wet season.

Its laboratory roof leaks. Its maternity rooms are full. The riverbank needs reinforcement. The region has one construction team with the right skills, a limited supply of structural steel, and six dry months before the rains return.

No slogan can make all three jobs happen first.

The hospital has encountered the core of economic calculation. People have needs. Nature, labour, machinery, knowledge, and time set limits. Choosing one path changes what remains possible on every other path.

Capitalism answers such questions through a mixture of private plans, state decisions, credit, prices, contracts, and competition. Communism would have to answer them consciously. The disappearance of the market does not make the problem disappear. It makes society responsible for solving it in the open.

The calculation debate therefore begins with a concession that should never have been difficult:

Any society that cannot compare possible uses of its powers cannot govern those powers.

The harder issue is what a comparison can reveal, where its information comes from, and who has the right to choose after the comparison is made.

The problem exists before the unit of account

Economic calculation means working out what different courses of action require and what each course would prevent, postpone, damage, or enable.

The hospital must know at least:

  • which materials each project needs;
  • which workers and skills are available;
  • how long each stage takes;
  • what other activities depend on the same workers and materials;
  • which substitutions are safe;
  • what the river is likely to do;
  • which needs become more urgent when delayed;
  • what reserves must remain untouched;
  • what ecological damage each option could create; and
  • how uncertain each estimate is.

The best alternative lost through a decision is its opportunity cost. If the flood barrier uses the remaining steel, the cost includes the maternity rooms that cannot yet be built. If the maternity wing is built first, the cost includes the people and equipment exposed to a higher flood risk.

Opportunity cost is not a capitalist invention. It names a material relation among mutually exclusive possibilities. Money may represent part of that relation, distort it, or hide it. The relation remains.

Prices speak, but never with a neutral voice

The strongest market argument says that prices do more than count money. They gather scattered judgments about scarcity, demand, and substitution. No planning office can know everything that producers and users know.

That warning matters. A plan that treats local knowledge as noise will become stupid. A plan that assumes all relevant information can be collected once, sent upward, and frozen into a table will begin decaying before the table is printed.

Prices, however, do not arrive from nature.

A price forms inside ownership, income, law, bargaining power, monopoly, expected profit, unpaid labour, imperial relations, and ecological costs that firms may never have to bear. It can carry information about scarcity while allocating a good according to purchasing power. It can rise because a medicine is difficult to make, because a patent blocks competitors, or because somebody expects panic tomorrow. The same number can contain very different realities.

Market demand is effective demand, which means a desire backed by money. A wealthy district can register a stronger demand for decorative construction than a poor district registers for drainage. Nothing in the price itself explains that the second need is greater.

Prices are real information formed by a particular social relation. They should be studied, especially during a transition. They should never be mistaken for the unedited voice of human need.

Capitalism plans after separating the planners

Capitalist firms plan production internally. States plan roads, war, energy, money, public health, and the legal field in which firms move. Banks and investment funds direct enormous flows of resources toward expected returns.

The system is full of plans.

What it lacks is common control over their purpose and connection. Separate owners decide what to produce, then confront one another through exchange. Social validation arrives after labour and material have already been committed. A sale confirms that a product counted within the market. An unsold useful object appears as a business failure, even beside a person who needs it.

This is ex post coordination, coordination after the act.

Communist planning aims at ex ante coordination, coordination before the act. Labour becomes social from the beginning because associated producers decide in advance how their powers will be joined.

That does not mean every detail is predicted. It means society no longer waits for profit and sale to reveal whether its own activity belonged to society.

The world has more than one account

No single measure can carry every fact a plan needs.

In-kind calculation records things in their actual units. Steel appears in tonnes. Electricity appears in kilowatt-hours. Water appears in litres. Nurses appear through skills, shifts, and time. Land appears through location, area, soil, and ecological relation.

Labour-time accounts show how much of society’s living activity different paths require. Ecological accounts show carbon, water, toxicity, habitat, regeneration, and thresholds. Use reports show whether a service reaches people and whether a product performs well. Reliability records show failures, repairs, and uncertainty.

Each account answers a different question:

Account What it can reveal What it cannot decide by itself
Physical account Whether the required materials, energy, machines, and outputs fit together Whether the proposed use is worth pursuing
Labour-time account How much social effort a path uses and where scarce skills become a bottleneck Whether saving an hour justifies greater danger, ugliness, or ecological damage
Ecological account Whether a path crosses limits or weakens future reproduction How present and future needs should be balanced in every conflict
Use and quality account Whether an activity actually serves people well How its need ranks against every other unmet need
Risk account What may fail and how severe the result could be How much risk people should accept for a particular benefit
Local report What direct participants know about a place or process How one local need fits the requirements of the whole society

The point is not to find the master account. It is to keep each necessary reality from vanishing inside another.

A worked choice with no automatic winner

Consider a simplified planning exercise. The figures below are illustrative. They show the form of a decision, not a forecast for any real hospital.

The region can commit the construction team to one of three six-month plans:

Plan Immediate work Main gain Main sacrifice Important uncertainty
A Reinforce the riverbank and repair the laboratory roof Protects existing care and equipment from flood damage Maternity expansion waits The next wet season may be milder than expected
B Build the maternity wing and make a temporary roof repair Expands care soonest Existing rooms and equipment remain exposed A major flood could interrupt the whole hospital
C Complete a smaller river barrier, repair the roof, and prepare foundations for the wing Spreads protection and preserves a path toward expansion Delivers less of either major benefit immediately The smaller barrier may require redesign after new river data

Engineers can reject a plan that violates safety or material constraints. Clinicians can explain what delay means for patients. Construction workers can reveal whether a sequence is realistic. River communities can describe floods that a national dataset blurred together. A model can show how each plan affects later capacity.

After all that work, two or three plans may remain feasible.

Nothing has gone wrong.

The calculation has succeeded by clarifying the choice. It has not failed because it did not replace the people choosing.

Plan A may protect more lives under one flood forecast. Plan B may answer a present care crisis that is certain rather than a flood that is probable. Plan C may preserve flexibility at the cost of slower visible progress. Different judgments about urgency, risk, equity, and the future can support different answers while using the same material facts.

No scalar can quietly decide how much present suffering equals how much future danger. When unlike human goods conflict, the final act is political.

Feasible does not mean desirable

A plan is feasible when its requirements can be met within the stated constraints.

Feasibility is powerful. It can expose fantasies, reject impossible combinations, and show the material price of a promise. It can also compare many ways of reaching the same goal.

It cannot decide whether the goal deserves to be reached.

A model might find the lowest-labour route for a railway. That route may divide a neighbourhood, damage a wetland, or make the stations inaccessible to disabled passengers. Those effects can be represented more clearly, and some can become binding constraints. The decision to treat them as constraints has already expressed a social purpose.

Every optimization has an objective. Every objective has a history. Somebody chose what counted as success.

This remains true when the computation is extremely advanced. Faster calculation enlarges the set of consequences society can see. It does not produce a rightful ruler from the calculation.

Knowledge lives at several scales

The market challenge is correct about one thing that central command repeatedly forgets: knowledge is distributed.

A machine operator hears a bearing change pitch. A patient knows why an appointment system is inaccessible. A farm worker knows which part of a field holds water. A neighbourhood knows which road floods before the sensor records it.

Some of this knowledge can enter reports and models. Some becomes meaningful only through practice. The people doing the activity must retain power over its methods and a direct path for reporting danger, capacity, failure, and discovery.

Wider planning bodies possess knowledge that no single locality can hold. They see connections among regions, long projects, reserves, training systems, watersheds, grids, and future requirements. Local knowledge cannot decide alone how much steel the whole society can spare. Central knowledge cannot decide alone how a particular machine can safely be repaired.

A viable plan joins them through bargaining, nested responsibility, feedback, and investigation when normal communication fails. It does not demand that either scale impersonate the other.

Choice should enter before production

A democratic planning process begins with multiple possible plans.

One plan may favour immediate consumption and require less labour now. Another may commit more labour to railways, power, clinics, housing, and training so later planning periods can release more free time. Several middle paths may be materially possible.

Experts, workplaces, and planning bodies can develop the alternatives and make their assumptions visible. Associated people can then choose among them. Once adopted, a plan establishes real commitments. It cannot remain a collection of suggestions whenever a difficult obligation arrives.

Public choice does not mean voting on every bolt. It means that the direction, sacrifice, and purpose of production no longer belong to owners, markets, or a technical caste.

The calculation should let people see:

  • what each plan provides;
  • what it postpones;
  • what labour it asks from whom;
  • what capacities it builds;
  • what reserves it consumes;
  • what risks remain;
  • which groups receive the gains and burdens; and
  • which assumptions would most change the result.

That is not arithmetic decorated with democracy. It is the form calculation takes when society recognizes itself as the author of the plan.

Error must have somewhere to go

All plans are made before the future arrives. They will be wrong in places.

A storm changes a harvest. A new method saves labour. A target turns out to be unsafe. People want less of one product and more of another. A local unit reports that an input specification cannot work.

The decisive question is whether error returns as information or as punishment.

If workers fear reporting a failed target, the centre receives success on paper and failure in reality. If every correction dissolves binding commitments, connected production becomes unreliable. A living plan needs protected interruption, direct communication, investigation, revision, and a clear path back to common decision.

Calculation is therefore continuous. The plan compares expected and actual results, learns why they differ, and changes at the appropriate horizon.

The real argument with the market

The calculation problem should not be dismissed as propaganda. It asks how society can compare alternatives, use dispersed knowledge, coordinate vast interdependence, and correct error without relying on market exchange.

Those are genuine requirements.

The market answer should not be treated as a proof either. Prices do not explain their own social formation. Profit does not represent human need. Ex post validation wastes labour and material when purchasing power fails to meet use. Private plans can coordinate inside an enterprise while producing chaos among enterprises and ecological ruin across generations.

Communist calculation has to achieve something more demanding than a market imitation:

  1. describe the material world directly;
  2. carry knowledge across several scales;
  3. compare feasible paths before labour is committed;
  4. preserve qualities that should not collapse into one number;
  5. expose uncertainty and foregone alternatives;
  6. let associated people choose the social purpose; and
  7. remain revisable through experience.

This is a research and institutional programme, not a solved equation. Its openness is no argument for surrendering human purposes to an existing mechanism whose purposes are already built into property and profit.

The number returns to its proper place

The hospital must count.

It must count steel, hours, rooms, rain, patients, reserves, and years. It must compare what happens when one project comes before another. It must listen to the people who know the roof, the ward, the river, and the construction site.

Then people must choose.

A number can rule out a bridge that cannot stand. It can reveal the labour saved by a machine or the danger hidden in a schedule. It can show that two promises cannot both be kept this year.

It cannot tell society which promise makes the future worth building.
