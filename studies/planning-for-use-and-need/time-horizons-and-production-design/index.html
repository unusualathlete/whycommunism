04Studies · Planning for Use and Need

The Plan Has More Than One Clock

At 8:14 in the morning, a railway signal turns red.

The action takes less than a second. A train is already inside the next section of track. Nobody calls a national assembly. The signal stops the approaching train because two trains cannot safely occupy the same space.

The rail beneath it took years to plan. Steel was ordered months ahead. The sensor entered a maintenance schedule last week. The driver began a shift this morning. A technician may discover tomorrow that the signal caught a fault hidden for years.

One red light contains a second plan, a daily plan, a maintenance plan, an investment plan, and a plan for the future shape of the region.

They belong together.

They cannot all be governed in the same way.

Time changes the planning problem

A planning horizon is the stretch of future a decision must take into account.

Electricity balances from moment to moment. Bread responds to tomorrow’s expected use. Farms move through seasons. A nurse’s training takes years. A railway can reorganize settlement for generations. A watershed may need decades of repair before the full result appears.

At short horizons, the main problem may be speed and safety.

At long horizons, the main problem may be uncertainty, investment, and the relation between present sacrifice and future power.

Horizon Typical decisions Knowledge that matters most Appropriate authority
Seconds and minutes Stop a machine, balance a grid, respond to bleeding Direct perception, automatic safeguards, trained judgment Immediate operational control
Day Assign shifts, route vehicles, schedule care, sequence work Local use, current capacity, bodies, weather Workers and local coordinating bodies
Week and month Adjust output, order components, plan maintenance Repeated demand, delays, stock, emerging patterns Connected operations and regional coordination
Season and year Plant crops, renew infrastructure, train workers, build reserves Natural cycles, wear, demographics, reproduction Wider planning bodies with local and scientific input
Several years Build rail, power, housing, hospitals, and productive capacity Alternative social paths, total labour, dependencies, future gains Public choice among feasible plans
Generations Restore ecosystems, shape settlement, preserve knowledge Ecological limits, irreversible effects, uncertainty Institutions answerable to people who cannot yet speak

No row can be sealed off from the others. A second-by-second failure can revise a national standard. A long decarbonization plan changes which skills a workshop teaches this month.

The second belongs near the event

Some conditions cannot wait for consultation.

A line overheats. A worker enters a dangerous zone. A grid loses a generator. A surgical instrument fails.

Real-time control means adjusting a process while it is happening. It belongs with trained people and reliable systems close enough to perceive the event.

The wider society still matters. It establishes safety standards, supplies equipment, supports training, and defines responsibilities before the emergency. It reviews the event afterward. It cannot script every movement in advance.

This gives local autonomy its strongest case. The person seeing the danger needs the power to act.

The stop is not outside the plan. It is one of the ways the plan remains attached to life.

The day must fit a human body

Daily planning arranges the rhythms people actually experience:

  • shifts;
  • deliveries;
  • school hours;
  • appointments;
  • meals;
  • charging and power peaks;
  • transport;
  • rest;
  • care; and
  • maintenance.

A schedule can balance every machine and still fail.

Workers travel. Children need care. Disabled people may require different timing. Bodies tire. Concentration changes. Weather slows a task. Learning requires time that produces no immediate output.

The daily plan should be shaped close to the activity, within the commitments connecting it to others. A clinic knows when its neighbourhood arrives. A depot knows where a morning crowd forms. A workshop knows which sequence lets several jobs share one machine.

Planning for use treats these facts as conditions of production, not inconveniences left for private life to absorb.

Weeks and months turn events into patterns

One request may be chance. A month of similar requests can justify a change.

Weekly and monthly planning shows:

  • which products people actually use;
  • where queues persist;
  • which components fail;
  • how much maintenance was postponed;
  • where illness or exhaustion is rising;
  • whether deliveries match promises;
  • which local invention deserves wider study; and
  • whether an expected demand was mistaken.

This horizon is long enough to see recurrence and short enough to revise production before large stocks accumulate.

It is also where production to order becomes practical for many durable goods.

Social reproduction lives through seasons and years

A society produces more than finished goods. It reproduces the conditions that let production and life continue.

Machines wear down. Workers need food, housing, health, education, and rest. Soil loses fertility or gains it. Students become skilled workers. Buildings collect small faults. Rivers rise and fall.

An annual plan asks whether this year’s activity preserved the powers needed next year.

That question changes the meaning of output.

A factory may reach its numerical target while exhausting its workers and wearing machines beyond repair. A farm may harvest heavily while consuming the soil. A bridge may remain open while hidden corrosion spreads.

The account must include replacement, maintenance, training, reserves, and ecological renewal. Reproduction is the test of whether a success can continue without destroying its source.

Long projects consume before they return

Marx’s Capital, Volume II examines a problem every mode of production faces.

Some activities take labour, productive means, and means of subsistence for a long time before supplying a useful result. A railway may absorb years of work before the first passenger travels. A power plant, hospital, watershed restoration, or training programme can do the same.

Capitalist production mobilizes such work through money-capital, credit, stock companies, state guarantees, contracts, and expected future returns. A disturbance in money and credit can stop a materially possible project. Speculation around long investment can also drive sudden expansion and collapse in connected markets.

Socialized production still faces the material problem. It must calculate how much labour, machinery, material, and subsistence can be committed for years without injuring the branches that keep society alive in the meantime.

The difference is that money-capital no longer stands between society and its own productive powers.

The long project can be asked directly:

  • How many workers and skills does it require each year?
  • What must they and their dependants receive while the project produces no immediate result?
  • Which machines and materials will be unavailable elsewhere?
  • Which present needs will be delayed?
  • What new capacity will exist when the project is complete?
  • How uncertain is that result?
  • Can the project be staged, paused, or redirected?

Long planning does not abolish sacrifice. It makes the sacrifice a conscious social decision.

Several futures can be materially possible

Suppose a country has enough capacity for one of three major five-year paths.

One path directs more labour toward immediate housing repair and consumption.

Another builds rail, grid capacity, clinics, and training at a faster rate, asking more labour now in order to shorten necessary labour later.

A third stages investment more slowly, preserving larger reserves against ecological or international shocks.

All three may be feasible.

The population should see the difference before a path is adopted. A long plan establishes a social promise about present work and future life. Its purpose cannot belong only to the office capable of drawing it.

Once chosen, major commitments bind. A railway cannot be rebuilt from a new political mood each morning.

Binding does not mean petrified. A failed technology, war, epidemic, migration, scientific discovery, or ecological shock can alter the world in which the plan made sense. Revision rules belong inside the plan from the beginning.

Dynamic planning follows the film

A static plan balances one picture. A dynamic plan follows change from one period to the next.

The machine built this year changes next year’s capacity. The forest cut this year changes future water and soil. The student trained today becomes the engineer available later. The bridge repaired now avoids a larger reconstruction.

Time creates paths. Some choices can be reversed cheaply. Others lock in infrastructure, settlement, skill, or ecological damage for decades.

A dynamic plan therefore needs:

  • starting conditions;
  • flows during each period;
  • changing stocks and capacities;
  • maintenance and depreciation;
  • delays between work and result;
  • reserves;
  • uncertainty;
  • points where revision remains possible; and
  • feedback from actual outcomes.

The numbers trace consequences. People still decide how much the present owes the future and how much suffering the future may ask from the present.

Finished inventory is only one way to provide choice

Capitalist retail often makes variety visible through stock.

Many finished products are made before anyone has chosen them, transported, displayed, discounted, stored, and sometimes discarded. A car can sit on a lot while weather, financing, and fashion determine whether it sells. One model may include features physically present but disabled so price tiers can be maintained.

Production for use can move some choice earlier into the production process.

For a durable product, people could test standard designs, select useful options, and place a request. A factory could then assemble a particular combination from common modules. The product would begin its life already connected to a use.

This is production to order.

The proposal changes the time relation. Society trades instant possession for:

  • less finished inventory;
  • fewer unwanted variations;
  • more personal choice;
  • less weathering and storage;
  • a clearer demand signal; and
  • production that begins from a registered use.

It is not suitable for everything.

The shelf and the queue are both real

A society cannot answer every problem by asking people to wait.

Bandages, staple foods, firefighting equipment, essential medicine, spare grid components, emergency shelter, and many daily goods need stocks and reserves. Producing them only after a request arrives would be cruel or dangerous.

Other goods can be made after selection. Furniture, some tools, vehicles, specialized equipment, and many repair parts may tolerate a planned wait.

The design problem can be stated clearly:

Product condition Better default
Immediate need, predictable use, severe cost of delay Keep finished stock or a protected reserve
Durable good with many optional features Produce to order from common modules
Rare failure with severe consequences Hold strategic spare parts even if most are never used
Easy local fabrication from common feedstock Keep design, machine capacity, and feedstock ready
Highly uncertain demand with rapid obsolescence Delay final assembly until use is registered
Long natural production time Forecast, reserve, and diversify well before demand appears

The aim is neither an infinite warehouse nor an infinite queue. It is the right mixture of stock, capacity, information, and patience for the use.

Modular production separates readiness from completion

Modular production builds products from standardized components that can be combined in different ways.

A factory may keep motors, frames, control systems, panels, and fasteners ready without deciding the final form of every product. Once a request arrives, final assembly creates the chosen configuration.

This can widen variety without multiplying every finished combination.

Modules also create risks. A common component can become a common point of failure. Standardization can erase local needs. A badly chosen interface can lock future production into an inferior design.

Good modular planning therefore asks:

  • Which parts are stable across many uses?
  • Which parts should remain open to variation?
  • Which interfaces make repair easy?
  • How much reserve is needed for a shared component?
  • Can another workshop produce a substitute?
  • Does the standard preserve the knowledge of the people using it?

Modularity is a production design, not a substitute for judgment.

Waiting can become a social power

A society capable of waiting for some things can receive better things with less waste.

Capital trains people to experience speed as a universal virtue. Instant availability appears convenient because warehouses, transport, discarded stock, rushed labour, and ecological cost remain out of sight.

A planned wait can be reasonable when it means receiving a durable object made for the actual use.

Waiting is unacceptable when it comes from neglect, unequal access, artificial shortage, or administrative failure. A person needing medicine does not become more communist by standing in a longer queue.

The difference lies in knowledge and control. People should know why the wait exists, how long it is expected to last, what alternatives remain, and whether the production design actually creates a better social result.

Patience deserves no praise when somebody else imposed it.

Local fabrication is a proposal, not a universal cure

Publicly available additive manufacturing offers one possible extension of order-led production.

If communities have machines, feedstock, tested designs, and trained workers, some parts and tools could be produced near the point of use. Finished inventories would become inventories of capacity, material, and knowledge.

This remains a bounded proposal.

Additive methods have limits in material, speed, energy, quality control, safety, and scale. A printed part for a shelf is different from a pressure vessel or a railway axle. Public ownership does not remove engineering.

The useful idea is broader than one machine: keep flexible capacity ready, then complete production when a real need becomes known.

Planned production can still make mistakes

Order-led production reduces one form of overproduction: making finished goods speculatively and hoping purchasing power validates them later.

It cannot make error impossible.

People can request products they stop using. Forecasts can be wrong. Modules can pile up. A plan can build the wrong capacity. Political pressure can preserve an obsolete project. Emergency reserves can expire.

The advantage is not perfection. It is a more direct path between use, production, feedback, and revision.

Waste becomes an error society can study rather than a normal cost of private competition.

Each clock needs a different kind of power

The railway signal can stop a train instantly.

It cannot decide where the next railway should go.

A workshop can choose the sequence of today’s orders.

It cannot decide alone how much steel every region receives for five years.

A national plan can establish decarbonization and infrastructure commitments.

It cannot tell a driver what obstacle appeared on the track one second ago.

Authority should be defined by task, horizon, and review.

Permanent emergency authority mistakes the second for all of history. Endless consultation during danger mistakes the long public plan for the immediate operation. Human planning learns to change its form as the clock changes.

The second speaks to the generation

The signal turns green.

The train moves because a thousand plans met at one piece of track. A worker’s immediate judgment, a daily schedule, a monthly maintenance pattern, a five-year investment, and a generation’s choice about settlement and climate all entered the moment.

Then the moment returns information to them.

A fault found today can change next year’s maintenance and the next railway’s design. A long social purpose can decide which sensor is installed tomorrow.

The clocks stop fighting when each can speak in its own time and still be heard by the others.
