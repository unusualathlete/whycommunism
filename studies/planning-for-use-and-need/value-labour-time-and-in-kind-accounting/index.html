Count the Work, Not Value

A community needs a bridge.

The bridge will use concrete, steel, cranes, survey equipment, engineering knowledge, transport, food for the workers, maintenance for the machines, and thousands of hours of living activity. None of those requirements vanish because production is no longer organized through money.

The bridge has a material cost.

Does it therefore have value?

The answer depends on what the word means. In everyday speech, value can mean importance, usefulness, beauty, or moral worth. Marx uses it more exactly. Value is a social form that labour takes when separate producers make commodities for exchange and their labours become social through the exchange of products.

A communist society still counts. It counts more directly than a society whose common labour first appears through the movement of commodities and money.

The difficult distinction is this:

Labour time can remain a fact of production after value has ceased to organize society.

That distinction separates three things often folded into one:

  1. value formed through commodity exchange;
  2. social accounts used to plan labour and material; and
  3. transitional certificates used to regulate part of individual consumption.

Confusing them makes communism look either incapable of calculation or condemned to rebuild money under a new name.

A commodity has to meet society after it is made

Commodity production begins from separated producers.

The carpenter makes a table. The farmer grows wheat. The textile firm produces cloth. Each activity is private in the sense that no common plan has already determined its place within society’s total labour. The products go to market and face one another in exchange.

Exchange performs social validation. The sale confirms, after the work has happened, that this private labour counted under the prevailing social conditions.

The table can be useful and still fail this test. A person may need it and lack the money to buy it. Another table may sell because an owner can afford novelty while a school has no desks. Usefulness is necessary for a commodity, but use does not govern the allocation.

Competition also presses different producers toward a socially necessary labour time. A firm that takes far longer than the prevailing conditions allow cannot usually make the market reward every extra hour. Concrete carpentry, farming, sewing, nursing, and coding enter a social comparison through the abstraction of value and its expression in money.

Value is therefore neither a substance hidden inside the table nor a synonym for the effort spent on it. It is the form through which labour becomes social in a society of commodity producers.

Directly social labour starts from the connection

Associated production reverses the order.

The bridge is not built privately and offered to society afterward. Its place in common labour is decided before the steel is cut. Surveyors, steelworks, transport, construction teams, communities, and environmental knowledge enter one connected plan.

Their labour is directly social because the association recognizes and coordinates it from the outset.

Society can then ask:

  • How many tonnes of steel are required?
  • Which grade of concrete fits the river and climate?
  • How many engineering, fabrication, transport, and construction hours are needed?
  • What food, housing, safety equipment, and care support the workers during the project?
  • Which other projects use the same cranes and skills?
  • How long will the bridge last?
  • What maintenance will keep it safe?
  • What damage could it do to the river?
  • How much travel time will it return to people?

These questions can be answered in tonnes, hours, litres, shifts, years, energy, risk, and useful effects. No product needs to acquire value before the plan can see it.

Engels makes the point sharply in Anti-Dühring. Once labour is directly social, society can know the average time required for an article without expressing that time indirectly in a third product. Useful effects and expenditure of labour remain in the decision. Value does not.

The same hour can belong to two different relations

The arithmetic alone cannot tell us whether a labour-time figure is value.

Imagine two statements:

  1. This pump normally requires eight hours of social labour under current conditions.
  2. This pump must sell for an amount representing eight hours of abstract labour.

The first can inform a common plan. The second belongs to products being related through exchange.

Under capitalism, socially necessary labour time asserts itself behind the producers through competition and sale. Under associated production, people calculate average labour time directly and use it inside a plan they govern.

The number may look similar. Its social power is different.

Question Commodity production Associated production
How does labour become social? Private labour receives validation through exchange Labour is coordinated as social before production begins
What connects the products? Commodity exchange and money Planned relations among activities and uses
What does average labour time do? Regulates producers through value and competition Informs the common use of labour and productive means
Who governs the account? The movement of value acts back upon separate producers Associated producers interpret and revise the account
What confirms success? Sale and profitable reproduction Useful effect, material reproduction, human development, and ecological fit

Calling both systems “labour accounting” would erase the relation that matters most.

In-kind calculation begins with the thing

In-kind accounting records the actual things a process needs and produces.

The bridge account does not begin with a money total. It begins with a design and a material world:

  • tonnes of several kinds of steel;
  • cubic metres of concrete;
  • machine-hours for cranes and fabrication;
  • kilometres of transport;
  • energy by source and timing;
  • workers with particular skills;
  • safety equipment;
  • land and river effects;
  • expected years of service; and
  • inspection and repair intervals.

A money sum can conceal a missing bolt. Physical balance cannot.

In-kind does not mean every object can be compared directly with every other object. Ten tonnes of steel do not equal ten nursing shifts. The plan follows how activities depend on one another, where substitution is possible, and where a requirement is absolute.

The account is plural because the world is plural.

Labour time reveals the piece of life being used

Labour-time accounting tracks the human activity required to reproduce society and carry out its chosen projects.

This matters for at least four reasons.

First, labour is limited. A society can ask people to do only so much before health, care, learning, and freedom are damaged.

Second, saving necessary labour can release time for rest, art, science, relationships, play, and political life.

Third, a labour account can expose bottlenecks. A project may require ordinary material but an unusually scarce group of trained workers.

Fourth, comparisons can reveal where conditions deserve investigation. If similar workshops need very different hours for the same reliable output, one may have worn machinery, unsafe conditions, bad inputs, or a better method worth spreading.

The figure does not pronounce sentence.

An average can become a managerial whip if local conditions disappear. A workshop taking longer may be protecting safety. A workshop appearing faster may be hiding defects or unpaid effort. Workers must help define, interpret, and challenge the account.

Time is precious because life is precious. That is exactly why the clock cannot be allowed to rule life.

One project, several ledgers

The bridge can be placed across three linked social accounts:

Ledger Example entries Planning use
Material ledger Steel, concrete, machinery, power, land, spare parts Tests physical feasibility and dependencies
Social labour ledger Design, training, fabrication, transport, construction, inspection, maintenance Shows the distribution and timing of social effort
Use and reproduction ledger Journeys shortened, access widened, river effects, service life, repairs Tests whether the result justifies the material and labour it absorbs

No ledger becomes a universal currency.

The material account may show that one design uses less steel. The labour account may show that it requires rare skills. The use account may show that a more repairable design lasts longer and keeps the bridge open during storms.

The decision needs all three. Reducing them to one number too early creates the appearance of exactness by throwing away the qualities people needed to judge.

A labour certificate is a personal claim, not the social account

Marx’s lower phase of communist society still bears marks of the society from which it emerged.

After society makes common deductions for replacement, expansion, reserves, administration, schools, healthcare, care for people unable to work, and other common needs, an individual producer may receive a certificate recording a contribution. The certificate allows a corresponding claim on the portion of consumer goods still distributed through individual accounting.

This labour certificate is not the same thing as the social labour-time ledger.

The ledger helps society plan bridges, bread, hospitals, power, and maintenance. The certificate regulates one person’s access to a limited part of personal consumption during a transitional phase.

The certificate also differs from money when its institutions make these limits real:

  • it does not circulate from person to person;
  • it cannot earn interest;
  • it cannot buy labour-power;
  • it cannot buy productive assets;
  • it cannot become capital;
  • it cannot be hoarded into social command;
  • it is cancelled when used; and
  • it records a claim inside an already socialized system of production.

Printing the word “labour” on circulating money changes none of money’s powers. The distinction lies in what the token can do.

Owen, Dühring, and Marx do not propose the same thing

Nineteenth-century debates over labour notes are often compressed into one family of schemes.

Robert Owen’s labour notes belonged to a transitional cooperative project. They attempted to relate products through labour and could be described as labour money. Their transitional intention placed the scheme closer to a movement beyond exchange than a system that made exchange eternal. Their ability to act or be converted as money still left the relation unstable.

Eugen Dühring retained exchange and metallic money while trying to command money to behave as a mere certificate. Engels’s criticism is institutional: where hoarding, speculation, commodity exchange, and money remain, their social logic cannot be abolished by instruction. The system reproduces the relation it claims to escape.

Marx’s certificate begins after the decisive change. Productive means are common, labour is directly social, and products do not confront one another as commodities. The certificate is a limited claim within common production, incapable of circulation and oriented toward its own disappearance.

The sequence matters:

  1. social relations change;
  2. the certificate performs a narrow transitional function;
  3. common provision and productive capacity grow; and
  4. the personal certificate loses its reason to exist.

A token cannot create the social relation that alone makes it non-monetary.

Equal labour claims preserve inequality

Suppose two people contribute the same measured labour and receive the same personal claim.

One lives alone. The other supports children. One is healthy. The other lives with a disability. One can sustain intense work more easily. The other requires more rest or care.

The equal measure produces unequal lives.

Marx calls this persistence bourgeois right. Individuals are viewed from one side, as workers, while other differences and needs disappear. The political answer is equity rather than equality: support must respond to unequal conditions if people are to share real freedom.

The common deductions already move beyond the individual exchange of equivalent claims. Healthcare, education, public transport, care, reserves, administration, and productive replacement return to people socially. They are not private purchases balanced against one person’s hours.

As that common field grows, the part of life governed by an individual labour claim can shrink.

The certificate disappears before the clock does

The fate of the personal certificate must be separated from the fate of the social account.

When food, housing, transport, healthcare, education, culture, and other goods become reliably common, checking each person’s contribution before access becomes wasteful and unjust. The certificate turns from a transitional aid into a fetter.

The social labour account can still remain.

A community may let people take potatoes freely because production is ample and already recorded. It still needs to know the labour, land, water, storage, and transport required for the next harvest. Free access does not make the crop self-producing.

The mature movement is therefore:

  • personal claims recede;
  • common provision expands;
  • distribution approaches need;
  • labour-time remains available for planning; and
  • necessary labour itself is reduced and transformed.

Communism does not honor labour by making every hour an eternal ticket. It honors human life by returning as many hours as possible to free development.

Difficult work does not fit inside a flat hour

Labour-time accounting raises real disputes.

Training takes time before a skill can be used. Some work is intense, dangerous, exhausting, irregular, or inseparable from care. Creative and scientific activity may produce no predictable result. Disabled people may work under different conditions. Machinery contains past labour and needs maintenance and replacement.

These problems do not prove that labour time is meaningless. They prove that labour time cannot be interpreted apart from the concrete activity.

The account may compare like processes more reliably than unlike ones. It may record training separately rather than multiplying a skilled hour by a mysterious coefficient. It may treat dangerous work as a burden to remove, automate, shorten, and share rather than create a permanent premium that gives somebody an interest in preserving danger.

Exact procedures remain open to experiment and review. The settled point is narrower: no technical conversion can rank human worth, and no one coordinate can replace the map.

What abolition changes

Value abolition changes how society learns whether work belonged to society.

Under commodity production, the answer arrives through exchange after separate labour has acted.

Under associated production, the answer begins with a plan, remains open to feedback during the work, and returns through use after the product enters common life.

This direct connection makes a different kind of efficiency possible. A durable bridge may use more labour now and far less later. A safer method may be slower during one shift and save years of injury. A repairable product may reduce total output because people no longer need constant replacement.

Value seeks expansion in its own form. Common accounting asks whether social powers are reproducing a rich human world.

The bridge and the morning it returns

The bridge is finished.

Its steel, concrete, labour, river effect, and maintenance remain countable. Children cross it to school. A clinic becomes reachable. Workers repair it before small cracks become danger.

Nothing about those uses requires the bridge to possess value.

Society counted the work because work uses life. It counted material because nature is not infinite. It counted the useful result because neither sacrifice deserves praise without purpose.

Then it placed the account back in human hands.

Source notes

Primary texts

  • Karl Marx, Capital, Volume I, chapter 1, 1867, on the commodity, value, abstract labour, social validation through exchange, and associated production.
  • Karl Marx, Grundrisse, written in 1857 to 1858, especially the material on communal production and economy of time.
  • Karl Marx, Critique of the Gotha Programme, 1875, on common deductions, labour certificates, bourgeois right, need, and the phases of communist society.
  • Karl Marx, Capital, Volume II, 1885, on social reproduction, long production periods, and non-circulating certificates under socialized production.
  • Friedrich Engels, Anti-Dühring, Part III, chapter 3, “Production”, 1877, on Owen, Dühring, labour notes, direct labour-time calculation, useful effects, and production without value.

Further study

  • Group of International Communists, Fundamental Principles of Communist Production and Distribution, 1930, a historically important proposal for social labour accounting whose institutional conclusions require critical comparison with Marx’s phases of communism.
  • Tadashi Tsushima, Understanding “Labor Certificates” on the Basis of the Theory of Value: The Law of Value and Socialism (1956), from the economic-planning reading programme.
  • Jan Philip Dapprich, Rationality and Distribution in the Socialist Economy (2020), on calculation, distribution, and rational planning.
  • Paul Cockshott and Allin Cottrell, Towards a New Socialism, 1993, especially the treatment of labour time and democratic planning.
