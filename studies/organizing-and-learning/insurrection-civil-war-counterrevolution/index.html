The Morning After the Government Falls

Imagine a capital city at five in the morning.

The government has collapsed. Crowds still fill the square. The president has fled. Soldiers have abandoned some checkpoints and joined the uprising at others. People climb the steps of a ministry that frightened them yesterday and discover ordinary desks, half-drunk cups of coffee, and telephones ringing for officials who will never answer.

Across the city, a baker turns on an oven. A hospital checks its oxygen. A railway worker waits for a signal. Somebody has to decide whether the reservoir gates should open. Somebody has to tell the grain trucks where to go.

The old command has broken.

Common life has not paused to celebrate.

This is the morning when a political victory reveals its real size. Taking a government building may end one chain of command. It does not automatically create the social power needed to feed a city, protect a revolution, or change the relations that return people to work each day under somebody else’s control.

A government can fall in a night. A social order cannot.

When obedience stops traveling downward

A political crisis begins when rulers can no longer govern in the old way and large numbers of people refuse to be governed in the old way.

Commands arrive but are ignored. Institutions that looked permanent turn out to depend on thousands of daily acts of cooperation. Soldiers hesitate. Civil servants leak documents. Workers occupy a plant instead of leaving at the manager’s order. Tenants refuse eviction together. A scandal, famine, lost war, economic crash, mass strike, colonial revolt, or split inside the ruling class may open the crisis.

Misery alone does not do it. People can suffer terribly and remain divided, frightened, exhausted, or trapped inside the belief that nothing else can work. The decisive change comes when an old authority loses its grip while another organized power begins to appear.

That power is easy to misunderstand. It is not just the crowd in the square. It lives in the ability to keep a clinic supplied, settle a disagreement, publish trustworthy information, move food, defend a neighborhood, and make decisions people recognize as their own.

Anger can break a door.

On the other side lies the work of governing.

An insurrection opens the question

An insurrection is an organized uprising that directly challenges political authority. Mass strikes, mutiny, occupations, demonstrations, the collapse of official command, and armed confrontation can meet inside it.

The visible moments become history’s favorite images: a gate opens, a prison empties, a police unit retreats, a flag changes. They matter because power has a physical body. Offices, communications, weapons, records, transport, and trained personnel do not vanish when people declare a new world.

But an insurrection is still an opening.

A coup can remove a ruler too. A few generals seize the radio station, arrest ministers, and inherit the same machinery. Orders continue flowing from the top. Most people learn that power changed hands by hearing a different voice on the news.

The deeper difference is not the number of guns or the name announced afterward. It is where political initiative lives.

Are workers, residents, soldiers, peasants, and public servants building organs through which they can deliberate and act? Can they question those who coordinate? Do they know why decisions are made? Can they remove people who abuse authority? Or has one small body occupied the empty chair and begun speaking as everybody?

The chair is the smallest part of power.

Two authorities enter the same kitchen

Sometimes the old state remains standing while councils, communes, assemblies, strike committees, or other popular bodies begin directing part of social life. This is called dual power.

The phrase sounds as if two governments have placed offices on opposite sides of a street. The conflict reaches much farther.

A city may receive one order from a ministry and another from a workers’ council. A landowner points to a title. Peasants point to the harvest they planted. A company orders a factory closed. Workers keep it running for public need. One authority claims the law; another holds the trust and activity of people who make daily life possible.

Soon the disagreement enters a kitchen. Which grain shipment arrives? Which money is accepted? Whose safety rule governs the bakery? Which family receives fuel first?

No society can obey two final answers to these questions forever. Dual power is a crossing, not a destination.

Its outcome depends on more than who wins a battle. One power may possess official buildings yet lose the social cooperation that gives those buildings meaning. Another may command enormous loyalty yet fail to coordinate beyond the places where it first grew.

The side able to reproduce common life begins turning authority into reality.

War bends the institutions built to fight it

The displaced rulers may accept defeat. History gives little reason to count on it.

They can retain wealth, military officers, foreign allies, control over credit, technical knowledge, media, land, and the confidence of people trained from birth to obey them. Their resistance can turn a struggle for power into civil war, sustained armed conflict among organized forces within the same country or political territory.

War changes the meaning of ordinary things.

A railway timetable becomes a question of military supply. A bridge becomes a route that can save one town and expose another. A radio report becomes intelligence. A missing grain shipment might come from broken machinery, frightened drivers, a blockade, corruption, hoarding, or deliberate sabotage.

Every delay feels like a threat. Every doubt arrives at the worst possible hour.

Under such pressure, command narrows. Decisions speed up. Information is restricted. Criticism begins to sound like disloyalty. People who are good at giving orders gain power over people who are good at asking whether the orders still make sense.

Some concentration may be unavoidable while lives depend on a decision made in minutes. That necessity leaves a mark. A military form built to act under fire can begin swallowing the political life it was meant to defend.

This is the central danger of revolutionary war:

A revolution unable to coordinate its defense can be destroyed. A revolution that turns all of society into an army can survive and cease to be a revolution.

Counter-revolution does not always wear the old uniform

Counter-revolution is organized activity that protects or restores the class order a revolution is trying to end.

Its clearest face is an army marching under the old flag. Its other faces can be harder to recognize.

An owner closes a factory and moves its money. A foreign government blocks fuel and medicine. Senior officials misplace records, withhold expertise, or wait for the new order to fail. Banks freeze credit. Newspapers turn every shortage into proof that ordinary people cannot govern. A coup grows inside the institutions created to prevent a coup.

Then there is a quieter restoration.

New officials begin controlling appointments, information, housing, supplies, and political access. Their positions harden into privileges. The public hears revolutionary language while losing the ability to correct the people speaking it. What belonged to a class of private owners begins returning as the collective property of an official layer.

Counter-revolution can cross the border with an army. It can also grow in the distance between a population and a government that claims to embody it.

This is why fear cannot be allowed to define the enemy by itself.

A class relation is not a bloodline

Suppose a wealthy owner pays an armed group to restore the old regime. A technician from the same social class keeps a hospital generator running. A shopkeeper hides food because she fears there will be none tomorrow. A conscript follows an order because his family is being threatened. An administrator destroys records because he expects the revolution to fail.

They do not present the same problem.

Classes are relations of ownership, work, and power. They are not families marked forever by birth. People can break with the interests attached to their position. They can also defend those interests with enormous violence.

Revolutionary defense must therefore ask what a person or organization has done, what power it controls, and what danger it creates. When institutions punish a category of people instead of acts and relations, private revenge and political convenience quickly enter the room. Nationality, ethnicity, religion, accent, education, or family background can become evidence. Officials who control classification acquire frightening power.

A revolution has to dismantle the ability of a class to rule.

It does not need a permanent population whose existence makes permanent repression seem necessary.

Three histories that refuse to become slogans

The Paris Commune of 1871, the Russian Revolution, and the Spanish Civil War are often treated like three vending machines. Insert the correct political tendency and each delivers the lesson you already wanted.

The Commune becomes proof that revolutionaries must always strike faster. Russia proves either that disciplined leadership solves everything or that every revolution ends in dictatorship. Spain becomes a courtroom where one part of the left prosecutes another forever.

Real history will not stay that flat.

The Commune joined popular self-government to war, isolation, hesitation, invention, and a savage counter-revolutionary massacre. Russia joined councils of workers and soldiers to party struggle, state collapse, social transformation, foreign intervention, hunger, civil war, and the growth of bureaucratic command. Spain joined extraordinary worker and peasant initiative to anti-fascist war, competing revolutionary projects, international abandonment and intervention, and destructive conflict within the forces resisting Franco.

Each history asks several questions at once.

What organs had working people already built before the decisive crisis? Could those organs reach beyond their first strongholds? How did war alter the relationship between civilian judgment and military command? Who organized food, land, production, care, and transport? Could bad news travel upward without being punished? Which emergency powers survived the emergency?

A lesson worth carrying forward must survive all those questions.

No civil war stays inside one country

Fuel crosses borders. So do weapons, debt, refugees, propaganda, food, military advisers, family ties, sanctions, and hope.

A revolution may begin on national ground, but it immediately enters a world already divided by imperial power. Foreign states can invade, fund proxy armies, close markets, control shipping, manipulate debt, or offer aid with a chain attached.

The choices are cruel. Refuse outside help and isolation may allow hunger to win. Accept it and a revolution may become dependent on a state pursuing another purpose.

Internationalism cannot mean friendship among governments that happen to use similar words. It means working people across borders developing the power to support one another without buying one another’s obedience. It means judging every alliance by what it lets ordinary people become able to do.

A national apparatus may survive by attaching itself to a stronger state.

The revolution survives only by enlarging the international power of the class itself.

The army must know how to disappear

When shooting ends, military habits remain.

Weapons, intelligence offices, emergency supply systems, special courts, censorship, secret budgets, and temporary appointments have gathered people around them. Those people have knowledge, careers, authority, loyalties, and reasons to believe that the danger is not truly over.

Demobilization means more than sending soldiers home. It means returning the useful functions created by emergency to visible and accountable common life, while dissolving the coercive privilege built around them.

This requires deliberate work. Records must become reviewable. Temporary powers need real endings. Civil institutions must regain knowledge they surrendered. People affected by command need ways to challenge abuse. Resources organized for survival must be turned toward repair.

The state does not wither because a theory predicts that it will. Separate power contracts only when society develops the ability to take its functions back.

That is the measure provided by the Commonalty, society directly organizing its shared life. Defense serves emancipation when it protects that growth. It becomes another ruler when the institutions of defense need people to remain politically weak.

What victory has to mean

At five in the morning, after the old government falls, the city asks thousands of questions.

Who opens the reservoir? Who decides where the grain goes? Who answers the frightened shopkeeper? Who prevents an old general from taking back the barracks? Who tells the truth when the first plan fails? Who can remove the person giving orders?

An insurrection can open the gate. Civil war may decide whether anyone gets through it. Counter-revolution will use wealth, fear, habit, foreign power, and every weakness inside the new society to push people back.

Survival matters. So does the form of the life that survives.

The working class has not won if it ends the rule of one power only to receive its own decisions as commands from another. Victory means that more people can understand, decide, coordinate, correct, and defend the common world than before.

The morning after the government falls, the revolution begins learning whether power has changed hands or whether hands have merely changed at the top.

Source notes

From the Commune to the revolutionary crisis

  • Karl Marx, The Civil War in France, 1871, and its surviving drafts, on the Commune’s institutions, its armed defense, and the reabsorption of state power by society.
  • Friedrich Engels, 1891 introduction to The Civil War in France, on the standing army, elected and recallable office, and the concrete institutional meaning Engels drew from the Commune.
  • Karl Marx and Friedrich Engels, Address of the Central Committee to the Communist League, 1850, on independent class organization, political preparation, arms, and the danger of surrendering initiative to a temporary ally.
  • Karl Marx, The Class Struggles in France, 1850, on crisis, provisional alliances, counter-revolution, and the changing political form of class conflict.
  • Rosa Luxemburg, The Mass Strike, the Political Party and the Trade Unions, 1906, on how mass action develops through the reciprocal movement of economic and political struggle.
  • Vladimir Lenin, The State and Revolution, 1917, on the destruction of the old state machine and the contradiction between necessary coercion and its eventual disappearance.

The international dispute after 1917

  • Communist International, The Platform of the Communist International, 1919, written amid revolution, civil war, and international intervention.
  • Communist International, Manifesto of the Second World Congress, 1920, on civil war, colonial revolt, counter-revolution, mass organization, and the refusal to sanctify a single form of struggle.
  • Vladimir Lenin, draft theses on national and colonial questions, 1920, together with the Second Congress debate and supplementary theses, where revolution in the imperial centers was argued in relation to anti-colonial struggle.
  • Anton Pannekoek, “World Revolution and Communist Tactics”, 1920, and Herman Gorter, Open Letter to Comrade Lenin, 1920, on the danger of generalizing Russian tactics across different class structures.
  • Amadeo Bordiga, “Party and Class Action”, 1921, on the changing relation among party, class, conditions, defeat, and revolutionary capacity.
