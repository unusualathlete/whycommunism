Two Powers, Neither a Substitute

A union can unite people who disagree about almost everything except the fact that they must act together.

A communist party cannot.

The union begins from a shared material position: the same employer, trade, workplace, industry, rent burden, or immediate fight. The party begins from a programme for the whole class movement and the abolition of capitalism.

This difference is not a rank.

The party possesses historical memory, political concentration, and a view of the final aim that a broad economic organization cannot require from every member. The union, strike committee, or workers’ council possesses mass experience and direct class power that a political minority cannot manufacture.

The class needs both.

The danger begins when either tries to become the other.

Why this became a century-long argument

Before the First World War, powerful socialist parties and trade unions grew together in Europe. Their size seemed to prove a steady road toward working-class power.

In 1914, many of those organizations supported their own states in war. The same apparatus that had educated and organized millions also tied workers to national governments and negotiated their sacrifice.

Revolutionaries drew a necessary conclusion: economic organization alone does not produce communist politics. A union can defend a trade at another worker’s expense. It can become part of the administration of capitalism. A parliamentary party can convert organization into an election machine.

After the Russian Revolution, the Communist International answered by giving the party a commanding political role. Its 1920 theses ranked party, councils, and unions within one revolutionary movement.

That answer met a second danger. If the party alone is said to embody class consciousness, councils can become transmission belts for decisions made elsewhere. The name “workers’ council” survives while workers cease deciding.

Council communists pressed the opposite truth: no organization can emancipate the class in its place.

The mature relation keeps both lessons.

Function decides the boundary

Form Membership basis Distinctive capacity Failure when isolated
communist party agreement with a communist programme theory, memory, strategy, education, international continuity becomes a circle speaking to itself
union or broad economic organization shared material position or struggle mass action where exploitation is organized treats bargaining within capitalism as the whole horizon
workers’ council direct delegation from workplaces and other class bodies coordinates struggle and can become an organ of class rule becomes a name without power if another organization commands it
commune common life in a territory joins production, care, provision, and public decision can turn inward if wider systems and class struggle disappear from view

These capacities overlap. Party members organize strikes. Unions educate politically. Councils develop strategy. Communes coordinate production.

Overlap does not erase the distinction.

An organization should be judged by the function it performs and the authority through which it performs it.

Four relations

Cooperation

Communists participate fully in broad class organizations. They do ordinary work, connect struggles, preserve memory, make political arguments, and help prepare action.

The class organization gives the party access to living experience. The party helps the struggle see beyond the immediate settlement.

Independence

The party keeps its programme and freedom to criticize. The class organization keeps its own mandate, membership, and right to reject the party’s proposal.

Without the first independence, the programme dissolves into immediate opinion. Without the second, workers become an audience for a political apparatus.

Leadership

Leadership exists when a party’s account proves clearer, its preparation matters, its members remain reliable under pressure, and its proposal leaves workers more capable of acting.

It must be won again.

Past correctness is not a license over the next struggle.

Substitution

Substitution begins when the party performs the class’s political activity in the class’s place.

Its signs are practical:

  • council decisions require party approval;
  • party discipline silently overrides a public mandate;
  • membership becomes a route to work or office;
  • disagreement is treated as disloyalty to the class;
  • armed and administrative bodies answer to a party center; and
  • workers are mobilized to carry decisions they did not make.

Apparent unity then destroys feedback. The party receives reports shaped to confirm its own line. The class organ loses initiative. Both become less intelligent.

The result test

Imagine a warehouse fight over an algorithmic quota.

The union can document injuries, compare departments, protect organizers, and stop work. Communists can connect surveillance and speedup to subcontracting, unemployment, logistics, and workers’ control.

If the union wins the removal of the quota, the victory is real and incomplete. The party helps preserve the organization, explain the wider relation, and prepare the next development.

If the party orders a walkout that workers have not discussed, chiefly to display militancy, a correct final aim does not rescue the act.

Ask what remains:

WORKED TEMPLATE

Are workers more organized, more knowledgeable, and more able to decide?

If yes, political leadership has enlarged class power.

If workers are waiting for the next instruction, substitution has weakened it.

Councils sharpen the rule

A council can become an organ of working-class government. Its delegates may also be party members.

In the council, they answer to the council’s mandate.

The party may believe the council is making a disastrous decision. It should argue, publish, organize, and help experience reveal the consequence. It cannot solve the disagreement through a hidden command that makes the council ceremonial.

The party carries a concentrated political horizon. The council carries the direct authority of class self-government.

One without the other can lose direction. Fused together, both lose the power to correct each other.
