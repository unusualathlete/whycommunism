When Does Force Set People Free?

At sunrise, workers block the gate of a factory that has not paid them in two months. The owner calls the police. Shields press forward. A baton breaks somebody’s wrist. By lunchtime, a bottle has flown back through the air.

The evening news says the protest turned violent when the bottle was thrown.

Everything before that sentence disappears. The missing wages. The threat of hunger. The owner’s legal power to lock people out of the place their own hands keep alive. The line of armed officers sent to restore that power. All of it becomes background. Violence enters the story only when obedience ends.

That trick is older than television. Class society teaches us to notice the blow that disturbs order and overlook the force that keeps order standing.

So the question cannot be, “Is violence bad?” Nearly everyone says yes until they meet the violence that protects the world they are used to.

The harder question is this:

When does force break domination, and when does it build domination again with a different flag?

The quiet force inside an ordinary day

Coercion means making somebody yield through force, threat, punishment, or conditions they cannot reasonably escape.

It does not always look like a fist.

A landlord does not personally drag a family into the street. A court order arrives first. Police wait behind it. A worker may be “free” to refuse an unsafe shift, but rent and hunger are already in the room. A border can be a line on a map until a person tries to cross it.

These harms are not identical. Losing a job is different from being imprisoned. Being imprisoned is different from being killed. Clear thinking needs those differences.

But clear thinking also needs the connection. Property, wages, courts, police, prisons, borders, and armies are not separate planets. Together they make a social order in which some people command the conditions of life and others must ask for access.

A gun is easy to see. A property title enforced every day is quieter.

When a revolution challenges ownership itself, the quiet part often becomes loud.

There are no two clean buttons

Politics is often presented as a machine with two buttons.

One says peaceful change. The other says violence.

A movement proves its goodness by pressing the first. It proves its seriousness by pressing the second.

Real history has no such machine.

A ruling class may tolerate speeches, elections, and reforms while its control remains safe. It may close those doors when people reach for the house itself. A movement may seek a peaceful transfer of power and meet a coup. People under occupation may be forbidden even to gather in public. Somewhere else, a small group may reach for weapons because years of organizing feel slow and glory feels close.

All of these situations contain force. They do not contain the same politics.

Materialism asks what is actually happening. Who has power? Who is moving? Who chose the danger? Who will pay for failure? Does the action enlarge the people’s capacity to govern, or does it reduce them to an audience watching somebody else perform history?

The answer cannot be hidden inside the word violent.

A class cannot be rescued from the outside

Imagine that ten brave people overthrow a mayor. For one night, they possess the building, the records, and the keys.

In the morning, the city still needs water pressure. Medicine must reach clinics. Buses must move. Children must eat. Sewage cannot wait for the manifesto.

Political power is not the chair behind the mayor’s desk. It is the organized ability to keep common life moving while changing the relations inside it.

This is why Marx’s claim that the working class must emancipate itself matters so much. A party, army, or committee can help a class organize its power. It can preserve knowledge, connect struggles, and act decisively in a crisis. It cannot secretly perform self-emancipation on everybody else’s behalf.

When an organization begins treating its own action as a replacement for the class, substitutionism has begun. The organization stops serving a movement and starts impersonating it.

The danger is not only that the group may lose. It may win the first battle and still teach everyone else to obey.

The romance of the spark

There is a seductive picture of revolution in which one spectacular act wakes the masses. A small group strikes. The illusion of stability shatters. Everyone sees that resistance is possible.

Sometimes courage does travel. One person’s refusal can expose fear that millions already share.

But a spark can also burn the people it was supposed to awaken.

Adventurism begins when action races ahead of collective capacity. A dramatic attack may let a few people feel history moving through their hands while the wider class inherits the arrests, fear, isolation, and public consequences. The state receives an enemy shaped perfectly for its cameras. Patient work begins to look cowardly beside spectacle.

Risk alone does not make an action adventurist. A strike can risk livelihoods and still be understood, chosen, and led by the workers taking that risk. A revolt can begin before any committee decides that the calendar is ready.

The difference is not bravery.

It is ownership of the struggle.

Do the people placed in danger have a real hand in the decision? Does the act draw new human powers into the world, or does it ask everyone to admire powers concentrated somewhere else?

The enemy is real

None of this makes counter-revolution imaginary.

Owners do not lose factories, land, banks, military command, and political privilege with perfect manners. They retain money, contacts, technical knowledge, international allies, and habits of being obeyed. They can fund armed restoration, withhold supplies, move capital, sabotage administration, spread panic, or invite foreign intervention.

A revolution unable to defend itself may become a beautiful week in somebody else’s history book.

But defense carries its own poison.

Suppose grain fails to arrive in a city. It might be weather. It might be broken rail. It might be hoarding, corruption, bureaucratic stupidity, or deliberate sabotage. A frightened government can stop asking which. Every shortage becomes conspiracy. Every complaint becomes sabotage. Every person who brings bad news begins to look like the author of it.

Then information dies.

Officials report success because failure is dangerous to mention. Innocent people confess because the institution needs an explanation. The revolution becomes less able to see reality while claiming harsher measures are needed because reality has become so dangerous.

Defense is necessary. So is the ability to tell defense from a machine feeding on fear.

The Commune carries two lessons in the same wound

The Paris Commune of 1871 lasted a little more than two months. Workers and residents of Paris broke with the old government and began reorganizing public power. Officials could be recalled. Public servants were paid ordinary wages. The standing army was replaced by an armed people. Functions that had looked like the private mysteries of a ruling caste were pulled into daylight.

The old order answered with massacre.

The Commune’s defeat taught Marx and Engels that a ruling class does not become peaceful when its power is threatened. The working class could not simply inherit the old state machine and expect it to serve a new purpose.

Yet the Commune also showed something about form. The answer to an armed ruling class was not a stronger machine floating above the people. It was public power being dragged back into society.

These lessons belong together:

A revolution must be able to survive. The people must remain the power that survives.

Break the first sentence away, and self-government can be crushed. Break the second away, and defense can become a new government over the class it claims to defend.

Methods leave fingerprints on the future

People sometimes hear “the means shape the ends” as a sermon about keeping one’s hands clean.

The material point is stronger.

Every method trains bodies, habits, and institutions.

Open deliberation trains people to speak, listen, decide, and live with disagreement. Military command trains speed and unity under danger. Secrecy protects sensitive work, but it also teaches small groups to live inside unequal knowledge. Public review exposes error. Emergency power rewards those who can classify information and give orders.

None of these methods can be judged outside its conditions. A movement under dictatorship cannot organize exactly like a book club. A town under attack may need instant command where a long meeting would be fatal.

Necessity is real.

It should remain a burden to overcome, not a virtue to preserve.

If emergency command produces a permanent caste, then yesterday’s necessity has become today’s interest. If secrecy continues after the danger passes, perhaps it is now hiding the organization from its own members. If weapons give a body authority no one can recall, that authority has become a social relation of its own.

The future begins growing inside the way people fight for it.

The test is what people become able to do

Revolution may require armed struggle, coercive defense, military preparation, illegal work, and the defeat of counter-revolution. It also has to reject conspiratorial shortcuts, isolated heroics, bureaucratic command, factional punishment, and party rule over the class.

These are not two polite opinions waiting for a compromise. They are one hard problem. A revolution must defeat the powers trying to destroy it without turning the people into material for another power.

Tactics must grow from conditions and strategy. The party must remain distinct from the class. Working people must govern themselves. The Commonalty begins as society takes public power back into its own hands, and it grows as separate coercion loses its reason to exist.

No formula can decide every case. There is still a test.

After an act of force, are people more capable of directing common life? Can they correct those who command? Is emergency power shrinking as the emergency shrinks? Or has a new body learned to need the danger that gives it power?

No innocence, no blank check

Rejecting the worship of violence does not mean promising that every ruling class can be removed peacefully.

Recognizing that force may become necessary does not bless every person who calls their force revolutionary.

Explaining civil war does not erase decisions made inside it.

Condemning repression does not make counter-revolution harmless.

Class society offers no place outside history where our hands remain perfectly clean. It still demands judgment.

We have to ask what relation an act destroys, what relation it builds, whose powers it awakens, whose voice it silences, who bears its danger, and what remains standing when the smoke clears.

The heart of the problem

Emancipation may have to defend itself.

That defense is worthy of its name only while it protects the growth of human beings from objects of power into authors of their common life.

The moment a movement can survive only by making the people small, silent, and governable, it has begun defending something else.

Source notes

The historical argument

  • Karl Marx, The Civil War in France, 1871, on the Commune, the destruction of the old state machine, the armed people, recallable office, and the return of public functions to society.
  • Karl Marx, first draft of The Civil War in France, 1871, where the Commune appears most explicitly as the reabsorption of state power by society.
  • Friedrich Engels, 1891 introduction to The Civil War in France, on the standing army, elected and recallable officials, ordinary workers’ wages, and the historical meaning of the dictatorship of the proletariat.
  • Karl Marx and Friedrich Engels, Address of the Central Committee to the Communist League, 1850, on independent working-class organization, arms, political initiative, and the danger of being subordinated to another class.
  • Karl Marx, The Class Struggles in France, 1850, on revolution and counter-revolution as a developing relation among classes rather than a single heroic event.
  • Rosa Luxemburg, The Mass Strike, the Political Party and the Trade Unions, 1906, on the movement between economic and political struggle and the limits of manufacturing mass action by command.
  • Vladimir Lenin, The State and Revolution, 1917, on smashing the old state machine, arming the people, and the withering away of a power separated from society.

Arguments carried forward and contested

  • Communist International, The Platform of the Communist International, 1919, a contemporary statement of civil war, soviet power, mass action, and the world character of revolutionary conflict.
  • Communist International, Theses on the Fundamental Tasks of the Communist International, 1920, on preparation, mass organization, the conquest of power, and the relation between communist leadership and working-class action.
  • Anton Pannekoek, “World Revolution and Communist Tactics”, 1920, on the different conditions of Western European revolution and the development of the proletariat’s own capacity.
  • Herman Gorter, Open Letter to Comrade Lenin, written in 1920 and published in English in 1921, a direct dispute over mass action, leadership, tactics, and whether Russian experience could be generalized.
  • Amadeo Bordiga, “Party and Class Action”, 1921, on party action, class movement, revolutionary conditions, and the impossibility of replacing a living relation with an organizational formula.
