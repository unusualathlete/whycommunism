One World, Unequal Starting Lines

In one city, a third car is sold to a household whose first two spend most of the day parked.

In another, a clinic loses medicine because the refrigerator has no reliable electricity.

Both require metal, energy, labour, roads, and land. Counting them as equal units of “consumption” erases the difference that ecological politics exists to decide.

The world has common limits and unequal starting lines. Colonial conquest, slavery, land seizure, unequal trade, debt, patents, military power, and control over investment concentrated productive capacity in some regions while shaping others around extraction and export. Rich societies now use a disproportionate share of materials and ecological space. Poorer societies often face greater exposure to climate damage while still lacking infrastructures of a secure life.

A single command to “produce less” would freeze that inequality in green language.

A single promise that every country can follow the old industrial path would deepen the crisis.

The real movement is double:

Excess throughput must contract where it is concentrated. Necessary productive power must expand where it has been denied.

That is the argument for degrowth, development, and world equity as one problem.

Degrowth is not recession

The word degrowth is often heard through the experience of capitalist crisis.

In a recession, investment falls, firms close, workers lose income, homes are repossessed, and public budgets contract. Useful production may disappear while luxury wealth remains protected. Need rises precisely when the capacity to meet it is thrown idle.

Degrowth means something else: a deliberate reduction of excess energy and material throughput, organized to improve well-being, restore ecological conditions, and distribute work and wealth more equally.

Its object is not gross domestic product as a number. GDP may fall as wasteful activity disappears, or it may move unpredictably as public services and ecological repair expand. The object is the physical scale and composition of production.

This distinction matters:

Capitalist recession Planned ecological degrowth
Production contracts through crisis and failed profitability Selected throughput contracts through public decision
Unemployment and insecurity discipline workers Income, services, retraining, and shorter hours protect people
Owners defend profitable and luxury uses Basic needs and ecological repair receive priority
Idle capacity coexists with unmet need Capacity moves from waste toward necessary work
Burdens fall downward Excess use and accumulated power surrender first

No society can choose degrowth while leaving everyone dependent on endless growth for jobs, debt service, pensions, tax revenue, and basic security. The social relations must change with the material scale.

“Less” needs an object

An ecological programme becomes cruel or meaningless when it speaks only of less.

Less what?

Less fossil combustion. Less planned obsolescence. Less military destruction. Less private aviation, oversized vehicles, speculative vacancy, disposable fashion, competitive advertising, needless packaging, and duplicate infrastructure built to defend market share.

But more insulation may be needed before less heating energy becomes possible. More rail, buses, grids, storage, and accessible housing may be needed before fewer private cars become freedom rather than confinement. More restoration labour may be needed before a damaged ecosystem can renew itself with less intervention.

Material contraction often requires a temporary material build-out. The transition has a cost in steel, minerals, machinery, energy, and work. A plan that celebrates the final efficiency while ignoring the construction phase lies about its own metabolism.

The right question is not whether production rises or falls in the abstract. It is which use-values expand, which throughputs contract, which capacities are transitional, and which conditions must be renewed.

Development is the power to live

Necessary development is the expansion of social and productive capacities required for people to live securely and govern their common life.

It includes:

  • durable and climate-safe housing;
  • sanitation and clean water;
  • reliable electricity and cooling;
  • food security and resilient agriculture;
  • clinics, medicine, care, and public health;
  • education, research, and technical training;
  • accessible public transport and communication;
  • disaster preparation and recovery;
  • independent capacity to maintain tools and infrastructure; and
  • time released from exhausting and avoidable labour.

These are not concessions granted after ecological targets are met. They are part of ecological feasibility. A household cannot conserve water that never arrives safely. A region cannot choose a clean industrial path while patents, debt, foreign ownership, and unequal exchange keep it dependent upon imported machinery and export revenue.

Development also means control. A mine owned abroad, an export plantation that displaces food, or a power project whose surrounding community remains unserved may raise GDP without developing the people whose land and labour make it possible.

The measure is not whether capital has accumulated inside a national border. It is whether human powers, public systems, ecological resilience, and self-determination have grown.

The world market hides the footprint

Territorial statistics count emissions and extraction where production occurs. Consumption accounts follow some of the burden to the final user. Neither measure is politically innocent, but the second exposes a relation the first can hide.

A wealthy economy can close a factory, import the same commodity, and report a cleaner domestic record. The atmosphere receives no benefit from the change of address. Workers and ecosystems in the producing region receive the damage.

Research on unequal ecological exchange traces net flows of embodied materials, energy, land, and labour through international trade. The methods and interpretation are contested. A tonne of exported material does not by itself prove exploitation, and not every country fits neatly into a North-South binary. Oil exporters, settler colonies, rapidly industrializing states, tax havens, internal colonies, and class divisions complicate the map.

Yet several findings are difficult to escape:

  1. high-income systems of provision are far more materially intensive than low-income ones;
  2. much of that material use depends on extraction and labour beyond the consuming territory;
  3. environmental damage and waste are displaced along the same chains; and
  4. market prices do not measure the ecological renewal or human freedom lost at the point of extraction.

The category Global North is therefore a relation, not simply a compass direction. It names regions and classes that command a disproportionate share of world resources through the history and present organization of accumulation. Global South names a diverse set of societies subordinated within that order, not a single political subject with identical interests.

Class runs through both. A billionaire in a poor country and a precarious worker in a rich one do not hold the same power. National responsibility must never become a story of collective guilt that hides owners, states, militaries, and high consumers behind an average citizen.

The atmospheric commons was used unequally

The atmosphere has a limited capacity to receive greenhouse gases without escalating danger. Industrial powers consumed much of that capacity while building wealth, infrastructure, and military power.

This creates an ecological debt. The term does not imply that every injury can be converted into money. It identifies an obligation arising from unequal use of common ecological space and unequal exposure to harm.

The United Nations climate convention expresses a restrained legal version through common but differentiated responsibilities and respective capabilities. All states have duties, but those with greater historical contribution and present capacity should lead.

For a materialist politics, responsibility has at least four dimensions:

  • historical contribution: who used ecological space during accumulation;
  • present capacity: who possesses technology, wealth, industry, and institutions;
  • benefit: who gained durable advantages from the damaging activity;
  • vulnerability: who now faces danger with the least protection.

No single formula settles the weights. Starting dates, population, consumption-based accounting, land use, and colonial history all matter. The dispute should remain visible rather than concealed inside one moral number.

The direction is clear. Rich regions must cut faster, fund adaptation and loss-and-damage responses, share knowledge and technology, cancel obstructive debt, accept displaced people, and release material space for necessary development elsewhere.

Aid governed by the donor can reproduce dependence. Repair should return power.

A common limit is not equal austerity

Planetary limits are physical, but their allocation is political.

A sufficiency floor guarantees the material conditions of a dignified and developing life. A ceiling restrains uses whose expansion consumes the conditions everybody needs. The floor and ceiling cannot be identical across bodies and places.

A hospital requires more energy than a casino should. A disabled person may need more equipment and transport than an able-bodied neighbor. A cold region needs different housing from a tropical one. A city rebuilding after a typhoon may temporarily use more concrete and steel than a wealthy district replacing kitchens for fashion.

Equity does not mean equal quantities. It means that differences answer to concrete need rather than purchasing power.

One way to organize the distinction is by direction:

What should contract rapidly in rich, high-throughput regions

  • fossil fuel use and infrastructure;
  • luxury emissions and private high-energy mobility;
  • military production and destructive logistics;
  • planned obsolescence and disposable goods;
  • speculative construction, vacancy, and oversized private space;
  • meat and land-use systems where they exceed health and ecological need;
  • advertising and sales systems devoted to accelerating turnover; and
  • long supply chains maintained only by cheap labour and externalized damage.

What should expand almost everywhere

  • renewable and low-emission energy fitted to ecological conditions;
  • grids, storage, maintenance, and public technical capacity;
  • public transit, walking, cycling, and accessible mobility;
  • efficient, durable, safe housing;
  • care, health, education, research, and culture;
  • ecosystem restoration and non-toxic material recovery;
  • disaster protection and mutual aid;
  • repair, reuse, and product longevity; and
  • free time.

What should expand most in formerly colonized and underdeveloped regions

  • universal electricity, water, sanitation, housing, and cooling;
  • food sovereignty and resilient agricultural capacity;
  • medicine, public health, and care infrastructure;
  • regional industry able to meet social needs;
  • public transport before car dependence hardens;
  • scientific, engineering, and maintenance capacity;
  • technology transfer without permanent patent dependence; and
  • institutions capable of deciding development locally while coordinating globally.

These categories will change by place and through time. Their purpose is to replace the blunt opposition between growth and no growth with a plan of qualitative transformation.

Public abundance can use less

Capitalist abundance is measured by the range of commodities a person can buy. Common abundance asks what powers people can actually exercise.

A library gives access to more books with fewer private purchases. A frequent train gives more mobility with fewer vehicles and less road space. A public pool gives more people the ability to swim than thousands of private pools. A universal health system can organize prevention and care without making every illness a market opportunity.

This is public abundance: rich access to shared capacities whose organization can reduce material duplication and free time.

Decommodification changes ecology because it changes the path through which needs are met. A person does not need to own every instrument they use. A durable good can be repaired through public infrastructure. A neighborhood can share workshops, laundries, kitchens, gardens, communication, and care without erasing private life.

Less private accumulation can become more lived possibility.

This is not a romantic claim that services are immaterial. Libraries need buildings, books, electricity, and workers. Digital systems need mines, servers, networks, cooling, and repair. Public abundance remains a material design problem. Its advantage is that scale and access can be chosen around use instead of repeated sales.

The debate inside degrowth

Degrowth contains real disagreements.

Some writers treat it as a strategy for high-income economies, others as a name for a global post-growth transition. Some emphasize local autonomy and commons. Others stress national and international planning. Some avoid the word because people hear deprivation. Others defend it because polite language has hidden the necessity of contraction.

Marxists have raised further questions. Can degrowth explain exploitation and class power, or does it reduce capitalism to a machine that produces too much? Does a focus on consumption hide production and ownership? Can small-scale localism coordinate global grids, medicine, transport, and climate? Does hostility to “development” become hostility to the aspirations of formerly colonized peoples?

These criticisms are decisive when degrowth becomes an ethics of consuming less for everybody.

They are answered only by rooting contraction in class, empire, and production:

  • workers do not pay for capital’s crisis;
  • useful productive forces are developed, not worshipped or destroyed;
  • the world scale of modern cooperation is preserved and consciously governed;
  • universal services expand;
  • underdeveloped regions receive material and political room to grow;
  • rich regions surrender excess and imperial command; and
  • the aim is free human development within ecological reproduction.

The strongest degrowth argument is therefore not “small is beautiful.” Some systems must be continental or planetary. It is that production has a scale, composition, and purpose that human beings can choose.

Development without another sacrifice zone

A rapid clean-energy build-out will require minerals, land, industry, and labour. Calling the final product green does not make its supply chain just.

A democratic world plan would ask:

  1. Can total demand be reduced through public systems and durable design?
  2. Can existing materials be recovered safely?
  3. Which extraction remains necessary after those reductions?
  4. Who lives on and from the proposed site?
  5. Can they refuse, alter, or govern the project?
  6. How will water, habitat, health, labour, and restoration be protected?
  7. Where will processing and technical knowledge reside?
  8. Who owns the result and receives the useful output?

Formerly colonized regions need not repeat every stage of imperial industrialization. But the alternative cannot be designed by rich countries that keep the high-consumption endpoint for themselves.

There is no ecology in preserved poverty.

There is no liberation in a national elite offering its people and land as a cheaper green frontier.

The common plan

World equity does not require one office assigning every object on Earth. It requires binding common limits, transfers of power and capacity, and coordination across the scales where ecological systems actually exist.

Communities can govern land, water, housing, and local production. Regions can coordinate watersheds, grids, transport, and settlement. A world republic can set shared ecological ceilings, organize large material flows, support migration, transfer technology, and prevent one region from exporting its costs into another.

The levels need reciprocal power. A global target must hear the community living beside a mine. A community cannot claim an unlimited right to destroy a river, atmosphere, or species shared beyond it.

The material account should include at least:

  • territorial and consumption-based emissions;
  • energy and material footprints;
  • stocks, durability, and rates of replenishment;
  • land, water, toxicity, and biodiversity effects;
  • labour and care requirements;
  • historical responsibility and present capacity;
  • access to essential services; and
  • uncertainty, trade-offs, and displaced harm.

No dashboard can decide the good life. It can make the consequences of a decision visible enough for people to decide without pretending the cost vanished.

More life is not the same as more throughput

The ecological transition is sometimes framed as a sacrifice made now so that normal life can resume later.

Normal life is part of the problem when it means insecure work, long commutes, private isolation, manufactured dissatisfaction, disposable goods, colonial supply chains, and time sold back to us as products.

Contraction can return something.

When unnecessary production ends and necessary labour is shared, productivity can become shorter working time. When transport is public, mobility can rise while vehicles fall. When housing is common and durable, security can rise while speculation falls. When knowledge and culture are freely available, human development can grow without matching growth in raw material.

Useful global production may rise during a long period of building homes, clinics, grids, transit, water systems, and ecological repair. Destructive throughput can fall at the same time if rich regions contract faster, waste ends, goods last, and production shifts toward less material forms of wealth.

That outcome is not guaranteed by moral preference. It requires common control over production.

One world does not mean one belt tightened around every waist.

It means looking first at who already has three cars, who still lacks a refrigerator for medicine, what each object makes possible, and which social order has the power to choose between them.
