The Promise Moves. The Smoke Remains.

An airline burns fuel today and sells a promise about a forest tomorrow.

A car uses less energy per kilometer, then grows heavier and travels farther.

A government celebrates lower emissions inside its borders while importing steel, electronics, food, and clothing made through extraction somewhere else.

A company announces that a machine not yet built at scale will remove the carbon its present investments keep adding.

In every case, something real may be happening. A forest can store carbon. Efficiency can save energy. Cleaner production can lower emissions. Carbon removal can be necessary for residual pollution that cannot be eliminated.

The deception begins when the useful part is enlarged into a complete answer. The promise moves across time, territory, accounting category, or technology. The smoke remains.

Capitalist ecological solutions fail in a distinctive way. They try to solve a crisis of material reproduction while preserving production for accumulation as the unquestioned purpose. Tools are selected, delayed, limited, or expanded according to profitability and political power. The thing that must be transformed is placed in charge of the transformation.

The test is physical

Ecological success is not a moral mood. It is a set of material outcomes.

Greenhouse-gas emissions must fall rapidly and stay down. Fossil infrastructure must be retired rather than cosmetically balanced. Forests, soils, oceans, and wetlands must retain their living functions. Material extraction and toxic waste must fit within renewal, safety, and justice. People must receive food, water, shelter, healthcare, energy, mobility, and protection through the transition.

Any proposed solution should therefore face five tests:

Proposal What it can genuinely do What capital asks it to do Structural failure
Efficiency Reduce energy or material needed for a given use Lower cost and open room for further sales Savings are partly or wholly taken back through expansion
Carbon pricing Attach a cost to emissions and influence choices Preserve decentralized investment and profitable growth A price does not guarantee a physical ceiling or equitable allocation
Offsets Fund protection, restoration, or removal Let pollution continue elsewhere under a net claim Additionality, reversal, leakage, delay, double counting, and land power
New technology Replace harmful processes and remove residual carbon Rescue present accumulation without early contraction Future scale, energy, materials, land, time, and side effects are assumed
Green consumption Shift demand and normalize better practices Turn ecological responsibility into a product segment Choice remains bounded by income, infrastructure, design, and corporate supply

None of these tools is useless by definition. None can decide the purpose and scale of production.

That is the work capital cannot allow them to do.

Growth is not one desire among others

The strongest defense of green capitalism says that economic output can continue growing while emissions and resource use fall. This possibility is usually called decoupling.

Relative decoupling occurs when environmental pressure grows more slowly than measured economic output. Absolute decoupling occurs when the pressure falls while output rises. Absolute declines in particular pollutants have occurred, especially where law, public investment, and technological substitution forced them.

The ecological question is harder. Can a world economy driven by accumulation achieve absolute reductions across emissions, energy, materials, land, water, toxicity, and biodiversity, at the speed required, while also allowing necessary development?

Gross domestic product does not answer. It adds monetary activity without asking whether a society built a hospital, cleaned an oil spill, sold luxury property, treated an illness caused by pollution, or repaired a product designed to break. GDP can rise when life improves and when damage creates another market.

Capital’s need for growth is not merely cultural greed. Individual firms must invest, expand productivity, defend market share, service debt, and survive competition. States depend on employment, tax revenue, credit, investment, and geopolitical power. A government may promise stability only while accumulation continues. When growth stops involuntarily, capitalism produces recession, unemployment, debt crisis, and fiscal pressure.

This is why “no growth” cannot simply be selected from a policy menu while all underlying relations stay unchanged.

Efficiency can slow material demand. Services can carry less material per unit of price than heavy industry. Renewable energy can replace fossil energy. But production also moves across borders, new markets open, infrastructures multiply, and lower cost stimulates new use. The International Resource Panel continues to find rising global extraction and highly unequal material footprints.

The question is not whether any decoupling exists. It is whether it is broad, absolute, durable, global rather than territorial, fast enough, and just.

Capitalism has not shown a mechanism that guarantees all six.

The rebound was visible in the coal age

William Stanley Jevons observed in the nineteenth century that more efficient steam engines did not necessarily conserve coal. Greater efficiency lowered the cost and expanded the range of profitable uses. Total coal consumption could rise.

A rebound effect occurs when part of an efficiency saving is taken back through increased use. Jevons paradox names the strong case in which the expansion exceeds the saving.

This is not an argument against efficiency. Wasting energy and material would be absurd. It is an argument about who controls the saving.

Suppose an insulated home uses less electricity for cooling. The household may gain comfort and spend less. That is valuable. Suppose a freight company saves fuel per delivery and responds by expanding delivery volume, distance, and speed. The same technical gain enters a different social logic.

Under accumulation, a saving appears as lower cost, competitive advantage, disposable income, or unused capacity seeking another outlet. Under production for use, society can decide that the saved energy remains unused, shortens labour, serves an unmet need, or protects a reserve.

Efficiency answers, “How much is needed for this act?” It cannot answer, “How many acts should occur, for what purpose, and within which total limit?”

Price is an instrument, not a climate

Markets coordinate enormous amounts of information through prices. A rising price can signal scarcity, reduce demand, attract supply, or reward substitution. The argument for carbon pricing begins from a real power.

But climate stability is not a preference revealed at checkout.

A price does not by itself set a hard physical quantity. Its effect depends on incomes, alternatives, industrial structure, bargaining power, enforcement, expectations, and whether firms pass the cost onward. A wealthy buyer can continue an activity a poor household must abandon. A clinic and a casino do not enter the market with equal money or equal social necessity.

Future generations place no bid. A disappearing species has no account. A tipping process does not negotiate a discount rate.

Governments can tighten a cap, ban a process, subsidize alternatives, build public systems, and compensate households. At that point, political authority is already setting purposes that price alone could not discover.

The relevant contrast is not “prices or no information.” A plan may use administered prices, scarcity indicators, opportunity costs, and markets in limited domains. The contrast is between price as one signal and price as the sovereign allocator.

An ecological minimum must remain binding even when violating it is profitable.

Offsets exchange unlike things

An offset allows an emission in one place to be balanced against an asserted reduction, avoidance, or removal elsewhere.

The accounting appears simple because both sides are expressed in tonnes of carbon dioxide equivalent. The material realities are not identical.

Fossil carbon moved from geological storage into the active carbon cycle can persist for very long periods. Carbon stored in a forest can be released by fire, drought, logging, disease, or political change. Avoiding a proposed emission is different from removing carbon already added. Protecting one forest may shift extraction to another.

Offset integrity therefore depends on difficult questions:

  • Additionality: Would the project have happened without the credit?
  • Permanence: How long will the carbon remain stored?
  • Leakage: Has the damaging activity moved elsewhere?
  • Measurement: What baseline and uncertainty define the claimed saving?
  • Double counting: Have two actors claimed the same result?
  • Timing: Does a future removal balance a present emission during the dangerous interval?
  • Land and power: Who lived from the territory, and who gained authority over it?

High-integrity restoration and removal projects can be worth funding. The Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change is clear that carbon dioxide removal will have complementary roles, especially for residual emissions. It is equally clear that removal cannot substitute for deep emissions reductions.

The capitalist attraction of an offset lies precisely in substitution. The buyer keeps the profitable activity. The difficult transformation occurs somewhere else, often on land held by people with less power.

Neil Smith described the growth of “nature as accumulation strategy,” in which conservation, biodiversity, and ecological functions become new commodities and financial assets. The forest is protected, perhaps. It also becomes a claim through which investors seek returns and emitters purchase legitimacy.

Nature banking does not automatically protect nature from capital. It can give capital a new title over protection.

The machine from tomorrow

Ecological transformation requires technology. Renewable power, storage, public transport, efficient buildings, low-emission industry, ecosystem restoration, and carefully governed carbon removal all belong in a serious response.

Technological solutionism begins when a possible technique is asked to remove the need for social transformation.

Carbon dioxide removal shows the difference.

The IPCC assesses several forms of removal, including biological methods such as reforestation and soil carbon, and engineered methods such as direct air capture with storage and bioenergy with carbon capture and storage. These methods have different maturity, durability, energy needs, land demands, water demands, costs, co-benefits, and risks.

Some removal will likely be necessary to counterbalance genuinely hard-to-eliminate residual emissions. But the amount needed depends strongly on how fast gross emissions fall and how much energy and material society demands. Greater delay creates greater dependence on future removal.

That dependence is a political wager:

  1. pollution continues because later removal is expected;
  2. continued pollution expands the eventual removal task;
  3. the larger task requires more land, energy, infrastructure, and public money;
  4. those requirements collide with food, biodiversity, adaptation, and development;
  5. if the technology underperforms, the warming has already occurred.

“Emit now, clean later” privatizes the benefit of delay and socializes the risk of failure.

This does not justify rejecting research. It justifies treating a pilot as a pilot, a model as a model, and residual emissions as residual rather than a shelter for avoidable ones.

The green consumer is offered a tiny steering wheel

Personal action matters. Diet, travel, heating, repair, voting, organizing, and consumption all have material effects. People also learn through practice. A culture of care and restraint cannot be postponed until after a change in ownership.

Capitalist lifestylism takes this truth and removes the system around it.

A person is told to drive less where no safe public transport exists. A tenant is blamed for an inefficient building they cannot alter. A household is asked to avoid plastic while producers determine packaging. A low-paid worker is offered an expensive ethical product as the form of their ecological citizenship.

The consumer chooses among commodities already designed, produced, transported, and priced by institutions outside their control.

Collective power changes the menu itself. Public transport makes low-car life ordinary. Durable-product standards make repair possible. Social housing allows insulation at neighborhood scale. Public food systems can alter land use and nutrition without making every meal a private moral test.

The goal is not to make individuals innocent. Nobody lives outside the inherited system. The goal is to make ecological action a common power rather than a premium purchase.

The state can discipline capital and still defend it

Capitalism is not a pure market. States build infrastructure, fund research, regulate industry, prohibit substances, protect property, socialize losses, organize war, and rescue firms during crisis.

Strong public action can win time and save lives. Standards, bans, public ownership, investment, rationing, and planning should be judged by their real effects, not dismissed because the revolution has not yet happened.

But the capitalist state faces a contradiction. It must sometimes discipline particular capitals to preserve the conditions of accumulation as a whole. It also depends upon and defends the property order that gives capital its power.

Ecological emergency can therefore produce a directed capitalism that meets selected targets while preserving class rule, imperial privilege, militarized borders, and unequal sacrifice. A green state can build trains and also seize Indigenous land. It can ration household energy and exempt strategic industry. It can protect a national supply chain by deepening a sacrifice zone abroad.

This is why regulation is a terrain of struggle, not the final form of ecological reason.

The political test is whether each reform reduces harm, transfers power, builds common capacity, and opens the next transformation. A reform that creates a stronger fossil constituency or a new private tollbooth may close the road it claims to begin.

What a non-empty answer requires

Capitalist answers become empty when they promise a material result without surrendering the relations that prevent the result from being guaranteed.

A non-empty answer needs:

  • binding physical ceilings and replenishment rules;
  • direct priorities among uses rather than allocation by wealth;
  • public control of investment and productive capacity;
  • international responsibility for embodied materials and displaced damage;
  • planned contraction of wasteful and luxury throughput;
  • expansion of universal services and necessary development;
  • technology governed as a common instrument;
  • democratic authority able to stop a profitable harm; and
  • institutions that revise their plan when nature, workers, or communities report failure.

Efficiency then remains a saving. Removal serves residual emissions rather than delay. Restoration returns power to the people who care for land. Personal practice joins public capacity. Innovation answers to a plan whose purpose it did not choose.

The smoke does not disappear because the promise acquired a market price.

It disappears when society can follow the whole material chain, decide what must stop, build what must replace it, and refuse to move the cost into another person’s lungs or another century.
