No Party Can Be the Proletariat

Three statements have to remain true at once.

  1. The working class needs a communist party.
  2. The party cannot make the revolution in the working class’s place.
  3. A revolution isolated within one state cannot complete communism.

Drop the first statement and every defeat threatens to erase memory, programme, preparation, and international connection. Drop the second and political leadership becomes rule by a minority. Drop the third and an embattled state begins calling scarcity, military concentration, and dependence on the world market a finished new society.

The relation among party, class, and international is difficult because each answers a real weakness carried by the others.

The party concentrates.

The class emancipates itself.

The international makes that emancipation materially world-historical.

What has to be concentrated

Capital already organizes production across workplaces, industries, countries, and generations. Workers experience this total relation in fragments. One group meets a speedup. Another meets a rent increase. Another sees a border regime, a war contract, a collapsing river, or a supply chain moving to break a strike.

A communist party connects these experiences to the structure producing them.

It concentrates:

  • a programme joining immediate struggle to class abolition;
  • memory that survives repression and retreat;
  • theory tested against changing conditions;
  • international relationships;
  • political education;
  • strategic preparation;
  • trained organizers; and
  • the ability to act coherently when a crisis compresses years into days.

This concentration is necessary because these capacities are uneven. It is temporary because communist development makes them common.

The party fails if it guards its capacities as proof that everyone else should obey. It succeeds when more working people can understand, organize, teach, coordinate, and govern without needing a separate political minority to do so for them.

The class has organs the party does not own

The party is a political organization united by a communist programme. The class creates many organizations around different functions:

Organ Immediate function Political danger if absorbed by the party
union or strike committee organize conflict at work and across an industry members become an audience for party decisions
workers’ council govern production and exercise class power public mandate is replaced by internal party discipline
commune govern common life in a territory residents lose authority over their own institutions
militia or defense body defend the revolution armed force answers to a political center rather than the class
planning body coordinate social production technical and political information becomes a party monopoly
party concentrate programme, strategy, memory, and education its distinct function expands until it claims every other function

Party members can and should work inside all these bodies. They argue, investigate, organize, persuade, and often lead. They answer to the body’s mandate when exercising its public power.

Membership cannot create a second chain of command.

If a council elects a party member to coordinate food distribution, that person answers to the council. A party committee cannot secretly replace the council’s decision. If the party disagrees, it must criticize, persuade, organize, and return to the class struggle openly.

This distinction protects both sides. The class keeps its self-government. The party keeps the independence required to criticize a state institution, including one staffed by its own members.

Fusion destroys that independence. Every administrative compromise becomes party doctrine. Every criticism of the office becomes an attack on the programme. The party begins defending the apparatus that supplies its positions.

The weapon closes around the hand.

Leadership is a relation, not a title

A party leads when its analysis explains events, its preparation helps people act, its courage matters at the decisive moment, and its proposals survive practice.

Legal monopoly replaces this social relation with an office.

An official party no longer has to win confidence each time. It can appoint, censor, distribute, and punish. Failure can be hidden because the channels reporting failure belong to the same apparatus. Agreement becomes difficult to distinguish from dependence.

This does not mean the party should flatter every immediate opinion. A workplace may defend its own price, wage, or resource claim against the interests of the wider class. A region may resist necessary redistribution. Workers formed by nationalism or racism may support an oppressive demand. The party’s programme exists partly to oppose such limits.

It must oppose them politically.

Leadership means enlarging the class’s capacity to understand and act, even when the immediate argument is sharp. A party that wins every vote while workers become less capable has not led them toward communism.

A revolution cannot remain inside the border

The world market links production before workers consciously do.

Food, medicine, energy, machinery, communication, and finance cross borders. So do armies, sanctions, debt, patents, propaganda, and capital flight. A revolution in one territory inherits these dependencies immediately.

Isolation does more than deprive it of goods. It changes its internal institutions.

Blockade strengthens offices that ration scarce imports. Military attack strengthens command. Broken supply chains reward emergency improvisation and secrecy. A hostile world market pressures public enterprises to earn foreign currency. Scarcity gives administrators leverage over workers and communities.

These measures may be necessary for survival. Necessity can harden into a social interest.

Internationalism must therefore be material:

  • coordinated industrial and transport action;
  • shared defense against coups and invasion;
  • common scientific and technical capacity;
  • movement of food, medicine, machinery, and skilled labor according to need;
  • support for revolutionary organization across borders;
  • abolition of imperial debts and unequal claims; and
  • institutions through which working people make common decisions at the scale of the system.

Solidarity as a feeling cannot keep a hospital supplied.

From an international to a world republic

A Communist International coordinates political struggle across states. A world republic names a further development: common self-government at the world scale.

The two should not be confused.

An international party does not become the government of the world. It carries programme, strategy, education, and coordination across the class movement. Communes, councils, and associated producers develop the institutions through which the world population governs common systems.

Text alternative: International communist organization helps workers organize power across borders. Revolutions share defense, production, and knowledge. Common institutions then govern systems that already operate at world scale. As a world republic of associated commonalties develops, separate class and party functions lose their basis.

A world republic is not one enormous ministry. It joins binding universal purposes to local and regional autonomy. Climate, oceans, migration, pandemics, communications, and intercontinental production require world decisions. Housing layout, language, daily schedules, and many forms of care require knowledge and authority closer to life.

The scale of the consequence should guide the scale of the decision.

The party’s final task

The party begins from uneven consciousness and organization. Communist society abolishes that unevenness as a political separation.

During capitalist rule, the party concentrates programme and preparation. During rupture, it helps coordinate decisive action. During transition, it defends direction, educates, warns against restoration, and organizes criticism. As the Commonalty develops, theory, administration, planning, and conscious direction become ordinary social capacities.

The party then loses its separate function.

This is not a punishment for success. It is success.

A political organization that must rule forever has chosen a society that will require rulers forever. A communist party prepares the conditions under which no party, including itself, can stand over common life.

No party can be the proletariat.

The task is to help the proletariat cease being a proletariat at all.

Source notes

Primary texts and debates

  • Karl Marx, General Rules of the International Working Men’s Association, revised 1871, on working-class self-emancipation and political organization.
  • Karl Marx and Friedrich Engels, Manifesto of the Communist Party, section II, 1848, on communists as the most advanced and resolute section without interests separate from the proletariat.
  • Amadeo Bordiga, “Proletarian Dictatorship and Class Party”, 1951, for a strong formulation of party function and class direction.
  • Anton Pannekoek, “The Party and Class”, published 1941, for the council-communist criticism of a party taking power over workers.
  • Jacques Camatte, “Origin and Function of the Party Form”, 1961 with a 1974 postface, on party, programme, and human community.
