WHY COMMUNISM? STUDY · Proletarian Self-Governance
01Studies · Proletarian Self-Governance

A Power Meant to End Power

A revolution wins control of the capital. The old cabinet falls. A workers’ council enters the central bank. Soldiers refuse the generals’ orders. The largest factories, ports, railways, warehouses, and power stations pass into working-class hands.

At that moment, one form of power has been broken. The relations that made it necessary are still moving.

The city needs food before dawn. Hospitals need medicine, sterile water, electricity, records, trained hands, and supply chains crossing regions that may not share the revolution. The defeated owners still possess wealth, contacts, technical knowledge, foreign allies, and the habits of command. Millions of people still depend on wages and markets. Administrative knowledge remains divided among offices built to govern them from above. Racism, patriarchy, nationalism, competition, and deference survive in bodies and institutions.

Political victory changes what can be done. It does not finish the doing.

The proletarian transition is the organized period in which working people use class power to abolish the relations that keep producing classes. Its necessity comes from the world the revolution inherits. Its purpose comes from the world it must make possible.

That purpose is unusually demanding. Other ruling classes consolidated the conditions of their own rule. The proletariat can complete its rule only by abolishing the conditions that make it a proletariat.

Its power is measured by what it makes unnecessary.

A concept born inside changing struggles

The phrase dictatorship of the proletariat did not arrive as a complete constitutional diagram.

In 1848, Marx and Engels described the first step of a workers’ revolution as raising the proletariat into the ruling class. The claim was political before it was administrative. A class that had been organized by capital as labour-power would have to become an independent power capable of reorganizing society.

Marx sharpened the historical claim in his 1852 letter to Joseph Weydemeyer. His distinctive contribution, he wrote, was not discovering classes or struggle between them. It was showing that class struggle leads to proletarian political rule, and that this rule forms a transition toward the abolition of every class.

The last clause controls the first. Proletarian dictatorship has no permanent class order to defend. Its destination is classlessness.

The Paris Commune of 1871 changed the institutional content of the argument. Parisian workers did not simply occupy the existing state and appoint new ministers. They replaced the standing army with an armed people, made public offices elective and recallable, paid officials workers’ wages, and joined legislative and executive work in a body responsible to the population.

Marx treated the Commune as a political form through which the social emancipation of labour could begin. In his first draft of The Civil War in France, he described the reabsorption of state power by society. This was more than a demand for honest government. Social powers that had separated from people and returned as police, bureaucracy, army, hierarchy, and official mystery had to become common capacities again.

The Commune was brief, besieged, incomplete, and violently destroyed. It could not settle the problems later revolutions would face. Its importance lies in the direction it revealed:

  • the working class governs through its own organized bodies;
  • delegates remain subordinate to the people who sent them;
  • necessary coordination survives without a privileged political caste;
  • public functions become understandable and answerable;
  • armed defense remains rooted in the population; and
  • the state begins losing its form as an authority standing above society.

After the Commune, Marx and Engels increasingly defined the transition through this double movement: stronger class power against restoration, deeper social reabsorption against a new separation of rulers and ruled.

Why the morning after victory is still a class society

A capitalist is not only a person with a title deed. Capital is a social relation reproduced through property, wage dependence, production for exchange, competition, accumulated command over labour, and institutions that protect the whole movement.

Removing the old owners can therefore leave capitalist relations alive in new forms.

A nationalized factory may still sell for revenue, discipline workers from above, hoard information, and compete with other enterprises. A ministry may inherit a corporation’s management and continue treating labour as a cost to be controlled. Money may remain the condition of access to food and housing. Specialists may continue monopolizing decisions because the division between intellectual and manual labour has not been overcome.

The old relation can survive a new legal owner.

The transition has to act on several times at once:

  1. Immediate continuity. People must keep eating, healing, traveling, learning, communicating, and caring for one another.
  2. Defense. Organized restoration, blockade, sabotage, invasion, and armed counterrevolution must be defeated.
  3. Economic transformation. Productive property, the purpose of production, accounting, distribution, and access must change.
  4. Social transformation. Household dependence, racial and national hierarchy, gendered labour, education, culture, and the division of labour must change with production.
  5. Administrative reabsorption. Knowledge and useful public functions must move out of a separate caste and into common control.
  6. International development. No isolated territory can complete communism inside a hostile world market and state system.

These tasks interact. A food shortage can strengthen a black market and an old owning class. A secret emergency office can monopolize information and become a permanent bureaucracy. A shorter working day can give people time to govern, study, care, and supervise officials. Public provision can reduce the force of wages and money. International isolation can force military and technical centralization that then reshapes domestic political life.

There is no honest way to reduce this to a countdown.

Six abolitions

The transition has a direction because it has a determinate historical work. It must abolish:

  1. the economic relations that produce classes;
  2. the social relations built upon those economic relations;
  3. the ideas, habits, and institutions reproduced by class society;
  4. the separation of administrative power from society;
  5. the need for a coercive state; and
  6. the proletariat’s own existence as a class.

These are not six departments that can finish their tasks independently.

1. Economic relations that produce classes

Private control of major production must end, but expropriation is only the opening. Production itself must cease being governed by exchange, profitability, and competing claims.

Workers and communities need control over what is produced, how labour is organized, what ecological limits bind it, what is deducted for common services and reserves, and how products reach people. Labour becomes directly social when it begins from recognized need and a common plan rather than waiting for sale to validate it afterward.

2. Social relations built upon them

Property relations enter the household, school, workplace, city, and border. Dependence on a wage shapes obedience. Private responsibility for childcare and elder care shapes gender. Unequal land and infrastructure shape regions. Citizenship and imperial hierarchy shape whose labour and suffering count.

Social revolution therefore reaches care, housing, transport, education, public space, family, health, disability, sexuality, culture, and the time available for participation. The economy cannot be transformed while the rest of life is treated as scenery.

3. Ideas, habits, and institutions of class society

People formed inside competition do not step outside it through a speech. Administrators carry the habits of command. Workers may carry habits of obedience or resentment toward other workers. A party may inherit careerism and treat loyalty as competence. A plan may reproduce the prestige of numbers while hiding the people who bear its cost.

Consciousness changes through organized activity. People learn common responsibility by exercising it, criticizing it, and experiencing institutions that make solidarity materially possible. Education and political struggle belong inside production and self-government rather than waiting beside them.

4. Administrative power separated from society

Complex societies need records, logistics, engineering, medicine, scientific coordination, conflict resolution, and emergency response. Abolishing bureaucracy cannot mean burning the water map or dismissing the person who knows how the grid fails.

The problem is the relation in which knowledge becomes a title to rule.

Offices must become transparent, intelligible, recallable, and robust enough to survive an incompetent or corrupt occupant. Expertise must travel. Workers and communities need direct channels to challenge a decision. Audit must examine exceptional success as well as failure, spreading labour-saving knowledge instead of merely punishing deviation.

5. The need for a coercive state

Armed restoration makes coercion necessary during the transition. Coercion also contains the danger of reproducing a power above society.

The answer is neither ritual pacifism nor worship of force. Working people require the organized capacity to defeat military attack, sabotage, blockade, violent dispossession, and counterrevolution. That capacity must remain politically subordinate to the class and the communes whose freedom it defends.

Its scope, target, evidence, appeal, supervision, and duration matter. Criticism cannot be renamed sabotage. Emergency cannot become a social identity. A military or security body that controls the population’s organizations has reversed the relation it was created to protect.

As class antagonism loses its material base, coercive functions must contract. An institution that invents new enemies to preserve itself has become a barrier to the transition.

6. The proletariat itself

The proletariat is not an eternal moral community. It is the class compelled to sell labour-power because the conditions of production confront it as another class’s property.

When those conditions become common, labour ceases being a commodity, production becomes associated activity, and class divisions disappear, the proletariat disappears with the bourgeoisie.

This final abolition is the truth of every earlier one. A workers’ state that preserves workers forever as workers ruled by managers has not completed proletarian power. It has preserved the class relation under another name.

The Commonalty begins inside the transition

The Commonalty is not a reward delivered after the state has finished governing.

It begins wherever people directly organize a shared power: a commune governing its locality, workers directing production, a public service made transparent and accountable, a network of councils coordinating a region, a community bringing care and knowledge into common provision.

At first these relations are partial. They coexist with money, class remnants, emergency bodies, inherited administration, unequal development, and external threat. Their incompleteness does not make them unreal.

They are the new society learning to exist.

Wider participation weakens class relations and the need for separate coercion. The growing Commonalty then gives working people greater capacity for direct power.

The state and Commonalty overlap during this movement, but they name different sides of it. The proletarian state suppresses organized restoration. The Commonalty reabsorbs useful social functions and develops direct self-government. The first loses work as the second gains capacity.

If the state continually expands while the population remains politically weak, calling the result transitional does not change its direction.

Contradictions have to become institutions

The transition repeatedly needs two things that can pull against one another:

Necessary capacity Danger carried inside it Institutional test
binding coordination the center treats society as a passive object Can affected units report, bargain, refuse under defined conditions, and force review?
specialist knowledge expertise becomes a right to rule Can knowledge be inspected, taught, contested, and separated from final political authority?
armed defense emergency command becomes a caste Are mandate, evidence, duration, appeal, and communal supervision real?
continuity of production inherited relations survive because disruption is feared Does continuity expand common control or preserve managerial command?
local autonomy connected systems fragment and shift burdens outward Are common purposes binding while local method and knowledge remain active?
political organization the party substitutes itself for the class Do councils, communes, unions, and public administration retain independent authority?

Calling for balance does not resolve these contradictions. They need procedures, rights, channels, and material capacities.

A local right of refusal is one example. Workers may halt unsafe or irrational production, bargain over a plan, and require investigation. A locality cannot cancel every common obligation while demanding unrestricted access to the common product. Refusal becomes part of planning when repeated objections change what the system knows.

Audit is another. It investigates a failure when ordinary channels break down, but it can also ask why one workplace achieved more with the same inputs and spread its labour-saving method. Audit becomes bureaucratic when it measures compliance with procedure instead of consequences and learning.

The same logic applies to political office. Recall matters, but a system that replaces one failed official without redesigning the office merely prepares the same failure for another person.

No date can substitute for direction

Some measures can happen in days. Secret budgets can be opened. Privileged salaries can be cut. A landlord’s claim can be ended. A standing military command can be broken.

Other changes depend on harvests, housing, medicine, technical education, international supply, ecological restoration, and people gaining experience in administration. A society with intact infrastructure and powerful workers’ organizations begins from different conditions than a country devastated by civil war and blockade.

An early movement away from money and production for exchange might begin in a period measured in years. Any exact range remains a conjecture. It cannot become a promise about the whole transition.

A short transition can concentrate power disastrously. A long transition can turn necessity into an alibi.

The useful questions are directional:

  • Are working people deciding more and understanding more?
  • Is common provision reducing dependence on wages, rent, debt, and private charity?
  • Is labour becoming directly social before production begins?
  • Are officials easier to supervise, remove, and replace?
  • Is expertise spreading beyond the institutions that once monopolized it?
  • Are councils, communes, unions, and public bodies independent of party command?
  • Is emergency power becoming narrower?
  • Is the Commonalty taking up more of society’s common work?
  • Are class differences and the conditions reproducing them actually shrinking?

No single indicator answers all of them. Together they reveal whether transitional forms are developing toward their own disappearance or hardening into a new order.

A power that can relinquish its function

The need for transition is often used to defend authority as such. The final aim is then postponed whenever people ask for power now.

That reverses the argument.

Transition is necessary because working people must acquire the organized capacity to defend and transform society. Every serious measure should develop that capacity. Public provision gives time and security for participation. Shorter necessary labour makes administration less exclusive. Open information enables criticism. Common education spreads technical knowledge. Recall prevents delegation from becoming sovereignty. Independent class bodies stop a party from absorbing the people it claims to lead.

The destination has to live inside the movement without pretending the movement is complete.

The dictatorship of the proletariat is therefore absolute class authority with a self-abolishing purpose. It defeats the old ruling class, dismantles the machinery of its rule, transforms production and social life, and draws alienated social powers back into common activity. In doing so, it abolishes the bourgeoisie, the state as a separate coercive power, and finally the proletariat itself.

Nothing guarantees that outcome. The transition can be defeated from outside, consumed by scarcity, or reversed by institutions that acquire interests of their own.

Its answer cannot be a promise that good leaders will remember the goal.

The answer is a Commonalty that begins immediately, learns to govern, and takes the work of ruling away from every separate ruler.
