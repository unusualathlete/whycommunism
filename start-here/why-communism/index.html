Why Communism?

At four in the morning, while most of a city is asleep, its shared life is already moving.

A baker lifts the first trays from an oven. A nurse checks a sleeping patient’s breathing. Someone repairs a cable under a street. Someone else drives vegetables in from a farm. Water moves through buried pipes. Computers keep records, balance electrical loads, and guide ships toward a port. Long before any one person eats breakfast, thousands of people have helped make that breakfast possible.

This is the first fact communism asks us to see: we already make the world together.

Nobody builds a city alone. The baker did not grow the wheat, build the oven, invent written recipes, generate the electricity, or make the road that brought flour to the shop. Every pair of hands begins inside a world shaped by other hands. The farther we look, the more people enter the loaf.

Yet the power created by all this cooperation does not belong to everyone who creates it. It faces us as property, prices, rent, debt, management, and orders from institutions most people cannot control. The city depends on workers every hour of every day. Those same workers can lose their homes, medicine, food, or electricity the moment their money runs out.

We make the power. Then the power stands over us.

Communism is the struggle to end that separation. It places society’s shared productive powers under the conscious direction of the people whose lives depend on them. Its destination is a classless society where people organize common life together, produce directly for human and ecological need, and no separate state stands above them as a permanent force.

That is a large definition. The loaf lets us enter it.

When bread meets a locked bin

At closing time, a supermarket worker may be told to throw away edible bread. A hungry person can be standing nearby. The ovens worked. The grain grew. The trucks arrived. The shelves are full. The need is real.

Still, the bread and the hungry person cannot meet.

The bread was made to be sold. Under production for exchange, a human need counts inside the economy most powerfully when money stands behind it. Hunger without purchasing power becomes almost invisible. A shop may destroy food to protect its price, while a farm may leave crops unpicked because selling them would cost more than letting them rot. The absurdity comes from the purpose governing production.

Production for use begins from a different purpose. It organizes work to meet needs directly.

This does not remove the hard work of deciding. Grain, land, water, fuel, storage, transport, and human time are finite. People still have to count them. They have to prepare for drought, keep reserves, compare priorities, and notice when a plan fails. A society producing for use asks these questions openly: How much food is needed? What can the soil renew? Where is hunger greatest? Which work can be reduced? What knowledge is missing? Who will live with the consequences?

Money no longer gives the final answer. Human beings do.

Wealth is larger than what anyone can own

Think of everything you can use without being able to fit it in your pocket.

A language. A sewage system. A public park. The knowledge needed to perform heart surgery. A railway running across an island. A library containing centuries of thought. The electrical grid. A stable climate. A song known by millions of people.

These are forms of common wealth. Common wealth is the store of useful powers, knowledge, places, and systems that people inherit, maintain, and develop together. Even when the law assigns parts of it to private owners, its real creation is social.

A company may own a patent, but generations of scientists made the knowledge around it. A landlord may own an apartment tower, but workers mixed its concrete, wired its rooms, carried its materials, taught its architects, and built the city that makes its location valuable. Private ownership draws a fence around a piece of collective history and says: mine.

Communism brings the great conditions of life into common responsibility. Land, major production, infrastructure, knowledge, and public services become powers people govern for shared use.

Shared use does not mean cutting every object into identical pieces. A wheelchair belongs with the person who needs it. A quiet room matters more to someone overwhelmed by noise. A coastal community may need flood protection that an inland town does not. Equity gives people what their different bodies, circumstances, and capacities require. Equality without attention can become another form of neglect.

The common purpose is that everyone can live and develop. The route must answer to real life.

The individual is a social achievement

People are sometimes taught to fear that communism would dissolve every person into a grey mass. Look closely at any strong individuality and the opposite becomes visible.

A musician becomes distinctive through instruments made by others, songs carried across generations, teachers, rehearsal space, food, electricity, free time, and listeners able to hear what the musician has learned to express. A brilliant mechanic relies on tools, metals, engines, diagrams, and skills gathered from countless lives. A child finds a unique voice by entering a language nobody invented alone.

Human beings become individual through a social world.

Capitalism gives some people extraordinary room to develop while exhausting the time and powers of many others. One child receives lessons, books, travel, safety, and years to experiment. Another arrives home too hungry or tired to think. Later, society calls the difference personal talent, as if each person grew alone.

Communist freedom gives everyone access to the conditions from which individuality grows. Reliable food, housing, care, education, mobility, knowledge, privacy, community, and time become a floor beneath every life. From that shared floor, people can become more different, more skilled, more surprising, and more fully themselves.

The goal is far richer than distributing a larger pile of objects. It is to release human powers now trapped by poverty, overwork, exclusion, private property, and fear.

Society must become able to answer itself

A society this complex cannot run on good feelings. Hospitals need exact supplies. Trains must arrive on connected tracks. Crops follow seasons that votes cannot change. Rivers carry pollution from one region into another. Decisions about energy and land reach people who may never meet.

Communism therefore requires planning, public knowledge, accountable coordination, and institutions capable of acting at different scales. It also requires the freedom to criticize those institutions. A plan that cannot hear bad news becomes dangerous. An official who cannot be removed begins to stand above the people. Information held as a privilege can grow into a new form of rule.

Putting a factory under state ownership does not settle any of this. If workers continue taking orders from an untouchable layer of managers, their social power has acquired a new owner. The name on the deed changed. Daily life did not.

The working class has to govern the transition itself. It must break and transform the machinery that protects capitalist rule, defend the new order against counterrevolution, and at the same time draw more and more administration into ordinary social life. Public functions must become things people can understand, perform, inspect, and correct.

That transformation will contain argument, failure, scarcity, resistance, and danger. Communism offers no magic protection against human error. It offers a way for society to confront its problems as shared problems instead of allowing property and profit to decide them in private.

Many exact forms remain open. Different places may use different methods of delegation, local coordination, auditing, planning, and public participation. Technology can help reveal needs and test possibilities. It cannot inherit political responsibility from human beings. Detailed timelines and future designs are proposals to examine, never promises that history has signed in advance.

The direction, however, is clear: class power gives way to associated production, common wealth, human development, ecological repair, and a common life governed by the people living it.

The world is already covered in our fingerprints

By the time the city wakes, it appears finished. Bread waits on a shelf. Water comes from a tap. A bus turns a corner. A message crosses an ocean in seconds. The labour inside these things disappears, and the world can look as if it simply arrived.

It did not arrive. People made it.

They made its beauty and its waste, its hospitals and its weapons, its abundance and its locked doors. They inherited conditions they did not choose, then added their own lives to them. The same collective power that built a world ruled by capital can build a world organized around life.

Communism begins when people recognize their own strength inside the things that command them. It grows when they organize that strength, take responsibility for it, and turn it toward the full development of everyone.

The world already belongs to human labour in fact. The struggle is to make it belong to humanity in truth.
