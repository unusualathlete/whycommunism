15Planning for Use and Need

Open Mathematical Questions for a Planned Economy

On Monday, a planning team calculates how much water the farms will need.

On Tuesday, the rain fails.

The columns remain perfectly aligned. The river does not.

This is the beauty and danger of mathematics in planning. Equations can make a complicated relation clear enough to act on. They can also hold still a world that has already moved.

No formula can run society. The mathematical work is to build a public collection of methods able to count material reality, compare possible paths, expose tradeoffs, carry uncertainty, and remain corrigible when life answers back.

Start with what must physically fit

Every product arrives from other products and activities.

Bread needs grain, water, ovens, energy, transport, buildings, and work. Ovens need metal and electricity. Electricity needs generators, grids, maintenance, and fuel or renewable flows. Follow the chain, and one loaf opens into a map of society.

An input-output model represents these dependencies among branches of production. Each branch uses inputs from others and produces outputs they may need in return.

The model can ask whether a proposed plan is physically coherent. If housing requires more cement than all cement plants can produce, the contradiction appears before half-built walls stand in the rain.

This is already powerful. It is also only a beginning.

Input requirements change with technique, place, scale, quality, and time. A national average may hide an efficient plant and a damaged one. A new process can alter the table. Drought can change the water coefficient overnight.

Its technical coefficients must be tested against production and revised when methods or conditions change.

The main methods answer different questions:

Method What it helps reveal What it cannot settle
Physical inventory What materials, machines, land, and energy exist Which social use deserves priority
Input-output model How branches depend on one another Whether its technical coefficients still match reality
Optimization Which feasible path best fits stated objectives Which objectives society should choose
Dynamic model How present decisions change future capacity How much sacrifice the present owes the future
Scenario or robust planning Which plans survive several plausible futures Every surprise or irreversible harm
Feedback and statistical revision Where forecasts differ from results Whether the original category measured the right thing

No row replaces the others. Together they make disagreement more concrete.

Optimization needs a purpose

Optimization searches for the best feasible result according to a stated goal.

The stated goal is often written as an objective function. A model might minimize labour time, greenhouse gases, material use, delivery delay, or some weighted combination. Constraints keep the result within available resources, technical requirements, and ecological limits.

Leonid Kantorovich’s work on linear programming showed how scarce means could be allocated across competing uses under defined conditions. Modern solvers can search enormous fields of possible combinations.

Yet the phrase “best result” contains a human decision.

Minimizing labour hours can favour a toxic process. Minimizing carbon can ignore disability access. Maximizing calories can erase taste, culture, and nutrition. Maximizing one number often pushes every uncounted quality toward the edge.

The mathematics should force objectives into the open. It must never smuggle a political priority into a coefficient and return it wearing the authority of proof.

Several goods refuse to become one

Money gives capitalism one common unit, but that unity carries the relations of property and exchange inside it. Communist planning can work with several direct measures instead.

One plan may record:

  • tonnes of material;
  • hours of different kinds of labour;
  • energy by source and time;
  • land and water;
  • carbon and other pollutants;
  • expected service life;
  • repair needs;
  • health and safety;
  • accessibility;
  • ecosystem effects; and
  • waiting time or unmet need.

The difficulty is not simply technical clutter. Some goods are genuinely different.

How many minutes of a person’s daily commute equal the loss of a wetland? How many tonnes of concrete equal the cultural meaning of a neighbourhood? A public decision may still choose a tradeoff. Mathematics should display the loss, not pretend the two sides became identical.

Multi-objective methods can show a frontier of feasible choices, where improving one aim requires giving up some of another. That gives politics a more honest object: several real paths and the cost carried by each.

Labour time raises precise disputes

Labour accounting can reveal social effort and compare methods. It also raises questions that cannot be answered by adding raw hours.

How should training be recorded? What counts as the average when conditions differ sharply? How are intensity, danger, disability, care, waiting, and creative uncertainty represented? How is past labour embodied in machines spread across their useful life? How should a failed experiment be counted when its knowledge prevents later failure?

One possible answer may be correct for a particular planning purpose and wrong for another.

The labour time needed to schedule workers is not necessarily the measure needed to compare technologies. The social account used to reduce drudgery should not become a personal score of human worth.

The mathematics must preserve the distinction among planning production, allocating consumption, and understanding social burden.

Opportunity cost without a market price

Choosing steel for a hospital wing means not using the same steel for a bridge. A plan needs some way to reveal the cost of the alternative forgone.

Optimization methods can generate indicators of scarcity inside a model. In linear programming these are often called shadow prices. They show how much improving a constraint would change the stated objective.

Such indicators can be useful without becoming market prices. They are conditional on the model’s goal, data, and constraints. Change the objective from minimum labour to minimum ecological harm, and the significance of the same scarce material may change.

The indicator is a message about one plan. It should not become a universal substance hidden inside the steel.

Time makes every equation harder

A static model balances one period. Real plans build a future.

A new rail line consumes labour and steel now, changes travel next year, reduces fuel demand later, and may reshape where people live for decades. A forest cut today cannot be restored by inserting next year’s seedlings into the same row. Training a doctor has a delay. A machine wears down.

Dynamic models connect periods so present actions alter future possibilities. They must represent investment, construction delay, depreciation, learning, ecological renewal, stocks, reserves, and changing needs.

The number of possibilities can grow rapidly. Some relationships are nonlinear: doubling one input does not double the result. Thresholds and feedback loops can change the system’s behaviour altogether.

This raises the problem of tractability: can a useful solution be found with the time, data, and computing power available?

A theoretically perfect method that finishes after the decision is needed has failed in practice.

The future arrives as a range

Rain, migration, illness, invention, crop disease, political conflict, and human desire cannot be predicted exactly.

A plan built around one forecast can be efficient and brittle. A robust plan performs acceptably across several plausible futures.

Robustness may require spare capacity, diverse suppliers, modular equipment, stored necessities, repair skills, and routes that can be changed. These can look wasteful in a model that assumes the average day will last forever.

Probability helps where past patterns carry information. Scenario analysis helps where the future could take several distinct forms. Precaution matters where damage may be irreversible and probabilities are poorly known.

Uncertainty should enter the plan as structure, not as a footnote.

Feedback turns calculation into learning

No model should be expected to remain correct without contact with results.

Actual production, use, quality, waiting times, ecological effects, accidents, and local reports return to the plan. Forecast errors become new information. Parameters change. Categories may need revision.

The mathematical challenge is therefore continuous. How quickly should a plan update? Which changes are noise? Which reveal a new pattern? When should local adjustment happen automatically, and when does a change require political reconsideration?

Too little revision makes planning rigid. Too much can make long commitments impossible. A railway cannot be redesigned every afternoon because one passenger changed route.

The system needs memory as well as reflex.

Preference is not a sales total

People want variety, beauty, convenience, privacy, novelty, familiarity, and control over personal choices. A plan must learn these without treating wealth as the strength of a vote.

Registered requests, pre-orders, use rates, waiting lists, surveys, consumer bodies, local deliberation, repair records, returns, and experiments can all provide information.

Each has limits. People can request only what they know exists. Surveys can flatten intensity and context. Past use can preserve old deprivation. A long waiting list can reveal high desire or simply a broken supply system.

Planning mathematics needs a theory of how desire changes as the world changes. The new train answers an old preference and teaches people that another way of moving is possible.

Ecology does not stay outside the matrix

Ecological constraints have spatial and temporal shape.

One tonne of pollution released near a wetland differs from the same mass in another place. Water used during drought differs from water used during a wet season. Carbon accumulates globally, while many toxins act locally. Biodiversity does not fit one smooth scale.

Models must connect production to regeneration, thresholds, cumulative damage, and unequal global responsibility. They also need ways to represent qualities that remain partly outside formal calculation.

An ecological limit should bind the feasible set where science is clear enough. Where knowledge is incomplete, uncertainty must remain visible to political judgment.

Nature is the moving ground inside every economic answer, never an external cost to add afterward.

Verification must be social

A planning model can contain millions of entries and still rest on one mistaken assumption.

Verification needs several levels. Producers check whether technical coefficients match work. Communities check whether categories match lived need. Ecologists test material boundaries. Mathematicians inspect methods and proofs. Independent teams reproduce results. Public bodies compare alternative models.

Open code helps, but code alone is not understanding. Data, definitions, documentation, objectives, uncertainty, and institutional responsibility also need inspection.

The most important outputs should be explainable in the language of the decision:

If we choose this plan, the hospital opens two years sooner, the bridge waits, this much labour shifts, this watershed bears the risk, and these assumptions could change the result.

That sentence is part of the mathematics because it is what lets society use the mathematics.

An unfinished programme

The open questions are large:

  • Which input-output and optimization methods scale best?
  • How should several objectives be presented for public choice?
  • How can models combine labour time, physical quantities, ecology, and opportunity cost without collapsing their differences?
  • What level of detail belongs at each recursion?
  • How should uncertainty, rare disasters, and irreversible harm enter?
  • How can local innovation update common coefficients without letting private interests manipulate them?
  • Which data must be public, and which must remain protected?
  • How should international planning work where capacities and historical responsibilities differ?
  • How can people contest a model without needing a career in mathematics?

These questions do not weaken the case for planning. They define the work planning requires.

Markets also calculate through institutions, approximations, missing data, unequal power, and repeated failure. Their mathematics is no less political because its politics arrive through money.

Conclusion

On Wednesday, the planning team changes the water table. It adds the failed rain, widens the range of possible futures, protects drinking water first, revises crops, and sends the result back to the farms for challenge.

The equation has not conquered uncertainty. It has become more honest.

Mathematics reaches its highest use here when it stops pretending to be a ruler. It becomes a common instrument for seeing farther, choosing openly, and learning when the river refuses to follow the line.
