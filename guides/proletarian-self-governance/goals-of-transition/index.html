02Proletarian Self-Governance

What the Transition Has to Accomplish

Suppose the owners of a hospital network are removed on Monday morning. The signs change. The buildings now belong to the public. Yet nurses still work double shifts, patients still cannot see the budget, medicine still follows a supply chain built around profit, and the same executives still control the information that keeps the hospitals running.

Something important has happened. It is nowhere near enough.

A communist transition cannot be measured by the name on a deed or the color of a flag. It succeeds only as the relations that keep producing owners and workers, rulers and ruled, are pulled apart and replaced. Political power, productive property, daily life, public administration, and the world system have to change together.

That gives the transition six connected tasks.

1. Working people must become a ruling power

The first task is political. Working people need institutions through which they can make binding decisions, act on them, supervise public servants, and defeat attempts to restore capitalist rule.

This is Proletarian Self-Governance, the positive content of the historical phrase dictatorship of the proletariat. Here dictatorship means the organized rule of a class, not the personal rule of a dictator. Capital already rules through property, law, investment, management, police, and military force. The transition reverses that class relation.

The dispossessed owning classes do not retain a veto over their own dispossession. Proletarian authority over attempts to restore exploitation has to be decisive. Its democratic measure is not whether former capitalists remain free to command, but whether working people themselves hold and direct the power that defeats them.

Workers cannot do this by placing sympathetic ministers at the top of machinery built to protect capitalist property. The ready-made state has its own chain of command, professional caste, secret channels, and habits of obedience. Its repressive machinery must be broken. Its necessary knowledge and practical functions must be taken up in new organs answerable to the working population.

A government that calls itself proletarian while workers remain spectators has failed at the first task.

2. The conditions that recreate classes must be abolished

The abolition of class relations means ending the arrangement in which one group controls the conditions of production while another must submit to that control in order to live.

Factories, large farms, mines, banks, energy networks, transport, land held for rent, and communication systems are powers over life. Private ownership of these systems lets a minority decide what will be produced, where people will work, which places will develop, and who can survive a shortage.

Expropriation removes that private command. Yet a public title is only the legal doorway. Social ownership becomes real when associated workers and communities can direct production, know how it works, and connect it to a common plan. A state-owned company that keeps wage dependence, executive privilege, secrecy, and production for exchange can reproduce much of the old relation under a new owner.

The test is control, not paperwork.

3. Production must acquire a different purpose

Economic transformation is the movement away from production for exchange and accumulation toward production for use, need, free time, and human development.

Society still has to count. It must know how much labor, steel, medicine, water, electricity, land, and reserve capacity are available. It must decide among competing needs and respect ecological limits. Communism does not free anyone from material reality. It frees the organization of material life from profit.

During the transition, inherited markets, money, wages, and unequal capacities do not all vanish at once. Common services can expand while dependence on rent, debt, and private purchase contracts. Labor accounting may help compare costs and capacities. Transitional forms should be judged by whether they prepare the conditions for their own disappearance.

A fairer market preserved forever would still leave production mediated by exchange. The transition makes social production directly social.

4. Everyday life must stop manufacturing submission

A social revolution reaches beyond ownership. Capitalism is reproduced in the household, school, city, clinic, media system, laboratory, and daily division of time.

A worker may hold a formal vote in a council and still lack the time to attend it. A parent may be legally equal and still carry nearly all childcare. A disabled resident may possess a right on paper and face a meeting room they cannot enter. A rural community may be invited to govern itself while every hospital, school, and cultural institution remains hours away.

Shorter necessary work, socialized childcare, quality housing, public transportation, accessible public space, free education, and common care turn political liberty into a material capacity. Cultural struggle confronts racism, patriarchy, nationalism, ableism, and the learned belief that ordinary people need rulers.

This cannot be done by policing private thought. New human relations grow through new ways of living, arguing, producing, learning, and caring.

5. Social power must return to society

The withering away of the state is often mistaken for a government deciding to become smaller. It is something deeper: the disappearance of a separate coercive power as the class antagonisms requiring that power are abolished.

Water still has to be cleaned. Trains still have to be coordinated. Records, laboratories, emergency reserves, and conflict resolution remain necessary. What disappears is their organization as powers standing above society, guarded by official privilege and separated from ordinary control.

The Commonalty grows as these useful functions become the direct, accountable activity of communes, councils, associated producers, and common institutions. This begins during the transition. It cannot be postponed until after every danger has passed, because the capacity for self-government is built by governing.

The state withers through replacement.

6. The revolution must outgrow the border

Capitalism has already made production global. Medicines cross oceans. Energy systems, finance, migration, climate, and military power do not stop at customs booths. A transition isolated within one country would inherit dependence on the world market and face blockade, capital flight, military pressure, and technological isolation.

Internationalism is therefore part of the transition’s content, not an act of charity added later. Working people must connect their revolutions, share knowledge and resources, defend one another, and overcome the imperial divisions that built wealth in some regions through deprivation in others.

The horizon is a world republic of self-governing commonalties. Its exact constitution cannot be written in advance. Its necessity is already visible in every planetary problem that rival capitalist states cannot solve.

The class that abolishes itself

The tasks strengthen or weaken one another.

Seen from the side of what must disappear, the transition carries six abolitions: the economic relations that create classes; the social relations built upon them; the habits, ideas, and institutions produced by class society; the separation of administrative power from society; the need for a coercive state; and finally the proletariat’s own existence as a class forced to live by selling its labor.

The last abolition gives proletarian power its direction. The working class does not seek an eternal workers’ state. It becomes the ruling class in order to end the relations that make it a class at all.

Text alternative: Working-class power makes expropriation possible. Expropriation allows production for need. Social provision gives people the time and capacity to govern. Self-government absorbs public functions. International cooperation protects and enlarges the whole process. Each advance strengthens working-class power.

Return to the hospital. Public ownership now opens the books. Health workers and communities direct priorities. Regional bodies coordinate medicine and specialist care. Shorter hours and common childcare make participation possible. Administrators explain decisions and can be removed. Care is planned as a social need rather than sold as a revenue stream.

No single reform completes the transformation. Together they change what the hospital is, who commands it, and what kind of people can take part in running it.

The transition has a severe standard. With every major step, working people should gain the power and knowledge to govern, while property rule, official privilege, and separate coercion lose ground. A measure that strengthens a new authority over workers while promising freedom later moves in the wrong direction, whatever name it carries.

The transition exists to abolish the need for transition.

Source notes

Primary texts

  • Karl Marx and Friedrich Engels, Manifesto of the Communist Party, section II, 1848, on proletarian political supremacy, the abolition of bourgeois property, and the transformation of production.
  • Karl Marx, The Civil War in France, 1871, on the Commune as a political form for the emancipation of labor.
  • Karl Marx, Critique of the Gotha Programme, 1875, on the political transition, common deductions, and the lower and higher phases of communist society.
  • Friedrich Engels, Anti-Dühring, Part III, chapter 2, “Theoretical”, 1877, on direct social appropriation, production according to a common plan, the end of government over persons, and the state’s eventual dying out.

Further study

  • V. I. Lenin, The State and Revolution, written in 1917, on Marx and Engels’s theory of the transition and the Commune.
  • Rosa Luxemburg, The Russian Revolution, 1918, on socialist democracy, mass participation, and the dangers of political substitution.
