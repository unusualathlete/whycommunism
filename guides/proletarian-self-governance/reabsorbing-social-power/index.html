05Proletarian Self-Governance

Who Owns the Water Map?

A water main breaks beneath a crowded street. Within minutes, a whole hidden world becomes visible.

Someone knows where the valves are. Someone has a map of the pipes. Someone can test whether sewage entered the line. Someone controls the repair crew, the road closure, the warning message, the emergency supply, and the money required to act.

All of these powers were created by social labor. Generations of workers surveyed the ground, laid pipe, kept records, studied disease, built pumps, and learned the system. Yet the people who depend on the water may meet that accumulated knowledge as a locked office, a waiting line, or an order with no explanation.

Their own power stands over them.

Marx called this relation alienated political power: society’s capacity to organize common life takes the form of an authority separated from society. Reabsorbing social power means taking that capacity back.

Keep the map. Change the relation.

A revolution should not burn the pipe map because an old ministry owned it. The map is useful. So are the laboratory, the repair depot, the trained hydrologist, and the regional reservoir records.

These are social functions, necessary activities through which common life continues. The political question is how they are organized.

Under an alienated system:

  • information can become the private territory of an office;
  • residents appear mainly as customers, cases, or risks;
  • workers report upward but cannot redirect the institution;
  • decisions arrive without reasons people can inspect;
  • class and wealth shape whose complaint receives attention; and
  • police or courts enforce the order when people resist it.

Reabsorption changes each relation. Water workers gain collective control over their labor. Residents and communes can see conditions, state needs, and review priorities. Regional bodies coordinate rivers, treatment plants, drought reserves, and equipment that no locality can manage alone. Specialists explain material possibilities. Public servants remain removable and their decisions leave an intelligible record.

The function survives. Its power changes hands.

Engels used the phrase administration of things for the coordination of production and resources after class rule disappears.

The phrase can sound colder than it is. Water administration includes chemistry and pipe pressure, but it also includes human judgment. During drought, society must decide how water is shared among homes, hospitals, food production, industry, and damaged ecosystems. No formula can choose the purpose of society.

What disappears is government over people as members of a subordinate class. A landlord can no longer cut off a household through ownership. A corporation can no longer drain a watershed because it can pay. An official cannot turn technical access into a private throne.

Things are administered. People govern together.

The state does not fade because officials announce a smaller budget. It loses its separate existence as society becomes able to perform its useful work directly and as class antagonism stops producing a need for organized repression.

This happens unevenly. A neighborhood meal service may pass quickly into common administration. An electrical grid may require long preparation, wide coordination, and a large body of specialized knowledge. A public-health emergency can demand temporary powers that would be intolerable as ordinary government.

The direction stays clear. Every transferred function should enlarge social knowledge and initiative. Every exceptional authority should have a defined purpose, public limits, and a route back into ordinary self-government.

Reabsorption has failed if the new institution changes its emblem while preserving secrecy, privilege, and command from above.

Return to the flooded street.

Residents know the water has changed color. Utility workers can stop the flow. Laboratory staff can identify contamination. Health workers know who faces the greatest risk. A regional network can move clean water and repair equipment. A commune can open accessible distribution points and check that nobody is abandoned.

None possesses the whole answer alone. Together, they already contain the social power required.

The question is whether that power can move freely through them or whether it must wait behind a door.

The Commonalty grows each time society opens that door, takes hold of the map, and recognizes the knowledge inside it as its own.

Source notes

Primary texts

  • Karl Marx, The Civil War in France: First Draft, 1871, especially the description of the Commune as society reabsorbing the state power as its own living forces.
  • Karl Marx, The Civil War in France, 1871, on the transformation of public office and the destruction of the old state machinery.
  • Friedrich Engels, Anti-Dühring, Part III, chapter 2, “Theoretical”, 1877, on the end of government over persons and the administration of things and productive processes.
  • Friedrich Engels, letter to August Bebel, March 18, 1875, on replacing the misleading phrase “free state” with Gemeinwesen or Commonalty.

Further study

  • Peter Hudis, Marx’s Concept of the Alternative to Capitalism, 2012, chapters 4 and 5, on the Commune, freely associated labor, and the transcendence of alienated social power.
