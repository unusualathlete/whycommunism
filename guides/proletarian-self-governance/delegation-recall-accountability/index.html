09Proletarian Self-Governance

Design for the Day the Good Person Fails

Every political system has a favorite imaginary person.

Monarchy imagines a wise ruler. Liberal government imagines an honest representative. Technocracy imagines a neutral expert. Revolutionary movements often imagine a devoted cadre who will never confuse personal power with the needs of the class.

Real people become tired, frightened, vain, misinformed, overconfident, isolated, or simply wrong.

Self-government cannot depend on finding better saints. It needs public roles that remain useful when an ordinary person makes an ordinary mistake.

Design brief: build for fallible people

The brief has four requirements.

1. Power stays with the sending body

A delegate is entrusted with a defined task or mandate by an organized body. The people who send the delegate remain politically active. They can supply new information, review a decision, revise the mandate, and replace the person.

This is different from handing someone a free political mandate for several years. An elected representative may claim to embody the public after the public has lost any practical way to intervene.

The distinction lives in the surrounding relation:

If the sending body retains power If the office absorbs power
the task or mandate is clear the official decides what the mandate really meant
information moves both ways information collects around the office
reports use understandable language expertise and procedure hide responsibility
removal is practical removal exists only at a distant election
another person can take over the office becomes dependent on one personality
compensation stays within ordinary life office creates wealth, status, and a separate social circle

Delegation widens self-government only while the people sending power can still use it.

2. Selection follows the work

There is no democratic magic inside a ballot box.

An election can express a broad political choice. Sortition, selection by lot from an eligible group, can bring people into public work who would never campaign for office. Rotation can spread knowledge and break permanent possession of a role. A recallable appointment can match a trained person to a specialized task. Mixed forms can combine qualification, public choice, chance, and review.

Each method has a danger. Elections reward popularity and access to publicity. Appointment can harden into professional closure. Sortition without training can abandon a person inside an unfamiliar job. Rotation can destroy memory if everyone leaves at once.

The right method is the one that fits the function while preserving social control. A regional political body and a laboratory safety panel do not have to be chosen in the same way.

3. Recall is usable before scandal

Recall is the power to remove a delegate or public servant before a term ends.

The Commune tradition made recall central because election alone could not prevent deputies from separating themselves from the people who chose them. Yet a right written on paper may sleep for years.

People need enough information to know when a mandate has failed. They need a simple procedure, an available replacement, protection from retaliation, and a political culture that does not treat criticism as betrayal. Scheduled reports and confidence reviews can keep recall from appearing only after scandal.

Recall should also distinguish failure from blame. A water coordinator cannot produce rain. A clinic director cannot solve a medicine shortage hidden by the regional system. Removing one person may be necessary, but the investigation must still ask whether the office, resources, or instructions made success impossible.

Public accountability is a search for truth before it is a search for a culprit.

4. The office survives the officeholder

A robust office is designed so that mistakes become visible and repairable before they become disasters.

Five questions test its strength:

  1. Can another person understand the role? Duties, records, and reasons must be clear enough for succession.
  2. Can bad news travel around the officeholder? Workers and residents need direct routes to other responsible bodies.
  3. Can the public see the result? Reports should connect decisions to real conditions, not bury failure under activity counts.
  4. Can knowledge spread? Training and rotation should reduce dependence on one gatekeeper.
  5. Can the role shrink or end? A public function should not manufacture work to preserve itself.

This is organizational robustness: institutions acknowledge human fallibility and make correction normal.

The opposite is the heroic office. One brilliant person collects every decision, keeps half the method in memory, and becomes impossible to replace. Admiration hides fragility.

Stress test: the shared reservoir

Several towns share a reservoir. Rainfall has fallen. Farms, homes, hospitals, and ecosystems all need water.

Each town sends delegates to a regional water body. Some are elected because allocation is a public political question. Treatment engineers are appointed for technical responsibility. A rotating group of residents reviews accessibility and household effects. The delegates receive mandates stating minimum needs and the conditions under which priorities can change.

Now imagine one delegate quietly gives a factory extra water after meeting its manager.

A weak system discovers the favor months later, if at all. The records are technical, the meeting is private, and removing the delegate requires proving a crime.

A robust system makes the allocation visible. Treatment workers can report the mismatch directly. The sending town can demand an explanation and recall its delegate. Another trained person can take the role without collapsing the whole body. The audit asks about the individual favor and the rule that allowed one person to grant it.

The scandal becomes a correction because the institution was built to survive honesty.

What the failure test reveals

Reasons must be public

Public accountability means that an institution must give reasons, show relevant evidence, accept review, and face correction.

Technical language is sometimes necessary. A reservoir report cannot replace every measurement with a friendly metaphor. But jargon becomes political armor when it prevents ordinary people from locating the decision.

A good public explanation answers:

  • What changed?
  • Who decided?
  • What evidence and limits mattered?
  • Who gained or carried a burden?
  • What result will be checked?
  • How can the decision be challenged?

This discipline also improves thought. An official who cannot explain the chain from evidence to action may not understand it.

Office must not become a class position

Public service can separate a group from society through salary, housing, access, information, protection, prestige, and career.

Worker-level compensation in the Commune was meant to cut that separation. The exact arrangement will change across time and place. The enduring principle is that office should not purchase a different life from the people governed.

Rotation, ordinary compensation, open records, term limits where useful, and a return to other social work can reduce official interest. None is sufficient alone. Informal privilege can survive formal equality, and rapid rotation can leave permanent technical staff ruling from behind the chairs.

Accountability is therefore a whole ecology of relations, not one constitutional trick.

Leadership, expertise, and trusted people all remain. Trust never requires surrender.

Choose capable people.

Then build as if they can fail.
