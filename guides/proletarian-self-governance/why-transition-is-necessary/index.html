03Proletarian Self-Governance

The Morning After Victory

Imagine that a revolution wins tonight.

Tomorrow morning, babies still need milk. Dialysis machines still need electricity. Trains must arrive before workers can operate the power stations that keep the trains moving. Crops do not wait for a new constitution. A storm may already be forming over the sea.

The owners may be gone from their offices, but their world has not vanished with them. They still possess money, contacts, records, technical knowledge, media influence, and allies abroad. Managers trained to obey profit targets still occupy key positions. Courts still contain property law. Police and military commands still carry the shape of the old order. Millions of people still know how to survive inside capitalism better than they know how to govern production together.

This is why a transition is necessary. Society has to uproot one mode of production while continuing to eat, heal, travel, learn, and defend itself. Communism cannot be announced into existence. It has to be made.

Walk through the first day.

6:00 a.m. The relation is still there.

Class antagonism is conflict rooted in opposed positions within production. Capitalists command productive property and depend on extracting labor from others. Workers depend on access to those same conditions of production and struggle over their work, time, security, and product.

Removing several capitalists does not abolish the relation. If workplaces still compete for revenue, managers still command workers, and everyone still needs a wage to live, the old logic continues to reproduce itself.

The problem is larger than personal greed. A kind owner still operates under competition. A cruel owner can be replaced while ownership survives. Class abolition means transforming the relation that keeps placing people on opposite sides of production.

That takes organized power and time.

8:00 a.m. The old machinery opens its doors.

The ready-made state machinery is the network of official hierarchy, property law, courts, police, military command, secret executive power, and bureaucracy formed to administer capitalist society.

Some of its workers possess knowledge every society needs. A water engineer does not become useless because the ministry changes. Disease records, weather systems, electrical maps, emergency stores, and legal evidence remain valuable.

But the machinery is not a neutral toolbox. Its authorities are separated from ordinary life. Its coercive bodies are trained to protect the existing order. Its procedures treat private property as normal and collective intervention as an exception. New leaders sitting at the top will continually be pushed toward the old result.

The working class must therefore break the machinery as a structure of class rule while taking hold of the knowledge and functions needed for common life. It replaces permanent command with accountable organs, secret channels with public ones, privileged officials with removable servants, and administration over people with administration people can direct.

Preservation and destruction happen inside the same act. The water map is kept. The social relation around it changes.

10:00 a.m. The defeated class starts moving.

A displaced ruling class has rarely accepted dispossession because an assembly voted correctly.

Counter-revolution is organized activity that tries to restore the defeated class order. It can include capital flight, lockouts, destruction of records or equipment, food and credit blockades, political conspiracy, foreign intervention, military attack, and the use of racism or nationalism to split the working population.

The answer cannot be a polite request that former rulers stop ruling. Working people need organized force capable of defeating armed restoration and keeping essential systems under social control.

That truth does not bless every act committed in the name of defense. Revolutions have faced invasion, civil war, assassination, and sabotage. Revolutionary states have also imprisoned critics, crushed independent working-class organization, used terror beyond any defensible necessity, and protected new bureaucracies by calling opposition counter-revolutionary.

History gives no permission slip. Every coercive act must be judged by what danger existed, whom it targeted, what alternatives were available, what controls existed, and whether it enlarged or destroyed the self-government it claimed to protect.

A revolution unable to defend itself can be murdered. A revolution that can survive only by silencing the class that made it has already lost its purpose.

Noon. Knowledge reveals where power was hiding.

Power is also hidden inside competence.

An electrical grid contains generations of accumulated labor: maps, routines, maintenance knowledge, software, trained hands, and the ability to hear when a machine sounds wrong. Much of this knowledge lives inside particular people and teams. It cannot be seized like a building.

Workers already possess enormous practical intelligence, but capitalism divides knowledge. One person sees a valve, another a spreadsheet, another a regional load forecast. The owner or top office appears to unite what the labor process has separated.

The transition must reconnect this divided intelligence. Experienced specialists remain necessary. Their knowledge must become public, teachable, and answerable to common decisions. Workers need time and education to take on wider administration. Experts need direct contact with the people whose labor and lives their decisions shape.

The division between those who decide and those who execute cannot be wished away. It is overcome through practice.

By evening, every answer pulls against another.

A transitional contradiction appears when two necessary aims strain against one another.

The transition needs The transition must prevent
binding coordination across connected systems a center that treats society as a passive object
force against armed restoration coercion becoming a permanent political caste
specialists who preserve complex systems expertise becoming a right to rule
continuity in food, health, energy, and transport continuity preserving capitalist relations
rapid decisions in genuine emergencies emergency becoming the normal form of government
local initiative and direct knowledge isolated units breaking common provision

Choosing one column against the other does not solve the problem. Coordination without self-government produces a new authority over workers. Local autonomy without common obligation can leave hospitals without medicine and cities without food. Defense without limits can turn inward. Limits without defensive capacity can invite restoration.

The solution has to be institutional: communes and councils with real power, mandates, recall, open information, protected refusal, direct communication, public review, and common planning that can learn from local knowledge. The contradiction is not solved once. It is governed through institutions that keep knowledge and power moving through the class.

Midnight does not start an honest countdown clock.

Some changes can begin quickly. A council can publish a hidden budget. A landlord’s claim can be ended by law. A privileged salary can be cut.

Other changes depend on harvests, housing, education, international conditions, infrastructure, and the development of trust and skill. A region dependent on imported insulin faces different limits from one that manufactures medicine. A society emerging from civil war begins differently from one with intact public systems and a highly organized working class.

Under favorable conditions, an early movement away from money and production for exchange might begin within five to fifteen years. That range is a conjecture, not a promise and certainly not a timetable for the whole transition.

Time by itself proves nothing. A short transition can centralize power disastrously. A long transition can become a permanent excuse. Direction is the real measure:

  • Do working people know more and decide more?
  • Is common provision reducing dependence on wages, rent, and debt?
  • Are officials easier to supervise and replace?
  • Is private class power being dismantled?
  • Is coercive authority becoming narrower as the Commonalty grows?

The transition cannot become a waiting room where freedom is postponed. People learn freedom by building the material conditions of its survival.

On the morning after victory, society will inherit every wound, skill, machine, prejudice, dependency, and unrealized power of the old world. The necessity of transition lies there. People must keep the world alive while changing the way life is organized.

Nothing about that is automatic.

That is precisely why working people must do it themselves.

Source notes

Primary texts

  • Karl Marx, letter to Joseph Weydemeyer, March 5, 1852, on class struggle, proletarian dictatorship, and the transition to a classless society.
  • Karl Marx, The Civil War in France: First Draft, 1871, on the Commune, class rule, and the reabsorption of state power.
  • Karl Marx, The Civil War in France, 1871, on breaking the ready-made state machinery.
  • Friedrich Engels, The Origin of the Family, Private Property and the State, chapter IX, 1884, on the state as a power arising from class antagonism and placing itself above society.

Further study

  • Rosa Luxemburg, The Russian Revolution, 1918, especially the relation between revolutionary defense and active socialist democracy.
  • Maximilien Rubel, “The Dictatorship of the Proletariat,” 1976, on the place of the concept in Marx’s political thought.
  • Étienne Balibar, On the Dictatorship of the Proletariat, 1977, on the transition as a problem of class struggle and state power.
