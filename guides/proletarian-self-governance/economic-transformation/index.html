06Proletarian Self-Governance

Expropriation Is Only the Beginning

Economic transformation contains several different acts. They are often collapsed into one word, as if ending private ownership automatically created communist production.

It does not.

Taking a factory from a capitalist breaks one relation of command. It does not by itself tell society what to produce, how workers and communities will govern the factory, how its materials will be allocated, how ecological damage will be counted, or how its product will reach people.

The transition has to move from private command, through common ownership, into social control and production for use.

Four terms that should not be confused

Expropriation removes the property through which a capitalist or landlord commanded other people’s labor or access to life. It does not yet answer who governs the activity afterward.

Nationalization moves legal ownership to a national public body. Workers and communities may still face a hierarchy they cannot control.

Socialization brings the activity under real social direction by workers, communities, and accountable coordinating bodies. The wider economy may still depend on money and production for exchange.

Collectivization joins separate producers in cooperative or common forms. Its meaning depends on whether that joining is supported, voluntary, coerced, well coordinated, or destructive.

These acts can overlap. They are not synonyms.

Property is a power over people

Personal possession and capitalist property perform different social functions.

A coat keeps somebody warm. A mechanic’s tools let her repair an engine. A family home provides somewhere to live. None of these facts gives one person command over the work and survival of hundreds of others.

Ownership of a factory, a large apartment portfolio, a bank, a power grid, or a logistics system does.

Capitalist property is backed by contracts, courts, police, debt, managerial authority, and the dependence of people who do not own the conditions they need in order to work. The owner may never enter the factory. The property title still organizes everyone inside it.

Expropriation ends that private right of command. Marx called this the expropriation of the expropriators.

The target is not ordinary use or personal life. It is the class relation through which some people own the gates that everyone else must pass through to work, eat, travel, obtain shelter, or make use of society’s productive powers.

Different activities need different paths

A national grid, a worker cooperative, and a family bakery do not begin from the same position.

Large banks, ports, railways, energy systems, industrial supply chains, and communication networks already coordinate the work and needs of millions. Capital has made production social while keeping its command private. Their actual scale demands public coordination from the beginning.

A worker cooperative may already have elected management, shared records, and collective control of its immediate workplace. Yet it still buys and sells in a market. Competition can force its members to cut costs, intensify work, neglect ecological damage, or exploit workers outside the cooperative. Its internal democracy does not abolish the pressure of production for exchange.

A small farm, repair shop, food stall, or family workshop may depend on knowledge and relationships held by only a few people. Absorbing it into a remote administration overnight could destroy useful capacity. Common access to tools, storage, energy, transport, health care, education, and income can make wider association practical without treating small producers as obstacles to be crushed.

The diagram does not prescribe one timetable. It shows why a single legal formula cannot govern every transition. The same direction can require different immediate forms.

The decisive question is: how does this activity enter common life without preserving private command or destroying the knowledge that makes it useful?

Public ownership can preserve private habits

Nationalization can stop dividends, unify investment, and protect a necessary service from private closure. Those changes matter.

It can still reproduce the old relation inside public property.

A minister replaces a board. A public manager replaces a private manager. Workers receive decisions they did not help form. Communities encounter new priorities only after they are final. Information moves upward, authority moves downward, and performance is judged through measures nobody affected can challenge.

The public owns the institution in law but remains outside its control.

Socialization requires more:

  • workers can change work processes, report failures, and challenge priorities;
  • communities and users can place needs into the plan;
  • technical bodies disclose constraints and consequences;
  • delegated authority has a defined task and remains reviewable;
  • plans can be corrected when results differ from forecasts; and
  • wider coordinating bodies connect local activity to regional and global needs.

Social control is not a vote on every technical act. A grid operator must respond quickly to a failure. A surgeon cannot pause an operation for an assembly. Expertise determines what is possible and safe within its field. It does not create a general right to decide society’s ends.

From exchange to use

Under capitalism, an enterprise must sell its product and recover more money than it spent. A useful thing that cannot be sold may never be made. A harmful or wasteful thing can attract enormous labour if its sale expands capital.

That is production for exchange.

Production for use begins with another set of questions:

  1. What do people need?
  2. What physical resources and kinds of labour are available?
  3. What must be preserved, replaced, repaired, or expanded?
  4. What ecological limits cannot be crossed?
  5. Which choice develops human capacity and reduces necessary labour?

The answers cannot all appear on the first day of a revolution. Money, taxes, public enterprises, cooperatives, small production, regulated exchange, and inherited supply chains may coexist with expanding common provision.

The transition is visible in what gains and loses power. Health care, housing, water, energy, education, transport, communication, food, and care can become guaranteed capacities rather than purchases. Investment can follow social and ecological priorities. Labour used for speculation, advertising battles, planned waste, rent collection, and guarding private claims can move into useful work or return to people as free time.

Money loses social power whenever access to life no longer depends on payment.

Planning needs more than one measure

A market price compresses many relations into one comparable number. It does not reveal them fairly.

A cheap product may depend on poisoned water, exhausted workers, unpaid care, subsidized transport, weak bargaining power, or damage imposed on another region. A costly repair may save more material and labour than a cheap replacement. Price reports what can be exchanged under existing property and power. It cannot decide what society should value.

A communist plan needs several kinds of information at once.

Labour accounting reveals the hours and kinds of work required, including scarce skills. It cannot decide whether the activity is wanted, safe, or ecologically possible.

Physical accounting reveals steel, water, land, energy, transport, storage, and other material limits. It cannot decide which needs should take priority.

Ecological accounting reveals emissions, habitat effects, soil, waste, repair, and long time horizons. It cannot decide how competing human and ecological claims should be judged.

Capacity accounting reveals machines, infrastructure, trained people, and bottlenecks. It cannot decide whether existing capacity should be expanded or replaced.

Democratic judgment decides among needs, purposes, burdens, acceptable risks, and different qualities of life. It cannot replace the technical facts it must use honestly.

Labour time is indispensable and incomplete. One hour written in a ledger cannot create a trained nurse. Ten hours spent on an unwanted object do not become useful because they were measured accurately.

Some transitional designs use labour certificates to record contribution or access. Such a certificate is not automatically money if it cannot buy productive property, hire labour, earn interest, circulate between people, or accumulate as capital. Its exact role remains a question of institutional design and material conditions.

No measure should become a new ruler. The people living with a plan need to see what is counted, what is missing, and how to challenge the result.

Equity and the common product

Equal quantities do not always meet equal claims.

A growing child, a pregnant person, somebody with diabetes, an older adult with difficulty chewing, and a worker doing intense physical labour may need different food. Giving each the same basket produces formal equality and unequal care.

Equity responds to different needs and capacities so people can participate in common life.

Marx called the equal standard that survives in the lower phase of communism bourgeois right. Distribution according to contribution treats people as comparable workers while their bodies, households, abilities, and responsibilities remain different. Communist development moves beyond that narrow measure toward provision according to need.

Before individual distribution, part of the social product must also maintain society itself. Marx’s common deductions include:

  • replacement of used-up means of production;
  • expansion where additional capacity is needed;
  • reserves for disruption and disaster;
  • administration;
  • schools and health services; and
  • support for people unable to work.

These deductions return wealth as capacities people share. A clinic, school, accessible railway, public park, reserve grain store, or repaired water system becomes common wealth: developed powers available because society maintains them together.

How to judge the direction

No date on a decree proves that economic transformation has occurred. A neat prediction of five, ten, or fifteen years cannot account for productive capacity, war, ecology, geography, international isolation, institutional knowledge, or the strength of counter-revolution.

A transition can be judged through observable movement:

  • Is private command over production contracting?
  • Is production for exchange losing ground to production for use?
  • Are common services expanding in quality and reach?
  • Can workers and communities change actual plans?
  • Is necessary labour becoming shorter?
  • Are different needs being met with greater equity?
  • Is productive and administrative knowledge spreading?
  • Are ecological costs entering decisions before damage occurs?

If ownership becomes public while these movements reverse, nationalization has not completed socialization.

Expropriation removes the old owner’s command. Economic transformation is complete only when society can consciously direct its own productive powers and no new minority possesses them in its name.

Source notes

Primary texts

  • Karl Marx and Friedrich Engels, Manifesto of the Communist Party, section II, 1848, on ending bourgeois property, centralizing the means of production in proletarian hands, and using political power to transform production.
  • Karl Marx, The Civil War in France: First Draft, 1871, on replacing class property with the conditions of free and associated labor rather than merely changing the legal owner.
  • Karl Marx, Critique of the Gotha Programme, 1875, on common deductions, individual claims, unequal capacities, and the development from the lower to the higher phase of communist society.
  • Friedrich Engels, Anti-Dühring, Part III, chapter 3, “Production”, 1877, on society taking possession of the means of production and consciously organizing production according to a common plan.

Further study

  • Paul Cockshott and Allin Cottrell, Towards a New Socialism, 1993, especially the chapters on labor time, planning, information, and democratic control.
  • Pat Devine, Democracy and Economic Planning: The Political Economy of a Self-Governing Society, 1988, on social ownership and negotiated coordination among affected producers and communities.
  • Peter Hudis, Marx’s Concept of the Alternative to Capitalism, 2012, on directly social labor, the transcendence of value production, and Marx’s distinction between capitalism and communist association.
