13Proletarian Self-Governance

When the Weapon Closes Around the Hand

The first party member enters an office because workers trust her.

She organized under repression. She understands the programme. She stayed when others fled. Her election makes sense.

Then another party member is chosen for the same reasons. The office needs assistants, and trusted comrades seem safer than unknown applicants. Appointments begin to move through party channels. Reports reach the public only after the party reviews them. A criticism of one administrator starts to sound like an attack on the organization, then on the revolution.

Nothing in this sequence requires a secret conspiracy. Trust can harden into staffing. Staffing can harden into control. Control can make the party the owner of a state built in the name of the working class.

The danger begins before anyone announces a party-state.

A party-state is a social relation

A party-state exists when a political party controls public administration, appointments, coercive forces, communication, and the worker organizations that are supposed to govern.

Its constitution may still contain councils, elections, unions, communes, and public ownership. Those names cannot answer the decisive question: who can make a binding decision, enforce it, obtain the information needed to challenge it, and remove the people carrying it out?

The progression often looks like this:

  1. experienced party members receive public mandates;
  2. party membership becomes an informal qualification for responsibility;
  3. a second chain of command appears behind public institutions;
  4. careers, housing, information, and prestige begin to depend on party approval;
  5. councils keep meeting but lose control over appointments and resources;
  6. criticism of administration becomes evidence of political disloyalty;
  7. the party treats its survival as identical with the survival of the revolution.

This is institutional substitution. The party first speaks for the class, then acts for the class, then rules the class.

Bureaucracy begins from a real problem

Large organizations need precise communication.

A hospital cannot issue an order that says, “Handle the dangerous material carefully.” It must specify the material, the equipment, the procedure, the person responsible, and what happens when something fails. Railways, electrical grids, laboratories, and public budgets require standardized records and exact language.

Different sections also develop knowledge that others do not possess. Translators, coordinators, clerks, and technical functionaries make cooperation possible.

Bureaucracy becomes a power when the people who translate and transmit decisions gain control over the channels through which the organization moves.

The shift can be traced:

flowchart TD
    A["Complex work requires precise communication"] --> B["Functionaries translate between specialized sections"]
    B --> C["Access to information passes through the functionaries"]
    C --> D["Rules and standards become tools of gatekeeping"]
    D --> E["Resources and decisions depend on administrative permission"]
    E --> F["The apparatus protects and expands its own position"]

Text alternative: Complex work requires translation. Translators can become gatekeepers. Gatekeepers can control information, rules, resources, and access to decisions. The apparatus then begins preserving itself instead of serving the purpose that created it.

This form of bureaucracy is not committed to a coherent ideology. It can speak any political language that protects its place. Its instinct is to stop uncontrolled action, because every direct relation between workers and decision-makers makes the gatekeeper less necessary.

Procedures then replace outcomes. A hospital can fail patients while completing every form. A factory can miss the plan while reporting perfect compliance. Each failure creates another approval step, and the added step becomes evidence that more functionaries are needed.

The party-bureaucratic interface

Party-state fusion gives bureaucracy political armor.

The party-bureaucratic interface is the set of relations through which party position and administrative position reinforce one another.

Party supplies Administration supplies
political trust and ideological legitimacy jobs, offices, information, and material security
disciplined networks authority over resources and appointments
protection against criticism framed as disloyal the means to reward loyalty and isolate dissent
a claim to represent the whole class daily control over the institutions where the class acts

Careerists do not need to believe the programme. They learn the phrases that open the door. Superficial loyalty becomes a job qualification, and public administration creates a new division of labor between those who authorize and those who obey.

The party changes under this pressure. It stops selecting people mainly for revolutionary understanding and begins absorbing people who want the life attached to office. The state changes too. Administrative survival replaces communist transformation as its practical aim.

An organization formed to abolish a ruling stratum can become the recruitment system for another one.

Power is revealed by control, not legal language

A party can lack a formal constitutional monopoly and still rule.

It rules if its leadership controls:

  • the military and armed public bodies;
  • appointments to administration;
  • access to information and mass communication;
  • the promotion and removal of officials;
  • the resources needed by councils and communes;
  • the organizations through which workers are expected to participate.

It may allow several names on a ballot while making every important path depend on its approval. It may call councils sovereign while withholding the records, staff, funds, and physical force required to act.

The reverse is also true. Party members can hold many offices without the party ruling, provided those officials answer to public mandates, can be recalled by the bodies that selected them, and cannot use a hidden party command to overrule those bodies.

Count power where it moves.

The phrase “ceding power” gives the game away

Sometimes a party promises that, as the transition matures, it will gradually cede power to councils, communes, or worker organizations.

That promise admits the problem.

If the party possesses power that it can later hand to the working class, the working class does not possess that power now. The arrangement is a party dictatorship preparing a possible future self-government, not the dictatorship of the proletariat.

The Commonalty cannot be donated from above. It grows only through the class exercising power itself, including the power to make mistakes, correct them, replace officials, and challenge the party.

The party’s role may shrink. Its functions may be absorbed. Its influence may rise and fall. The class’s sovereignty is not a future gift.

Emergency can freeze a temporary relation

Civil war, invasion, sabotage, famine, and blockade force concentration. Orders become urgent. Secrecy may protect lives. Military organization requires discipline that ordinary administration should not imitate.

These conditions are real. So is the transformation they can produce.

A party acting as general staff during armed struggle can begin treating military command as the proper model for society. The officers and clandestine networks that protected the revolution can become permanent channels of appointment and information. Every attempt to restore ordinary public power can then be accused of weakening defense.

The military organization required by a revolutionary crisis must contract and dissolve as that crisis passes. The party must remain capable of changing its form with the struggle. A fortress that never opens becomes a prison for the people inside it.

Necessary force belongs to the class struggle. It cannot become the party’s private property.

Self-government needs structural independence

Good intentions cannot prevent party-state fusion. The safeguards have to alter the material relations through which power concentrates.

Public office follows the public mandate

A party member serving in a commune, council, court, planning body, or militia answers to that body. Party discipline cannot secretly cancel a public instruction.

Worker organs control their own life

Councils, unions, communes, and assemblies keep their own records, communication, selection procedures, funds, and power of recall. They cannot be administrative branches whose leaders arrive from outside.

Communication can bypass gatekeepers

A worker can reach responsible bodies above, below, and beside the immediate office. Public information is understandable, timely, and available without permission from the person being criticized.

Office does not purchase another life

Ordinary compensation, public access rules, rotation where useful, and a return to common work prevent administration from becoming a route to a superior material position.

Audit measures reality

Inspection asks whether the institution fulfills its purpose, reduces labor, protects people, meets the plan, responds to users, and learns from superior methods. It does not reward the production of more procedure.

Political disagreement remains political

Criticism, refusal, and rival proposals supply information. Armed restoration and material sabotage are fought as class enemies. Disagreement among workers cannot be collapsed into the same category without destroying the feedback on which self-government depends.

These safeguards reinforce one another. Recall without information is blind. Transparency without independent worker organs produces spectators. Rotation without shared knowledge leaves permanent staff in control. Several parties without public power can decorate the same bureaucracy.

The test is simple and severe

Ask what happens when workers say no.

Can a workplace reject an unsafe order without a party official redefining refusal as sabotage? Can a commune remove a popular party member from office? Can an audit publish a failure that embarrasses the leadership? Can workers organize to change a plan without first receiving permission from the organization they are challenging?

If the answer is no, the class is not governing.

The party exists because the proletariat needs theory, preparation, memory, coordination, and a conscious communist direction. Those needs remain. Party-state fusion does not satisfy them. It converts them into claims of possession.

The party serves the class by making class power stronger, more conscious, and more capable of acting without it.

When the weapon closes around the hand, it has ceased to be a weapon of liberation.

Source notes

Primary texts

  • Karl Marx, The Civil War in France: First Draft, 1871. It presents the Commune as society reabsorbing public power and turns officials from mysterious rulers into removable servants under continuous supervision.
  • Karl Marx, The Civil War in France, 1871. It grounds public responsibility in recall, ordinary compensation, and working bodies rather than a separate parliamentary caste.
  • Friedrich Engels, introduction to Marx’s The Civil War in France, 1891. It identifies safeguards against workers losing their newly won power to their own deputies and officials.
  • Rosa Luxemburg, The Russian Revolution, chapter 6, written 1918. It traces how the suppression of broad political life leaves bureaucracy and a small circle of party leaders as the only active element.
  • Gabriel Miasnikov and the Workers’ Group of the Russian Communist Party, Manifesto of the Workers’ Group of the Russian Communist Party, 1923. It records a worker opposition confronting the growing separation between party-state administration and proletarian power.

Further study

  • Anton Pannekoek, “The Party and Class”, published 1941. It gives the clearest council-communist account of substitution and insists that working masses must think, decide, and act through their own organizations.
  • Amadeo Bordiga, “Proletarian Dictatorship and Class Party”, 1951. Its strong claim for the party’s central role provides the tension that any theory of proletarian self-government must resolve without weakening programme, preparation, or class action.
  • Jacques Camatte, “Origin and Function of the Party Form”, 1961, with a 1974 postface. It follows the relation among party, programme, Gemeinwesen, and the eventual passage from political organization to human community.
