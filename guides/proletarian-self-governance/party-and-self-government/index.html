12Proletarian Self-Governance

The Party Is a Weapon, Not the Hand

A strike begins over a stolen hour of pay. One group knows the payroll. Another knows the labor law. Someone remembers an earlier defeat. Someone has contacts at the depot supplying the factory. Another worker sees that the stolen hour belongs to a larger attack happening across the industry.

No one person contains the whole struggle.

Organization joins what experience has divided. It turns memory into preparation, scattered anger into common purpose, and a local fight into part of a class movement. This is the ground from which a communist party grows.

The party becomes necessary because consciousness, experience, and organization develop unevenly. It becomes dangerous the moment it treats that unevenness as a permanent right to rule.

The party is a weapon the working class makes through its own struggle. The weapon does not become the hand.

What the party concentrates

A communist party is the organized, advanced, and resolute part of the proletariat united around a communist programme.

The programme gathers a historical understanding of capitalism, class struggle, revolution, the transitional tasks of working-class power, and the abolition of class society. It connects an immediate fight to the whole movement instead of offering a list of promises for a future government.

The party concentrates capacities that are otherwise scattered:

  • theory developed from past and present struggles;
  • historical memory that survives defeat and retreat;
  • analysis of changing capitalism;
  • education and propaganda;
  • strategy across local and national boundaries;
  • international coordination;
  • material preparation for decisive struggle;
  • organizational experience;
  • a clear view of the communist aim.

This concentration is social. No brilliant leader owns the party’s knowledge. It exists in the whole activity of investigation, argument, education, organization, failure, correction, and struggle across generations.

A party cut off from this activity becomes a library with a flag. A movement that rejects every durable concentration of knowledge is forced to relearn old defeats.

The party is necessarily selective. It cannot contain every political current at once and still carry a definite programme. Reactionaries, careerists, and people opposed to the communist aim have no claim to membership.

That boundary belongs to the party. It cannot become a boundary around the working class’s right to govern. Political selection inside a voluntary organization never grants that organization the right to decide who counts as the class.

General staff does not mean substitute army

The party can act as the general staff of the revolution. That phrase names a demanding function: studying the whole terrain, connecting fronts, preparing for crises, preserving direction, and helping millions act together.

The proletariat remains the force that fights and governs.

The party can The party cannot
propose a common strategy declare its own decision to be the will of the class
prepare people and material resources seize power on behalf of passive workers
coordinate militants across many organs turn councils and communes into party departments
maintain the final communist aim use theoretical clarity as a title to public office
criticize a failing state institution place party discipline above a public mandate
lead through earned confidence replace persuasion with a legal monopoly

A Blanquist minority imagines that a disciplined group can capture the state and perform the revolution for everyone else. The communist party prepares for the moment when the working class itself moves. It then places its organization, knowledge, courage, and discipline at the disposal of that movement.

Workers create councils, communes, militias, unions, assemblies, and planning bodies through their own activity. Party members work inside them as workers and communists. They argue, organize, and often lead. The organs remain the class’s organs.

If workers go home while the party governs, the class has not become the ruling class.

Leadership has to be won again

Calling an organization a vanguard proves nothing. Leadership exists when people follow an argument because it explains reality, when an organization anticipates danger accurately, when its proposals survive practice, and when its members can help others act.

The party leads through:

  • clarity that makes a confused situation intelligible;
  • courage when hesitation would surrender the initiative;
  • organization that turns agreement into power;
  • results that earn trust;
  • criticism that names failure before enemies do;
  • a willingness to learn when reality defeats an expectation.

Legal privilege weakens this kind of leadership. Once the party can enforce its correctness through appointments, police, media control, or administrative command, it no longer has to prove anything. Error can be renamed disloyalty. Agreement becomes difficult to distinguish from fear.

The party cannot become a mirror of immediate opinion. Workers formed under capitalism can support a course that protects one trade, locality, or short-term gain at the expense of the whole class. Historical memory and a clear programme allow the party to oppose that course, sometimes sharply. It must win the argument through evidence, organization, and the consequences of practice.

This is why the party acts proactively. It studies where a crisis is moving, prepares before the opening appears, and uses long-range planning to prevent avoidable defeats. A leadership that can only echo events is not leading. A leadership that commands because it predicted events has turned foresight into a throne.

Real leadership increases the independent capacity of those being led. A party that wins every decision while the class grows less capable has failed.

Revolutionary education travels both ways

Revolutionary pedagogy joins theory to action.

Workers do not become politically conscious by receiving the correct doctrine as a package. They investigate a problem, struggle over it, observe the result, connect it to wider relations, and return to action with deeper understanding.

The party helps organize this movement. It brings history, theory, and experience from other struggles. It also approaches workers and communities as sources of knowledge.

Consider a public hospital. Party organizers may understand national supply chains, preventive care, pharmaceutical ownership, and the class structure of health. Nurses know what staffing ratios do at night. Cleaners understand infection pathways hidden from the budget. Patients know which services are unreachable. Disabled residents know when efficiency has been calculated around bodies unlike theirs.

The programme gives these facts a common horizon. The facts keep the programme alive.

Education becomes domination when the party speaks and everyone else repeats. It becomes revolutionary when people acquire the ability to investigate, judge, organize, and teach in turn.

The party and the proletarian state are not the same organ

The proletarian state organizes class power, defense, common decisions, and the economic and social transition. The party develops political direction, consciousness, continuity, and strategy.

These functions interact constantly. They must not fuse.

Party members will often hold public responsibility because struggle has given them experience and trust. In office, they answer to the commune, council, service, or public body that gave them the mandate. Party membership cannot create a second, hidden chain of command above that mandate.

The party must retain the freedom to criticize public institutions, organize against bureaucratic degeneration, and call the class into action. An official party fused with the state loses this independence. It begins defending every administrative compromise as if the communist programme required it.

The reverse danger is equally real. A state may fall under opportunist control, preserve capitalist relations, repress workers, or attack the organs of self-government. The party cannot answer with neutrality. It must help the proletariat understand, resist, and rectify that defeat.

If the apparatus violently turns against the class, political struggle may again become armed class struggle. The working class and its own organs must remain the force capable of acting. The party prepares, argues, coordinates, and fights within that class power. It does not convert emergency leadership into a permanent sovereignty over the class.

One programme, no magical title

The class party seeks unity around a communist programme. It fights fragmentation, sectarianism, careerism, and the reduction of class struggle to disconnected local interests.

Unity cannot be created by a law declaring one organization identical with the proletariat.

The class is larger than the party. It contains people moving at different speeds, institutions formed around different functions, and arguments that no decree can abolish. A communist party proves its general character by helping these struggles converge upon class power and communism.

The decisive unity is the unity of class action. When a workplace stops, workers do not become separate armies according to party membership. Political tendencies argue inside the common organization of the struggle, and the workers decide how to act.

A party worthy of unity makes the whole class more coherent. A party that demands the class become its membership list has confused organization with possession.

Its success makes it less necessary

The party’s role changes as the revolution changes society.

1. Under capitalist rule. The party concentrates theory, organization, propaganda, agitation, preparation, and the connection of struggles. Class consciousness, durable worker organization, and international unity must spread beyond it.

2. During revolutionary rupture. The party offers strategy, coordination, material readiness, and disciplined action. The mass capacity to seize and defend power must become the decisive force.

3. In the early transition. The party emphasizes political education, vigilance against restoration, strategic coordination, and defense of the final aim. Direct government belongs to communes, councils, militias, and planning bodies.

4. As the Commonalty develops. Advice, study, education, and criticism of stagnation or privilege become more important than direct intervention. Administration, planning, theory, and conscious social direction become common capacities.

5. In classless community. No separate political function remains. Society consciously organizes its own life.

The party begins as a concentration because the capacities of communist action are uneven. Its historical task is to help generalize them.

As the proletariat takes up organization, administration, planning, education, and conscious direction, direct party intervention decreases. The party becomes more educational and advisory. Its remaining functions pass into communal life.

Eventually, what was once concentrated in an advanced and exclusionary organization becomes ordinary social capacity. All society performs the conscious work that required a separate party.

At that point, everyone is in the party in the only meaningful sense, so the party no longer exists.

The party’s disappearance proves that it served communism rather than itself.

The party is necessary because the class is not yet fully organized around its historical task. It is temporary because communism abolishes the conditions that made such a separation necessary.

The working class needs a weapon capable of seeing farther than one fight.

It must never lose the hand that holds it.

Source notes

Primary texts

  • Karl Marx and Friedrich Engels, Manifesto of the Communist Party, section II, 1848. It defines communists as the most advanced and resolute section of the proletariat without interests separate from the class as a whole.
  • Karl Marx, General Rules of the International Working Men’s Association, revised 1871 from the 1864 Provisional Rules. It establishes that working-class emancipation must be conquered by workers themselves and joins political organization to the abolition of class rule.
  • Ernesto Che Guevara, “On Revolutionary Medicine”, August 19, 1960. It develops revolutionary education as learning and transformation through practical service with the people rather than instruction delivered from above.
  • Amadeo Bordiga, “Proletarian Dictatorship and Class Party”, 1951. It presents the party as the organization that unifies political struggle, programme, preparation, and the conquest of class power.
  • Amadeo Bordiga, “Characteristic Theses of the Party”, December 1951. It gives the party responsibility for theory, historical continuity, material preparation, international unity, and leadership as the general staff of the class.

Further study

  • Jacques Camatte, “Origin and Function of the Party Form”, 1961, with a 1974 postface. It connects party, programme, human community, historical emergence, and the passage from a party-community to the human community.
  • Anton Pannekoek, “The Party and Class”, published 1941. Its council-communist challenge sharply states the danger of a party seeking power for itself and the need for the masses to think, act, and decide through their own class organizations.
