10Proletarian Self-Governance

The Red Button and the Plan

In many factories, a red button can stop the whole line.

Workers are told to press it when someone is in danger. Under wage discipline, they may also know that stopping production for a false alarm can bring punishment. The button belongs to safety. The fear belongs to capital.

Self-government removes that fear. A worker who sees danger can stop the process because protecting human life is part of governing production.

That small button contains a large political problem. A common plan has to bind thousands of connected activities. Yet no planner can see every loose bolt, sick worker, failed delivery, poisoned stream, or better method. The people nearest the work need power to interrupt the plan. The plan needs a way to learn from the interruption.

Refusal begins where reality touches the order

The right of refusal is the protected ability of workers or a local body to stop, challenge, or renegotiate an instruction under defined conditions.

It belongs mainly at the operational level:

  • danger appears;
  • a required input never arrives;
  • equipment cannot meet the target;
  • the method is damaging people or nature;
  • local conditions have changed; or
  • the order contradicts its stated purpose.

Refusal is not an unlimited right for one unit to abandon every common obligation while continuing to draw on everyone else’s labor. A hospital cannot decide that medicine delivery is optional. A railway crew cannot invent its own track standard.

The right protects living knowledge from a dead instruction.

Bargain before the button is needed

Bargaining brings needs, capacities, burdens, and alternatives into the plan before targets become binding.

A planning body might present several feasible plans. One requires less labor now and expands consumption sooner. Another directs more labor toward infrastructure that could shorten work later. Workplaces can demand maintenance, training, safer methods, or investment that makes a target possible. Communities can identify needs a production table missed.

Good bargaining does not abolish conflict. It puts conflict where people can see and decide it.

Once a plan is adopted, connected units are expected to carry it out. Refusal then responds to concrete trouble rather than reopening every settled priority from the beginning.

What should happen after a refusal?

flowchart TD
    A["A worker or local body stops or challenges an instruction"] --> B{"Is there immediate danger?"}
    B -->|Yes| C["Stop first and protect people"]
    B -->|No| D["Keep safe activity running where possible"]
    C --> E["Report facts through direct channels"]
    D --> E
    E --> F{"Can the local and coordinating bodies resolve it?"}
    F -->|Yes| G["Revise method, target, resources, or schedule"]
    F -->|No| H["Use independent review or a defined appeal"]
    H --> I["Audit disputed conditions"]
    G --> J["Return the result to the wider system"]
    I --> J
    J --> K["Correct the plan and share what was learned"]

Text alternative: Stop immediate danger first. Report the facts. Let the local and coordinating bodies try to resolve the problem. If they cannot, use a review body that can investigate the disputed conditions. Return the result to the wider planning system so the plan or method can change.

A review body with no direct stake in the dispute can sometimes mediate. In that setting, the planning center appears as one party making a case, not as judge of its own decision. Another dispute may need technical inquiry, a regional assembly, a court-like public body, or direct renegotiation.

No single harmonizing organ fits every scale. The principle is that an appeal must be real, informed, and capable of changing the result.

Feedback is disagreement made useful

Feedback is information about an action returning to whoever can alter the next action.

One stop may reveal a broken sensor. Twenty stops at similar plants may show that the common target is impossible. Treating all twenty as disobedience destroys the evidence.

Feedback also carries good news. If one depot moves the same freight with less labor and fewer injuries, something may have improved. The method should be studied and shared rather than hidden inside local pride.

This is where audit belongs. An audit is a fact-finding investigation used when ordinary communication is incomplete, disputed, or surprising. It can verify harm, find a bottleneck, expose false reporting, or discover an advance.

Auditors need access. They also need limits. Their purpose, evidence, and conclusions must be reviewable so investigation does not become a permanent police power over production.

Recursion: a whole inside a whole

A recursion is a working whole nested inside a larger whole. A maintenance team belongs to a rail depot. The depot belongs to a regional network. The region belongs to a wider transport system.

Each level needs enough autonomy to handle its environment and enough connection to serve the larger purpose. Each needs operation, coordination, review, knowledge of changing conditions, and a common direction.

The levels should rhyme. They should not be identical.

Policy cannot be swallowed by control

The Viable System Model separates operational control from policy.

System 3 coordinates present operations, allocates shared resources, and checks whether the parts are doing what the organization needs. System 5 holds identity, purpose, and policy. It decides what the organization is for and balances immediate operations against knowledge of the changing world.

The separation is political. If operational control becomes the source of policy, the apparatus that manages today’s plan also decides society’s direction. Means quietly become ends.

Every recursion needs its own policy function. A factory council determines the character of its working life within the common plan. A regional network determines regional priorities within wider commitments. At the broadest level, policy belongs to the self-governing Commonalty through removable servants under public supervision.

Modern computation can move enormous amounts of information across them. Autonomy cannot depend on a center being technically unable to see. It must be established through clear authority, bounded functions, and accountable purposes.

An algorithm can reveal that output fell. It cannot decide whether the cause justifies more resources, a different product, a slower pace, or an end to the process. Even a general intelligence would possess needs and purposes that are not identical with human needs. Models organize facts inside goals chosen by people. They do not become System 5.

The red button is powerful because it joins responsibility to knowledge. The worker does not press it to escape the plan. The worker presses it to keep the plan connected to life.

A plan that cannot be interrupted cannot learn.

A refusal that cannot return to a common decision cannot coordinate.

Self-government needs both.

Source notes

Primary texts

  • Friedrich Engels, “On Authority”, 1872, on binding coordination in factories, railways, and ships. Its examples illuminate a real problem without settling the democratic form of authority.
  • Karl Marx, Critique of the Gotha Programme, 1875, on common deductions, social allocation, and material constraints within cooperative production.
  • Karl Marx, The Civil War in France: First Draft, 1871, on public supervision and removable administration.

Further study

  • Stafford Beer, Brain of the Firm, 2nd edition, 1981, on viable systems, recursive organization, coordination, audit, and policy.
  • Stafford Beer, The Heart of Enterprise, 1979, on the five-system model and the relation among operation, coordination, control, intelligence, and identity.
  • Stafford Beer, Diagnosing the System for Organizations, 1985, a compact method for applying the Viable System Model.
