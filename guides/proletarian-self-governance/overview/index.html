01Proletarian Self-Governance

Who Governs When Workers Govern?

Changing who sits in government is not the same as changing who governs.

A ministry can fly a workers’ flag while workers still receive decisions as completed facts. A party can call itself proletarian while the class remains politically passive. An industry can become public property while the people who operate and depend on it still have no power over its purpose.

Proletarian Self-Governance begins from a stricter claim: working people must possess and exercise the power to reorganize society themselves.

This power has a historical name, the dictatorship of the proletariat. It is a transition, not a permanent constitution. Its purpose is to defeat capitalist rule, remove the conditions that reproduce classes, and develop forms of common life that no longer require a state standing above society.

The contradiction is severe. The working class needs organized power. It must use that power in a way that makes separate rule less necessary.

A class, not a single ruler

In ordinary speech, a dictatorship means the unchecked rule of a person or small group. Marx used the word in a different political sense. He was asking which class holds organized power.

Capitalist class rule can exist through a monarchy, a parliamentary republic, a presidential system, a military government, or a multiparty democracy. These forms are not identical. Rights, elections, and civil liberties matter. Yet as long as one class owns the main conditions of production, that class possesses a power no ballot by itself can remove.

Owners decide whether workplaces open or close. Banks direct credit. Landlords control access to homes. Investment moves toward profit. The state protects the property relations on which these powers depend.

The dictatorship of the proletariat reverses that relation. The working class becomes the organized ruling power and uses that power to abolish class rule itself.

This definition excludes three substitutions:

  1. A leader cannot substitute for the class.
  2. A party cannot substitute for the class.
  3. State ownership cannot substitute for the class’s actual control.

The historical term should not be hidden. It should also never be allowed to excuse a reality in which workers remain governed by somebody else. Proletarian Self-Governance states the decisive test directly.

Why there is a transition

Capitalism cannot be ended by changing a law while leaving its social relations intact.

After a revolutionary break, the old world still has material force. Ownership records, banks, debts, managerial hierarchies, military commands, courts, police, supply systems, borders, and international dependencies remain. Knowledge of administration and production is unevenly distributed. People still need food, water, medicine, care, energy, and transport while new institutions are being built.

Former owners can use wealth, technical control, foreign allies, media, factory closures, capital flight, sabotage, and armed force to restore their command. New institutions can also fail through inexperience, scarcity, isolation, or internal separation from the people who created them.

A political transition is the period in which capitalist power is being dismantled while society develops the capacity to organize common life directly.

It has four connected tasks:

  • defeat attempts to restore capitalist class rule;
  • keep necessary social life functioning;
  • reorganize production and distribution around use, need, and ecological limits; and
  • transfer knowledge, initiative, and authority into the hands of working people.

None can safely wait for the others. Defense without self-government can produce a new commanding minority. Participation without the power to defeat restoration can leave the old ruling class intact. Social provision without economic transformation remains dependent on profit. Expropriation without shared knowledge can leave society dependent on a new administrative caste.

The ready-made state is not neutral

Not every rule, school, railway, hospital, or public worker belongs to the state in the political sense.

In class society, common functions are organized through a power separated from much of the population. Permanent officials, professional political offices, courts, police, prisons, armies, and chains of command stand over people whose activity makes the whole structure possible.

This separation is built into how decisions move. Information travels upward through restricted channels. Authority travels downward. The people living with a decision often meet it only after the decisive moment has passed.

Marx drew a hard conclusion from the Paris Commune of 1871: the working class cannot simply take hold of the ready-made state machinery and use it for its own purposes.

Breaking that machinery does not mean abandoning medical records, railway knowledge, water systems, or disaster response. Necessary functions and skilled workers must be preserved. The relation of rule must change.

Public work has to become transparent where possible. Delegated authority must have a defined purpose and remain answerable. Specialized knowledge must spread instead of becoming a private title to govern. Workers and communities must be able to initiate decisions, inspect the information behind them, correct failures, and remove people who misuse authority.

The old state is not made revolutionary by installing new leaders at its summit. Its useful capacities have to be transformed into society’s own organized activity.

The Commonalty begins immediately

Commonalty names society directly organizing its shared life. It develops Engels’s use of the German word Gemeinwesen, meaning a communal body or common being, as a better name than a “free state.”

The Commonalty is not a reward postponed until every danger has vanished. If people are kept politically weak throughout the transition, a separate governing body will accumulate knowledge, careers, privileges, and interests of its own. There is no reason to assume it will later surrender them.

The Commonalty begins wherever organized people take real responsibility for a common function:

  • a workplace council gains authority over production and opens its records;
  • a commune directs local services and resources;
  • users of a public service can change its priorities;
  • delegates coordinate across localities under defined mandates;
  • technical bodies make their evidence and limits available for public judgment; and
  • institutions of defense remain subordinate to the organized population.

These early forms coexist with conflict, scarcity, inherited habits, and the remaining need to resist counter-revolution. They are incomplete. Their direction still matters.

As common capacity grows, separate power should contract. Administration becomes knowledge more people can use. Problems can be reported without passing through an administrative gatekeeper. Local bodies can act without becoming isolated from regional and global needs. Coordination remains, but ruling over a politically passive population does not.

Five tests of actual worker rule

Names and ownership documents are not enough. A political form can be judged through what people are actually able to do.

Initiative. Workers and communities can raise questions, propose changes, and place needs on the agenda. If they may comment only after leaders have chosen the options, initiative remains above them.

Information. Plans, constraints, records, and failures are understandable and open to scrutiny. If a specialist or office controls the facts needed to challenge it, information has become a private power.

Delegation. Authority has a defined task, a limited duration, public reporting, and a real means of recall or replacement. If appointment becomes a career and a general license to rule, delegation has become possession.

Correction. Refusal, complaint, audit, appeal, and feedback can alter a decision. If participation merely collects opinions while policy remains untouched, the public is being studied rather than governing.

Direction. Each successful institution spreads capacity and reduces dependence on a separate caste. If emergency bodies, party offices, or managers become more permanent after every success, the transition is moving backward.

Not every person must decide every technical detail. A surgeon must be able to act during an operation. A grid coordinator must respond quickly to a failure. An engineer can rule out an unsafe design.

Technical authority belongs to the task. It does not create a general political right to govern.

The people affected need a real path into the purposes, priorities, and consequences of expert work. Experts state what is possible, what is dangerous, and what different choices require. Society decides which ends to pursue. Good institutions connect these forms of knowledge instead of pretending one can replace the others.

Force does not disappear from the question

No ruling class gives up command simply because a better constitution has been written.

The working class needs the organized capacity to defeat violent restoration, disarm the old repressive institutions, hold strategic systems, and defend the new social order. Pretending otherwise leaves existing capitalist force unanswered.

Revolutionary purpose does not make every revolutionary act harmless. The use of coercion raises political questions that cannot be delegated to armed specialists:

  • Who authorizes it?
  • What actual threat is being answered?
  • How are error and abuse exposed?
  • Can a decision be appealed or reviewed?
  • Does emergency authority shrink when the emergency ends?
  • Does the institution leave people more capable of governing?

State violence should not disappear inside words such as order and security. Revolutionary violence should not disappear inside words such as necessity and defense. The act, target, reason, consequence, and alternative all remain open to judgment.

The central question is not whether the new power can command. Any state can command. The question is what happens to power after it succeeds.

A power proved by its own disappearance

The transition begins with a working class able to rule. It develops by abolishing the social relations that make classes possible.

Capitalist property is expropriated. Production for exchange gives way to production for use. Common provision expands. The division between those who decide and those who carry out decisions contracts. Education and shorter necessary labour give more people time and ability to administer common affairs. International revolution removes the world market and hostile states that continually reproduce coercive pressure.

Through this movement, the Commonalty absorbs useful public functions while the state’s separate coercive character loses its social basis.

The result is not a society without organization, rules, expertise, disagreement, or responsibility. It is a society in which these no longer belong to a class power standing above everyone else.

Proletarian Self-Governance is therefore measured in two directions at once:

  • Can working people defeat the powers that keep them subordinate?
  • Does every victory increase their capacity to govern without a separate ruler?

If the first answer is no, capitalist power survives.

If the second answer is no, the transition has changed its rulers without overcoming rule.

Source notes

Primary texts

  • Karl Marx, letter to Joseph Weydemeyer, March 5, 1852, on class struggle, proletarian dictatorship, and its transition to a classless society.
  • Karl Marx, The Civil War in France and the first and second drafts, on the Commune and the ready-made state machinery.
  • Karl Marx, Critique of the Gotha Programme, on the political transition and the development of communist society.
  • Friedrich Engels, letter to August Bebel, March 18 to 28, 1875, including his proposal to use Gemeinwesen, or Commonalty, in place of the “free state.”

Further study

  • V. I. Lenin, The State and Revolution, especially Chapters III and V on the Commune and the transition.
  • Rosa Luxemburg, The Russian Revolution, on socialist democracy, mass political life, and the dangers of rule by a small circle.
  • Anton Pannekoek, Workers’ Councils, on councils as organs of working-class self-activity.
