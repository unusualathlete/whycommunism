07Proletarian Self-Governance

Freedom Needs a Place to Live

At 4:45 in the morning, a hospital cleaner wakes before her children.

She prepares breakfast, checks her father’s medicine, and walks to a bus that may or may not arrive. At the hospital, she cleans rooms where infection can kill. Her job is necessary, but the schedule is set far above her. By the time a neighborhood meeting begins that evening, she has been awake for fifteen hours. The notice says everyone is welcome.

On paper, she has a voice.

In life, the door is almost closed.

A social revolution is the work of opening that door. It changes the hours, spaces, institutions, and relationships through which people become able to govern. Public ownership gives society new power. Social revolution makes that power live in ordinary people.

Before dawn: who carries the day?

Every society must produce people as well as things.

Children have to be raised. Food has to be prepared. Homes have to be kept habitable. Sick, disabled, and older people need care. Language, knowledge, health, trust, and the ability to work have to be renewed.

This is social reproduction: the labor through which human beings and social life are sustained across days and generations.

Capitalism depends on it while treating much of it as somebody’s private problem. Care is pushed into separate households, assigned mainly to women, underpaid when sold, and taken for granted when unpaid. The wage arrives at the workplace as if the worker had produced herself overnight.

Economic ownership can change while this burden survives. A publicly owned hospital will still reproduce inequality if women perform two shifts, one paid and one hidden at home. A workers’ council remains formally democratic if caregivers are too tired to enter it.

Socialized childcare makes care a common responsibility. It means skilled and well-supported caregivers, safe and beautiful places, broad access, family participation, and enough variety for children with different needs. The same social principle extends to elder care, disability support, communal meals, health, laundry, and other work that isolated households now repeat under pressure.

Common care does not abolish intimacy. It stops love from being punished with exhaustion.

At the bus stop: time and distance govern too

Political power has a geography.

A right located two hours away is a weak right. A clinic without an accessible route excludes before anyone reaches its door. A meeting held while buses have stopped already knows which voices it will hear.

Social architecture means organizing buildings, streets, transport, services, and public space around the social relations people want to cultivate.

Quality housing gives privacy, comfort, safety, and room to develop. Shared kitchens, gardens, workshops, childcare, libraries, and meeting rooms can make association easier without forcing constant communal life. Reliable public transportation connects work, care, education, culture, and political activity. Accessible design lets disabled people enter as participants rather than special cases.

The physical world teaches. A city of locked compounds, highways, distant workplaces, and isolated houses trains separation. A city with safe public rooms, frequent transport, nearby care, and common workshops lets cooperation become ordinary.

Architecture cannot create communism by itself. It can make human encounter possible or spend every day obstructing it.

Imagine housing built from private rooms and adaptable living spaces arranged around shared seating, gardens, childcare, kitchens, laundry, libraries, workshops, and meeting rooms. Residents can combine or separate spaces as relationships and needs change. Children help decide the places where they learn and play. Frequent public transport reduces the labor lost to travel and connects these communities without designing life around private cars.

This is a direction, not a universal floor plan. Climate, ecology, disability, culture, density, and personal desire will produce different forms. The common principle is that residents possess social power over the spaces that shape them.

At work: dignity is more than ownership

Our cleaner’s labor protects every patient who enters the room after her. Yet class society often treats the surgeon as a mind and the cleaner as a pair of hands.

A social revolution attacks this division between intellectual and manual labor. Workers need access to the knowledge surrounding their activity. Specialists need contact with practical intelligence that professional hierarchy has trained them to ignore.

This does not mean pretending every person can perform surgery tomorrow. It means that expertise stops becoming a social rank. Cleaners can study infection control, participate in hospital planning, and develop new capacities. Doctors share necessary care work and answer to the people their decisions affect. Education continues throughout life, and movement among activities becomes possible.

Equal prestige for every occupation would leave the deeper confinement untouched. The social revolution ends a society that traps a whole person inside one narrow function.

At school: learning how to act

School often gives children their first long experience of an institution they cannot control. Bells divide time. Adults set tasks. Scores return as judgments about intelligence. Curiosity survives, but often in spite of the structure.

Revolutionary pedagogy joins knowledge to practice, cooperation, criticism, and self-direction. A student studying water can learn chemistry, trace the local supply, test samples, hear from treatment workers, examine who receives clean water first, and present findings to a public body. The lesson develops scientific knowledge and the confidence to use it socially.

Students should learn how to investigate a claim, repair an object, understand a budget, care for another person, recognize propaganda, regulate emotion, disagree without submission, and continue learning without waiting for an authority to assign the next step.

This is political education in its richest sense. It develops people capable of self-government.

Education becomes propaganda in the worst sense when official conclusions replace inquiry. A revolution that teaches children to repeat the correct answer while fearing the wrong question is training a bureaucracy.

In the evening: culture is made in practice

Capitalism leaves habits inside people. Competition, racism, patriarchy, nationalism, ableism, obedience, and consumer status do not disappear when property changes hands.

A cultural revolution is the transformation of these habits, values, and institutions through new social activity and deliberate struggle.

When care becomes common, gender loses part of its old assignment. When workers direct production, obedience stops appearing as the natural shape of labor. When public knowledge opens, expertise loses some of its mystery. When art and media no longer depend on owners and advertisers, more people can describe their own lives.

Culture also needs argument. People can carry domination into new institutions without noticing it. Journalism, art, education, public criticism, and movements of oppressed people make hidden relations visible.

Material domination is not left to voluntary persuasion. Organized racist violence, exclusion from housing or work, denial of reproductive freedom, and discrimination in public services require direct protection, enforceable rights, and the use of common power against those imposing harm.

Coercive conformity defeats this work. Humiliation does not teach freedom. A censor can silence a racist sentence while leaving the material hierarchy that produced it untouched. Persecution may create obedience to new language while preserving old domination underneath.

The aim is a society capable of changing its mind because people can examine their life together.

At the meeting: a right becomes a capacity

Now imagine the cleaner’s day again.

Necessary working time has been shortened. Frequent public transport brings her home. Her father receives dignified community care. Her children choose among excellent childcare, family time, play, and study. The neighborhood meeting is nearby, accessible, and partly held during recognized social time. The hospital’s budgets and infection data are public. Her experience of cleaning is understood as knowledge about health.

She may still decide not to attend. Freedom includes that choice.

The difference is that absence is no longer manufactured by exhaustion.

A formal right says A material capacity requires
You may speak time, safety, information, and a place where speech can matter
You may leave an abusive job or home food, housing, care, income security, and protection
You may study teachers, books, tools, health, rest, and freedom from ruin
You may participate politically shorter work, transport, childcare, accessibility, and understandable institutions
You may create free time, materials, education, space, and access to an audience

Social revolution turns permission into power.

Science belongs to the common future

Knowledge is also a social power. Under capitalism, research follows patents, military budgets, corporate secrecy, prestige, and the ability to pay.

Common research would answer urgent needs such as disease, clean energy, disability technology, ecological repair, and reduced necessary labor. It must also protect inquiry whose usefulness nobody can prove in advance. Number theory, astronomy, particle physics, theoretical biology, and foundational computing have often changed distant fields years later.

Freedom of research should extend beyond an academic caste. Public laboratories, open data, shared equipment, free publishing, and lifelong education can widen the general intellect, society’s accumulated scientific and practical knowledge.

There will still be disputes about risk, resources, privacy, and purpose. Experts can explain what is possible and what danger follows. They cannot privately decide which future everyone should inhabit.

What the revolution is really changing

The social revolution does not manufacture one approved personality, family, culture, or way of living. It removes the material compulsion that forces millions of different people through a few narrow forms.

People may choose private homes or rich communal life, family bonds or new forms of association, specialist work or many activities. Equality does not require sameness. Equity gives different people the support needed to participate with dignity.

At 4:45 in the morning, freedom can sound very far away. Yet that hour contains the whole question. Who wakes first? Who carries the care? Who controls the bus? Who has energy left to decide? Whose knowledge counts?

A revolution becomes social when its answer can be felt in the shape of an ordinary day.

Freedom needs time.

Freedom needs care.

Freedom needs a place to live.

Source notes

Primary texts

  • Karl Marx and Friedrich Engels, The German Ideology, Part I, written 1845 to 1846, on the production of material life, social relations, and human development.
  • Karl Marx and Friedrich Engels, Manifesto of the Communist Party, 1848, on family, education, class relations, and the free development of each and all.
  • Friedrich Engels, Anti-Dühring, Part III, chapter 5, “State, Family, Education”, 1877, on education, the division of labor, and social control of production.
  • Friedrich Engels, The Origin of the Family, Private Property and the State, 1884, on family forms, property, women’s oppression, and the material development of social institutions.

Further study

  • Alexandra Kollontai, “Communism and the Family”, 1920, on common care, domestic labor, and the transformation of family relations.
  • Lise Vogel, Marxism and the Oppression of Women: Toward a Unitary Theory, 1983, on the reproduction of labor power and women’s oppression.
  • Paulo Freire, Pedagogy of the Oppressed, 1970 English edition, on education as dialogue, inquiry, and collective transformation.
  • Marta Harnecker, Rebuilding the Left, 2007, especially the discussion of popular protagonism and institutions that develop people’s capacities.
