WHY COMMUNISM? GUIDE · Proletarian Self-Governance
04Proletarian Self-Governance

Power Where Life Happens

The people who keep a city alive already know much of what no mayor can know.

A line worker knows which machine is being held together by improvised repairs. A nurse knows why the official staffing plan fails at three in the morning. A bus driver knows where the timetable lies. A parent knows that the new clinic is useless if the last bus leaves before appointments end.

This knowledge is scattered across society. Capitalism gathers it into reports, filters it through managers, and returns it as orders. Proletarian Self-Governance begins by turning that relation around.

Working-class power is real when working people can organize what they know, decide what should be done, coordinate with others, and correct the institutions acting in their name. A government that speaks for workers while leaving them at the receiving end of decisions is still governing workers as an object.

Three organs, three kinds of common life

Self-government does not require one universal meeting that decides everything. Different parts of life produce different organs.

Workers’ council

  • Rooted in a workplace, service, or field of production.
  • Governs the labor process, safety, capacity, maintenance, local organization, and workplace delegation.
  • Connects that work to supply, distribution, regional plans, users, and ecological limits.

Commune

  • Rooted in a locality and the people living within it.
  • Governs housing, local services, public space, care, neighborhood priorities, and communal institutions.
  • Connects local life to production, watersheds, transport, health networks, and neighboring communes.

Federated body

  • Rooted in several councils, communes, or regions.
  • Governs shared standards, large infrastructure, resource balancing, and wider plans.
  • Remains connected to the organized bodies that give it authority and carry out the work.

These forms overlap because human life overlaps. Hospital workers belong to a workplace body and live in communities. A commune needs information from farms and factories. A railway runs through many localities but works only as one connected system.

An organ of self-government is any durable institution through which people exercise common power: a council, commune, service assembly, cooperative association, planning body, care network, or another form built for a real function.

The name proves nothing. A council that receives orders without the power to alter them is scenery.

The workers’ council

A workers’ council lets the people performing connected labor govern that labor together.

Take a distribution warehouse. Under capitalist management, scanners measure every movement. Managers know the target. Workers know the floor. The knowledge meets mainly when something goes wrong.

A council changes the direction of command. Workers can determine the pace and arrangement of labor, stop unsafe activity, report actual capacity, choose coordinators, examine inventory, and join their knowledge to the wider distribution plan. Technical managers may remain, but their authority belongs to the task and remains answerable to the workers and the social purpose of the service.

This is more than workplace democracy understood as a nicer management style. The council participates in social power. It helps decide how the warehouse fits food, medicine, transport, and common need.

Nor can each workplace become its own little owner. If a warehouse controls a scarce medicine as private group property, the workers there have replaced one exclusive interest with another. Associated production requires councils to govern their activity while joining it to the whole.

The commune

A commune is a self-governing association through which people directly manage the common affairs of a locality.

It begins from residence and shared life rather than one labor process. Housing, childcare, streets, disability access, sanitation, local health, culture, public safety, and the use of common space all meet there.

A city council does not become a commune by raising a red flag. In an ordinary municipality, people choose representatives and then return home while a permanent apparatus governs. In a living commune, the population remains organized. Residents deliberate in assemblies and other bodies, public workers report into the community, mandates travel outward, and people can initiate action without waiting for a remote office to notice them.

This continuing capacity is direct power. It means the people affected retain organized control before, during, and after a decision.

Direct power does not mean everyone spends every evening in a meeting. A society that requires permanent attendance would exhaust the people it claims to empower. Shorter working time, socialized care, accessible information, paid participation, reasonable delegation, and well-designed public space make self-government part of life without swallowing life.

Authority follows the real work

Self-government does not flatten every difference in knowledge or erase the need for decisive coordination.

When a train enters a dangerous section of track, the signal worker’s instruction binds. During surgery, responsibilities follow training. When a typhoon floods several districts, neighborhood teams, water engineers, transport workers, and a regional emergency body have different knowledge and different powers.

The question is not whether authority exists. The question is whether its scope follows the task, whether the people affected can understand and review it, and whether technical responsibility grows into a general right to rule.

A signal worker controls the signal because the railway requires one safe sequence. That does not give the signal worker power over housing policy. A surgeon directs part of an operation. That does not entitle surgeons to decide the whole health budget.

Task authority can be precise without becoming a caste.

The class also governs force and justice

Political power includes the power to defend a territory, investigate harm, restrain violence, and defeat organized restoration. Leaving these functions inside an inherited army, police hierarchy, or secret service would leave the old state standing at the center of the new society.

The standing army gives way to an armed people organized through local militias and a coordinated people’s army. Workers remain rooted in their communes and productive life. Training, logistics, intelligence, and command still require central organization and technical skill. Officers receive ordinary compensation, remain publicly accountable, and exercise authority within the military task. The army belongs to the class it defends instead of becoming a force that can be turned against it.

Communal justice changes the purpose of law. Property, debt, and obedience cease to organize the whole legal world. Communal courts, mediation bodies, and recallable judges protect people, repair harm, resolve conflict, and defend the revolution.

Armed conspiracy, sabotage, and organized attempts to restore capitalist rule are suppressed. Criticism, error, political disagreement, and refusal are not treated as the same thing. A secret police that can define its own enemies, hide its methods, and escape communal review would recreate an authority above the class.

Force becomes proletarian only when the proletariat can see it, direct it, and call it back.

A mandate carries power without giving it away

No commune can manufacture every medicine or rail component it needs. No workplace can decide alone how much electricity the region can use. Local organs must join wider ones.

A mandate is a defined responsibility or instruction carried by a delegate into that wider coordination. The sending body keeps the power to review, revise, or withdraw it.

Delegates do not all have to be selected in one way:

  • An election can express a broad political choice.
  • Rotation can spread knowledge and prevent permanent officeholding.
  • Sortition, which means selection by lot from an eligible group, can bring in people who would never become candidates.
  • A recallable appointment can fit a task requiring specific training.
  • A mixed system can combine public choice, qualification, rotation, and review.

Democracy is not hidden inside the selection device. It lives in the relation between the delegate and the organized people: clear responsibility, available information, continuing communication, ordinary compensation, public explanation, and removal when trust or performance fails.

A society made of connected societies

There is a shallow argument that every political problem must choose between centralism and decentralization. The real world refuses the choice.

A neighborhood clinic needs freedom to respond to its patients. A regional health network must coordinate laboratories, specialist care, training, medicine, and emergency reserves. A national system may need to move resources across regions. An international system may be necessary to contain a pandemic.

The scale of the organ should follow the scale of the relation.

    B --> C["Mandated delegates carry needs and capacities outward"]
    C --> D["Wider bodies coordinate what crosses boundaries"]
    D --> E["Decisions, reasons, and resources return"]
    E --> F["Local bodies implement, report, refuse danger, and correct"]
    F --> A

Text alternative: Knowledge begins among workers and residents. Their councils and communes decide local matters and send mandates into wider bodies. Wider bodies coordinate shared systems. Decisions and resources return for implementation. Local experience then corrects the next decision.

The arrows matter in every direction. If information travels up but power never returns, the center rules. If mandates travel out but no common decision binds, federation becomes correspondence. If local bodies cannot object, knowledge is wasted. If wider bodies cannot coordinate, connected systems break apart.

The Paris Commune of 1871 did not solve every part of this problem. It lived for just over two months under siege and civil war before the French army drowned it in blood. Marx treated it as a discovery because working people had begun to replace the old state hierarchy with their own political form: removable public servants, working bodies, communal organization, and federation.

The Commune is neither a sacred blueprint nor a decorative ancestor. It leaves a standard.

Power belongs where the knowledge, labor, and consequences live. Wider coordination is legitimate when it joins that power instead of confiscating it. The working class governs itself when no ministry, party, expert body, or heroic minority can remove the class from its own emancipation.

Nobody can hand self-government to people.

It exists in the act of governing.

Source notes

Primary texts

  • Karl Marx, The Civil War in France: First Draft, 1871, on the Commune as the reabsorption of state power by society and public servants acting under supervision.
  • Karl Marx, The Civil War in France, 1871, especially the account of the Commune’s working, removable, and federative form.
  • Karl Marx, Conspectus of Bakunin’s Statism and Anarchy, 1874, including Marx’s answer that the process begins with the self-government of the commune.
  • Friedrich Engels, introduction to Marx’s The Civil War in France, 1891, on dismantling the old repressive machinery and making deputies and officials recallable.
  • Friedrich Engels, The Origin of the Family, Private Property and the State, chapter IX, 1884, on the state’s separate public force, territorial organization, taxation, and officials standing above society.

Further study

  • Kristin Ross, Communal Luxury: The Political Imaginary of the Paris Commune, 2015, on communal form, federation, education, and the social imagination of 1871.
  • Donny Gluckstein, The Paris Commune: A Revolution in Democracy, 2006, on the Commune’s institutions, social composition, achievements, and limits.
