When a Staffing Fight Becomes a Fight for Power

At 2:00 a.m., one nurse is responsible for eleven patients.

Two need constant observation. One is confused and trying to leave. Another has just returned from surgery. The hospital’s staffing software calls the ward fully covered.

The nurses begin recording every unsafe shift. They demand another nurse at night.

What kind of struggle is this?

The answer changes as the object of the struggle changes.

First movement: one employer, one condition

The hospital workers organize to force their employer to improve staffing on the ward. They compare schedules, file grievances, refuse unpaid extensions, and threaten a withdrawal of labour.

This is an economistic struggle. It fights over the terms under which labour is sold and performed inside the existing relation.

The word does not mean trivial. A second nurse may prevent a death. A wage increase can keep a family housed. A shorter shift can give someone enough life to study, care, and organize.

Marx made this point in Value, Price and Profit in 1865. He opposed the claim that workers should abandon wage struggles because gains would simply disappear. Resistance is necessary. Without it, workers become degraded into what he called a mass of “broken wretches past salvation.”

He also warned that the wage struggle fights effects while leaving the wage system intact.

The hospital can meet the demand while preserving private command over staffing, budgets, and care. The workers have won more room inside the relation. They have not yet changed who rules it.

Second movement: the demand takes a general form

The records show that the unsafe ratio exists across the city. Nurses at several hospitals organize together. Patients and cleaners join them. They demand a binding staffing standard for every hospital and public control over compliance.

The conflict has become political-economic.

It remains rooted in work and material need, but its object is now general. The workers confront hospital owners, insurers, and the state as organized social powers. They are no longer bargaining only with one employer.

Marx explained this difference in an 1871 letter to Friedrich Bolte. A strike for shorter hours at one factory is economic. A movement for a general eight-hour law is political because the class tries to enforce its interest in a socially coercive form.

The staffing movement now asks questions that the first demand did not have to answer:

  • Who sets the standard?
  • Which hospitals must obey?
  • Where will trained workers and resources come from?
  • Can private owners close an unprofitable service?
  • How are patients and workers represented?
  • What power enforces the common rule?

The demand is still not communist by itself. A capitalist state can regulate staffing while preserving wage labour and private health systems. Yet the struggle has taught workers to act as a class across employers and to confront power in a general form.

Third movement: who governs care?

A deep crisis arrives. Private hospitals close wards, hoard supplies, and discharge patients who cannot pay. Workers occupy several facilities to keep them open. Neighborhood assemblies organize food, transport, and patient records. Hospital councils begin coordinating beds and staff across the region.

The struggle has become political in the full class sense when its object is social rule.

The question is no longer only what limit the state should impose on hospital owners. It is whether owners and the capitalist state retain the right to decide at all.

Political struggle joins the immediate need to the conquest and transformation of social power. Workers and communities must break the institutions that protect capitalist command, build their own organs, and reorganize care as a common function.

The night-shift problem has not disappeared. It has grown roots.

Character of struggle Immediate object Social reach What remains unresolved
Economistic Staffing on one ward or under one employer Workers directly involved Employer command and the wage relation
Political-economic A general staffing standard and power to enforce it Workers and users across institutions Capitalist ownership and state power
Political Which class governs health, production, and public authority The working class organized as a social force The whole transition toward classless common life

A struggle can move backward

The three movements are not guaranteed stages.

A citywide campaign can shrink into a private settlement at one hospital. A political organization can use revolutionary language while bargaining for seats inside the existing administration. An occupation can be isolated and defeated before it creates wider organs.

No demand carries its next form inside it like a seed following a biological schedule.

The movement changes through organization, political argument, altered conditions, and practical discovery. Workers learn that one employer is tied to an industry. They encounter the state. They find allies and enemies. They build institutions capable of acting at a new scale.

This is where the party has a distinct role. It brings the memory of earlier struggles, exposes the whole class relation, and argues for a direction beyond the settlement. It cannot manufacture the workers’ experience or perform the movement for them.

Immediate gains and the final aim

Two errors break the connection.

The first worships the immediate. It treats whatever workers currently demand as the whole content of class politics. This can trap a movement inside one trade, one nation, or one bargain.

The second treats immediate life as beneath revolutionary politics. It speaks of power while remaining absent from the struggles through which workers learn their own capacity.

Communists refuse both.

They fight for the extra nurse because a patient’s life matters now. They help the fight become general because isolated workers remain weak. They keep social power in view because no staffing formula can make health fully human while care remains subordinate to profit and class rule.

The real thread is not a more radical slogan. It is a growing capacity to understand, organize, coordinate, and govern.

At the beginning, the workers prove that the staffing model lies.

At the end, they must become capable of making the plan themselves.

Source notes

Primary texts

  • Karl Marx, Value, Price and Profit, address to the General Council of the First International in 1865, especially section 14 on the necessity and limit of wage struggles.
  • Karl Marx, letter to Friedrich Bolte, November 23, 1871. It distinguishes an economic movement against particular employers from a political movement that enforces a class interest in a general form.
  • Vladimir Lenin, “Where to Begin?”, 1901. Written against Russian Economism, it argues for organization able to connect local exposures and immediate demands to nationwide political struggle.
  • Amadeo Bordiga, “Party and Class Action”, 1921. It traces the movement from local and trade interests toward a general class objective while rejecting the idea that a party can command that movement into existence by will.

Further study

  • Vladimir Lenin, What Is to Be Done?, chapter III, 1902, on trade-union politics, political exposure, and the struggle over Economism.
  • Rosa Luxemburg, The Mass Strike, the Political Party and the Trade Unions, 1906. It studies how economic and political struggles crossed and transformed one another during the Russian Revolution of 1905.
