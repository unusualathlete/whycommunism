14Organizing and Learning

Prefiguration Without Illusion

A communist organization cannot move outside capitalism before it begins to fight capitalism.

It uses money. Its members have unequal free time. Law, employers, landlords, police, borders, and the market shape what it can do. Skills remain uneven. Urgency sometimes requires a clear technical command.

None of this gives the organization permission to reproduce every relation it claims it will abolish.

That is the contradiction of prefiguration: present organization should develop capacities and relationships needed for the future without pretending the future already exists inside it.

The impossible claim

A party is not a communist society in miniature.

It cannot abolish wage labour among its members while they still need wages to live. It cannot make all social wealth freely available. It cannot dissolve the state, end the world market, or remove every inequality inherited from family, disability, geography, education, and class.

A cooperative kitchen still buys food. A mutual-aid network may depend on donations. A political group relies on members whose available time was distributed unequally before they arrived.

Calling these projects complete communism would hide the relations surrounding them. It would also turn their real achievements into a fantasy.

The necessary refusal

An organization can still refuse to make those relations its principle of life.

Negative prefiguration begins there. It does not claim to have constructed communism. It actively resists relations that would train people for the opposite.

The organization cannot yet abolish It can refuse to normalize
money political office as a private career or route to material privilege
unequal knowledge secret knowledge used to make other members dependent
necessary expertise expertise becoming permanent authority over unrelated questions
unequal free time exhaustion treated as proof of loyalty and care work treated as somebody else’s problem
external repression internal coercion used to avoid political explanation
the whole social division of labour avoidable gatekeeping, mystified offices, and succession without teaching

The point is not moral purity. It is capacity.

An organization that hoards information teaches dependence. An organization that makes members guess what leaders want teaches servility. An organization that rewards intrigue teaches bourgeois politics even while reciting a communist programme.

Shared knowledge, open political disagreement, accountable authority, dependable conduct, care, education, and initiative prepare people for self-government.

The electrician should lead the electrical work

Prefiguration is often reduced to mechanical sameness. Everyone must rotate through every role, every meeting must decide everything, and expertise is treated as a disguised class claim.

That can become another way to destroy collective power.

An experienced electrician should direct dangerous electrical work. The authority comes from the activity and ends with it. Others should be able to learn, inspect the purpose, report a danger, and replace the person when competence or trust is lost. Technical direction does not grant political command over the kitchen, budget, membership, or programme.

The communist alternative to hierarchy is not a society in which nobody knows more than anybody else. It is a society in which developed powers become common wealth instead of private rule.

A kitchen can teach without becoming a kingdom

A neighborhood kitchen publishes its budget, provides translation, shares ordinary coordination, teaches food safety, and lets the people using it report what is missing. It meets a need and develops cooperation.

It also depends on a market, donated time, unequal skills, and food produced through relations it does not control. These limits are not a reason to dismiss the kitchen. They tell the kitchen what it is:

  • a form of provision;
  • a school of cooperation;
  • a place where practical knowledge can become common; and
  • a test of institutions that wider transformation may generalize.

The kitchen fails when it mistakes itself for the whole mode of production or turns its own survival into the final aim.

The party must prefigure its own disappearance

A communist party concentrates capacities that society does not yet possess equally: historical memory, programme, organization, political education, and international coordination.

It should use that concentration to generalize the capacities, not protect their scarcity.

This gives party authority a direction and a limit. As communist understanding, administrative competence, and the ability to coordinate common life become ordinary social powers, the party’s separate function should contract. Its success cannot be measured by how permanently society depends on it.

An organization that plans to rule forever is not preparing people for a classless society.

Four questions for any present form

Before praising an organization as prefigurative, ask:

  1. Which capacity for common self-government does this practice develop?
  2. Which capitalist relation can it only manage because it cannot yet abolish it?
  3. Which domination could it refuse now but has chosen to reproduce?
  4. Does the form prepare its function to become common, or make everyone permanently dependent on the form?

Means and ends are not identical. They are also not strangers.

Source notes

Primary texts

  • Karl Marx, Comments on James Mill, on human activity becoming mutual affirmation rather than alienated exchange.
  • Karl Marx, The Civil War in France, on working-class institutions that begin breaking the separation of public power from society.
  • Karl Marx, Critique of the Gotha Programme, on the limits inherited by the lower phase of communist society and the development beyond them.
  • Karl Marx, Provisional Rules of the International Working Men’s Association, on the emancipation of the working class being conquered by the working class itself.

Further study

  • Il Partito Comunista, The Party Does Not Arise from “Circles” (1980), on the relation among programme, activity, organization, discipline, and party form.
