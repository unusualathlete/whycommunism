02Organizing and Learning

Two Powers the Working Class Needs

A union meeting can include a communist, a social democrat, a religious conservative, and someone who wants nothing more than tomorrow’s shift to be safer. They can still stop work together.

A communist party cannot be built on that kind of limited agreement. Its members unite around a programme for abolishing capitalism and class society.

The difference is not a ladder with the party above and the union below. It is a difference of function.

The political party Economic and class organizations
Unites around a communist programme Unite around a shared material position, need, or struggle
Preserves historical memory and the final aim Gather workers directly where exploitation and social life are organized
Develops theory, strategy, education, and international coordination Exercise mass power through strikes, councils, assemblies, occupations, bargaining, and administration
Selects members by political commitment Often include workers with many political views
Proposes a general direction Produce knowledge and initiative no political minority can manufacture
Must lead through earned confidence Must remain organs of the class, not departments of a party

Each form possesses something the other cannot replace.

Why the difference became a fight

After the Russian Revolution, communists argued fiercely over which organizations expressed working-class power. At the Second Congress of the Communist International in 1920, its theses placed the party first, workers’ councils second, and unions third.

These arguments answered real failures. Trade unions had supported the First World War and tied workers to national states. Reformist parties had treated parliament and collective bargaining as the horizon of the movement. A revolution could not be reduced to a larger wage negotiation.

But the opposite danger also became real. If a party declares that it alone embodies class consciousness, it can treat workers’ councils as transmission belts for decisions already made elsewhere. The council name survives while the council’s power dies.

Council communists forced this contradiction into view. A party can clarify aims and join struggles, but workers themselves must decide and act through their own organizations.

The answer is not to choose political organization or class organization. The working class needs both, related without fusion.

Four possible relations

1. Cooperation

Party members work inside unions, councils, tenant organizations, strike committees, and other broad bodies. They do the ordinary work, bring political arguments, connect struggles, and help people prepare.

The economic organization gives the party access to living class experience. The party gives the struggle memory, wider connections, and a view beyond the immediate settlement.

Neither arrives complete. Cooperation changes both.

2. Independence

The party keeps its programme, publications, internal discipline, and freedom to criticize. The wider organization keeps its own decision-making, membership, mandate, and right to reject the party’s proposal.

Independence protects the relation.

Without party independence, the communist programme dissolves into whatever demand currently commands a majority. Without class-organizational independence, workers become an audience for a political apparatus.

Political independence is not neutrality. A union decision can strengthen nationalism, defend one protected trade against migrants, or accept layoffs for another workplace. Communists should fight those positions openly. They cannot solve them by secretly possessing the union.

3. Leadership

Leadership is a relation of trust proved in practice.

A party leads when its account of the situation is clearer, its warning arrives before the danger, its members are reliable under pressure, and its proposal helps workers act with greater unity and power. It leads because people find that direction true and useful.

This leadership must be won again as conditions change. Past correctness is not a permanent license.

A workers’ council may adopt a party proposal. That does not make the council a party organ. It means the party won an argument in the council. The council retains the power to review the result, hear another argument, and change course.

4. Substitution

Substitution begins when the party performs the class’s political activity in its place.

The signs are concrete:

  • council decisions require prior party approval;
  • union officers receive instructions through a hidden chain of command;
  • party membership becomes a route to employment or office;
  • disagreement inside a mass organization is treated as disloyalty to the revolution;
  • the party controls appointments, armed bodies, administration, and public communication;
  • workers are mobilized to carry decisions they did not make.

At that point the party no longer leads a class organization. It owns one.

The loss cuts both ways. The council becomes less capable of governing, and the party loses the independent information that could correct it. Apparent unity produces practical blindness.

A warehouse dispute

Management introduces a tracking system that raises the pace and punishes workers for delays caused by broken equipment.

The union can document penalties, compare departments, protect organizers, and coordinate a refusal of the new quota. Its broad membership is a source of power.

Communists inside the struggle can show how surveillance, speedup, subcontracting, and unemployment reinforce one another across the logistics industry. They can connect the warehouse to drivers and port workers, argue against sacrificing temporary workers, and place workers’ control of production on the horizon.

Suppose the union wins the removal of the worst penalties. That is a real victory.

The party should neither dismiss it as “only economic” nor pretend capitalism has been overcome. It helps workers preserve the organization, draw out the political lesson, and prepare for the next conflict. If its members proved useful, trust grows.

Now suppose the party orders a walkout that the workers have not discussed, chiefly to display the party’s militancy. A correct general aim does not rescue a substituted action. The party has treated the workers as material for its own performance.

The difference between leadership and substitution is visible in what remains afterward. Leadership leaves workers more organized, knowledgeable, and able to decide. Substitution leaves them waiting for the next instruction.

Councils make the issue sharper

A union normally organizes workers within capitalist production. A workers’ council can emerge as an organ of struggle across workplaces and, in a revolutionary situation, become part of working-class rule.

That broader power makes independence more important, not less.

Party members may be trusted delegates. In a council role, they answer to the council’s mandate and recall procedures. Party discipline cannot silently overrule the class organ that gave them public responsibility.

The party must retain the freedom to argue that a council is making a disastrous decision. The council must retain the power to decide. The contradiction is resolved through political struggle, evidence, organization, and consequences, not by declaring one side infallible.

The communist party carries the historical horizon. Economic and class organizations carry the direct self-activity through which that horizon can become real.

One without the other loses its way. Fused together, both lose their freedom.

Source notes

Primary texts

  • Karl Marx and Friedrich Engels, Manifesto of the Communist Party, section II, 1848. It distinguishes communists from the class as a whole while denying that they possess separate interests.
  • Communist International, “Theses on the Role of the Communist Party in the Proletarian Revolution”, adopted by the Second Congress in 1920. It states the strongest early Comintern case for party leadership over the various forms of proletarian organization.
  • Amadeo Bordiga, “Party and Class”, 1921, and “Party and Class Action”, 1921. Together they define the party as a school of political thought and an organization of struggle, then examine its changing relation to the mass of the class.

Further study

  • Anton Pannekoek, “The Party and Class”, published 1941. It presents the council-communist challenge to party rule and insists that working people think, decide, and act through their own class organs.
  • Onorato Damen, “Amadeo Bordiga: Beyond the Myth and the Rhetoric”, 1970. It defends the party’s permanent political function while criticizing attempts to turn tactical or organizational formulas into myths.
