01Organizing and Learning

How a Movement Learns to Act

On September 28, 1864, workers and political exiles from several countries crowded into St. Martin’s Hall in London. They did not share one doctrine, one trade, or one national struggle. British unionists, French mutualists, Italian republicans, Polish nationalists, and German communists arrived with different histories and purposes.

What they built became the International Working Men’s Association.

Its first rules began from a sentence that still decides the whole question: working-class emancipation must be conquered by workers themselves. Yet the same rules called for durable links among local societies, national bodies, and an international council. Self-emancipation did not mean that every struggle should remain alone. It meant that coordination had to become the workers’ own power.

That tension is the beginning of proletarian organization. People must act for themselves, but no one workplace, neighborhood, or individual can see the whole field. A movement needs ways to preserve memory, compare struggles, develop a programme, prepare action, and learn from what happens.

Text alternative: Class struggle produces organization. Organization gathers experience. Political education connects that experience to a communist programme. Strategy turns programme into coordinated action. The results change both conditions and participants, then return as knowledge that can correct organization, education, and strategy.

This is not a staircase with a group of theorists waiting at the top. Every part changes every other part.

A strike may reveal a hidden supply chain. A failed tenant campaign may expose a legal weakness and an internal failure to listen. A successful safety method in one warehouse may travel across an industry. Theory lets people recognize the larger relation. Practice tests whether the recognition was true.

The movement learns by acting, and acts differently because it has learned.

Organization is more than being together

People cooperate constantly under capitalism. They run hospitals, ports, schools, data centers, farms, households, and transport systems. Capital already brings millions of workers into connected activity, then places the purpose and product of that activity outside their control.

Proletarian organization begins when workers turn connection into an independent power. They identify common interests, preserve what they learn, divide work consciously, coordinate across boundaries, and act against the class relation that keeps them subordinate.

Durability matters. Anger can gather a crowd. Organization allows people to return tomorrow with records, responsibilities, relationships, and a better understanding of the opponent.

Yet durability can become a trap. An office built to serve a struggle may begin serving its own survival. A leadership chosen for knowledge may start hiding information that threatens its position. A structure may outlive the function that justified it.

No organizational form is revolutionary by its shape alone. Its real test is what capacities it develops, whose activity it enlarges, how it receives correction, and which social purpose it serves.

The programme carries the horizon

A programme is not a campaign menu. It is a connected account of capitalism, the class capable of abolishing it, the transition through proletarian self-government, and the classless society that gives the movement its direction.

Immediate pressure pulls every struggle toward the nearest settlement. A wage increase, a canceled eviction, or a safer shift can be worth winning. None abolishes the relation that keeps wages, rent, and managerial command in place.

The programme holds the whole movement inside the immediate fight. It asks:

  • Did this action strengthen workers’ independent organization?
  • Did it unite or divide the class?
  • Did it reveal more of the system people face?
  • Did it enlarge the capacity to govern?
  • Did it preserve the communist aim?

The party gives this programme a durable political organization. It concentrates historical memory, theory, education, coordination, strategy, and preparation. It exists because these capacities develop unevenly.

That concentration is necessary. Its purpose is to spread what it concentrates.

If the party turns its knowledge into a title to rule, councils and unions become its departments, criticism becomes disobedience, and working-class self-emancipation disappears. The party leads by explaining reality, preparing well, organizing successfully, and earning trust. Trust can grow, shrink, and be lost.

Economic organizations begin from common need

Workers also create unions, strike committees, councils, tenants’ associations, mutual-aid networks, and other organizations around a direct material relation.

Their breadth is a strength. People who disagree about the whole future can still resist a wage cut or stop an eviction together. Through that action they discover powers and conflicts that no lecture could supply in advance.

These organizations do not become politically neutral because they begin from an immediate need. A union can protect one trade at the expense of migrants. A council can defend a factory while leaving exchange and wage labour untouched. Immediate organization can grow toward class-wide power or settle into a protected corner inside capitalism.

The party and these wider organizations therefore need one another without becoming the same thing. The party brings programme, memory, and international direction. Economic and class organizations bring mass participation, situated knowledge, and the direct power of workers acting where social life is produced.

The relation should raise both.

Pedagogy is how capacity travels

Political pedagogy is the organized process through which people turn experience into shared understanding and understanding into a greater ability to act.

It includes study, explanation, debate, practical work, reflection, criticism, and the redesign of activity. It cannot be reduced to a knowledgeable speaker giving correct answers to a silent room.

Political education fails when knowledge moves only one way. An organization that cannot learn from the people whose activity it hopes to coordinate will mistake its own reports for reality. The educator must remain answerable to the world that learners are investigating and changing.

This does not mean that every opinion is equally true. A good teacher knows something worth teaching. Historical study can expose an error that local experience alone cannot see. The answer is neither worship of expertise nor contempt for it. Knowledge must be testable, teachable, answerable, and capable of becoming a common power.

The nurse who understands an infection pathway, the mechanic who hears a failing bearing, the historian who recognizes an old tactical trap, and the organizer who knows how fear moves through a workplace each hold part of a situation. Pedagogy lets those parts meet without pretending they were identical.

Centralism means coordination, not possession

A movement spread across workplaces and countries needs common decisions. Centralism names the coordination of communist activity around a shared programme and strategy. It does not mean sending every decision upward or concentrating every power in one office.

The center can compare fields, allocate shared resources, and see relations no local group can see. Local organs know conditions the center can easily flatten. Information must travel upward, downward, and sideways. Initiative must exist where knowledge and consequences exist.

Common coordination and local initiative have to be designed together. Local bodies need routes to one another as well as to the center. Wider decisions need explanation on the way back. Unity and autonomy are relations to build, not words that solve one another.

Feedback keeps the movement alive

Feedback is information about the result of an action returning to people who can change what happens next.

It may arrive as a report, a refusal, a falling attendance count, an unexpected victory, a criticism from below, or a method that quietly works better. If bad news cannot travel, leadership loses contact with the world. If no one is responsible for acting on the news, criticism becomes ceremony.

The deepest purpose of feedback is not organizational survival. It is fidelity to the communist aim. Sometimes the right correction is to change a tactic. Sometimes it is to rebuild a relationship. Sometimes an office, rule, or even an organization has finished its historical use.

Communist organization prepares its own transformation. The party succeeds as political understanding and organizing capacity become ordinary social powers. Education succeeds when the student can investigate and teach. Leadership succeeds when others can act with greater independence.

The movement does not become conscious because one part thinks for all the others. It becomes conscious when divided knowledge, memory, purpose, and action form a common intelligence that people can govern.

That is organization at its highest: not the command of living people by a perfected machine, but their growing ability to make history together.

Source notes

Primary texts

  • Karl Marx and Friedrich Engels, Manifesto of the Communist Party, section II, 1848. It defines communists as the advanced and resolute section of the proletariat with no interests separate from the class as a whole.
  • Karl Marx, General Rules of the International Working Men’s Association, revised 1871 from the 1864 Provisional Rules. It joins self-emancipation, international combination, national coordination, local communication, and organizational independence.
  • Amadeo Bordiga, “Party and Class Action”, 1921. It develops the party as a factor of consciousness and coordinated will while warning that no fixed numerical ratio can define its relation to the class.
  • Il Partito Comunista, The Party Does Not Arise from “Circles”, 1980. It examines the unity of programme, organization, activity, and discipline, as well as the danger of a party dissolving into disconnected circles.

Further study

  • Jacques Camatte, “Origin and Function of the Party Form”, 1961, with a 1974 postface. It follows the relation among party, programme, historical conditions, and human community.
  • Anton Pannekoek, “The Party and Class”, published 1941. It argues that workers must think, decide, and act through their own class organizations rather than transfer emancipation to a party.
