Strategy Is a Hypothesis, Not a Prophecy

In October 1919, the Italian Socialist Party met in Bologna while strikes, land occupations, food riots, and factory organization were spreading across Italy. The two years became known as the Biennio Rosso, the Red Two Years.

The revolutionary left was strong inside the party. It had an abstentionist programme against parliamentary corruption and reformism. Yet the Communist Party of Italy was not founded until the Livorno Congress in January 1921.

By then the balance had changed. Factory occupations had ebbed. Fascist squads were attacking workers’ halls, cooperatives, organizers, and socialist municipalities.

Decades later, Onorato Damen argued that the revolutionary left had misread the strategic moment. It treated abstention from parliament too much like a platform and waited too long to form an independent communist party. A tactic that clarified the left could not substitute for a strategy of party formation while the movement was still rising.

Whether every part of Damen’s judgment is accepted or not, the problem is exact: a correct principle and a defensible tactic do not choose the right sequence by themselves.

That is the work of strategy.

Three different kinds of direction

Level The question it answers How often it should change
Programme What social relation are we fighting, which class can abolish it, and toward what form of life? Only through deep theoretical and historical correction
Strategy What broad route can advance that programme in this period and balance of forces? When the period, capacities, or tested assumptions change
Tactic What limited action should be taken in this concrete situation? As the situation and evidence require

The programme is not a distant decoration. Strategy is not clever maneuver detached from it. Tactics are not a bag of approved names.

Each level gives the next one purpose and limits. Information also returns upward. A series of tactical failures may expose a false strategic assumption. A historical transformation may force the programme’s language to become more exact.

Write strategy as a hypothesis

A strategic hypothesis states how present forces might be developed toward a political objective and why that path appears possible.

Its basic form is:

WORKED TEMPLATE

If these classes and organizations act through this broad sequence, under these conditions, then this capacity can grow and this relation of power can be broken, because these material tendencies make the path possible.

The sentence must be filled with evidence.

For the Italian revolutionary left in 1919, a hypothesis might have asked:

  • Was the Socialist Party still a usable field for forming a communist party, or had its reformist apparatus become a barrier?
  • Were factory councils and strikes producing a durable class organization?
  • How quickly was fascist reaction developing?
  • Which militants, publications, local branches, and international relations already existed?
  • Would abstentionism unite a revolutionary party or narrow its platform?
  • What event would prove that waiting had become more dangerous than splitting?

A prophecy announces what history will do. A hypothesis states what the organization thinks is happening, what it plans to develop, and what evidence could prove it wrong.

Objective and subjective conditions

Objective conditions are material relations that cannot be changed simply by deciding to think differently:

  • ownership and development of productive forces;
  • the composition and concentration of classes;
  • crisis, war, ecology, and the world market;
  • law, state capacity, and repression;
  • transport, communication, food, energy, and geography;
  • divisions inside the ruling class.

Subjective conditions are organized human capacities:

  • political understanding;
  • trust and morale;
  • parties, unions, councils, and networks;
  • tested leadership;
  • practical skill and discipline;
  • communication and material preparation;
  • memory of victory and defeat.

Subjective does not mean imaginary. A printing network exists. A trained organizer exists. So does fear, confusion, and a broken relationship.

Objective conditions do not act without people. Subjective capacity cannot wish its environment away. Strategy studies their movement together.

The balance of forces has several dimensions

The balance of class forces is not a headcount.

One class may own the state and media but be divided by crisis. Workers may be numerous yet separated by contract, citizenship, industry, and distance. A small organization may have a correct analysis but no social reach. A mass organization may command loyalty while leading the class into a dead end.

A strategic assessment should examine at least:

  1. material leverage: what activity can be stopped, redirected, or governed;
  2. organization: what can endure beyond one explosion;
  3. political clarity: what people understand about the conflict and its horizon;
  4. legitimacy and trust: whose direction people will test in action;
  5. state and capitalist capacity: what repression, concession, and division the ruling class can use;
  6. international relation: what support, isolation, and external pressure shape the field;
  7. time: which side is developing faster and what window may close.

Exaggerating strength produces adventure. Treating weakness as eternal produces surrender.

A strategic record should include its own possible defeat

A serious strategy records:

The claim. What broad development is expected?

The mechanism. Through what organizations, struggles, and changes in capacity?

The dependencies. What must be true for the path to remain possible?

The warning signs. What evidence shows that the route is closing?

The review point. When and by whom will the assessment be reconsidered?

The fallback. What knowledge, people, and organization must be preserved if the opening collapses?

This does not make politics predictable. It makes judgment answerable.

Revision is not betrayal

An organization can cling to a failed strategy because leaders fear admitting error, members built identities around it, or the apparatus depends on its continuation.

That is how a means becomes an end.

Materialist revision asks whether the mechanism still exists. It does not rewrite yesterday’s expectations to make leadership appear permanently correct. Nor does it abandon principles each time a route becomes difficult.

The answer is neither a timetable written once nor an organization that changes line with every headline. Strategy needs a stable programme, an explicit account of conditions, disciplined action, and feedback strong enough to break a mistaken expectation.

History does not hand the movement a route.

It presents a field that people can study, enter, alter, and lose.

Strategy is the wager made responsible to that field.

Source notes

Primary texts

  • Georg Lukács, “Tactics and Ethics”, 1919. It defines tactics as the link between the ultimate objective and reality and confronts the responsibility involved in political action under incomplete knowledge.
  • Communist Party of Italy, Theses of Rome, 1922. Its theses on tactics place particular actions within programme, class conditions, international direction, and the danger of opportunist improvisation.
  • Onorato Damen, “Amadeo Bordiga: Beyond the Myth and the Rhetoric”, 1970. Its discussion of Bologna, Livorno, abstentionism, party formation, and fascist reaction provides the historical strategic dispute at the center of this Guide.

Further study

  • Amadeo Bordiga, “Party and Class Action”, May 1921, on the party, mass movement, revolutionary conditions, degeneration, and the rejection of a fixed numerical measure of political capacity.
  • International Communist Party, The Communist Party in the Tradition of the Left, part V, “The Party’s Tactics”, 1974, on tactical plans, class contact, and the argument against both pure propaganda and improvisation.
