13Organizing and Learning

Knowing Is Not Yet Willing

A worker can know that the schedule is dangerous, that the company can afford another person, and that everyone becomes safer if the shift refuses the work.

She may still stay silent.

Perhaps she has children to support. Perhaps the last person who complained lost every good shift. Perhaps her coworkers agree in private but will not look at one another when the supervisor arrives. Perhaps she has learned, through a thousand ordinary defeats, that power belongs to money, management, and the state.

There is no mystery here. Understanding exploitation does not automatically produce the confidence, relationships, habits, and organized strength required to confront it.

Class consciousness is not a fact stored inside an individual head. It is the working class recognizing its common position, its historical power, and its capacity to act as a class.

Political will is that recognition becoming durable action.

Neither appears through information alone.

The world rehearses capitalism

Everyday life trains people before any communist meeting begins.

At work, survival depends on obeying a purpose somebody else owns. In the market, needs appear as prices. In politics, social power appears as an office occupied by specialists. Even cooperation returns as the achievement of a company, a brand, or a manager.

Marx once described money as a “real God” because the human act of connecting products and needs confronts people as the power of a thing outside them. The phrase is vivid, but the relation is material. Food may exist while a person without money goes hungry. A needed factory may close because it cannot produce profit. Human beings made the system, yet the system tells human beings what is possible.

This is alienation at the level of will. Our common power is real, but we meet it in an estranged form.

A lecture can explain the relation. It cannot, by itself, give people practical command over it.

Consciousness grows through life-activity

People change while changing the world.

The worker who compares schedules with a coworker has already broken a little of her enforced isolation. The group that maps injuries begins to see a pattern management called accidental. The first shared refusal teaches what a private opinion could not: who stands firm, where the threat lands, what support is missing, and what collective power feels like.

This does not mean action first and thought later. Blind activity can repeat the very relations it opposes. Theory gives action memory, names the structure, and keeps an immediate victory connected to the final aim.

The movement is reciprocal:

WORKED TEMPLATE

understanding guides activity; activity tests understanding; organization preserves what people learn; a changed capacity makes new activity possible.

That is praxis. Consciousness develops through activity guided by thought and corrected by consequence.

Conviction must survive the quiet years

A crisis can compress years of learning into weeks. People who accepted an institution yesterday may occupy it tomorrow.

But crises also pass.

A strike wins a raise. A government grants reforms. Capital finds a temporary stability. The organization born in a hot moment can cool into a mailing list, an office, or a memory. Most people can often win part of their immediate needs without pursuing the abolition of capitalism.

If communist conviction exists only while daily experience makes revolution feel obvious, it will disappear during the long periods when daily experience teaches accommodation.

Organization must therefore preserve more than conclusions. It must sustain the human capacities that make a future opening usable:

  • relationships strong enough to survive disappointment;
  • study connected to real activity;
  • confidence based on tested cooperation;
  • memory that does not turn defeat into destiny;
  • care for people carrying fear, grief, exhaustion, and risk;
  • habits of criticism that do not become humiliation;
  • a culture in which truth matters more than rank;
  • a final aim that an immediate settlement cannot quietly replace.

Will is not a heroic substance possessed by exceptional people. It is socially cultivated endurance.

Habits need a home

Every organization develops an ethos, whether it admits this or not.

People learn what receives praise, what gets hidden, who may speak plainly, whose exhaustion counts, and whether a promise matters. A rulebook can say “comradeship” while the daily organization rewards flattery and punishes bad news.

Communist principles must become forms of life.

Orthopraxy means repeated practice that carries a conviction into conduct. It can be as ordinary as beginning a review with facts rather than blame, rotating a skilled task while training the next person, caring for a member during illness, recording a minority warning, or returning after failure to repair the harm.

The word may sound religious because religions have long understood that belief is sustained by community, repetition, symbols, moral expectations, care, and shared time. Communist movements have also produced martyrs, revered texts, rituals, songs, symbols, confessions, saints, and heretics, even when they called themselves entirely secular.

Pretending these forces do not exist leaves them free to form around leaders and inherited habits.

Copying a church would reproduce another danger: sacred authorities, unquestionable texts, coerced belief, purified insiders, and suffering treated as proof of truth.

The answer cannot be an empty culture or a commanded faith.

Virtue without saints

A communist organization needs people capable of courage, honesty, patience, care, loyalty, discipline, curiosity, and self-command. These are not decorations added after the political line. An organization full of concealment, cruelty, vanity, and private ambition will turn even a correct programme into something poisonous.

Yet virtue must be made material.

Calling for courage while transferring every risk downward is hypocrisy. Praising honesty while punishing the first person to report failure teaches deceit. Demanding sacrifice without mutual care trains people to accept abuse.

The organization cultivates character through relations:

  • courage grows when people do not face retaliation alone;
  • honesty grows when truthful reports lead to learning and repair;
  • discipline grows when people understand the common purpose;
  • humility grows when expertise remains answerable and teachable;
  • loyalty grows through shared work, not personal devotion;
  • hope grows when action reveals a power that isolation concealed.

No leader should embody these virtues on everyone else’s behalf. The point is to make them common capacities.

An unfinished edge

How far should a communist organization deliberately shape symbols, rites, moral language, and collective identity?

The problem is not settled by laughing at religion, and it is not settled by calling the party a church.

What is already clear is that materialist knowledge alone does not create a reflex for class struggle; organization cannot survive on cost-benefit calculation; and political commitment needs social support during periods when victory feels remote.

It is equally clear that canon, martyrdom, confession, and sacred authority can protect opportunists, make leaders untouchable, and turn criticism into heresy.

The line worth developing is a consciously revisable common culture. Its practices must help people act, care, tell the truth, govern themselves, and correct the organization. Nothing becomes sacred against evidence. No one becomes holy by office. Suffering is never proof that a decision was right.

Communism does not ask people to place their power in another object above them.

It asks them to recognize that the power was theirs, build the relations through which they can exercise it, and become the kind of people who can keep it common.

Knowing begins that movement.

Organized life gives it will.

Source notes

Primary texts

  • Karl Marx, Comments on James Mill, 1844, on human mediation becoming an alien power embodied in money.
  • Karl Marx, Theses on Feuerbach, written in 1845, on human activity, changed circumstances, and self-change.
  • Anton Pannekoek, “Religion”, 1947, on religion as an expression of material forces that dominate people and on the difference between atheism and historical materialism.

Further study

  • Friedrich Engels, “On the History of Early Christianity”, 1894 to 1895, on the resemblance and decisive difference between an early movement of oppressed people and the modern working-class movement.
  • Karl Marx, A Contribution to the Critique of Hegel’s Philosophy of Right: Introduction, 1844, on religion as both the expression of real suffering and a protest against it.
