04Organizing and Learning

What Can One Communist Do?

Do not appoint yourself the central committee of an organization that does not exist.

Begin where you actually live.

Be part of the class

Join the struggles already produced by work, rent, care, migration, discrimination, war, and ecological damage. Listen before announcing a line. Learn who does the hidden work, who carries the risk, and why earlier efforts failed.

Being present is not the same as following every immediate opinion. Workers can defend a narrow trade, a national privilege, or a settlement that sacrifices someone else. Make the communist argument clearly. Do it as a participant whose conduct gives others a reason to listen.

Become dependable

Reliability is a political quality.

Keep promises. Arrive prepared. Share credit. Record decisions. Protect private information. Admit an error before someone else has to expose it. Do the dull work that lets common work continue.

Grand declarations are cheap because one person can make them alone. Trust is expensive because other people must be able to test it over time.

Carry memory into living struggle

Study should make reality easier to understand.

Learn the communist programme, the history of victories and defeats, political economy, organization, and the conditions around you. Translate knowledge into forms people can use: a clear explanation, a reading group, a well-sourced leaflet, a map of ownership, a record of what happened last time.

Do not turn theory into a password. If a term cannot yet be understood, teach it. If an inherited answer fails against evidence, investigate.

Help people and knowledge meet

One person may know labor law. Another knows the shift. Someone elsewhere fought the same scheduling system. A useful communist connects them.

Build relationships across workplaces, neighborhoods, and languages. Preserve contact after the event that first brought people together. Help knowledge move without making yourself its permanent gatekeeper.

The work is successful when others gain capacity, not when everyone must return to you.

Refuse the theater of substitution

A spectacular individual act can express real rage while leaving the class no more organized than before. Personal bravery cannot substitute for collective power. Neither can a tiny group copy the offices, titles, and commands of a mature party and thereby become one.

The 1980 text The Party Does Not Arise from “Circles” was right to reject the idea that incompatible little groups can be fused by discipline. It went too far when it implied that a party embryo already performs every mature function in miniature. Capacity changes form. Ten people cannot act like ten thousand without slipping into fantasy.

Name the organization honestly. A study circle can be valuable. A local network can be valuable. Their task is to grow real relations, knowledge, discipline, and class activity, not to cosplay a power they do not possess.

Prepare the conditions for a party

No clever individual can summon a class party by will. Neither does a party arrive automatically when a crisis becomes severe.

Preparatory communist work joins two truths:

  • organization grows from material struggle and shared need;
  • people can consciously preserve knowledge, build relationships, and prepare capacities before a larger opening arrives.

Your work may remain small. Do it so that it can connect.

Be useful in the fight in front of you. Keep the final aim in view. Help people remember, meet, understand, and act. Leave behind more confidence, more truth, and more organization than you found.

That is not the whole movement.

It is how one person stops being alone.

Source notes

Primary texts

  • Karl Marx, General Rules of the International Working Men’s Association, revised 1871 from the 1864 Provisional Rules. It begins from working-class self-emancipation and builds durable combination without attributing history to isolated leaders.
  • Ernesto Che Guevara, “On Revolutionary Medicine”, August 19, 1960. It joins professional competence, solidarity, daily service, learning from the people, and action carried out alongside rather than above them.
  • Il Partito Comunista, The Party Does Not Arise from “Circles”, 1980. It examines programme, activity, organization, discipline, and the failure of organizational maneuvers to manufacture political unity.

Further study

  • Vladimir Lenin, “Our Immediate Task”, 1899, on overcoming the isolation of local organizations through regular shared work.
  • Jacques Camatte, “Origin and Function of the Party Form”, 1961, with a 1974 postface, on the historical conditions under which a party emerges and the limits of founding it by direct will.
