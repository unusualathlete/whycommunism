The Same Boycott Is Never the Same Boycott

In 1905, the Russian Empire was shaken by mass strikes, peasant revolts, mutinies, and the birth of workers’ councils. Tsar Nicholas II offered a consultative assembly called the Bulygin Duma.

Revolutionaries called for an active boycott.

They did not mean staying home. They used the election period to organize meetings, expose the fraud, and direct energy toward mass action outside the assembly. In a rising revolution, the boycott could help workers refuse a false substitute for power.

Two years later, after the December uprising had been defeated and reaction was advancing, Lenin argued against boycotting the Third Duma. The institution had become more reactionary, not less. Yet the revolutionary upswing that had given the boycott its force was gone. Refusal without a stronger arena of struggle would no longer mean the same thing.

The word stayed still.

The relation changed.

A tactic is a relation

A tactic is a limited action chosen to advance a strategic purpose under concrete conditions.

It cannot be judged by asking whether boycott, election, strike, alliance, demonstration, withdrawal, occupation, clandestine work, or public education is revolutionary in the abstract.

The real questions are:

  • What purpose does the action serve?
  • What forces give it meaning now?
  • What can the organization and class actually sustain?
  • What will participants learn and become capable of doing?
  • What risks and losses fall on whom?
  • What does success, partial success, or defeat leave behind?

The tactic is the whole relation among these parts.

1905: boycott as concentration

The boycott of the Bulygin Duma belonged to a revolutionary upswing.

Mass action already existed outside the proposed institution. Workers were not being asked to replace activity with abstention. The boycott could concentrate attention on direct struggle, refuse constitutional illusions, and use the state’s own meetings as fields of agitation.

Its meaning depended on conditions:

Objective conditions: a discredited autocracy, rapid mass strikes, political crisis, peasant revolt, and open conflict over state power.

Subjective conditions: organizations able to agitate, workers already moving beyond ordinary legal channels, and enough confidence to make refusal active.

Remove those conditions and the same call becomes something else.

1907: boycott as withdrawal

By 1907, the state had survived the revolutionary wave. Repression had intensified. The movement faced defeat and retreat.

In those conditions, a boycott could no longer count on a mass struggle that gave abstention an active content. Lenin argued that revolutionaries should use even the narrow Duma arena for agitation while subordinating it to work outside parliament.

This was not an endorsement of the Duma. It was a reassessment of what refusal and participation could do.

The institution remained reactionary across both moments. Its moral character did not select the tactic. The changing relation of forces did.

The named action Under an upswing Under a retreat
boycott can concentrate mass activity in stronger organs outside the institution can become passive absence when no stronger arena exists
participation can divert a rising movement into a weaker legal channel can preserve a public platform and connection while forces rebuild

This table is not a rule saying “boycott in advance, participate in retreat.” It shows why a tactic has no life outside the mechanism that makes it work.

Conditions include the organization itself

Political writing often treats “conditions” as facts in the outside world. Internal capacity matters just as much.

A legal opening is useless if no one can use it well. A strike call backed by wide anger can still fail if communication, food, funds, and trust do not exist. A clandestine method needed under severe repression can deform an organization if secrecy spreads beyond the function that requires it.

Subjective conditions include:

  • the knowledge participants possess;
  • the relationships they can rely on;
  • confidence based on experience;
  • communication and material resources;
  • the ability to coordinate and change course;
  • the consequences the organization can survive.

Will can develop these capacities. It cannot substitute for them.

Flexibility requires memory

Changing a tactic after conditions change is not inconsistency. Repeating a tactic after its mechanism has disappeared is not principle.

Yet “conditions changed” can become an excuse for anything. Opportunism often presents a retreat from principle as practical flexibility.

Three anchors keep change from becoming drift:

  1. Programme: the action cannot trade away class independence or the communist aim.
  2. Strategy: the action must develop the broad route rather than become an end in itself.
  3. Historical memory: the movement must know how similar methods worked, failed, and altered organizations before.

This is why tactical plans need review. Before acting, an organization should name the conditions that make the action plausible. Afterward, it should compare expectation with result.

If a boycott fails, the answer is not automatically that boycotts are wrong. If an election produces a useful platform, the answer is not that parliament has become the road to power. Judgment returns to the relation.

Means educate

Tactics do more than win or lose an immediate demand. They teach people what power is, whom to trust, how decisions are made, and what kind of action appears possible next.

A tactic can win a concession while making the organization more dependent on a capitalist politician. It can lose an immediate demand while revealing a supply-chain power workers did not know they held. It can protect an organization physically while creating a secretive culture that later blocks criticism.

Consequences include the people and relations produced through the action.

That does not mean choosing only gentle or easy methods. Class struggle includes coercion, repression, insurrection, and counter-revolution. A method may be severe because the conflict is severe. Its necessity still has to be argued through purpose, conditions, capacity, and consequence.

No label performs that judgment for us.

A tactic is alive only in a situation. The situation changes while people act inside it. Good tactical judgment therefore does two things at once: it holds the final aim steady and keeps its eyes open.

Source notes

Primary texts

  • Vladimir Lenin, “Against Boycott”, 1907, especially sections I, II, and V. It compares the successful 1905 active boycott with the changed conditions after revolutionary defeat and rejects a slogan repeated outside its historical mechanism.
  • Vladimir Lenin, “The Boycott”, August 1906. It reassesses the Duma boycott and argues for using a limited parliamentary arena while subordinating it to struggle outside.
  • Communist Party of Italy, Theses of Rome, 1922. It places tactics within programme, class conditions, party capacity, and the development of revolutionary organization.

Further study

  • Georg Lukács, “Tactics and Ethics”, 1919, on tactics as the link between the ultimate objective and reality.
  • Onorato Damen, “Amadeo Bordiga: Beyond the Myth and the Rhetoric”, 1970, on abstentionism as a tactic formed against Italian parliamentary corruption and the danger of allowing it to become the defining platform.
