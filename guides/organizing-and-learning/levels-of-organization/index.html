05Organizing and Learning

A Movement Cannot Be Scaled with a Photocopier

A reading group of eight people can settle a meeting time in one chat.

An international organization cannot.

If the group grows, it must divide work, preserve records, connect places, train new people, resolve conflicts, and make decisions whose consequences reach beyond the room. Quantity changes quality. More people do not simply create a bigger version of the first meeting. They create new organizational problems.

The answer is neither one giant assembly nor a command chain that pulls every decision to the top. Different levels need different forms because they perform different work.

Level 1: the base organization

A base organization is the smallest durable body able to carry out a whole field of shared activity.

It may be a territorial branch, a workplace group, a publication collective, a study circle becoming active, or a local communist organization. It needs enough regularity to remember decisions, divide work, educate members, act, and review the result.

The base is where general claims meet actual people. A center may know that logistics workers face speedup across a country. A warehouse group knows which scanner rule causes injuries at 4:00 a.m.

Without the first kind of knowledge, local struggle can remain narrow. Without the second, the wider analysis floats above the world.

Level 2: differentiation inside the work

As activity grows, everyone cannot do everything at once. Differentiation creates distinct roles or organs for work that requires sustained attention.

One group may research an industry. Another teaches new members. Another maintains secure communication. Another coordinates a public campaign. A treasurer handles resources because money cannot be managed by general enthusiasm.

This creates technical hierarchy: temporary or durable authority based on a function, responsibility, and relevant knowledge.

Technical hierarchy is not political superiority. The person responsible for translation decides how to divide a translation workflow. They do not acquire the right to decide the organization’s strategy. A medical team may need a clinical lead in an emergency. The lead remains answerable for the purpose, limits, and result of that authority.

Specialization can become a private kingdom. Rotation, shared education, documentation, review, and the training of successors prevent knowledge from becoming a lock.

Level 3: local coordination

Several base organizations in one city or region encounter common systems: employers, transport, housing, media, police, supply networks, and law.

A local coordinating organ can combine reports, schedule shared work, allocate resources, and prevent every group from rebuilding the same tool.

It should not absorb ordinary decisions that the base can make with better knowledge. Every upward transfer costs attention and time. Centralizing a decision is justified when the effects cross units, shared resources are involved, or the lower level lacks the necessary view.

The local level needs a clear answer to three questions:

  1. What decisions belong here?
  2. What must remain with the base?
  3. How can either level correct the other?

Level 4: wider political organization

Regional and national organs confront structures that operate at those scales. They can compare industries, study the state, coordinate publications, preserve a programme, move resources, and plan activity across places with different immediate pressures.

This wider view is real power. It is also a source of blindness.

A summary removes detail. A leadership may receive six neat numbers where workers experience six different failures. Reports may be filtered by each layer until only good news survives.

Information must therefore travel in more than one route:

  • formal reports from local organs;
  • direct contact across levels;
  • horizontal exchange among comparable groups;
  • minority or dissenting reports;
  • audits that can inspect the actual work;
  • public explanations of wider decisions returned to the base.

The center coordinates. It must also remain capable of being surprised.

Level 5: the International

Capital organizes production across borders. A semiconductor shortage changes factories on several continents. A shipping strike can stop industries far from the port. War, debt, migration controls, and ecological breakdown divide one world working class into unequal conditions.

An international organization does more than express solidarity. It coordinates strategy, compares national situations, shares resources and refuge, fights social chauvinism, and helps workers concentrate force where the world system is vulnerable.

The struggle still meets states, laws, languages, and class compositions in particular places. An international center that erases those conditions cannot lead them. A national organization that makes its own temporary interest supreme has abandoned internationalism.

The relation is reciprocal. Each national and local field contributes knowledge to the whole. The wider organization returns a view no part could construct alone.

The levels rhyme

A recursion is a working whole nested inside a larger whole. Each level needs some way to hold:

  • purpose;
  • activity;
  • coordination;
  • knowledge of changing conditions;
  • correction and accountability.

The pattern repeats. The structure does not.

A local branch may decide through a weekly meeting. A regional research network may use delegated teams and asynchronous review. An international party facing repression may need forms that would be absurd for a public neighborhood association.

Copying the same committee chart at every scale confuses resemblance with coherence. The levels should rhyme because each remains a living organization. They should differ because each meets a different environment.

A road from one kitchen to a region

A neighborhood food collective begins by buying staples in bulk.

At first, members divide orders in a shared spreadsheet. When three neighborhoods join, a purchasing team negotiates with farms and warehouses. A city network maps storage and transport. A regional body compares harvest forecasts and coordinates emergency reserves.

Each scale adds a function:

  1. Household and neighborhood: needs, dietary knowledge, direct use.
  2. City: shared storage, routing, purchasing, and access.
  3. Region: seasonal supply, reserve capacity, and relations with producers.
  4. Wider network: climate risks, interregional exchange, and common standards.

If the regional body sets every household order, it drowns in detail and destroys local knowledge. If every neighborhood ignores the harvest plan, the common reserve fails.

The higher level needs inputs, outputs, capacities, and constraints from below. It does not need continuous command over the internal life of every part.

History offers structures, not tracing paper

The First International tried to join national combination with direct local correspondence and the continued independence of its affiliated organizations. It did not solve the problem for all time. It showed that wider unity does not require every relationship to pass through one center.

Modern encrypted networks can move information almost instantly, but speed does not create trust, shared purpose, accurate judgment, or a division of labour. New tools change the form of a problem. They do not abolish organization.

Every level exists to perform a function the movement actually needs and can actually sustain. When the function changes, the form must be tested again. When a temporary organ has finished its task, it should disappear.

A small organization should not imitate an international headquarters. A mature organization should not preserve an office because it once belonged on the chart.

The point of scale is not to make the center larger.

It is to let more people act together without making any part of the movement deaf.

Source notes

Primary texts

  • Karl Marx, General Rules of the International Working Men’s Association, revised 1871 from the 1864 Provisional Rules. Rules 7 through 11 combine national organization, direct local correspondence, international support, and the preservation of affiliated organizations.
  • Vladimir Lenin, “Where to Begin?”, 1901. It presents a shared newspaper as an organizer that connects local work, supports division of labour, and develops a flexible network under changing conditions.
  • Il Partito Comunista, The Party Does Not Arise from “Circles”, 1980. It examines organization, specialization, territorial activity, programmatic continuity, and the danger of disintegration into isolated circles.

Further study

  • Alexander Bogdanov, Essays in Tektology: The General Science of Organization, first published in Russian between 1912 and 1922, English edition translated by George Gorelik. It studies combination, disorganization, adaptation, and recurrent organizational patterns across different systems.
  • Stafford Beer, Designing Freedom, based on the 1973 Massey Lectures, on viable autonomy, information, coordination, and the design of institutions capable of responding to complexity.
