Factions and Organizational Autophagy

A local branch reports that a plan is failing. A regional office softens the report before sending it upward because nobody wants to look incompetent. The center sees no failure, repeats the plan, and praises the region for its discipline.

The branch learns that official communication does not work. Its members create a private channel. The center discovers the channel and treats it as proof of disloyalty.

Now every side possesses evidence that seems to confirm its fear.

That loop is the beginning of organizational autophagy: an organization starts consuming the trust, knowledge, people, and initiative it needs in order to live.

The problem is not simply that people disagree. The problem is that the organization can no longer turn disagreement into knowledge.

A faction can be guilty and still be a symptom

A faction is an organized current inside a larger organization. It may advance a distinct political line, defend a leadership, represent a social interest, or fight for control of the organization.

Some communist traditions use fraction more narrowly for a current that believes it is preserving or restoring a programme the larger organization has abandoned. The words have changed across movements and periods. Their names do not decide whether a group is right.

The first question is causal: what produced the grouping?

Careerism, private ambition, and sabotage are possible answers. So are filtered reports, protected leaders, a strategic failure nobody will examine, or a minority deprived of every ordinary route to be heard.

In Chapter 3 of The New Course, Trotsky makes the formal problem plain: “wherever there are two opinions, people inevitably group together.” An order against grouping cannot abolish the conditions that produce two opinions. It can only drive them underground.

Attacking a symptom while preserving its cause prepares the next faction.

Autophagy changes the purpose of the organization

An organization exists to perform a class function. It may investigate conditions, educate, coordinate action, preserve a programme, or connect struggles that would otherwise remain isolated.

Autophagy begins when internal survival replaces that function.

Watch for the change:

leaders who can be corrected leaders whose prestige depends on never being wrong
disagreement that tests a line victory over rivals as an end in itself

The camps may still speak the language of programme and unity. Their real activity tells another story.

The wrong cure strengthens the disease

Mechanical unity treats the faction as an object to remove. It changes officers, issues loyalty tests, forbids informal discussion, or demands repeated confessions. Each measure can make the record cleaner while the organization becomes less truthful.

This is why a factional crisis cannot be solved by finding the correct person for the correct office. If the office controls communication, protects itself from review, and rewards the suppression of failure, another person will inherit the same pressure.

The same warning applies to endless criticism and self-criticism. Criticism is useful when it establishes facts, identifies a political error, teaches the whole organization, and changes practice. It becomes destructive when it is compulsory humiliation, a test of verbal skill, or a permanent search for hidden enemies.

An honest member may struggle to explain a mistake. A dishonest member may deliver a brilliant confession and change nothing. Words matter, but conduct decides whether correction occurred.

Repair the relation, not just the meeting

Systemic repair changes the relations that keep reproducing the conflict.

In the opening example, repair would require more than ordering the private network to dissolve. The organization would need to:

  1. recover the original branch reports and make the changes traceable;
  2. let local members communicate the disputed facts directly;
  3. establish who benefited from filtering the record;
  4. restate the political disagreement without loyalty riddles or coded accusations;
  5. decide what evidence will test each claim;
  6. protect the implementation of the decision from quiet sabotage; and
  7. review both the result and the communication system that produced it.

Accountability remains real. A person who falsified records, threatened members, or deliberately damaged common work may need removal or sanction. Systemic explanation does not erase responsibility. It prevents punishment from becoming a way to avoid changing the institution that made the misconduct useful.

Unity is an achievement, not a lid

Unity does not mean the absence of every difference. It means enough shared programme, trust, method, and activity to turn differences into a stronger common line.

The 1980 text The Party Does Not Arise from “Circles” argues that discipline and organizational maneuvers cannot manufacture a party out of politically incompatible circles. It also warns that a party can dissolve back into circles when continuity of programme, activity, and organization breaks apart.

That distinction matters. A grouping can reveal a repairable failure. It can also express a real programmatic break that no procedure should disguise. Separation may be clearer than keeping incompatible organizations together through pressure and paperwork.

There is no universal device that decides when that point has arrived. The judgment requires the disputed programme, the social interests at work, the history of the conflict, the conduct of each current, and the consequences of continued unity or division.

The test

Ask one hard question:

WORKED TEMPLATE

Is this conflict making the organization more capable of serving its class purpose, or is the organization feeding on itself to preserve its present form?

If the answer is the second, calls for unity are not enough. Restore the truth of the situation, repair the channels through which people act together, hold actual harm accountable, and change or dissolve the form that has made self-destruction rational.

An organization survives by learning what it did not want to hear.

Source notes

Primary texts

  • Leon Trotsky, The New Course (1923), especially Chapter 3, “Groups and Factional Formations,” and Appendix I on bureaucracy and workers’ democracy.
  • Enver Hoxha, How We Should Understand Criticism and Self-Criticism (1964), on criticism as collective education, factual judgment, follow-through, and the danger of ritual confession.
  • Il Partito Comunista, The Party Does Not Arise from “Circles” (1980), on programme, organization, discipline, political activity, and dissolution into circles.

Further study

  • Rosa Luxemburg, Organizational Questions of the Russian Social Democracy (1904), on centralism, initiative, and the living development of a workers’ movement.
  • Leon Trotsky, The New Course, Chapter 4, “Bureaucratism and the Revolution”, on the apparatus, social pressures, and the limits of formal anti-bureaucratic measures.
