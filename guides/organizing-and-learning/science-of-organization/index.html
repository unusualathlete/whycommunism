10Organizing and Learning

The Organization Laboratory

Five local groups agree to distribute the same leaflet on the same morning.

By noon, one group has reached hundreds of workers. A second discovers that the factory gate moved months ago. A third never received the final file. A fourth changed the translation without telling anyone. The fifth reports success because nobody wants to be blamed.

The central plan looked clear.

The living organization was not.

A science of organization begins here. It does not ask which box on the chart disobeyed. It asks what relations produced five different realities, which differences mattered, and how the organization can learn before the next action.

The object on the table

An organizational system includes:

  • a purpose;
  • people and relationships;
  • work and division of labour;
  • tools and resources;
  • information channels;
  • authority and responsibility;
  • an environment that changes;
  • ways to detect and correct error.

The official organization may say, “The center sends a leaflet to five branches.”

The real system may say:

WORKED TEMPLATE

One exhausted editor sends a late file through an unreliable platform to five groups with different languages, schedules, gate access, printing capacity, and levels of trust.

The second description can be improved because it names what exists.

Test 1: Did the structure serve the purpose?

The purpose was not to complete a distribution report. It was to begin useful political contact with workers around a shared issue.

That distinction changes the investigation.

If one branch spoke with workers using an earlier draft because the final file arrived too late, it may have served the purpose better than a branch that followed every instruction and reached an empty gate. Compliance and function are not the same thing.

The first test asks:

  1. What was the organization trying to produce?
  2. What did it actually produce?
  3. Which rule helped?
  4. Which rule became an obstacle?

A structure is not vindicated by its own survival.

Test 2: Could the organization absorb the world’s variety?

The five sites differed in language, geography, shift times, security, public access, printing, and local political knowledge.

Variety means the range of different states a system can encounter or produce. An organization needs enough requisite variety to respond to the differences that matter.

This does not require a unique plan for every person. It requires distinguishing conditions that change the action.

A central office with one language and one schedule could not absorb the field’s variety. The answer might include:

  • local translation with a shared political review;
  • a deadline early enough for branches to adapt;
  • direct confirmation that the location and shift still exist;
  • several delivery channels;
  • local authority to alter distribution details within a common purpose;
  • a way to report a condition the plan did not anticipate.

Too little variety produces rigid failure. Unlimited variation dissolves common action. Organization decides which differences must be preserved and which must be coordinated.

Test 3: Did feedback close the loop?

Feedback is information about a result returning to someone able to change the next action.

The report “distribution completed” carries almost no learning. A useful return might record:

  • where and when contact happened;
  • how many real conversations occurred;
  • what workers asked or rejected;
  • which translation created confusion;
  • what resource failed;
  • what the branch changed and why;
  • what should be done differently.

Feedback must move upward, downward, and sideways.

The center needs local evidence. The local groups need to know the wider result and why the plan changes. Branches facing similar conditions need to compare methods directly.

When information only climbs, local groups become sensors for somebody else’s intelligence. When information only circulates locally, the movement cannot combine what it knows.

Test 4: Were the levels viable?

A recursion is a working whole nested inside a larger whole.

Each branch needs enough internal capacity to understand its purpose, organize its work, respond to local conditions, and correct itself. The wider organization needs the same kinds of capacity at another scale.

The levels rhyme. They do not copy.

The translation branch may need rapid peer review. The national center may need a strategic editorial body. Both connect purpose, activity, information, and correction, but their structures should differ because their environments differ.

A lower level is not viable if it must request permission for every ordinary adjustment. A higher level is not viable if its parts can alter the political purpose without a common decision.

The laboratory asks where autonomy belongs, where binding coordination belongs, and what information crosses the boundary.

Test 5: Can failure become common knowledge?

An audit begins because something does not fit. It may investigate a failure, a disputed report, or an unusually good result.

In the leaflet case, the fourth branch’s translation produced far more discussion than the approved version. An audit should not assume that deviation was the problem. It should compare the texts, speak with workers, and find out what the local group understood.

Perhaps the central language was abstract. Perhaps the local version introduced a political error. Perhaps both happened.

Organizational learning occurs when the result changes shared memory, training, structure, or future action. A lesson kept inside one person’s head is not yet an organizational capacity.

A useful laboratory record looks like this:

Observation Likely relation Change to test Evidence after change
final files arrive after local print deadlines planning ignores production time move political sign-off two days earlier fewer emergency edits and missed distributions
reports hide failures blame travels faster than help separate factual review from sanctions for bad faith reports contain more usable negative information
one translation creates better discussion local language carries knowledge build translator feedback into drafting shared text becomes clearer without losing programme

Audit can discover a superior method and spread it. It is not only a police function.

The model does not choose the purpose

Systems methods can make a prison, corporation, army, hospital, or communist organization more effective. The method does not decide what should exist.

Political purpose enters before every model:

  • Which class does the organization serve?
  • What relation is it trying to abolish?
  • What must remain under the participants’ control?
  • Which risk is unacceptable?
  • What kind of people and relationships does the activity produce?

Computation can find patterns, model communication load, and expose bottlenecks. It cannot become the source of political purpose. A dashboard sees what its categories allow. People must retain the right to ask why those categories exist and to change them.

Bogdanov’s wager

Alexander Bogdanov called his project Tektology, a general science of organization. He wanted to study how systems combine, separate, resist disruption, and reorganize across nature and society.

The wager is worth keeping: organization itself can become an object of investigation rather than a matter of instinct, office tradition, or leader worship.

The claim must be limited. No universal science supplies a politically neutral answer to the party question. Living organizations contain power, conflict, meaning, fear, and purpose. These are not engineering noise.

The organization laboratory is communist when the people inside the system can inspect it, correct it, and judge it from the movement’s aim.

Return to the five leaflets.

The failure was not that reality produced variation. Reality always will.

The failure was that the organization had no truthful way to turn variation into intelligence.

Source notes

Primary texts

  • Alexander Bogdanov, Essays in Tektology: The General Science of Organization, written from 1912 onward, English edition translated by George Gorelik. It studies formation, disorganization, equilibrium, selection, and recurrent organizational relations across fields.
  • Il Partito Comunista, The Party Does Not Arise from “Circles”, 1980. It connects programme, organization, activity, discipline, division of labour, and the danger that a party becomes a collection of disconnected circles.

Further study

  • Stafford Beer, Designing Freedom, based on the 1973 Massey Lectures, on variety, viable autonomy, communication, and institutions designed for freedom.
  • Stafford Beer, Diagnosing the System for Organizations, 1985, a practical guide to recursion, operational autonomy, coordination, control, intelligence, and policy in the Viable System Model.
