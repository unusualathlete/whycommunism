A Field Sheet for Judging a Tactic

Use this sheet before an action. Return to it while conditions change. Complete it again afterward with the people who carried the consequences.

Do not begin by writing the action’s name.

Begin with the relation it is meant to change.

1. State the purpose in one sentence

WORKED TEMPLATE

We propose to so that develops toward .

Name the immediate result, the capacity it should build, and the strategic direction. If the sentence ends at publicity, pressure, or victory, it is not yet clear enough.

2. Check the red lines

Reject or redesign the proposal if it:

  • subordinates workers to a capitalist party, state, employer, or national project;
  • requires the party to substitute itself for class action;
  • hides the real purpose from the people carrying the risk;
  • treats a section of the class as disposable;
  • reproduces racism, patriarchy, chauvinism, or other class divisions;
  • makes organizational survival more important than the communist purpose.

No favorable condition turns a violation of principle into a good tactic.

3. Write the conditions, not the mood

Objective conditions

  • What classes, institutions, laws, economic pressures, and forms of repression shape the field?
  • What other struggles are rising, stalled, or retreating?
  • Where does the opponent hold power, and where is that power vulnerable?

Subjective conditions

  • Who understands and supports the aim?
  • What organization, skill, trust, time, communication, and material support exist?
  • Which needed capacity is missing?

Separate evidence from hope. Anger is evidence of anger. It is not proof of readiness.

4. Follow three outcomes

If it succeeds If it partly succeeds If it fails
What changes materially? What compromise or unfinished relation remains? Who is exposed, exhausted, isolated, or strengthened?
What capacity grows? Can the gain be defended and extended? What organization and knowledge survive?
What danger appears next? What could turn the partial gain into a trap? Was the loss foreseeable or genuinely unexpected?

The proposal must be judged by the whole range, not by its best imagined ending.

5. Locate risk and decision

  • Who carries physical, legal, economic, social, and political risk?
  • Did those people help decide?
  • Can they refuse a role without punishment?
  • What protection, care, and recovery are available?
  • Is any secrecy limited to the function that actually requires it?

Leadership that transfers risk downward and keeps judgment upward is already teaching the wrong politics.

6. Ask what the tactic produces inside the movement

Does it develop:

  • class independence;
  • wider solidarity;
  • political understanding;
  • durable relationships;
  • initiative and coordination;
  • truthful communication;
  • the ability to act again?

Or does it reward:

  • spectacle over result;
  • loyalty over evidence;
  • internal rivalry;
  • reckless escalation;
  • dependence on one leader or gatekeeper;
  • the survival of the apparatus?

A tactic that feeds on the movement’s trust, people, or purpose is autophagous. It consumes the body meant to carry the struggle.

7. Name the feedback before acting

Complete these lines:

WORKED TEMPLATE

We will know the action is progressing when .

We will pause or alter it when .

We will stop it when .

Reports will reach through .

The review will include , especially those who carried .

Without a return path, new information becomes noise.

8. Record the judgment

Choose the description that fits the evidence:

Reject as harmful. The mechanism predictably weakens the class, violates principle, or spends people for no plausible development.

Reject as autophagous. The action makes internal survival, rivalry, secrecy, or performance consume the movement’s public purpose.

Redesign. The purpose is sound but capacity, risk, sequence, or feedback is missing.

Test under stated conditions. The tactic is contingent. A limited use can produce evidence without pretending certainty.

Support under stated conditions. Purpose, capacity, risk, likely consequences, and review form a defensible chain.

Write the conditions beside the judgment. Never carry the label to a different time as if history had approved the tactic forever.

9. After the action

Compare the actual chain with the expected one:

  1. What happened?
  2. Which assumption was true?
  3. Which assumption failed?
  4. What did participants learn that leadership did not know?
  5. What relation or capacity changed?
  6. What must be repaired?
  7. Does the tactic change, or does the strategy need review?

Do not use review as confession theater. Establish facts, teach the organization, repair harm, and change practice.

Source notes

Primary texts

  • Georg Lukács, “Tactics and Ethics”, 1919. It joins tactical knowledge, collective action, individual responsibility, and the ultimate objective under conditions where consequences cannot be known completely.
  • Communist Party of Italy, Theses of Rome, 1922. It relates tactics to principles, programme, conditions, organization, prediction, and the danger of eclectic improvisation.
  • International Communist Party, The Communist Party in the Tradition of the Left, part V, “The Party’s Tactics”, 1974. It treats tactics as a serious problem of class action rather than a substitute for action by theoretical proclamation.

Further study

  • Vladimir Lenin, “Against Boycott”, 1907, a worked historical demonstration that a tactic must be judged from the conditions that give it force.
