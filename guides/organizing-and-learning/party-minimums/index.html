Does This Organization Deserve to Be Called a Party?

The word party can name a ballot machine, a tiny reading group, a historical current, a clandestine organization, or millions of workers in motion.

The name settles nothing.

A party minimum is the least set of living relations required before “communist party” describes what an organization does rather than what it hopes to become.

This is a diagnostic, not a certificate.

First, identify the thing honestly

A circle

A circle gathers people around study, affinity, publication, or a local task.

It may preserve rare knowledge, translate texts, train serious organizers, and create lifelong trust. Those are real achievements.

It remains a circle when its life turns mainly inward, its reach depends on a few personal relationships, and it cannot connect programme to sustained class activity beyond its own setting.

The honest task is not to feel ashamed of being small. It is to refuse to make smallness into a complete theory of the movement.

A preparatory communist organization

This organization has moved beyond private study. It develops regular work, agreed responsibilities, public political activity, and relationships inside working-class life.

It may still lack a general strategy, geographic reach, internal education, continuity under pressure, or the ability to coordinate several fields at once.

Its value lies in building those capacities without announcing authority it has not earned.

A party embryo

A party embryo carries a coherent communist programme and a living orientation toward the whole class struggle. Its organization, education, strategy, and activity reinforce one another.

Some mature functions exist in potential or limited form. The embryo may maintain theory, join economic struggles, educate members, coordinate several local groups, and learn from action.

It cannot perform every function at the scale or quality of a mature party. The purpose of an embryo is to develop the capacities through which a larger political organ becomes possible.

A party performing mature functions

A mature communist party can act across several regions and moments of class life while preserving one political direction.

It can:

  • maintain and develop the programme;
  • investigate the present class relation;
  • educate members and the wider class;
  • connect economic, political-economic, and political struggles;
  • formulate strategy and revise it through evidence;
  • coordinate differentiated work at several scales;
  • prepare materially for rupture, repression, and transition;
  • participate in international organization;
  • survive defeat without becoming a museum;
  • lead through clarity, action, and earned trust;
  • generalize capacity rather than monopolize it.

Size matters because capacity matters. Size alone proves none of this.

The seven tests

1. Programme

Can members explain the communist aim, class agency, transition, and principles in a connected way?

Failure signs: a famous label replaces a programme; old quotations replace present understanding; every disagreement is hidden behind unity.

2. Organized continuity

Can the organization remember decisions, divide work, train successors, maintain resources, and act beyond one event?

Failure signs: one person holds every password; work disappears when enthusiasm falls; titles exist without dependable functions.

3. Life inside the class

Does the organization participate in real proletarian struggles, learn from them, and remain answerable to what it claims to understand?

Failure signs: workers appear only as an audience; online attention is mistaken for class relation; immediate struggles are dismissed as impure.

4. Political development

Can the organization connect a local demand to the wider class relation without using the final aim to evade the immediate fight?

Failure signs: laborism makes one section’s demand the whole politics; abstract revolutionism speaks of power without building class capacity.

5. Pedagogy

Does knowledge travel? Can new members investigate, argue, organize, and teach, or must they return to permanent interpreters?

Failure signs: vocabulary becomes rank; leaders grow more indispensable while members grow more passive.

6. Strategy, tactics, and feedback

Can the organization distinguish stable principle, broad strategic hypothesis, and limited tactic? Does evidence change practice?

Failure signs: a tactic becomes identity; conditions are invoked without investigation; failure produces blame rather than correction.

7. Anti-substitution

Does the organization increase the class’s independent capacity?

Failure signs: unions and councils become party departments; party membership grants public privilege; political leadership turns into administrative command.

Read the pattern, not one answer

An organization may be strong in programme and weak in class relation. Another may be deeply rooted in a workplace while unable to see beyond one industry. A third may possess large membership and resources but have become an electoral apparatus with no communist purpose.

The pattern identifies the next work.

Present form Central developmental task Typical self-deception
circle connect knowledge to durable class activity “purity makes us the historical party”
preparatory organization build programme, continuity, education, and wider relation “activity proves our line is complete”
party embryo deepen functions in proportion to real capacity “we already do everything, only at a smaller percentage”
mature party coordinate class movement without substituting for it “our capacity gives us the right to rule”

The embryo argument

The 1980 text The Party Does Not Arise from “Circles” makes a powerful claim: a party does not emerge by fusing incompatible circles through discipline and organizational maneuver. Programme, activity, organization, and centralism must form a living unity.

It also says that an embryo deserves the party name only if it contains the essential functions of the mature organization.

That formulation needs a material correction.

An embryo contains a direction and developmental potential. It does not contain a smaller copy of every adult capacity. Ten people cannot maintain international coordination, mass education, strategic research, legal and clandestine work, material defense, and class-wide leadership merely by assigning seven committees.

Quantity becomes quality through new relationships, specialization, experience, trust, and reach.

This correction does not make the name arbitrary. A group with no coherent programme or class activity is not a party embryo. It is another kind of organization, perhaps a useful one.

The most truthful name is the one that reveals both present power and missing work.

The final diagnostic

Ask four questions together:

  1. What communist direction does this organization carry?
  2. What class relation does it live inside?
  3. What can it reliably do now?
  4. What capacities does its activity cause other people to gain?

The last question protects all the others.

A party is not mature because it can make more people obey.

It is mature when it can help the working class become a conscious, organized power without making that power its possession.

Source notes

Primary texts

  • Karl Marx and Friedrich Engels, Manifesto of the Communist Party, section II, 1848. It defines the communists as a distinct, advanced part of the proletarian movement without separate class interests.
  • Amadeo Bordiga, “Party and Class”, 1921, and “Party and Class Action”, 1921. They join programme, consciousness, will, organization, class movement, and changing capacity while rejecting a fixed numerical threshold.
  • Il Partito Comunista, The Party Does Not Arise from “Circles”, 1980. It provides the embryo argument and the relation among programme, activity, organization, discipline, and party dissolution.

Further study

  • Communist International, “Theses on the Role of the Communist Party in the Proletarian Revolution”, 1920, a strong historical statement of the party’s necessary functions and its relation to other proletarian organs.
  • Onorato Damen, “Amadeo Bordiga: Beyond the Myth and the Rhetoric”, 1970, on party formation, intermediate bodies, strategy, and the need to distinguish a historical contribution from a myth.
