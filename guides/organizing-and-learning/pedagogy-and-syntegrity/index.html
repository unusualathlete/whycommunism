Knowledge Must Travel

Sixteen people meet about unsafe schedules at a regional bus company.

Drivers know which routes force speeding. Mechanics know which buses should not be on the road. Riders know where cuts have made people walk beside a highway. A researcher has the operating contract. Organizers know that management is trying to separate permanent workers from agency drivers.

In an ordinary meeting, all sixteen may speak and still leave with the knowledge divided exactly as it was when they arrived.

One person gives a long report. Two people argue over a demand before the facts have met. The mechanics wait for a technical question that never comes. A driver mentions a near collision, but the discussion has already moved on. At the end, the chair summarizes the loudest thread.

Everyone was present.

The whole intelligence was not.

Political pedagogy is the work of turning separate experience into a shared ability to understand and act. It includes teaching, but it cannot end with a knowledgeable person delivering the correct line. The educator must be educated by the world the group is trying to change.

Here is one way to organize that movement of knowledge.

A linked workshop

This is a small, adaptable workshop inspired by Syntegrity, not a complete reproduction of Stafford Beer’s formal method. Its purpose is to stop one discussion from becoming the only route through which every idea must pass.

1. State one real problem

Write a question that people can investigate and act on:

WORKED TEMPLATE

How can workers and riders stop the unsafe schedule while building enough shared power to govern the service differently?

“Discuss transportation” is too loose. “Approve the organizers’ proposal” has already closed the question.

The problem should name the conflict without pretending that its answer is known.

2. Gather the questions inside the question

For ten minutes, participants write what the group must understand before it can decide. The questions are clustered into four connected fields:

  1. Safety: Which schedules, vehicles, and staffing practices create danger?
  2. Power: Who can change them, and what leverage do workers and riders possess?
  3. Service: Which communities bear the cuts, delays, and risks?
  4. Action: What can be attempted, sustained, reviewed, and changed?

These are working fields, not departments. A fact may belong to more than one.

3. Link the groups through people

Each participant joins two fields. A driver might work on Safety and Action. A rider might join Service and Power. A mechanic might connect Safety and Service.

The double membership matters. People do not merely send a final report from one sealed room to another. They carry unfinished questions, disagreements, and discoveries while the thinking is still alive.

Text alternative: Four discussion fields are linked in pairs. Participants belong to two fields so knowledge about safety, power, service, and action can move while each discussion develops.

4. Produce a map, not a speech

In the first round, every field records:

  • facts it can establish;
  • relations it thinks explain those facts;
  • disagreements that remain;
  • knowledge that another field must supply.

The Safety group may discover that a dangerous timetable is also a wage issue because agency drivers lose shifts when they report delays. The Service group may find that the most affected route serves a hospital whose workers can join the campaign.

The point is not to make each group agree. It is to make the state of its knowledge visible.

5. Carry the tensions across

Participants move to their second field with the first field’s unresolved problems.

Now the Action group cannot design a strike around an imaginary fleet. The Power group cannot treat riders as public-relations scenery. The Service group must confront what workers can safely sustain.

Good information may disturb the proposal that seemed obvious at the start. That is a success.

6. Return with a decision and a learning plan

The whole workshop comes back together. Its result should distinguish four things:

The workshop now has The workshop still needs
facts strong enough to act on facts that require investigation
a decision within its authority questions that belong to a wider body
a practical next step warning signs that require a change
named responsibilities a date and route for feedback

The final record might say:

WORKED TEMPLATE

Workers will document three routes for one week, riders will map missed hospital connections, and the joint committee will prepare a coordinated refusal of the two most dangerous schedules. The action pauses if management isolates agency drivers before legal and material support is ready. All three groups review the result on Friday.

That is not merely a conclusion. It is a new round of education organized through activity.

What the method changes

A linked workshop gives several kinds of knowledge room to alter one another. It can make quiet expertise harder to ignore, reveal a disagreement before it becomes a faction, and prevent the final speaker from owning the synthesis.

It does not make every claim equally true.

The mechanic’s knowledge of brakes carries special weight on a brake failure. The driver knows the route from inside the labour process. Riders know consequences that an operating report erases. Political theory can reveal why a local compromise reproduces the same power. Each claim still needs evidence, explanation, and criticism.

The method distributes the work of producing judgment. It does not abolish judgment.

Tektology after the meeting

Tektology studies how organized things combine, encounter disruption, and reorganize. Here it becomes a simple habit of review.

After the action, ask:

  1. What could participants do together that they could not do separately?
  2. Which connection carried useful information, and which one failed?
  3. Did a temporary role become a gate through which everything had to pass?
  4. What new capacity now belongs to more people?

These questions direct attention away from personalities alone and toward the relations that made success or failure likely.

They also protect against a common educational fraud. A workshop can feel participatory while every consequence has already been decided elsewhere. If participants cannot change the plan, test an explanation, refuse a false report, or carry knowledge into action, linked discussion becomes an elaborate listening exercise.

Where the workshop stops

Syntegrity does not choose a communist programme. Tektology does not decide which class should rule. Neither replaces investigation, mass organization, accountable authority, or the right to make a decision.

A movement can use an excellent workshop to pursue a terrible purpose. It can also spend days perfecting discussion while the moment for action closes.

The tools are valuable when they develop a living political capacity:

  • people understand more of the whole;
  • specialized knowledge becomes teachable;
  • disagreement becomes precise;
  • decisions remain connected to those carrying them;
  • action returns as evidence;
  • more people can lead the next round.

Education has succeeded when people do not merely remember what the teacher said. They can investigate a new problem, connect what was divided, make a judgment, act, and help someone else do the same.

Knowledge becomes power only when it can travel.

Source notes

Primary texts

  • Karl Marx, Theses on Feuerbach, written in 1845, especially the third thesis on changing circumstances, human activity, and the need for the educator to be educated.
  • Paulo Freire, Pedagogy of the Oppressed, written in Portuguese in 1968, on problem-posing education, dialogue, reflection, and action against the treatment of learners as passive containers.
  • Stafford Beer, Beyond Dispute: The Invention of Team Syntegrity, 1994, which develops the formal Team Syntegrity method for linked deliberation.

Further study

  • Alexander Bogdanov, Essays in Tektology: The General Science of Organization, written from 1912 onward and translated by George Gorelik, on combination, disruption, adaptation, and organization across different fields.
  • Stafford Beer, Designing Freedom, based on the 1973 Massey Lectures, on institutions designed to preserve autonomy while responding to social complexity.
