Education for Self-Government

A society cannot govern itself if most people are trained only to obey instructions while a small group is trained to think, plan, and decide.

Communist education aims to overcome that split. It develops the knowledge, confidence, judgment, and cooperation people need to shape common life.

The old division of labour

Capitalism separates mental labour from physical labour. Some people are expected to design, explain, and command. Others are expected to carry out narrow tasks.

The separation is never complete. Physical work requires thought, and intellectual work depends on bodies, tools, and other people’s labour. Still, the social roles are real. They shape income, status, education, and power.

Ending the division does not mean everyone becomes equally skilled at everything. It means no class or permanent group monopolizes knowledge and direction.

Learning joins life

Education can connect theory to real activity:

  • mathematics through energy, transport, and production problems;
  • biology through health, farming, and ecological restoration;
  • history through the institutions people use;
  • art through public culture and personal creation;
  • engineering through building and repair; and
  • politics through genuine participation in shared decisions.

This should not turn school into unpaid labour. The purpose is to let knowledge become lived power rather than information memorized for a test.

Students need social power

Young people are affected by schools and public spaces. They should have a real voice in them.

That voice grows with knowledge and responsibility. Adults still have duties of care and must protect children from harm. But protection should not become an excuse to treat young people as empty containers.

Children can help shape playgrounds, schedules, learning projects, food, cultural activities, and parts of their living environment. Participation teaches self-government because it is self-government.

The general intellect

Marx used the phrase general intellect for society’s shared knowledge and productive power. It lives in people, machines, science, habits, language, and institutions.

Under capitalism, common knowledge can appear as the private property and power of a company. Under communism, the general intellect becomes openly common.

People gain access to research, tools, teaching, and one another’s experience. Knowledge grows through use and criticism.

Learning to learn

Education should produce people who can keep learning without waiting for an authority to assign a lesson.

This includes:

  • curiosity;
  • emotional self-control;
  • the confidence to ask for help;
  • the ability to judge evidence;
  • comfort with both practical and abstract work;
  • cooperation across differences; and
  • willingness to revise a view after experience.

These capacities are political. They reduce dependence on a permanent group of managers and experts.

Teachers and experts still matter

The abolition of a monopoly on knowledge is not the abolition of expertise. A skilled teacher can help a learner cross ground that would take years alone. A surgeon’s training cannot be replaced by enthusiasm.

The difference is that expertise circulates, explains itself, submits to review, and helps others develop. It does not become a mysterious claim to rule.

A school that forbids questions has failed

Education for self-government is not indoctrination. A society that claims to teach materialism while forbidding evidence or disagreement would defeat its own purpose.

It also does not require every learner to follow the same path. Common knowledge can coexist with varied interests, abilities, cultures, and projects.

No single communist classroom

Education must join learning with social life, overcome fixed mental and physical roles, and make knowledge a common capacity.

The exact design remains open. Age, care, specialization, disability, local culture, and individual desire all matter. Revolutionary pedagogy is a field of practice and debate, not one classroom technique.
