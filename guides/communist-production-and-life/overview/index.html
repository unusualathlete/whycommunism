01Communist Production and Life

Communist Production: Making Life, Not Profit

Every society must decide what to make, how to make it, and who gets to use it. Capitalism answers these questions through property, wages, prices, competition, and profit. Communism gives a different answer: the people who produce society’s wealth organize that work together and direct it toward human need.

Marx called this an associated mode of production. The word associated matters. It means people cooperate on purpose, know that their work is part of a shared system, and have power over that system.

A mode of production is bigger than an economy

A mode of production includes factories and farms, but it also includes homes, schools, hospitals, transport, software, energy, and care. It shapes:

  • who owns the tools and land;
  • who decides what work is done;
  • how people gain access to food, shelter, and medicine;
  • how much time people spend working;
  • what kinds of knowledge society develops; and
  • how people relate to one another and to nature.

Changing the mode of production therefore changes more than a budget. It changes daily life.

Production for use

Under capitalism, a business usually produces an item because it expects to sell it. The item must satisfy some need, but satisfying that need is not enough. The sale must also return money to the owner and usually bring back more money than was spent.

This is production for exchange.

Communist production begins from use. Society asks what people need, what resources exist, what nature can renew, what work people can do, and what they want to develop. It then organizes production around those answers.

This is production for use.

The difference becomes clear when a useful thing cannot be sold. A market may leave food unused while people go hungry because the people who need it cannot pay. Production for use treats hunger and available food as the important facts.

Associated producers

Communism does not mean that a distant office owns everything while everyone else follows orders. State ownership by itself does not create communist relations.

The central subject is the associated producers: people whose different kinds of work form one connected social process. They decide through institutions they can understand, enter, challenge, and change.

Some decisions will be local. Others will involve a region, a continent, or the whole world. A hospital needs freedom to solve the problems it knows directly. It also depends on wider systems for medicines, power, training, and emergency planning. Association connects these levels instead of pretending one level can do everything.

Common wealth

The result of social production becomes common wealth. This includes goods such as food and tools, services such as healthcare, and shared capacities such as science, infrastructure, free time, healthy soil, and public knowledge.

Common wealth is not a pile divided into identical portions. Part of the social product must replace worn equipment. Part supports children, older people, sick people, education, emergency reserves, and future projects. The rest supports individual and shared consumption.

The principle changes as society develops. At first, scarcity and habits inherited from capitalism may still limit distribution. As productive capacity and social organization improve, access can depend more fully on need.

Freedom grows through shared power

Capitalist freedom often means choosing among things already shaped by someone else’s property and plan. Communist freedom means taking part in shaping the conditions of life.

That freedom includes private space, personal choice, and the right to differ. It also includes powers that one person cannot build alone: a clean energy system, a good hospital, a library, safe housing, a rich culture, and enough free time to use them.

The individual does not disappear into society. The aim is a form of society in which everyone can develop because everyone helps sustain the conditions for development.

The boundary of the claim

Communism is not a finished picture that can be copied from a book. Marx and Engels described relations, tendencies, and conditions. They did not write a complete operating manual for a future society.

It also does not mean:

  • every decision is made in one meeting;
  • every person receives the same objects;
  • personal belongings become communal;
  • all work becomes pleasant at once;
  • technology solves political questions; or
  • a state bureaucracy automatically counts as the associated producers.

A firm direction, an unfinished construction

The direction is firm: common control, production for use, the end of wage labour and class rule, greater free time, and the development of each person through the development of all.

The exact institutions remain open. Labour accounting, consumer feedback, planning models, local autonomy, technical coordination, and the pace of change require further study and practical experience. Later writers disagree about many of them.

The road through this pillar

  • Bourgeois Right and the Phases of Communism explains why communist relations cannot become complete in one instant.
  • Associated Production shows what it means for labour to be social from the beginning.
  • Common Wealth follows the social product from production to shared and individual use.
  • Human Development and Alienation explains why control over production changes the people doing the work.
