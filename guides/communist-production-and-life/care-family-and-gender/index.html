13Communist Production and Life

Care, Family, and Gender Beyond Economic Dependence

Families are places of love, safety, conflict, care, work, inheritance, and economic dependence. They cannot be understood as private feelings alone.

Communist theory asks what intimate relationships could become when survival no longer depends on property, wages, marriage, or unpaid domestic labour.

The family is also an economic unit

A household organizes food, shelter, care, money, and the raising of children. Under capitalism, these tasks are treated as private responsibilities even though the whole economy depends on them.

Women have historically carried much of this work without equal pay, recognition, time, or power. Patriarchy is a system of male dominance reproduced through property, family roles, law, culture, and violence.

Legal equality matters, but economic dependence can keep unequal power alive inside a formally equal relationship.

Socializing care expands choice

If healthcare, housing, food, education, and childcare are secure social rights, no person must remain with a partner to survive.

Socialized childcare means society provides trained care, safe spaces, food, education, play, and support as a common responsibility. It does not mean taking children away from the people they love.

Children can have more trusted adults in their lives while parents and other close caregivers remain important.

Elder care, disability support, cooking, and cleaning can likewise become well-supported social activities instead of isolated burdens.

Love without property

Property and inheritance have shaped marriage and family forms. When wealth passes through a household line, controlling sexuality and parentage can become an economic concern.

Common ownership weakens that material pressure. Relationships can rest more fully on affection, consent, shared life, and mutual growth.

This does not predict one universal relationship form. Engels himself warns that future people will develop their own practices under conditions we have never lived.

The guiding principles are:

  • consent;
  • real ability to leave;
  • material independence;
  • safety;
  • care for children and dependents;
  • equality of social standing; and
  • freedom from purchase or coercion.

Gender changes with social life

Gender is shaped by bodies, identities, culture, work, family, law, and history. Ending capitalist property does not cause every gender hierarchy to disappear automatically.

A transformed society must confront:

  • violence;
  • unequal care work;
  • exclusion from public life;
  • medical inequality;
  • rigid roles;
  • discrimination against queer and trans people; and
  • inherited ideas about masculinity and femininity.

The material basis of oppression matters, but people also reproduce or challenge ideas through institutions and daily relationships.

Children are people in development

Children need care and protection because adults hold greater knowledge and power. They also need dignity, voice, play, privacy, and growing responsibility.

Communal care can widen their world. Children can learn from many ages, take part in shaping spaces, and receive support even when a family is in crisis.

The standard should be the child’s flourishing, not the preservation of one household form at any cost.

What abolishing the family actually means

The “abolition of the family” does not mean abolishing love, kinship, private life, or lasting bonds. It refers to overcoming the family’s role as a compulsory economic unit and a structure of property and dependence.

Socializing domestic labour does not mean every personal act becomes a public service. People remain free to cook, decorate, care, celebrate, and build chosen relationships.

No historical author should be treated as having solved modern questions of gender or sexuality. Later research and lived experience matter.

What communists can decide now

Economic dependence can be ended. Domestic labour can be shared and socialized. Care can become a common responsibility without ceasing to be intimate.

Future family and relationship forms are open. Claims about what people “will” prefer belong as proposals or possibilities, not decrees. The companion Studies will bring feminist, queer, trans, disability, and social-reproduction scholarship into direct debate.

Where care leads

  • Social Architecture examines the spaces that make shared care practical.
  • Daily Life Beyond Wage Labour connects care to shorter work and free time.
  • Human Development and Alienation explains why intimate life is shaped by material relations.
  • What Is Settled and What Is Open shows how the project labels speculation.

Source notes

Primary texts

  • Friedrich Engels, The Origin of the Family, Private Property and the State (1884).
  • Karl Marx and Friedrich Engels, The German Ideology, Chapter I (1845 to 1846).
  • August Bebel, Woman and Socialism (1879, revised through 1910).

Further study

  • Lise Vogel, Marxism and the Oppression of Women: Toward a Unitary Theory.
  • Tithi Bhattacharya, ed., Social Reproduction Theory: Remapping Class, Recentering Oppression.

These works open different arguments. The Studies will not compress them into one tradition.
