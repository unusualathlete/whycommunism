05Communist Production and Life

Social Production: Labour That Belongs to Everyone From the Start

Work under capitalism is social in fact. People depend on one another across the whole world.

Yet each business begins as a private unit. It decides what to produce, sends products to market, and then discovers whether society will accept the result through sale.

Communist production makes labour directly social from the beginning.

The market judges after the work is done

A company may produce thousands of objects and discover later that they cannot be sold. Another company may ignore a need because meeting it will not return enough profit.

The market provides information, but it often provides that information after labour and resources have already been spent. It also measures demand through money. A rich person’s small desire can count more than a poor person’s urgent need.

Marx described capitalism as a society in which separate private labours become social through exchange. Their connection appears through prices and things instead of through conscious agreement among people.

Planning validates work before production

In communist production, society identifies a need and commits resources before the work begins. The labour is recognized as part of the common plan.

This does not mean a planner predicts everything perfectly. New needs appear. People change their minds. Experiments fail. A good plan expects revision.

The difference is that feedback enters an organized social process. A mistake is corrected through information, discussion, and changed production, not only through bankruptcy, unemployment, or waste.

Social need is discovered, not announced

No office can simply declare what everyone needs. Need must be learned through many channels:

  • direct requests;
  • local councils and service bodies;
  • usage patterns;
  • surveys and public deliberation;
  • waiting times and shortages;
  • ecological measurements;
  • reports from producers;
  • research; and
  • the ability to propose something new.

People should also be able to ask for unfamiliar goods and experiment with new ways of living. Production for use must not become production for whatever an administrator already understands.

Pre-ordering and modular production

Modern car production and digital fabrication show a wider possibility.

Instead of filling large lots with every possible model, a person can test options, choose specifications, and have the product made after the choice. Modular design lets one productive system make many variations without guessing every final demand in advance.

This method requires patience and reliable delivery. In return, it can reduce unsold stock, storage, weather damage, duplicated products, and pressure to manipulate demand.

The same principle can apply to furniture, machine parts, tools, clothing, and local fabrication.

Production organizes distribution

Distribution cannot be designed as a separate final step. What people receive depends on what society chose to make, which resources were available, and how work was organized.

If accessible buses were never produced, no fair ticket system can create them. If the plan invests in accessible transport from the beginning, the distribution problem changes.

This is why Engels warned against imagining one fixed rule of distribution for all stages. Distribution develops with production and social organization.

Room for initiative and surprise

Directly social labour does not mean every activity must receive advance approval. Care, art, study, repair, and invention often begin from personal initiative.

The wider system can provide materials, space, knowledge, and channels for sharing results without controlling every act.

It also does not mean every useful result can be measured before it exists. Research and art require room for uncertainty.

Planning must not confuse data with society

Private labour validated through exchange and directly social labour are two different ways of making social connection.

Open questions concern the institutions that register demand, protect minority needs, allow experiments, and prevent a planning body from mistaking its data for society itself.
