14Communist Production and Life

Science and Technology as Common Powers

Science is built across generations. A discovery depends on languages, instruments, public knowledge, criticism, care, and the work that keeps researchers alive.

Technology gives this social knowledge a material form. Under capitalism, these common powers can become private property and tools of control.

Communism aims to return them to common use.

Technology is social knowledge in a machine

A machine contains more than metal and code. It contains mathematics, experiments, craft, failures, standards, and cooperation.

Marx’s general intellect helps name this accumulated social power.

When a company owns the machine, the worker may confront society’s knowledge as the company’s authority. The machine sets the pace, records behaviour, removes skills, or threatens unemployment.

Under associated production, the same technology can reduce danger and necessary labour while its design and purpose become public questions.

Automation can create time

Automation transfers parts of an activity to machines or software.

Under capitalism, automation can remove jobs and wages while increasing the owner’s control. Under communism, reducing labour can directly create free time.

The goal is not maximum automation in every activity. People may value human care, craft, conversation, teaching, or performance. The question is which parts are dangerous, exhausting, repetitive, or better performed by a tool.

Research cannot be limited to immediate use

Some discoveries begin without a known application. Pure mathematics, particle physics, astronomy, biology, and speculative engineering can later transform many other fields.

Freedom of research means people and communities can pursue questions that matter even when no short-term product is promised.

This freedom should extend beyond formal universities. Public laboratories, workshops, observatories, libraries, datasets, and equipment can let more people investigate.

Resources remain limited, so large projects need social choice. A particle collider uses common labour and material. Its defenders should explain the scientific case, and the public should compare it with other goals.

Appropriate technology

A technology is appropriate when it fits its human, ecological, and practical setting.

The most complex device is not always the best. A repairable pump may serve a community better than a sealed machine dependent on rare parts. A local tool may work better than a universal platform.

Planning should consider:

  • safety;
  • repair;
  • energy and materials;
  • accessibility;
  • human skill;
  • privacy;
  • resilience;
  • ecological effect;
  • time saved; and
  • who gains control.

AI should inform, not rule

Artificial intelligence can organize information, detect patterns, compare scenarios, translate interfaces, and help model complex systems.

It should not decide society’s values or govern people.

Models learn from limited data. They can reproduce old inequality, hide uncertainty, and make the preferences of their designers look objective. Even a far more capable intelligence would not automatically share human needs.

People and accountable institutions must choose goals, judge tradeoffs, and retain the right to challenge a model.

Compute and data should not be concentrated in one political centre. Local and social bodies need meaningful access, privacy, independent expertise, and the ability to inspect or reject a system.

Knowledge grows through openness

Private patents and paywalls can slow the spread of useful knowledge. Common science can publish methods, failures, data, and designs for others to test.

Openness still needs privacy and care. Medical records, personal communications, ecological locations, and dangerous technical details may require protection.

The principle is shared knowledge with democratically justified limits, not secrecy by ownership or openness without responsibility.

Two false promises

Science does not produce one automatic political answer. Evidence can show likely consequences. People still decide which consequences matter and what risks are acceptable.

Technology also cannot repair a destructive social relation by itself. Renewable energy inside endless accumulation can still expand mining and material use.

Questions that must stay public

Knowledge should become common wealth, technology should reduce necessary labour, and AI should not govern.

Open questions include data governance, allocation of research resources, dual-use knowledge, ecological limits, automation choices, and the relation between expert judgment and public power.
