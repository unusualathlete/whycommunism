08Communist Production and Life

Daily Life Beyond Wage Labour

Most people organize their day around earning the money needed to live. Work hours shape sleep, meals, travel, care, friendship, and rest.

Communism changes this centre of gravity. The question becomes how much necessary labour society needs and how that work can be shared, reduced, and made part of a fuller life.

Wage labour and necessary labour

Wage labour is work performed in exchange for a wage under the control of an employer or employing institution. The worker sells the ability to work for a period of time.

Necessary labour is broader. Food still has to be grown. Water systems need maintenance. Sick people need care. A society without wages still has work that must be done.

The communist aim is not to pretend necessity disappears. It is to reduce its burden, distribute it fairly, and place its purpose and organization under common control.

A great deal of work can disappear

Capitalism creates jobs whose purpose comes from competition, private property, sales, financial claims, and organizational failure.

Some advertising exists to move buyers from one nearly identical brand to another. Layers of finance administer ownership claims. Managers may monitor workers because wages place their interests in conflict. People spend time fixing defects that a better system would prevent.

It would be crude to declare every receptionist, lawyer, accountant, manager, advertiser, or repair worker useless. Many perform necessary communication, access, coordination, explanation, and repair.

The useful task should remain. The social relation that makes it wasteful can change.

Shorter work changes the whole day

Reducing work by even a few hours changes more than leisure. It changes who can:

  • care for others without exhaustion;
  • attend a local meeting;
  • learn a skill;
  • take part in culture;
  • exercise;
  • cook with other people;
  • repair a shared space;
  • conduct research; or
  • simply rest.

Participation cannot be demanded from people whose time has already been consumed. A self-governing society must create the material time for self-government.

Domestic labour becomes visible and shared

Cooking, cleaning, childcare, elder care, emotional support, and household organization reproduce daily life. Capitalism often hides this labour inside families, where women have carried a disproportionate share.

Communist society can make parts of domestic labour into shared social services while protecting intimate and private life.

Communal kitchens may prepare some meals. Public laundries may use better equipment with less labour and energy. Socialized childcare can give children wider relationships and caregivers reliable support. Home care can be well resourced without becoming cold or impersonal.

People may still enjoy cooking at home or caring for someone they love. Socialization removes compulsion and isolation. It does not ban affection.

Work can become life-activity

Marx’s phrase life-activity points to activity through which people express and develop their powers.

A person may repair a machine because they enjoy understanding it, grow food because they value the land and community, or study stars because the question matters. Their contribution serves others and develops the self.

Some necessary work may never become anyone’s favourite activity. It can still become safer, shorter, respected, rotated where sensible, and supported by technology.

Choice needs real conditions

Telling someone to “follow your passion” means little when rent is due and healthcare depends on a job. Real choice requires access to necessities, education, tools, space, and time.

Communism expands the conditions in which passion can matter. It also lets people change direction instead of being trapped by the training or career they could afford when young.

Freedom is not permanent vacation

Life beyond wage labour is not permanent vacation. Human life depends on activity, care, upkeep, and creation.

It also does not mean private time is selfish. Rest, solitude, play, and personal relationships are parts of human development. Social participation should grow through meaningful access and shared life, not compulsory attendance at endless meetings.

Questions a real society must answer

The direction is clear: shorter necessary labour, free access to the conditions of development, social recognition of care, and the overcoming of fixed divisions of labour.

The exact balance among rotation, specialization, automation, personal preference, and social obligation is open. It will vary by task and capacity.
