Common Wealth: What Society Makes Together

Wealth is usually pictured as money or private property. That picture misses much of what makes life possible.

A clean river is wealth. So are public knowledge, healthy soil, trained medical workers, a reliable power grid, safe housing, useful tools, and time that belongs to you.

Common wealth is the shared capacity created by social labour and nature.

The social product

The social product is everything society produces over a period of time. It includes physical goods, services, maintained systems, and knowledge.

It cannot all be handed directly to individuals. Before personal use, society must provide for the conditions that keep production and life going.

Marx described several common deductions:

  • replacing used-up equipment;
  • expanding useful production;
  • preparing for accidents and emergencies;
  • the general work of coordination;
  • schools and healthcare; and
  • support for people unable to work.

These are not taxes taken by an outside power in the ordinary sense. They are parts of the common product kept for common purposes.

Real wealth is the power to live

Money can command useful things, but money is not the thing itself. A fortune on paper cannot perform surgery without a hospital, knowledge, medicine, energy, and people able to work together.

Real wealth means actual powers and use values. It includes the ability to meet needs and the free time to develop human life.

This changes planning. A project is not judged only by whether it increases output. It is judged by what kind of life it makes possible and what it takes from people and nature.

A machine that raises output but fills the air with poison may reduce real wealth. A machine that removes dangerous labour and gives people more time may increase it even if fewer saleable goods appear.

Shared provision can grow

Some needs are best met as public systems. Healthcare works better when everyone can receive treatment before illness becomes an emergency. Public transport works better as a network than as separate tickets competing for profit. Libraries become richer when knowledge can circulate.

Common provision can expand as capacity grows:

  • food staples;
  • healthcare;
  • education;
  • housing;
  • energy;
  • public transport;
  • communication;
  • childcare;
  • cultural spaces; and
  • tools for creative and technical work.

This does not require every item to become unlimited at once. Scarce goods still need fair ways of allocation. The task is to reduce scarcity where possible and use equity where limits remain.

Individual use still exists

Common ownership of productive resources does not erase personal use. Your clothes, letters, musical instrument, room, and chosen objects remain part of your life.

The important distinction is between a thing used personally and property that gives someone power over other people’s work or access to necessities.

Housing offers a clear example. A home can be secure personal space without being a financial asset used to collect rent or speculate on a shortage.

Common wealth includes the future

A society can consume everything today or preserve and expand its capacities.

Common wealth therefore includes reserves, infrastructure, ecosystems, and long projects. Building a railway may use labour and materials for years before the first journey. Repairing soil may take seasons. Basic research may produce no immediate object and later transform many fields.

Planning must connect present needs to future powers. People must be able to choose among plans that place different weight on current consumption, future leisure, ecological repair, and development.

Common does not mean confiscated

Common wealth is not an excuse for an institution to claim everything in the name of society. People need visible accounts, public reasoning, rights of challenge, and control over the bodies that manage shared resources.

It also does not mean all resources are interchangeable. A wetland cannot always be replaced by money, energy, or another piece of land. Planning must respect qualities, locations, and ecological relationships.

Access remains an argument

The social product, common deductions, production for use, and the growth of shared provision form the clear basis.

Open questions include how much is allocated through direct access, requests, labour certificates, queues, deliberation, or other rules at different stages. Those methods must be tested against need, freedom, workload, ecology, and the danger of administrative power.
