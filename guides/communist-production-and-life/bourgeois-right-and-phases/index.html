Bourgeois Right: Why the Old Rules Outlive the Old Rulers

A revolution can change who holds power faster than it changes everything people inherited from the old society. Factories, skills, habits, shortages, laws, and expectations do not vanish in one night.

Marx used the phrase bourgeois right for a rule that looks equal but still carries the limits of capitalist society into a new one.

Equal measures can produce unequal lives

Suppose two people work the same number of hours and receive the same claim on the social product. The rule treats them equally.

But the people are not identical. One may have children. One may need medicine or mobility equipment. One may be able to work faster because of health, training, or natural ability. An equal measure can leave them with very different real lives.

This is the problem of bourgeois right. It views people from one narrow side, such as hours worked, and ignores the rest.

Equity responds to actual differences in need and capacity. Equity does not mean rewarding a social rank. It means refusing to let one abstract measure decide every person’s access to life.

The lower phase

Marx described an early communist society as emerging from capitalism with the marks of its birth still visible. This is often called the lower phase of communism.

Production is socially owned and planned, but abundance is limited. People may still receive individual claims connected to the labour they contribute. Society also deducts part of the total product before individual distribution.

Those common deductions support:

  • replacement of tools and infrastructure;
  • expansion of useful production;
  • reserves for accidents and disasters;
  • administration;
  • healthcare and education;
  • people unable to work; and
  • other shared needs.

The exact form is not fixed. Labour certificates are one proposal. Broad free provision can also grow quickly in areas where direct access is practical.

The higher phase

The higher phase of communism becomes possible as productive capacity, knowledge, cooperation, and human development change.

The old division between mental and physical work loses its force. People are no longer trapped in one role for life. Necessary work takes less time. Labour can become a chosen expression of ability and social purpose.

Distribution can then follow the principle: from each according to ability, to each according to need.

This does not promise infinite objects. Needs still exist within nature, technology, time, and the needs of other people. The change is that access no longer depends on wages, profit, or a person’s value to an employer.

Phases are development, not two locked boxes

Lower and higher phases are useful terms, but reality will not change everywhere at the same speed. Healthcare may be freely available while scarce luxury goods still require limits. One industry may be highly automated while another depends on difficult human work.

The transition therefore has uneven movement. Old rules can shrink as new capacities grow.

The Commonalty adds an important point. Social self-government begins immediately and expands. It does not wait outside history for a final phase. The old state form is displaced as society becomes able to carry out its useful functions directly.

A concrete example: access to transport

Imagine a town with too few accessible vehicles.

An equal rule gives every person the same chance to reserve one. An equitable rule gives priority to people who cannot travel safely without one. At the same time, the plan expands accessible public transport so that the shortage itself becomes smaller.

The goal is not to perfect rationing forever. The goal is to remove the condition that made rationing necessary.

Two traps in reading the phases

The phases of communism are not a calendar with guaranteed dates. Any rough transition estimate depends on war, productive capacity, ecological conditions, international support, and inherited institutions. A number must never be presented as a promise.

Bourgeois right also does not mean that every use of counting is capitalist. A society still needs to count medicine, energy, hours, land, and materials. The issue is whether one equal standard rules people while hiding their real differences.

The unresolved problem of allocation

The central distinction comes from Marx’s Critique of the Gotha Programme. Communist distribution must be understood as a process of development.

Open questions include:

  • which goods become free first;
  • whether labour certificates are useful or harmful;
  • how work that cannot be timed fairly should be treated;
  • how needs are identified without creating a new authority over people; and
  • how ecological limits affect abundance.

These belong in the companion Study and in practical research.
