04Planning for Use and Need

Production for Use and Distribution by Need

Before sunrise, a baker pulls the first loaves from the oven. The room fills with the smell of bread. By evening, some loaves will be locked behind glass, marked down, and thrown away. A hungry person can stand close enough to smell them and still be separated from dinner by a number in a bank account.

Production for use begins from the bread, the hunger, the soil, and the work. Distribution by need gives access according to human requirements rather than the size of a person’s claim on property.

Use changes the first question

Production for exchange asks whether a product can be sold under conditions that return value to its owner. Production for use asks what should exist and what it takes to provide it well.

The difference reaches the whole process.

A shoe made for exchange must win a sale and protect profit. Its lifespan matters only where durability helps that goal. A shoe made for use is judged by fit, safety, repair, comfort, material, and the labour required to make it. If a durable design reduces future work, that is a gain even when fewer shoes are produced next year.

Communist planning therefore measures success through useful effects and human development, not a growing pile of sales.

The social product belongs to a connected process

No worker produces alone. Bread depends on seed, farms, mills, ovens, transport, power, sanitation, knowledge, and care.

Marx criticizes the idea that each worker can simply receive an untouched personal share of everything they produced. Society must first maintain the conditions that make production possible.

Common deductions are portions of the total social product set aside before personal consumption. They include:

  • replacing worn machinery and infrastructure;
  • expanding useful capacity;
  • reserves for accidents and disasters;
  • administration;
  • healthcare and education;
  • care for people unable to work;
  • ecological repair; and
  • other common needs.

What seems taken from a person as an isolated individual returns as a clinic, a train, a clean river, a school, a storm reserve, and a productive system ready for tomorrow.

The movement can be read from top to bottom:

flowchart TD
    A["Total social product"] --> B["Common decision about allocation"]
    B --> C["Replace and expand useful production"]
    B --> D["Build reserves for accidents and crises"]
    B --> E["Provide healthcare, education, care, transport, and ecology"]
    B --> F["Provide means for personal consumption"]
    C --> G["Society can reproduce tomorrow"]
    D --> G
    E --> G
    F --> G

Text alternative: society first allocates part of its total product to replacement, useful expansion, reserves, and common services. The remaining means of personal consumption are distributed within a system whose common conditions have already been secured.

Need is concrete

Distribution by need begins by opening the identical box and looking at the person in front of it.

A child, a pregnant person, an athlete, and an older adult may need different food. A wheelchair user may require more material support to reach the same freedom of movement. A drought-struck region may need resources beyond its current contribution.

This is equity: organizing support around different real conditions so people can flourish together.

Need has personal and social dimensions. A household uses food, clothes, and living space. A whole community needs sewers, libraries, transit, hospitals, parks, and communication. The strongest forms of common wealth often cannot sensibly be divided into private pieces.

Common provision grows

Some goods can be freely available early because charging each user adds little or serves no useful planning purpose. A bus has spare seats. A digital book can be copied. A clinic is built to keep a population healthy.

Other goods remain scarce. A rare medicine, a specialized machine, or housing in a highly desired location needs an allocation rule. Need, urgency, waiting time, ecological effect, and fair procedure may all matter.

As productive capacity grows and waste falls, the field of free access can widen. Society may stop tracking individual use of a good when the burden of tracking becomes more absurd than the scarcity it was meant to manage.

Abundance here means reliable access to a rich common world, never an infinite mountain of objects.

People still express preferences

Use is not limited to survival. People care about colour, taste, privacy, music, craft, celebration, and surprise. A society that provides grey coats in one size has misunderstood human need.

Planning can register preferences through requests, use patterns, local assemblies, design teams, consumer bodies, trials, and direct feedback. Flexible production can make varieties without gambling on vast speculative inventories.

The point is to let preference guide useful production without turning purchasing power into political power.

Production and distribution form one movement

Distribution cannot be designed independently of production.

If housing is built as private investment, unequal access will return through rent and sale. If transport is organized as common infrastructure, mobility can be distributed directly through service. If labour is social from the outset, products enter a common stock rather than first confronting society as private property.

The way society produces already shapes who can receive, what counts as scarcity, and which choices exist.

Need also includes the future

A present population can satisfy itself by stripping soil, draining water, and heating the atmosphere. The feast would consume the table beneath it.

Production for use must therefore include ecosystems and future generations inside its accounts. A forest supplies much more than timber. It stores carbon, holds water, shelters life, protects soil, and offers human joy.

Good planning asks how use can continue, renew, and deepen without consuming its own ground.

Need is not a blank cheque

Need makes a claim on common wealth, not a blank cheque for any object in any quantity. Some wants conflict with ecological limits, safety, other people’s freedom, or more urgent needs.

Personal possessions remain distinct from private command over productive wealth. Clothes, keepsakes, and living space do not give their holder power to make other people work for them.

Individual accounting may also survive for a time. Labour certificates or other transitional controls may remain while common provision and productive capacity develop.

Rules that must move with conditions

Communist production is for use. The total social product requires common deductions. Distribution moves toward need, and equity cannot be reduced to one equal measure imposed on unequal people. These claims form the settled ground here.

The boundary between free access and rationed allocation will change with conditions. Procedures for scarce luxuries, unique goods, personal customization, privacy, and interregional claims remain open design questions.

Conclusion

Production for use joins the useful thing to the life it can serve. Distribution by need opens the door. Between them stands planning: the common work that lets bread complete its journey from the field and the baker’s hands to the person who is hungry.
