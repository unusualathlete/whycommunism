14Planning for Use and Need

Humans, Computation, and AI

A planning model marks a rural clinic as a low-demand site.

Its records show few appointments, so the model recommends sending the clinic’s medicine and staff somewhere busier. The calculation is clean.

A nurse objects. The clinic is quiet because the bridge washed out, the bus stopped running, and patients cannot reach it. Low use has been mistaken for low need.

The model found a pattern in the records. The nurse understood the life that made the pattern.

A model is a made thing

A model is a simplified representation built for a purpose. It selects some features of the world, leaves others out, and uses the result to compare, predict, classify, or plan.

Every map does this. A railway map leaves out trees and bedrooms so the routes can become clear. That is useful until someone tries to use it to judge a flood.

Computational models can hold relationships no person can track alone. They can connect millions of products, materials, routes, machines, skills, and time periods. They can find bottlenecks, test scenarios, estimate uncertainty, and notice unusual changes.

Their power comes from simplification. Their danger comes from the same place.

Data carries a history

Data is often treated as if the world wrote it down by itself.

People chose what to measure, how to name it, whose report counted, and which absence became a zero. Institutions shaped the activity before it entered the table.

The clinic’s appointment count carries the broken bridge inside it, but not in a form the model automatically understands. A wage record can count paid care and erase unpaid care. A crime database can reproduce patterns of policing. A sales record can make poverty look like lack of desire.

More data does not wash away these relations. It can describe them in finer detail.

Workers, users, communities, and specialists must be able to challenge the category itself. Sometimes the system learned to answer a broken question.

Computation should widen human sight

A good planning tool can show consequences that would otherwise remain hidden.

If steel goes to one railway, which housing projects wait? If a region closes a coal plant, what training, grid, and transport changes follow? If drought cuts a crop, which foods can substitute without damaging nutrition?

Computers can compare many feasible paths and return them to public judgment. They can translate a national plan into local constraints, then gather actual results and show where the two diverge.

This is variety absorption: the tool helps people handle more possible conditions than they could hold in unaided memory.

The model stretches human attention. It should not replace human rule.

Prediction is not a command

An algorithm may predict that one action will reduce travel time. It cannot decide how much safety, disability access, beauty, or neighbourhood continuity should be exchanged for that reduction.

Even a highly accurate prediction remains an if-then statement. If society chooses this route, these consequences are likely. The choice of route belongs to people.

Algorithmic governance begins when a model’s output receives authority over people without a genuinely accountable human decision. A welfare system automatically rejects a claim. A workplace score assigns punishment. A planning engine closes the clinic because its objective says so.

Putting a person at the final screen does not solve the problem if that person lacks time, power, or information to disagree. Human control must be real enough to alter the objective, inspect the evidence, stop the action, and repair harm.

Artificial intelligence has no human mandate

Current AI systems can classify, summarize, translate, forecast, search, generate possibilities, and detect patterns. Future systems may become far more capable.

Capability does not create political legitimacy.

An artificial general intelligence, if one is ever built, would not become human by becoming clever. It could have different needs, no body like ours, and no place inside the mutual dependence through which human values develop. Intelligence alone does not grant the right to decide what humans should sacrifice.

The same rule applies to a brilliant human expert. Knowledge deserves attention. It does not create a title to rule.

The right to remain partly unseen

Cheap computation can make constant observation tempting. A centre may claim that opening every local process improves planning.

The result can be a society where every pause, conversation, movement, and mistake becomes a data point sent upward. Local units stop experimenting. Workers learn to satisfy the metric. Privacy becomes suspicious by default.

Operational autonomy needs an informational boundary. Wider bodies can know relevant inputs, outputs, safety conditions, and common obligations without watching every inner detail.

Direct access may be justified when harm, deception, crisis, or persistent failure requires investigation. The reason, scope, and duration should be accountable.

Human beings need spaces in which they can think, try, fail, care, and speak without an invisible evaluator leaning over every moment.

Compute should not become a new throne

Large models require chips, energy, networks, laboratories, and specialized knowledge. If all of that capacity sits in one central institution, formal democracy may coexist with technical dependence.

Councils, workplaces, regions, public-interest researchers, and independent auditors need meaningful access to tools and expertise. Important models should be inspectable, testable, and replaceable. Competing models can reveal how conclusions change with different assumptions.

Every village need not own a supercomputer. No body can be allowed to rule by saying, “only our machine can understand.”

The general intellect belongs to society. Its computational organs must remain answerable to that whole.

Explanation has levels

Public inspectability does not require every person to read millions of lines of code.

A worker needs to know which data affected a target and how to contest it. A council needs to compare objectives and likely consequences. Independent specialists need access deep enough to test software, security, statistics, and bias. Educators need to make the system’s important ideas part of common knowledge.

Different people require different doors into the same system.

An explanation has failed when its only meaning is “the computer said so.”

Uncertainty must remain visible

Models can produce a single number even when the world is unclear. That precision can look like authority.

A responsible plan shows ranges, missing data, sensitivity, and competing scenarios. It says which result changes when an assumption changes. It records where direct experience contradicts the model.

Uncertainty is not weakness to conceal. It tells people where caution, reserves, experiment, and further learning are needed.

The nurse at the rural clinic supplies one more kind of evidence. The bridge must enter the model, but her judgment matters before the software is repaired.

Judgment stays with the associated people

Computation can inform. People and their accountable institutions decide.

That principle still leaves difficult design work. Who tests important models? How are harmful decisions reviewed? What information must cross the boundary of a local unit? How much energy should computation use? How does society prevent technical roles from forming a closed caste?

None of these questions can be delegated to AI, because each concerns the distribution of human power.

Conclusion

The clinic was never empty of need. It was cut off.

A machine saw the silence and called it an answer. A human being heard what the silence meant.

The promise of computation is immense: more connections made visible, more futures compared, more errors caught before they become suffering. Its proper place is beside human judgment, extending our sight while leaving our hands on the world.
