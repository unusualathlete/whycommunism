02Planning for Use and Need

Capitalist Planning and Communist Planning

At closing time, a supermarket worker wheels a trolley of edible food toward the bins. Every tin has a barcode. Every shelf has a number. A computer knew when the bread arrived, how many loaves sold, and when the price should fall. It may even have predicted the waste.

A family a few streets away goes to bed hungry.

The scandal is not that nobody planned. Almost every movement of the food was planned. The scandal lies in what all that intelligence was built to accomplish. Capitalism contains islands of exact planning inside a social system directed by profit. Communist planning would connect production before the fact around use, need, and ecological survival.

Order all the way to the bin

Capitalism is often described as the opposite of planning. Real capitalism has never looked like that.

A large company chooses investments years ahead. It forecasts sales, sets budgets, manages inventories, studies workers, designs supply chains, and commands production across continents. Banks decide which activities receive credit. States build roads, train workers, protect property, place military orders, and rescue important firms.

This is capitalist planning. Its purpose is shaped by capital accumulation. A firm must recover its investment and survive competition. Even a thoughtful owner cannot simply ignore those pressures for long.

Planning already exists. The decisive questions are who plans, what they plan for, and how separate plans meet.

The market judges after labour has been spent

Under production for exchange, independent enterprises decide what to make and then try to sell it. Their work becomes socially validated through exchange.

This is ex post coordination, meaning coordination after the act. Demand, prices, profits, losses, unsold stock, layoffs, and bankruptcy report whether private decisions fit together. The report can arrive after a harvest has rotted, a factory has closed, or a family has lost its home.

Competition can push firms to improve a product. It also rewards cutting safety, hiding ecological damage, exhausting workers, and creating desires that keep sales moving. A trainer built to wear out may be profitable precisely because another pair will be bought sooner.

The shelf can be orderly all the way to the bin. The hunger stays outside the account.

Prices speak with unequal voices

A market price gathers information about exchange, but it does not give every need an equal vote.

Ten families may need homes. A developer may build one mansion because the buyer can pay more. A medicine for a poor population may attract little investment, while a small improvement to a profitable cosmetic receives a laboratory.

The market has not misunderstood its rule. It follows effective demand, which means desire joined to purchasing power. Human need appears only through the money standing behind it.

Costs can also be pushed away. A company may show a cheap product because illness, unpaid care, poisoned water, and future climate damage sit outside its accounts.

Communist planning decides before production

Ex ante coordination means coordinating beforehand. Associated producers estimate needs, capacities, and limits before committing labour and material.

They may compare several feasible plans. One might devote more work now to housing and immediate consumption. Another might build trains, power systems, or machinery that reduce labour in later years. A population can choose among those futures within the bounds of what exists.

Once a plan is chosen, workplaces and communities translate it into real activity. Results return as feedback. The next plan learns from shortages, delays, changing preferences, and discoveries.

Errors remain, but their consequences change. A forecasting mistake becomes a reason to revise production instead of a reason to throw thousands of people out of work.

The purpose enters every calculation

A capitalist enterprise asks whether a railway will return enough money to justify its cost. A communist plan asks whether the railway moves people and goods with a good use of labour, material, energy, land, and time.

Both questions involve calculation. They calculate different social purposes.

The second can include benefits that resist private sale: cleaner air, easier access for disabled passengers, less traffic, safer streets, and time returned to families. It can also compare the railway with housing, clinics, or ecological repair instead of pretending every good can be reduced without loss to one money sum.

The contrast can be held in five questions:

Question Capitalist coordination Communist planning
What starts production? Expected sale and profit Registered use, need, and a chosen social plan
When is labour judged social? After production, through exchange Before production, through association
What happens when a forecast fails? Loss, closure, layoffs, or rescue Investigation, revised output, and shared correction
Who speaks most loudly? Whoever controls purchasing and investment power Producers, users, communities, and accountable common bodies
What counts as a cost? Costs borne by the enterprise Labour, material, ecology, care, time, and effects on others

The table compares governing relations, not every transaction that may survive during a transition.

State direction does not settle the question

A state can own enterprises while preserving wage labour, hierarchy, production for exchange, and goals measured through profitability. It can plan capitalist development, military expansion, or rescue for private accumulation.

Nationalization can create useful capacity for socialization, but ownership paperwork does not give producers control.

The dividing line is a social relation. Are productive means governed as common wealth? Do workers and communities shape the goals? Is output organized for use? Can officials be recalled and plans challenged? Does the system move beyond value and class power?

Without those changes, a more centralized capitalism remains capitalism.

Markets are neither magic nor noise

Capitalist markets are not pure chaos. They can transmit information, adapt, and coordinate activity. The criticism is that their information is formed through property, class, exchange, and competition, then enforced through profit and loss.

Communist planning does not eliminate every market on the morning after a revolution. Transitional forms may persist while production is socialized and planning capacity grows. Nor does a plan remove scarcity, conflict, or mistakes.

The point is direction: away from blind coordination through exchange and toward deliberate coordination by the associated producers. That requires public alternatives, local knowledge, accountable administration, meaningful report and refusal, and worker power over the labour process. Historical planned economies provide achievements and failures to study, not a template to copy or a verdict that ends inquiry.

Conclusion

Capitalism does plan. It plans the pieces while accumulation rules the whole. Communist planning begins when society takes hold of the whole connection and asks a different first question: what should all this power be for?
