10Planning for Use and Need

Development of the Productive Forces

Load the washing machine, press a button, and water begins moving through a pattern most of us never need to learn. Metal, electricity, chemistry, code, plumbing, mines, factories, and generations of experiment have condensed into a quiet turning drum.

The machine can save hours of exhausting work. It can also be built to fail, locked against repair, and sold by workers who fear that the next machine will erase their job. Technology does not carry one social meaning inside its metal.

Productive forces are the powers through which people create and sustain life. What they become depends on who commands them and what purpose they serve.

Productive forces are larger than machines

Productive forces include:

  • human skill and health;
  • scientific and practical knowledge;
  • tools, machinery, and software;
  • energy and communication;
  • farms, factories, laboratories, and infrastructure;
  • forms of cooperation; and
  • living ecosystems on which production depends.

A railway is steel and electricity, but also timetables, trained crews, maintenance habits, public trust, and generations of engineering.

Destroy the social knowledge and the machine becomes a stranded object. Exhaust the soil and advanced farm equipment cannot manufacture fertility from nothing.

The same productive power can carry opposite social effects:

Productive power Under capitalist command Under associated control
Automation Cuts labour costs and may throw workers out of wages Removes danger and shortens necessary labour
Scientific knowledge Becomes patent, trade secret, or profitable application Becomes a common capacity for research and use
Infrastructure Follows property values and profitable demand Connects people according to use, equity, and resilience
Worker skill Is fragmented, purchased, and managed from above Develops through education, participation, and control
Nature Appears as an input or external cost Enters as the living condition production must reproduce

Technology changes with the relation governing it.

Capital develops power against the worker

Under capitalism, machinery embodies accumulated labour owned by capital. It can confront living workers as an alien power: setting the pace, breaking skills into repetitive motions, recording behaviour, and making unemployment a threat.

Science becomes a business and invention follows expected returns. Useful research may be neglected when no profitable buyer can be found. An improvement that saves labour can mean free time for nobody and lost wages for many.

Workers may reasonably fear a machine that arrives as their competitor.

Socialist industrialization changes the relation

Socialist industrialization would develop productive capacity under social control for use, reduced necessary labour, ecological repair, and human development.

Necessary labour is the activity required to reproduce society and meet its chosen needs. Better tools, organization, infrastructure, and knowledge can reduce its burden.

If a dangerous cleaning process is automated, nobody needs to lose food or housing. The time saved can shorten everyone’s work, expand care, improve maintenance, or open study and leisure.

Living people begin to command accumulated labour.

Free time is real wealth

Marx’s Grundrisse connects advanced production with the possibility of reducing necessary labour and expanding the free development of individuals.

Free time opens room to rest, love, learn, create, play, govern, investigate, and change activity. Those human developments feed back into society’s powers. A person who has time to study may solve a production problem. A rested carer gives better care. A musician changes what a community can feel.

A society of developed people able to direct their common powers is a higher productive force than a factory running every hour.

Development is selective

Communism has no reason to maximize every inherited capacity.

An advertising platform built to manipulate attention may be dismantled or transformed. Fossil infrastructure may need rapid retirement. Weapons factories may be converted. A fast assembly line may slow down because the safer rhythm is worth the extra time.

Other capacities may grow quickly: renewable grids, rail, housing, medicine, water systems, public laboratories, repair networks, and socialized care.

Planning asks which powers widen life and which consume life to keep themselves moving.

Durability and flexibility matter

Capital can be trapped by the need to use fixed equipment until its investment is recovered. A factory may keep producing an unwanted item because machines, debt, and profit demand motion.

Common ownership can redirect labour and material when needs change. Modular equipment, shared standards, repairable design, and adaptable skills make that movement easier.

Resilience matters alongside efficiency. A system with no spare capacity may look efficient on an ordinary day and collapse during a storm. Reserves, backups, local repair, and diverse methods preserve the ability to act.

The producer develops too

Ending the division between thinking and doing does not make everyone identical. It removes technical knowledge from the private language of managers while workers execute commands they cannot question.

Education can join theory with practical activity. Workers can rotate where useful, deepen specialties, learn the wider process, and help redesign it. Scientists can work with communities and producers who know a problem through direct experience.

The machine improves. So does the person standing beside it.

Scale must follow the work

Some productive forces gain enormously from wide coordination. Power grids, railways, semiconductor fabrication, climate research, and watershed systems cross local boundaries.

Other work benefits from local control, varied design, and intimate knowledge. Care, food, building repair, and cultural production often lose quality when treated as uniform output.

Centralization is not a virtue by itself. The correct scale is the one that lets the activity serve common goals with strong knowledge, accountability, and ecological sense.

Machines do not carry freedom inside them

Worshipping technology and waiting for automatic abundance would surrender human judgment to machinery. Social relations decide whether a capacity frees people or disciplines them.

Many human activities should remain human. People value care, craft, performance, teaching, and direct service as relations with one another.

Technological development cannot erase natural limits. Efficiency can reduce pressure, but endless expansion can swallow every gain.

Choices that remain human

Productive forces are social and material powers, not machinery alone. Their communist development should reduce necessary labour, widen free time, support equity, and repair social metabolism.

Open questions include appropriate levels of automation, large-scale versus local production, research priorities, technological risks, reserve capacity, and which inherited systems should be converted or abandoned.

Conclusion

Humanity has built powers large enough to appear as a world standing over us. Communist development means bringing those powers home. The drum turns, the water runs, and a pair of human hands is free to do something else. The machine should carry the burden. The person should inherit the time.
