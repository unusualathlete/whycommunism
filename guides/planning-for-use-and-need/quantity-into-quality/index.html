07Planning for Use and Need

From Quantity to Quality

At first the bus comes every two hours. People memorize its timetable, arrive early, and still watch it disappear around the corner. Miss it, and a doctor’s appointment, school day, or job interview can vanish with it.

Then more buses arrive. Routes join. The gaps shrink from hours to minutes. One extra bus only shortens a wait, but enough buses can change whether a person needs a private car at all.

A change in amount has become a change in the kind of life people can live. Dialectics calls this movement quantity into quality.

More can change the rule

Quantitative change means a change in degree: more trains, fewer working hours, greater crop yields, wider medical capacity, better storage, or stronger public knowledge.

Qualitative change means the thing begins to work differently. Water cools little by little, then freezes. A service expands little by little, then individual payment stops serving any useful purpose.

The point at which the old rule breaks is a threshold. Social thresholds are rarely as exact as the freezing point of pure water. They depend on institutions, habits, distribution, ecology, and political choice. They are still real.

The phases of communism name a development

Marx distinguishes a communist society emerging from capitalism from a more developed communist society.

In the lower phase, productive means are common and labour is directly social, but inherited limits remain. Individual access to some consumption may still be measured against labour contribution. This carries bourgeois right into a new society.

As productive forces, cooperation, education, common provision, and human capacities develop, the reason for that personal comparison shrinks. More goods can be accessed without individual accounting. Necessary work becomes less burdensome and more freely organized. The division between mental and manual labour weakens.

At a higher economic maturity, distribution according to need becomes the ordinary rule because society has built the material and human powers to sustain it.

Count the powers that widen life

A warehouse full of phones does not prove abundance if food, housing, care, time, and ecological stability remain scarce. The relevant growth is reliable common capacity, free time, knowledge, resilience, equal access, and people’s ability to govern common life.

An average can hide a village without clean water or a disabled person locked out of transport. Common provision must therefore grow where need is greatest. Equity is part of the threshold, not a reward postponed until afterward.

New quality can release new quantity

The movement also runs back the other way.

Once healthcare becomes a universal public system, society can coordinate prevention, records, research, staffing, and supply more rationally. That new social form can save labour and resources. Once knowledge is open, discoveries can spread without a patent toll. Once transit is common, cities can use less land for parking and more for homes and parks.

A qualitative transformation creates new capacities. Development moves through loops, not a staircase with identical steps.

The movement can reinforce itself:

    A["Useful capacity grows"] --> B["Common provision widens"]
    B --> C["Individual payment and accounting lose functions"]
    C --> D["A new social rule becomes practical"]
    D --> E["More time, access, and human capacity"]
    E --> A

Text alternative: useful capacity can widen common provision; wider provision makes individual payment less necessary; the new rule releases time and access; those human capacities can then improve useful production.

No automatic finish line

Productivity does not carry communism inside it like a seed waiting to open.

Capitalism can use greater capacity to produce waste, unemployment, military power, and luxury beside hunger. A communist society can also make poor choices. The transformation depends on associated producers using capacity to widen common life.

Ecological limits matter. Some forms of material output must contract. Quality can improve through durability, repair, public provision, and time even when fewer raw materials are moved.

The goal is rich life, not maximum throughput.

Quantity into quality is not a magic law that predicts a date. A rough transition estimate cannot tell a country when certificates, prices, or rationing will disappear.

It does not justify postponing freedom until every machine is built. Worker control, equity, and commonality begin during the transformation or the new capacity may harden into another hierarchy.

Higher communism will still face allocation problems. Unique places, rare materials, emergency medicine, and ecological limits will require judgment.

Which goods become free first, how personal accounting declines, and how regions with different capacities coordinate must be discovered in practice.

Conclusion

Communism grows when material changes gather enough strength to change the rule itself. A bus becomes a network. A timetable that once ruled the day fades into the background. People stop arranging their lives around the scarcity of movement and begin using movement to enlarge their lives.
