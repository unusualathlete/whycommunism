13Planning for Use and Need

Cybernomic Feedback Loops

Deep under a street, a water pipe begins to leak.

At first the change is tiny. A pump runs a little longer. Pressure falls in one block. A maintenance worker notices damp ground where no rain has fallen. Three pieces of information, each weak by itself, begin to point at the same hidden break.

If the signals travel, the system can respond. If they are trapped, filtered, punished, or lost in a pile of reports, the water keeps escaping.

Cybernetics studies how a system remains capable of acting through communication, feedback, and control. Cybernomics brings that method into economic coordination. It asks a practical question: how can millions of connected activities keep learning without forcing every detail through one head?

A living system must handle variety

The world can change in more ways than any office can list.

Pipes break. People move. Rivers dry. New methods appear. A storm blocks a road. A local meal becomes popular. A disease changes. Workers find ten different ways to solve what looked like one standard task.

Cybernetics calls the number of possible states and disturbances variety.

A controller that can respond in only one way will fail before a world that can change in a thousand. The answer is not to make the centre know every detail. The answer is to distribute intelligence, give each level enough freedom to absorb the variety it understands, and carry upward only what the wider system needs.

The maintenance team does not ask a national water body which wrench to use. The national system does need to know when thousands of similar pipes are failing.

Viability means staying able to become

A viable system can preserve its identity and purpose while adapting to change.

For a communist water system, viability cannot mean preserving any institution at any cost. Its identity lies in the social purpose: safe water, equitable access, ecological care, and control by the people whose common life it serves.

If the organization survives by hiding contamination, exhausting workers, or denying poor communities, the offices may remain. The system has already betrayed what made it worth preserving.

Cybernetics is therefore political from the beginning. Feedback needs an answer to the question: feedback toward what?

Five functions inside one stream

Stafford Beer’s viable-system model separates several functions that any enduring organization must somehow perform. They are easier to see if we follow the leaking pipe.

System 1: operations. Pump crews, treatment plants, laboratories, repair teams, and local distribution units do the work that makes water flow. Each operation needs room to govern its own method.

System 2: coordination. Repair teams share road access. Pump schedules avoid damaging pressure swings. Neighbouring units warn one another about work that crosses their boundaries. Coordination keeps independent operations from tripping over one another.

System 3: internal direction. A wider body holds the current system together. It allocates crews, maintains common standards, balances immediate demands, and makes sure local activity serves the agreed plan.

System 3 star: audit. When ordinary reports leave a puzzle, direct inquiry enters. Why does one district lose twice as much water? Why does another use fewer parts and have fewer breakdowns? Audit checks truth on the ground.

System 4: intelligence. Someone must look outward and forward. Climate models predict drought. Researchers study new treatment methods. City planners see where people will live. This function asks what world the system is becoming part of.

System 5: policy and identity. Above the question of how to fix today’s pipe stands the purpose of the water system itself: what it exists to do, how present operations and future change should be balanced, and which principles cannot be traded away.

These are functions, not five sacred office buildings. One body may perform several in a small system. A large system may distribute one function across many institutions.

The same pattern returns at every level

The repair team is itself a viable system. It has operations, coordination, internal decisions, future learning, and a sense of purpose. So does the district. So does the national water network.

This is recursion.

Each level receives freedom in how it works and unity in where it is going. The pattern repeats, but the form changes. Three workers around a broken pipe do not need the procedures of a continental watershed body.

When each recursion has real capacity, the whole system avoids two failures at once. The centre does not drown in detail. The locality does not become an island.

Information must move in more than one direction

Command moving downward is only one channel.

Capacity, difficulty, and criticism must move upward. Local units coordinate horizontally with peers. Different branches communicate laterally when their work intersects. Strategic intelligence brings in the external environment. Crisis channels connect people rapidly across ordinary boundaries.

The network needs enough paths for important information to reach someone able to act.

This is why a complaint cannot be treated as noise by definition. Twenty districts reporting the same impossible target are not twenty separate failures. Together they may be the clearest picture of the plan.

The black box protects attention

At a higher level, a local operation can appear as a black box. The wider body knows its relevant inputs, outputs, capacities, constraints, and alarms without watching every inner motion.

The black box organizes attention. It grants no political right to secrecy.

No centre can understand every work process through constant surveillance. The people inside the process possess experience that data cannot fully replace. Wider bodies should intervene when signals show persistent failure, danger, deception, or an opportunity that requires direct study.

Cheap monitoring can tempt a centre to open every box all the time. That produces more data and less understanding. It raises administrative cost, weakens initiative, and turns local intelligence into obedience.

The boundary around a working unit must therefore become accountable institutional design, not an accident of old technical limits.

Audit should find causes, not spread suspicion

The leaking district uses more water than its reports predict. An audit finds a concealed break. That is the familiar case.

Another district uses less energy and loses less water with the same equipment. An audit finds that workers changed the sequence of pump maintenance. Their method can be tested and shared.

Audit can expose dishonesty, verify a complaint, find a bottleneck, or discover an invention. It should arrive as a fact-finding tool when routine communication is not enough.

If audit becomes permanent suspicion, people begin producing clean reports instead of clean water.

Refusal can carry information without dissolving the plan

A worker may stop a pump that is about to fail. A district may report that a target will drain its reservoir. These acts need protected channels and a process for rapid response.

Local refusal cannot make every common decision optional. Other communities depend on the same water. The larger body may investigate, bargain, adjust, or enforce a necessary minimum under defined conditions.

Peer bodies without a stake in a dispute may sometimes help harmonize or judge it. They are one tool among many. The deeper safeguard is a whole design in which bargaining happens before commitments, ordinary communication remains open, decision-makers remain accountable, and saying “the plan is failing here” is treated first as knowledge.

Cybernetics cannot choose the good

Feedback tells a system how its results differ from a reference point. It does not tell society which reference point deserves to rule.

A water system could optimize revenue, total litres, ecological renewal, equal access, or some mixture. Each goal changes what counts as success. A perfectly responsive system serving a cruel purpose becomes efficiently cruel.

The associated population must govern policy. Experts can model consequences. Councils and assemblies can choose among feasible plans. Workers and users can correct categories that erase their experience. Officials can be removed.

Cybernetics supplies nerves. It must never pretend to be the conscience.

The diagram remains unfinished

The viable-system model offers a language for problems every large plan must face: local autonomy, coordination, internal regulation, audit, future intelligence, common purpose, and recursion.

It does not provide a constitution ready to print. Communist institutions must decide how people enter each function, what information remains private, which powers bind, how appeals work, how minorities are protected, and how international levels avoid domination.

The answers will vary with the activity. A water network, a school system, a theatre, and an emergency service should not be forced into one organizational costume.

The test stays material: does information reach action, can action be corrected, and do the people remain rulers of the system?

Conclusion

The damp patch in the street was the system trying to speak.

A viable plan gives that small fact a path. It reaches a worker, a district, a pattern, a national repair programme, and perhaps a new way of building pipes. The whole learns because the part could be heard.

That is what feedback is for: society feeling what its own hands are doing.
