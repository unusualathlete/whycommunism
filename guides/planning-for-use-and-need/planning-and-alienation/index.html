11Planning for Use and Need

Planning and the Abolition of Alienation

The belt keeps moving.

A worker notices that the new packaging tears near the seal. She slows her station long enough to adjust it. A supervisor tells her to restore the target. The target came from an office she has never seen, based on sales she will never know, for owners who will never use the product. By the end of the shift, boxes are leaving quickly again. Some will split in the warehouse.

The worker understands part of the process better than the command does. Her hands know what the number cannot feel. Yet the number has authority and she does not.

That is one small shape of alienation.

When our own power returns as a command

Alienation can sound like a private feeling, as if a person is merely bored or lonely. Marx uses it for a social relation.

Workers create products, machinery, knowledge, institutions, and wealth. Those powers gather outside them under the control of property. They return as rent, schedules, prices, layoffs, debt, and orders. Society is made by human activity, but it confronts human beings with the force of weather.

The market deepens this reversal. Millions of people depend on one another, but their connection happens behind their backs. A fall in price closes a workshop. A rise in interest stops a housing project. Food rots because buyers lack money. Each result comes from human relations, yet it appears to have no author.

People propose. Capital disposes.

A plan can repeat the same wound

Replacing the market with a plan does not automatically heal alienation.

Suppose a planning office sets the worker’s target, hides its information, punishes bad news, and blocks any challenge. The command now arrives in the name of society instead of profit. It still arrives from a power she cannot recognize as her own.

This is alienated planning: coordination stands over the producers rather than becoming their conscious activity.

Its tables may be accurate for a while. Its deeper problem is political. Workers learn that honesty is dangerous, so they report what superiors want to hear. Local knowledge stops moving. Officials begin defending the plan as a sign of their own competence. The map becomes more real to the office than the ground.

Then the plan goes blind through its own authority.

The difference can be seen at the same packaging line:

Question Alienated planning Associated planning
Who sets the target? A closed office or market pressure Producers and users inside a wider common plan
What happens to local knowledge? It is filtered through obedience It can revise methods, standards, and forecasts
What does an error threaten? Wages, status, or punishment The quality of a shared activity that must be repaired
Where does authority rest? In ownership, office, and hidden information In defined tasks under public and worker control
What is the worker’s relation to the product? A means toward an outside target A recognized contribution to a social use

Self-management changes each row together. A suggestion box alone cannot do it.

Self-management begins at the point of work

Self-management means that the people carrying out an activity possess real power over how it is organized.

At the packaging line, workers can set safe rhythms, test materials, change methods, report capacity, and stop when a defect appears. They can choose coordinators or accept appointed specialists where the task requires it, while keeping duties clear and authority answerable.

This does not place each workplace outside society. The packaging plant uses common machinery, energy, transport, and material. Its output may be needed by hospitals across a region. Wider bodies therefore set shared purposes, coordinate dependencies, and hold local units accountable for common obligations.

The point is to join the knowledge inside the hands with the knowledge across the network.

The product can become a human relation again

Under production for exchange, the worker may never know who uses the thing. The user may never see the conditions in which it was made. Money completes the meeting.

Production for use can bring the connection into view.

The packaging team learns that its material protects sterile medical equipment. Hospital workers report that one seal is difficult to open with gloves. Patients need safety, staff need speed, waste teams need a material they can handle, and production workers need a process that does not poison them.

All five belong to one social use instead of meeting as outside pressures fighting over a sale.

The producers do not need to meet every patient. They need institutions that carry the patient’s need into production and carry the producer’s knowledge back.

Planning becomes a form of recognition

When production is directly social, a person can recognize their activity as one share of a common power.

The bridge engineer knows the bridge belongs to a transport plan. The farmer sees how soil care enters future food. The teacher sees education as part of society developing its own capacities. The maintenance worker knows that keeping a lift running gives another person access to the city.

Recognition alone is not control. A poster saying “your work matters” cannot replace the power to change a broken process.

Real recognition has an institutional body:

  • access to relevant information;
  • time for deliberation and learning;
  • control over local methods;
  • participation in setting wider aims;
  • channels that carry disagreement without criminalizing it;
  • accountable coordination; and
  • a visible connection between work, social use, and free time gained.

When these are present, planning stops being a message sent to workers. It becomes something workers do.

Freedom grows through coordination

Alienation is not abolished by breaking society into isolated workshops. A local group can control its room and still be ruled by exchange, competition, or missing supplies.

Human freedom has become social because human powers have become social. Nobody builds a modern clinic, power grid, or railway alone. To govern those powers, people need forms able to coordinate at the same scale.

This is conscious social power: the capacity of associated people to understand and direct the relations they create together.

The wider plan supplies what local freedom needs. It can guarantee material security, share discoveries, move resources toward a bottleneck, and prevent one unit’s decision from quietly harming another. Local self-government supplies what the wider plan needs. It brings lived knowledge, initiative, criticism, and the intelligence of direct activity.

Freedom moves in both directions.

Work itself must change

Planning for use can remove whole fields of activity built around competition, sales, private exclusion, and the repair of avoidable damage. It can shorten the working day and distribute difficult tasks more fairly.

The remaining work still needs transformation. Repetitive or dangerous parts can be automated. People can learn the whole process instead of repeating one motion for life. Technical knowledge can move out of a managerial caste. Care, craft, concentration, and judgment can receive the time they actually require.

Some necessary tasks may remain unpleasant. A sewer still needs cleaning. Freedom does not require pretending otherwise. It requires that the burden be reduced, protected, shared, respected, and never assigned to a permanent lower group.

Compulsion’s field should shrink, not receive a kinder decoration.

Administration must become ordinary

Planning creates administrative work: gathering information, comparing alternatives, scheduling, checking, communicating, and correcting.

If this work hardens into a mystery, administrators can become gatekeepers of society’s own knowledge. Jargon becomes a wall. Access to the plan becomes access to power.

Administration must therefore come down to earth. Its categories should be public. Its roles should be teachable. Officials remain removable and paid without a separate life of privilege. Rotation may widen competence where it suits the task, while long training remains available where expertise is genuinely necessary.

Coordination remains, but it becomes ordinary human work that people can learn, inspect, and govern.

The freedom to answer back

The worker at the belt slows the line again. This time the stop is recognized as information. Her team examines the torn seal. A materials group traces the defect. Hospital users test the alternatives. The plan changes its specification, and the better method travels to other plants.

Nobody has abolished authority. The medical standard still binds. Deadlines still matter. The difference is that the standard belongs to a process the producers and users can enter, inspect, and alter.

The target no longer has the final word simply because it is a target.

Some questions remain open. How much time should everyone spend in administration? How should expertise be challenged without making knowledge irrelevant? Which local decisions can wider bodies override, under what conditions, and with what appeal? How can international planning avoid placing powerful regions above weaker ones?

No diagram can settle these in advance. Every institution must be judged by the same living test: do people gain practical power over the social relations they create?

Conclusion

Alienation begins when human power leaves our hands and returns as something foreign. Planning can tighten that grip or break it.

The belt, the target, the product, the hospital, and the worker already belong to one social world. Communist planning makes the connection conscious, then places it where it should have been all along: inside the common life of the people who make it.
