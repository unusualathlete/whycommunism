03Planning for Use and Need

The Economic Calculation Problem

Rain has started coming through the ceiling of a hospital laboratory. The same hospital needs a maternity wing, and the river outside has climbed higher each wet season. There is one construction crew and enough steel to begin only one major job.

Choosing one first delays the others. That is an economic calculation problem. It is real under every social system. The dispute is whether conscious planning can solve it without private ownership and markets for the means of production.

What has to be calculated

Economic calculation means comparing possible uses of limited means.

A plan needs to know:

  • what people need and prefer;
  • which materials, skills, land, and machines exist;
  • how different products depend on one another;
  • how long each process takes;
  • what can be substituted;
  • which ecological limits bind;
  • what uncertainty and risk remain; and
  • what is given up when one path is chosen.

That last item is opportunity cost. It is the best alternative lost through a choice. Steel used for the flood barrier cannot be used at the same moment for the maternity wing.

No social system escapes this fact by changing its vocabulary.

The market challenge

Critics of socialist planning have argued that market prices perform two tasks a planning system cannot reproduce.

First, prices allow unlike goods to be compared in one unit. Second, changes in prices communicate dispersed information about scarcity, demand, and possible substitutions. No centre knows everything that workers, consumers, and local producers know.

These objections identify serious problems. A planner who ignores information, local initiative, or alternative cost will waste resources. A plan cannot be defended by announcing that goodwill will make arithmetic unnecessary.

The next question is whether capitalist prices solve the problems as completely as the challenge assumes.

A price is information formed by a social relation

A price arrives through ownership, income, market power, law, unpaid work, imperial relations, and expectations of profit. None of these relations is neutral.

If water is scarce, its price may rise. That communicates something about scarcity. It also allocates water toward whoever can pay. A wealthy household watering ornamental grass may outrank a poor household needing safe drinking water.

Prices can omit physical realities. A cheap material may carry a huge ecological cost that no seller is required to pay. A patent may make a medicine expensive even when producing another dose is easy. Monopoly can raise price without any matching increase in material difficulty.

Market calculation is calculation through capitalist relations. Its answers cannot be separated from the question it asks: where can capital expand?

Planning can calculate in physical terms

In-kind calculation records goods and processes in their actual units: kilowatt-hours, tonnes, litres, hectares, machine-hours, nursing shifts, or passenger journeys.

Modern production already generates detailed physical data. A power system does not balance electricity by asking only its price. Engineers track load, voltage, generation, storage, weather, and failures. A hospital does not plan an operation from a money total alone. It needs the right staff, blood type, room, instrument, and time.

Physical tables can show how one output requires many inputs. Labour-time accounts can show how much social effort different methods demand. Environmental accounts can record carbon, water, soil, toxins, and regeneration.

None is a universal answer by itself. They are different windows onto the same material world.

Models can compare feasible paths

Input-output tables trace dependencies among branches of production. Optimization methods can search for combinations that meet stated goals within constraints. Dynamic models can connect decisions across time, so building a power line this year changes what becomes possible later.

Computers make calculations possible at scales earlier planners could not attempt. They can compare scenarios, locate bottlenecks, and update forecasts quickly.

The result is still conditional. A model may show the lowest-labour way to meet a target. People must decide whether the target is worthy, whether the labour is safe, and whether an unmeasured ecological or human quality changes the choice.

Mathematics can tell us what follows from our assumptions. It cannot choose the assumptions for us.

Knowledge lives inside activity

Some information cannot be gathered cheaply in advance. Workers know the sounds of their machinery. Patients know how a clinic treats them. A town knows which road floods first.

A viable plan does not demand that one centre absorb every detail. Local units govern much of their activity, report meaningful capacities and outcomes, and coordinate within wider limits. Audit and direct investigation become necessary when ordinary signals reveal a persistent problem or a surprising success.

Consumer feedback matters too. Registered requests, use rates, waiting times, repairs, returns, surveys, assemblies, and direct complaints can reveal changing needs without forcing every preference through purchase.

Calculation ends in choice

Suppose two hospital plans are both feasible. One protects more people from floods. The other expands maternity care sooner. No technical trick can erase the conflict.

The population may debate several plans, examine their expected consequences, and choose. Experts should explain facts and uncertainties. People living with the result must hold political power over the priorities.

This is why economic calculation cannot be reduced to finding one perfect number. It combines material accounting with collective judgment.

Worked example: the hospital choice

Evidence What it reveals What it cannot decide alone
Physical account Steel, rooms, machines, medicine, and construction time Which need should come first
Labour-time account The social effort each path requires Whether a slower path is safer or more just
Ecological account Flood risk, energy, land, and material damage How society should weigh unlike harms
Local knowledge How patients, staff, and the river behave in practice How this hospital fits every other regional need

The decision becomes stronger when all four kinds of knowledge meet. None deserves to impersonate the whole.

No machine can take responsibility

Prices that survive during a transition should not be ignored. They contain useful, if socially shaped, information.

Historical planning offers no automatic certificate of success. Bad data, rigid targets, weak feedback, political repression, war, isolation, and institutional failure can damage planning severely.

Computation changes the speed and range of the work, not the responsibility for framing it. A faster answer to a badly framed problem is still a bad answer.

A research problem, not an excuse

Communist society would still have to calculate physical requirements, social labour, alternative uses, uncertainty, and ecology. Local knowledge and public choice cannot be replaced by a central equation.

Open questions include which optimization methods scale, how objectives should be represented, how privacy and transparency interact, how changing preferences enter, and how to compare qualities that should not be collapsed into one unit.

Conclusion

Money has not locked the calculation problem behind an eternal gate. The problem lives in the rain on the laboratory roof, the next child who needs care, and the river rising beside them both. A communist answer must count the real world, listen to the people inside it, and make the choice visible.
