05Planning for Use and Need

Value Abolition: Ending Production for Exchange

A carpenter runs a hand along a finished table. The wood is joined cleanly. Four people can sit around it. It can hold a meal, a homework book, a tired pair of elbows, or a conversation that lasts past midnight.

None of that guarantees the table a place in society. Under capitalism, its wood, design, and labour face another test: can it be sold?

Marx calls value a social form of wealth produced by labour carried on for exchange. Value abolition means ending the social relation in which products and labour must pass through exchange to gain power and recognition.

Value is not usefulness

Water can be useful without being sold. A useless object cannot become nourishing because it has a high price.

Marx distinguishes use-value, the usefulness of a thing, from value. Value does not name every kind of importance, effort, or moral worth. It belongs to a society where separate producers make products for exchange and their private labours are connected through the market.

The table maker works before knowing whether society will accept the work. A sale provides social validation after production. It confirms that this private labour counted as part of society’s total labour under market conditions.

No sale can mean wasted work, even when the table could have served someone who lacked money.

Exchange makes an abstraction real

Carpentry, nursing, programming, and farming are different concrete activities. In exchange, their products are compared through money. The many kinds of labour appear as quantities of abstract social labour.

This abstraction acts on people outside an economist’s head. Firms cut the labour time required for a product or lose against competitors. Workers are moved, sped up, or dismissed according to what counts as profitable labour.

Society’s connection returns as an alien command. A market no person controls tells factories to close, fields to change crop, and families to move.

Changing the label does not abolish the relation

Suppose money is renamed “labour credits,” but enterprises still trade products, accumulate credits, compete, and hire labour. The name has changed. The underlying movement has not.

This is why value abolition cannot mean inventing a fairer currency or fixing every price to labour time. If products still meet as exchange values and production remains organized through exchange, the law of value survives in a modified form.

The decisive change is that society treats labour as social from the outset. Producers coordinate their activity before products are made. The table enters common production because a plan calls for tables, not because its maker must later discover a buyer.

Society can count without value

Abolishing value does not abolish measurement.

A communal plan still needs to know how much wood exists, how long different tables take, which designs last, what forests can renew, and whether the same carpentry skill is urgently needed for housing.

It can count these directly. Physical units describe materials and output. Labour-time accounts compare effort within appropriate contexts. Ecological measures track effects on living systems. Feedback reports use and quality.

Engels argues that a society producing in common can know the labour required for a product without expressing that fact indirectly as its value in a third thing.

A recipe still uses minutes after a restaurant stops selling meals.

The same table can carry two different social relations:

Question Production governed by value Associated production
Why is the table made? A producer expects to sell it A social plan has registered a use for it
When does the labour count socially? When exchange validates it From the outset, as a share of common labour
How are products connected? Through money and exchange Through planned relations among activities
What directs efficiency? Competitive pressure and profitability Useful effect, labour, safety, ecology, and durability
What happens to the account? It acts back through value and price It remains a tool governed by the producers

Counting continues. The social power of the count changes.

Abolition is a material process

Value cannot be voted out of existence while private ownership, wage dependence, competing enterprises, and market reproduction remain intact.

The movement beyond it requires:

  • social control of major productive means;
  • coordination of production for use;
  • common access to necessary goods;
  • the decline of labour-power as a commodity;
  • planned relations among branches of production; and
  • institutions capable of replacing market validation with direct social knowledge.

During a transition, money and prices may continue in parts of the economy. Their reach can shrink as planning and common provision grow.

One working estimate places movement toward labour accounting within roughly five to fifteen years. That estimate is conditional speculation, never a promise. War, productive capacity, international isolation, technical preparation, and existing socialization could change the pace drastically.

Value abolition changes the meaning of efficiency

Under value production, a company can become “efficient” by making workers rush, transferring pollution, and producing a fragile object that must soon be replaced.

For associated producers, efficiency means obtaining a useful effect with a wise use of social time, human energy, nature, and equipment. A slower process may be better if it is safer, repairable, and less destructive. A faster process may be better if it frees time without degrading the work.

No single measure settles the judgment. The useful life of the object and the lives of its producers enter the account.

The end of value opens another kind of wealth

Capitalist wealth appears as an accumulation of value that must expand. Communist wealth appears in powers people can actually share: health, knowledge, durable tools, rich relationships, public space, ecological stability, and time.

The aim is to return accumulated social power to the people whose cooperation made it, rather than turn every person into a better claimant on the same system of accounts.

Nothing becomes costless

Useful things do not become costless. Steel still takes ore, energy, machinery, knowledge, and work. A plan must face those costs more honestly than a price that hides them.

Numerical comparison remains. What disappears is the independent social power numbers acquire through exchange.

Difficult labour remains until people can remove it. Its purpose, direction, reduction, and fair sharing become common questions.

The difficult passage out

Marx’s critique treats value as historically specific to production mediated through exchange, not as a timeless name for effort. A scheme that preserves production for exchange cannot abolish value by declaration.

Open questions concern transitional sequencing, remaining markets, international exchange with capitalist economies, and the exact relation among labour-time, physical, ecological, and opportunity-cost accounts.

Conclusion

Value is society’s own labour wearing the mask of an independent power. Abolishing it means more than removing the mask. The producers must take hold of the relationship beneath it, so a table can enter social life because people need a place to gather.
