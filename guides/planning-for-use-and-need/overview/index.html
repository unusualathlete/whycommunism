Planning for Use and Need

Picture a coastal town with a storm moving toward it. The clinic needs backup power. Families near the water need somewhere safe to sleep. Fishing boats must come home. The flood gates have to close at the right hour, and the people closing them must know the road will still be open when they leave.

Nobody can solve this by choosing only their own part. The generator, shelter, boats, gates, roads, weather reports, and human decisions form one problem because one town has to live through the night.

Every society faces a larger version of that night. It must decide where its hands, hours, tools, land, and knowledge will go. Socio-economic planning is the organized process by which people learn what is needed, discover what can be done, choose among real possibilities, carry out their choice, and correct it in practice.

Morning comes, and planning continues

After the storm, the same coastal region must prepare for the next twenty years. It needs homes that can survive stronger weather, clean electricity, farms that can handle changing rain, clinics, trains, drainage, and enough free time for people to live.

No single number can tell the region what to choose. The planners need physical facts: tonnes of steel, hectares of land, trained crews, construction time, river capacity, medical demand, energy, and likely weather. They also need human judgment. Should scarce concrete go first to a hospital, a seawall, or new housing? Each may be necessary. Their order is a political choice.

A feasible plan is one that fits the means actually available. It cannot promise ten bridges with material for three. Feasibility does not decide which three should be built. It clears away fantasies so people can argue about real alternatives.

Planning is already everywhere

Hospitals schedule staff and beds. Railways coordinate trains across thousands of kilometres. A factory arranges materials so one missing part does not stop the line. Families plan meals against time, money, health, and preference.

Large capitalist corporations plan enormous internal systems. What remains unplanned is their relation to one another and to social need. Each firm acts for its own survival and profit. Society discovers afterward whether the combined result was useful, wasteful, or disastrous.

Communist planning starts from the fact that production is already social. It makes that social character visible before work is spent.

Information must travel both ways

A planning body can see connections that one workshop cannot. It can know that a shortage of transformers will delay housing, transport, and hospitals across a country.

The workshop knows things the wider body cannot see from a table. Its workers can hear a machine failing, understand why a standard task takes longer on wet days, or discover a safer method no national model contains.

A working plan therefore needs communication upward, downward, sideways, and outward. Local units report capacities and problems. Wider bodies communicate common goals and constraints. Peer units coordinate shared work. Researchers study changes in the surrounding world.

Feedback is information about what happened after an action. A plan expected a clinic to use a certain amount of medicine. Actual use differs. The difference belongs in the next decision, where it can teach the plan what the earlier forecast missed.

The full movement is a loop:

    A["Needs, capacities, and limits"] --> B["Feasible alternatives"]
    B --> C["Collective choice"]
    C --> D["Coordinated action"]
    D --> E["Measured results and lived experience"]
    E --> F["Feedback and correction"]
    F --> B

Text alternative: needs, capacities, and limits shape feasible alternatives; people choose; workplaces and communities act; results return as feedback; and the next set of alternatives is corrected.

A plan is a commitment that can learn

Once adopted, a plan has force. A railway timetable cannot become a suggestion whenever one station dislikes it. A power grid cannot operate if every plant follows a private rhythm.

Binding coordination must still be corrigible. Workers need safety stops, channels for reporting impossible targets, bargaining before commitments lock, and accountable investigation when ordinary communication fails. Audits can uncover obstruction, but they can also find a labour-saving invention worth sharing everywhere.

The plan becomes intelligent when the people doing the work can answer it.

Need is more than demand backed by money

Markets register a purchase, not a need by itself. A hungry person without money appears as weak demand. A luxury buyer appears as strong demand. A polluted river may not send any price signal until damage becomes somebody’s bill.

Planning for use asks directly about nutrition, health, shelter, care, mobility, culture, ecological renewal, and free development. It also asks whose needs have been neglected and what repair equity requires.

This does not make needs simple. People disagree. Some needs can be measured closely, while others involve changing desires, dignity, beauty, or risk. Planning creates a public way to face those conflicts instead of letting purchasing power settle them silently.

Different decisions belong at different scales

A neighbourhood can decide how to use a community room. A river basin must coordinate water across many towns. A railway network needs a common timetable. Climate stability requires international action.

Communist planning is therefore layered. Local freedom grows where local knowledge is decisive. Wider coordination grows where actions cross boundaries. The institutional form should follow the real reach of the activity.

Recursion names a working unit that is both a whole with its own internal decisions and a part of something larger. The same broad functions can appear at many levels, shaped to their own conditions. The levels rhyme. They do not copy one another.

When a plan stands over the people

One office choosing every shoe size or lunch would be a parody of planning. Personal choice, technical expertise, experiment, uncertainty, and emergency judgment remain part of life.

Replacing owners with an untouchable planning caste would preserve the deeper problem. When workers and communities cannot inspect information, challenge measures, remove officials, and participate in choosing goals, the plan stands over them as another alien power.

Computation can compare millions of relationships. It cannot decide what kind of life people should build.

The work still ahead

Conscious production for use, social control of productive wealth, layered coordination, public accountability, ecological limits, and human rule over computation form the settled ground of this series.

Many designs remain open: how plans are proposed and selected, which measures work best, how much autonomy each level needs, how disputes are heard, and which mathematics can handle uncertainty without hiding political choices. These are research questions for a movement, not blank spaces to cover with confidence.

Conclusion

Planning is society learning to hold its own productive power. The goal is neither perfect prediction nor total command. It is a town able to meet the storm because its people can see their connections, choose their direction, and repair their course together.
