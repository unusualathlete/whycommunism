08Planning for Use and Need

Universals and Particulars: A Plan That Can Listen

A country decides that every home will have clean water. In one village, people can hear a mountain spring running over stone. In another, children grow up beside an ocean they cannot drink.

The right can be universal. The pipe, pump, filter, skill, and daily work must answer two different places. Planning must hold universals, the common purposes and limits, together with particulars, the conditions and knowledge found in a specific place.

A common direction needs many paths

Universal goals may include nutrition, decarbonization, healthcare, safe housing, reserves, or a shorter working week. They express what the whole society has chosen to secure.

Particular knowledge includes the slope of a field, the behaviour of a machine, the language of a neighbourhood, and the skill of a crew. It lives close to the activity.

A plan that sees only particulars can break into disconnected units. Each workplace may protect its own comfort while a railway, river, or power grid fails as a whole.

A plan that sees only universals can issue impossible commands. The number on its table may be clean while the pump on the ground is broken.

The art is unity in direction with freedom and intelligence in execution.

Recursion joins whole and part

A recursion is a unit that operates as a whole while also forming part of a larger whole.

A maintenance team has its own tasks, coordination, information, and judgment. It belongs to a depot, which belongs to a city transport system, which belongs to a regional network. Each level needs enough organization to govern itself and enough connection to serve the larger purpose.

These levels should rhyme rather than copy. A small team may coordinate by direct conversation. A national system needs formal information channels and accountable bodies. Forcing one shape onto every scale would ignore the very variety the design must handle.

The centre does not need to see everything

In Stafford Beer’s systems language, an operation can be treated as a black box at a higher level. The wider body knows relevant inputs, outputs, capacities, limits, and warning signals. It does not micromanage every movement inside.

This creates operational autonomy: the people doing the work govern how they meet agreed purposes within real constraints.

Autonomy is not secrecy from society. Workers remain accountable for common resources, safety, output, and ecological effects. The boundary protects useful self-government, not private power.

Direct investigation becomes appropriate when ordinary communication fails, serious harm is alleged, or results remain unexplained.

Bargaining begins before the plan locks

Local units report what they can do, what investment they need, what labour conditions are realistic, and what risks a target creates. Wider planning bodies present system-wide requirements and connections.

The plan forms through bargaining among these realities. Several feasible alternatives may go to the population or its accountable institutions for choice.

Once adopted, some commitments bind. A local power plant cannot abandon the grid without consequence. At the same time, no plan can command workers to perform the impossible.

Strong communication before adoption makes emergency refusal rarer because local knowledge has already shaped the target.

Refusal is a signal with weight

A worker should be able to stop a dangerous production line. A local unit should be able to report that a target cannot be met safely or that an unexpected condition changes the calculation.

This right does not turn the whole plan into a suggestion. It opens a defined process of report, bargaining, investigation, and decision. In a grave emergency, wider coordination may still require binding action.

Peer bodies without a direct stake could serve as one harmonizing or adjudicating form in some disputes. This is one possible tool, not the single organ that resolves every conflict.

The principle is broader: disagreement must carry information upward without being renamed sabotage simply because it is inconvenient.

Audit helps the system learn

An audit can check whether resources disappeared, figures were distorted, or a unit ignored common obligations.

It can also discover excellence. Suppose one water team uses the same equipment and labour as its peers but loses far less water. An audit may reveal a maintenance routine that can spread across the network.

Audit should be a fact-finding mission when ordinary channels leave a puzzle. Constant intrusion would raise administrative costs, destroy initiative, and encourage workers to manage appearances instead of reality.

Authority follows the activity

Some work requires precise coordination. Trains must respect signals. A surgical team needs clear responsibility. During a fire, decisions cannot wait for a general assembly.

Authority over a task does not have to become a permanent rank over people. Delegates, specialists, and coordinators can receive defined powers, public duties, review, and recall. Selection may use election, appointment, rotation, sortition, or another accountable method suited to the function.

The relevant question is what the work requires and how society keeps that authority answerable.

Autonomy is not private ownership

Local autonomy gives a unit power over its work, not ownership of its output or a right to trade independently. Wider coordination gives a centre responsibility for common connections, not a right to inspect every private detail or dictate every method.

Recursion offers a question rather than a ready-made constitution: does each level have the information, freedom, accountability, and connection required to remain viable?

Peer adjudication is not guaranteed to be impartial. Selection, evidence, appeal, power, and bias would still need institutional design.

The design remains open

Local producers need strong control over methods. Bargaining belongs before fixed commitments. Refusal must carry meaningful feedback, and audit belongs where the system needs to learn or resolve a discrepancy. The larger plan remains capable of binding coordination.

Open questions include the exact rights of each level, dispute procedures, privacy, emergencies, appeals, and how autonomy survives when cheap computation makes intrusive monitoring possible.

Conclusion

A living plan does not choose between the map and the ground. The clean-water line on the map must reach a real cup. Common purpose travels down, experience travels up, neighbours speak sideways, and every level gains enough room to make the whole more intelligent.
