Historical Movement: How Societies Change

Stand beside a container port at night and watch an entire society move.

A crane lifts a steel box from a ship. Inside may be medicine, machine parts, shirts, grain, or phones. Software identifies it. A worker guides it. A truck waits beneath it. Roads, warehouses, banks, customs offices, satellites, languages, laws, and people across several continents have prepared this moment.

The container looks like one object. It is a knot tied in a worldwide web of labour.

Now ask who owns the port, who gives the orders, who receives the profit, who bears the danger, and who decides what gets shipped.

The first view shows humanity’s productive power. The second shows the social relations governing that power. Historical movement grows through both.

A society is a way of making life

A mode of production is the connected organization of productive powers and the social relations that govern them.

The productive forces are all the capacities people use to produce life: bodies, skills, knowledge, land, tools, machinery, energy, infrastructure, science, and forms of cooperation. The crane is a productive force. So are the worker’s trained eyes, the engineer’s mathematics, the road beyond the gate, and the shared language that lets a crew act together.

The relations of production decide how people enter that activity and who controls its results. They include property, class, authority, wage contracts, access to resources, and the rules through which the product is claimed.

A crane has no class relation hidden inside its steel. It can move cargo under a private shipping empire, a state authority, or an association governed by workers and communities. The same physical machine enters a different social life in each case.

A property title cannot move the container either. Owners need workers, technology, law, and institutions capable of making ownership effective.

Productive forces and relations form one mode of production. They develop together, pull on one another, and sometimes come into conflict.

The shift begins before the gate

The port worker’s shift may begin at six. The life that makes the shift possible began much earlier.

Someone prepared food. Someone cared for a child, washed clothing, taught language, drove a bus, treated an injury, and kept the house from falling apart. Past workers built the school where the crane operator learned to read, the hospital that kept the operator alive, and the water system that reaches the kitchen.

Social reproduction is the activity through which a society carries itself from one day and generation into the next. It includes producing goods and renewing the people, knowledge, institutions, authority, and natural conditions production requires.

Much of this work is hidden because capitalism counts most clearly what passes through a wage and a sale. Care performed inside a household can disappear from the official picture while remaining necessary to every paid shift. A factory-only view of history starts the story after half the work is already done.

Social reproduction also trains people to live inside a relation. A school teaches reading and mathematics, then may also teach punctuality, ranking, competition, and obedience to a timetable. Law organizes property. Media gives some lives dignity and makes others appear dangerous. Family forms distribute care, dependence, inheritance, and unpaid labour.

These institutions do more than decorate an economy. They help make the kind of people and expectations through which the mode of production continues.

They can also become places where continuation breaks.

The machine that saves labour

Suppose the port installs an automated system that lets fewer workers move more containers with less dangerous lifting.

One human possibility is immediately visible. Necessary labour has been reduced. People could work shorter shifts while receiving the same goods. Dangerous strain could disappear. Time could return to workers as rest, care, study, or public life.

Capitalist relations press toward another result. The company can remove jobs, intensify the remaining work, cut costs, and move more cargo. Competitors adopt similar systems. A power capable of freeing time becomes a weapon in the race for accumulation.

The productive force and the relation are connected. They also pull in different directions.

This is a contradiction: a relation whose sides depend on one another while producing opposing pressures. A contradiction is more than inconsistency in an argument. It develops through real activity.

Capitalism makes production increasingly social. A single phone may contain materials and labour from many countries, scientific knowledge inherited from generations, and logistics coordinated across oceans. Control remains divided among private capitals competing to claim the result. Humanity learns to cooperate on a planetary scale while its common power returns as the command of corporations and markets.

The contradiction does not guarantee one ending. Automation can deepen domination for decades. It can also give workers a clearer basis for demanding shorter labour and common control. The relation becomes historical through struggle.

History happens in the working day

Class struggle is conflict among classes over production, property, power, time, and the shape of society.

It appears in revolutions and civil wars, when the structure of rule is fought over openly. It also appears in the length of a lunch break, the speed of a machine, the price of rent, the closure of a school, the right to organize, a law protecting land, or a battle over who controls a public budget.

When port workers stop work, ships wait and warehouses empty. The strike reveals a fact daily routine had hidden: property directs the port, but labour makes it move.

The outcome reshapes both sides. Workers may win a shorter day and discover their collective capacity. Owners may introduce machinery, move operations, change the law, divide the workforce, or concede enough to restore production. The next struggle begins on altered ground.

History therefore moves through advances, defeats, adaptations, and leaps. A victory can change an institution without ending the mode of production. A defeat can destroy an organization while leaving behind knowledge that returns years later. A revolution can break old property and still carry old habits of command into new institutions.

People make history with materials inherited from history.

When a relation becomes a chain

Marx used the image of a fetter for relations that begin blocking the development or human use of productive powers.

The port can move enough food to prevent hunger, while food travels toward buyers offering the highest return. Medical knowledge can circulate instantly, while patents and prices close access. Labour-saving machinery can shorten the working day, while workers experience it as unemployment and speedup. Society can coordinate across a planet, while rival capitals and states divide that cooperation through secrecy, competition, sanctions, and war.

The relation has become a chain around the power it helped develop.

Capitalism performed immense historical work. It transformed production, created a world market, concentrated workers, revolutionized science and transport, and expanded human capacities beyond anything earlier class societies achieved. The same accumulation now drives ecological destruction, crisis, planned waste, and a permanent hunger for growth on a finite planet.

Calling the relation a fetter does not mean the machine itself will break the chain. A possibility has opened. Only organized people can take hold of it.

Humanity can bring its social powers under conscious control through associated production. That requires workers to understand themselves as the people already carrying the world and to build the political capacity to govern it. The productive forces make such a transformation materially possible. Class struggle decides whether the possibility develops toward emancipation, reaction, or ruin.

There is no single staircase

History has often been drawn as one staircase that every society must climb in the same order.

Real development is uneven and connected. Colonial conquest destroyed some ways of life, forced others into export production, seized land, moved enslaved and indentured workers, and redirected entire regions toward the accumulation of distant powers. Industrial growth in one country could depend on raw materials, markets, and suffering elsewhere.

War can destroy decades of productive development in a month. Ecological conditions shape what forms of life are possible. Indigenous communities may preserve relations to land that a supposedly more advanced economy tears apart. A society can combine digital finance, subsistence farming, industrial factories, unpaid household labour, and older communal practices at the same time.

Historical materialism has to study these concrete paths. It cannot turn one European sequence into a timetable for humanity.

The same care applies to technology. A tool enters property, class, culture, ecology, and political struggle. It does not bring a social order folded inside its instructions. Neither does history move by economics alone. State power, science, religion, family, race, gender, nation, organization, and ideas can redirect the development of production because they are active parts of social life.

The hands inside the container

The crane lowers the container onto a truck. A gate opens. The truck disappears into the night, carrying a world of labour toward people who may never know one another’s names.

This movement can look automatic. Prices changed, demand rose, the market responded, history advanced.

Nothing in it is automatic. People learned the skills, built the machines, enforced the property, prepared the workers, fought over the wages, maintained the roads, and decided which need could appear as profitable demand. Each generation inherits this moving structure and helps reproduce it, usually before seeing the whole.

Historical materialism makes the structure visible without turning it into fate.

Societies change as people develop new productive powers, reproduce relations, encounter contradictions, and struggle over what those powers will become. Crisis can lead to reform, reaction, war, revolution, or collapse. No clock inside capitalism counts down to communism.

History has no engine outside human life. Its engine is human life producing itself, meeting the limits of its relations, and fighting to make different ones.

The world moves because people carry it. It changes when they learn they can carry it another way.
