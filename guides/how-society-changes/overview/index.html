01How Society Changes

Materialism: Start with the Real World

A child puts their head down on a school desk.

The teacher sees closed eyes during a lesson and writes a familiar word in the margin: lazy.

The word feels like an explanation. It is short, personal, and complete. The problem seems to live inside the child.

A materialist begins earlier.

What time did the child sleep? Was the room too hot, crowded, loud, or unsafe? Did someone work a night shift and leave the child caring for a younger brother? Was there breakfast? How much does food cost near the family’s home? How long is the trip to school? What wages enter the household, what rent leaves it, and what happens when both are due on the same day?

The child may still have made choices. Materialism does not erase them. It finds the world in which those choices became possible, difficult, or desperate.

That is its first discipline: explain the real through the real.

Rain does not wait for belief

Rain falls on an empty playground whether anybody understands the weather. A virus enters a body without asking which theory of illness the body accepts. A bridge carries its load or breaks beneath it.

Materialism begins from a world that exists independently of our thoughts about it. Nature did not wait for a human mind before it began to move. Human thought arose inside nature, through living bodies capable of sensation, memory, language, labour, and social life.

This does not make thought small. A weather forecast can move thousands of people away from a coast. Medical knowledge can stop a virus from moving through a community. An engineer’s calculation can keep the bridge standing.

Ideas become powerful when embodied people organize activity through them.

Materialism therefore asks two questions together. What exists outside our ideas? How do ideas become real forces inside that world?

The lazy label already has a history and a body. It may appear in a teacher’s report, shape a parent’s anger, lower an expectation, justify punishment, and settle into the child’s own sense of worth. A word becomes a material force through records, routines, authority, and repeated action.

A different understanding can also take material form. Teachers can record how many students arrive without food. Parents can compare wages and transport. Students can describe mornings adults never see. A breakfast programme can move from an idea to a kitchen, a budget, deliveries, schedules, warm bowls, and children awake enough to learn.

Thought matters because people can build it into the world.

A life is held up by other lives

Every society has to produce and reproduce life.

Food must grow. Water must remain safe. Sick people need care. Children have to be raised. Skills pass from one generation to another. Buildings and machines decay unless someone maintains them. People need sleep, affection, language, and a reason to continue.

These activities happen through definite relations. Someone owns the land. Someone sets the working day. Someone prepares the meal after paid work ends. Someone receives the finished product. Someone else is kept outside the gate.

Historical materialism studies how people produce and reproduce their lives, how these activities form classes and institutions, and how struggles within them move history. It looks at a society’s productive powers together with the relations that govern those powers.

This is why hunger in the classroom leads far beyond the classroom. The family wage may lead to a factory. The factory leads to owners, machines, laws, markets, and workers organized or divided. Food prices lead to farms, landlords, transport, weather, debt, and global trade. Rent leads to property and the shape of the city. The school budget leads to taxes, political power, and decisions about which children are considered worth feeding.

No child contains all these relations inside their stomach. Yet the stomach feels them.

Historical materialism follows the chain until a private trouble reveals the social organization inside it.

Things become themselves through relations

A bowl seems like one thing. Place it on a shelf and it holds air. Put food inside and it becomes breakfast. Place it at the center of a family dispute and it becomes a question of care, money, time, and power. Break it, and a hand that once ignored it may suddenly need to know who cleans the floor.

Dialectics studies things through their relations, tensions, movement, and development.

It asks what a thing depends on, what it acts upon, which sides hold it together, and where those sides pull against one another. A school is students and teachers, but also buildings, meals, transport, exams, family labour, state policy, childhood, and ideas about knowledge. Change one relation and the others may shift in ways nobody expected.

Some relations contain contradictions. A contradiction is a unity whose connected sides also struggle against each other. Capitalist employers need workers’ knowledge and effort, while their drive to control costs pushes them to reduce workers’ power and wages. A school says it develops every child, while a competitive ranking system may gain its meaning by placing some children below others.

Contradiction is not a magic word for anything confusing. It identifies a real relation that produces pressures for change.

Dialectics also follows development. The hungry child is shaped by conditions, then acts inside them. A parent speaks at a meeting. A teacher changes a report. Students organize. The school opens a kitchen. New problems appear: the food is poor, the hours are wrong, or children feel ashamed to enter. The solution changes the situation and gives people new knowledge for the next change.

The world moves while we are trying to understand it.

People are shaped and shaping

An older kind of materialism could describe people as objects pushed around by circumstances. Marx rejected that passivity.

People inherit conditions they did not choose. They also work on those conditions. In doing so, they change the world and develop new powers within themselves.

Praxis is this reflective, purposeful activity. It joins understanding with action and lets each correct the other.

Consider a tenants’ building where water stains spread across several ceilings. Each family complains alone. The landlord calls every leak a separate accident. Then tenants meet. They place photographs, dates, repair requests, illnesses, and apartment numbers on one table. A pattern appears that nobody could see from a single room.

Organization produces knowledge.

The tenants act on that knowledge. Perhaps they win repairs and discover that the plumbing is worse than expected. Perhaps management retaliates, revealing which legal protections exist only on paper. Perhaps one demand excludes disabled tenants, and the group has to learn how its own method hid them.

Action tests the first explanation. Reflection changes the next action. The people involved become more capable of investigation, trust, argument, and collective power.

Praxis is how a class can move from experiencing the world as fate to understanding itself as one of the forces able to change it.

Ideas have addresses

Materialism has sometimes been flattened into the claim that economics causes everything. That picture cannot explain the world it claims to study.

Law can organize property and redirect investment. Scientific knowledge can create new productive powers. Religion can sanctify a ruler or give oppressed people a language of revolt. Racism is false as a ranking of human worth, yet it can be built into borders, policing, employment, housing, medicine, and inherited wealth. Culture can train desire long before a person receives a wage.

These things are material because they exist through bodies, practices, texts, buildings, habits, communication, and institutions. They develop their own histories while remaining connected to the production of social life.

Pain is material even though nobody can hold it like a brick. Friendship is material because living people create and sustain a relation that changes what they can endure and do. Software is material activity organized through machines, energy, code, workplaces, and users. A promise can reorder a life if people act through it.

The material world contains relations, processes, meanings, absences, memories, and possibilities. It is larger than a pile of solid objects.

A method that has to remain alive

Materialism gives every later part of the communist project a habit of attention.

When an institution claims to serve everyone, ask how it is produced and who can control it. When a technology promises freedom, ask whose labour it saves and who receives the time. When a plan looks perfect, ask what knowledge it cannot see and how people can correct it. When an idea appears eternal, ask what daily practices keep teaching it. When a crisis arrives, ask which contradiction has broken into view.

No method can provide every answer before investigation begins. Serious disputes remain about dialectics, biology and social development, the independence of political and cultural institutions, and the philosophical language best suited to Marx’s work. These belong in deeper study, where disagreement can be shown rather than hidden.

The foundation is firm. Nature exists beyond our descriptions. Human beings think as embodied social beings within it. They produce a world that shapes them in return. Ideas gain power through organized life. Conditions limit action, and action can change the limits.

Return now to the child at the desk.

The word lazy made the child smaller than the world acting through them. Materialism widens the frame until the hidden people, prices, rooms, roads, meals, and decisions come into view. Then it asks where a hand can enter the picture.

The real world is not a prison around thought. It is the place where thought can grow hands.
