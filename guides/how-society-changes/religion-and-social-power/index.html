05How Society Changes

Religion and Social Power

At a wake, somebody brings rice.

Someone arranges chairs. Someone stays beside the family after midnight. People who cannot reverse death sing, pray, tell stories, wash cups, hold a shaking hand, and promise the dead will not vanish from memory.

Religion lives here before it appears in a book of doctrine.

It gives suffering a language. It gathers people around grief. It can preserve a community’s oldest memory and place a frightened person inside a story larger than one life. It can also place priests above that community, bless rulers, sanctify property, command obedience, and send hope into another world while injustice remains secure in this one.

Calling believers foolish explains none of this.

A materialist account asks what human need a belief answers, which social powers it carries, who controls its institutions, and what kind of action it makes possible.

Pain can speak in the language available to it

Marx’s famous comparison between religion and opium is often used as an insult. Read inside his argument, it holds two truths together.

Religion expresses real suffering. It is also a protest against that suffering. In Marx’s time, opium was known as a substance that could ease pain without curing the wound. The comfort was real. So was the wound.

A family praying through a child’s illness may be finding courage, community, and a way to endure uncertainty. Their prayer did not create the hospital shortage, the price of medicine, the polluted water, or the body in pain. Mocking the prayer leaves every cause untouched and may take away one of the few forms of care the family possesses.

The same belief can direct attention away from a preventable cause. Sickness may be treated as a test from God while an employer hides toxic exposure. Poverty may be praised as holy while a church owns land and protects the wealthy. A promised reward after death can make suffering appear easier to bear and therefore easier for rulers to preserve.

There is no timeless political effect called religion. The effect depends on the suffering, doctrine, class relation, institution, history, and people acting through it.

Materialism listens to the prayer and investigates the wound.

Human powers can leave home

Alienation is a separation in which people’s own activity, products, or social powers confront them as something foreign and independent.

Workers build an institution they cannot control. Citizens create a government that appears unreachable. Generations make a custom, and the living experience it as a command that came from outside history. Humanity creates an image of perfect love, justice, knowledge, and power, then places those capacities in a being above humanity.

The relation turns upside down. The creation becomes the creator. Human beings kneel before their own powers in an alien form.

This does not mean every believer consciously invented a god and forgot doing it. Religion is inherited. It enters through family, language, art, fear, ritual, community, and experiences people interpret as sacred. The materialist claim concerns the social relation: powers belonging to human life can appear as powers humanity lacks and must receive from elsewhere.

Picture a farming community after a failed harvest. Survival depended on soil, rain, seed, stored knowledge, shared labour, transport, credit, and irrigation. A ritual may help people grieve, distribute food, preserve cooperation, and carry hope into the next planting. It may also hide the fact that one landowner controlled the water or that debt forced farmers to plant a crop poorly suited to the soil.

The ritual can hold people together. The explanation can hold a relation of power out of sight. Both can be true in the same village.

Alienation is powerful because it begins with something genuinely human.

Capitalism builds altars without gods

A modern office may consider itself completely secular. It still speaks as if invisible powers demand sacrifice.

The market has lost confidence. Investors need reassurance. The economy requires lower wages. A factory must close.

Human beings produced the factory, goods, prices, contracts, and investment system. Then their own relations return as commands from a thing called the market.

Marx called one form of this inversion commodity fetishism. Under capitalism, social relations among producers appear as relations among commodities, money, and prices. The movement of things hides the people whose activity gives those things their power.

Money offers the clearest image. It can open a house, cross a border, command labour, buy silence, secure care, and turn a private wish into someone else’s working day. These are human powers organized through a social relation, yet they seem to live inside the money itself.

That is why Marx’s writing about capital reaches so often for gods, monsters, vampires, magic, and sacrifice. The images describe how alienated social power is experienced. Dead labour appears to wake and command the living. People feed capital with their time, then hear that its needs must come first.

The metaphor does not claim a bank account is supernatural. It reveals a society behaving as if its own creation were a god.

An atheist can escape belief in heaven and still kneel before profitability as an unquestionable fact.

A church can crown a king or shelter a rebel

Religious institutions gather resources, authority, memory, education, art, buildings, and disciplined communities. Those powers can serve opposite sides of a struggle.

A church can teach that rulers were chosen by God. It can control schools, police sexuality, own property, protect abusers, and make obedience a sacred duty. Religious differences can be used to divide workers who share material interests.

The language of faith can also give oppressed people dignity denied by the ruling order. A congregation can feed strikers, hide the persecuted, preserve forbidden songs, and say that no earthly ruler deserves worship. Engels studied early Christianity as a movement among oppressed people while refusing to collapse its aims into modern communism.

The same scripture may be read from a palace and a prison. The words do not act alone. Class position, organization, interpretation, and historical conditions give them direction.

This is why a communist movement gains little by declaring war on belief as belief. A worker does not stop being exploited upon entering a church, mosque, temple, or shrine. Believers and nonbelievers can fight together over wages, land, housing, war, racism, and public care.

Materialists should criticize clerical power, doctrines that justify oppression, and claims that fail against evidence. They should also recognize the human need carried through a belief and the forms of solidarity a religious community may already possess.

Secularism gives this relation a political form. Public institutions grant no religion the power to rule. Every person has freedom of conscience, including the freedom to believe, change belief, practice a faith, criticize it, or live without one. Institutions remain accountable to common public reason and law rather than clerical authority.

Secularism protects believers from other religions and protects everyone from rule by any of them.

A movement needs memory without a priesthood

Religion has learned how to carry meaning across centuries.

Ritual makes memory physical. A song lets strangers breathe together. A calendar returns people to shared stories. Repeated practices can cultivate patience, courage, care, sacrifice, and belonging. A community can hold a grieving person when individual strength has run out.

Revolutionary movements also need durable memory, moral responsibility, collective care, and the ability to continue through defeat. A pamphlet can explain a programme. It cannot by itself create trust at three in the morning, teach people how to mourn their dead, or make solidarity into a daily habit.

There is a real field of inquiry here. Movements can study shared practice, symbols, ethical formation, celebration, grief, and forms of consciousness developed through collective life.

The danger is equally real. An organization can turn its leaders into guardians of truth, make doubt feel like sin, use ritual to prevent thought, and demand emotional surrender as proof of loyalty. A party built to overcome alienated power can create a new sacred authority over its members.

The answer cannot be copied in advance from a church. Work on revolutionary virtue, ritual, faith, and church-like organizational capacities remains unfinished and contentious. Its value will depend on whether it develops people’s powers of judgment and solidarity or takes those powers away.

A communist organization needs memory without scripture, commitment without worship, and shared moral life without a priesthood.

Return the power without insulting the hope

At the wake, morning begins to enter the room. Cups are washed. Chairs are stacked. The family remains bereaved, but they have not crossed the night alone.

A materialist can see the labour inside this consolation. People carried food, words, music, memory, and one another. The strength appeared through religion because religion gave it a form.

The political task is larger than tearing away that form. It is to build a world in which people recognize and govern the powers they already create together. Grief will remain. Death will remain. Wonder will remain. Questions about existence may remain. Exploitation, abandonment, and helplessness do not have to.

Religion cannot be abolished by command because the human conditions that sustain it cannot be argued out of existence. Clerical rule can be broken. Freedom of conscience can be protected. Alienated social powers can be returned to common life.

The goal is not to empty heaven by force.

It is to bring justice, care, knowledge, beauty, community, and human power home.
