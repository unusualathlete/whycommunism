09Socialism or Extinction

Doomerism and Organized Hope

After a flood, a brown line remains across the kitchen wall. The water has gone. The line stays, measuring a night the family will never live before again.

There is no honest way to make the ecological crisis small. Homes are being lost, bodies hurt, and forms of life erased.

There is no honest way to call the future finished either. Organized hope begins where comfort ends: people look at the danger together, build power, and increase what they are able to save.

“Too late” can become a wall

Climate doomerism is the belief that collapse has become so complete or inevitable that meaningful action no longer matters. It sounds scientific when it borrows the language of thresholds and irreversible change. Then it takes one step the evidence cannot take.

Some losses are irreversible. Some feedbacks may continue for generations. Climate change still has no single switch marked SAVED on one side and LOST on the other. Different levels of warming and ecological damage bring different risks.

A family whose house cannot be defended still needs a safe home. A damaged wetland may shelter surviving species. A failed crop does not make next year’s seed bank useless. Preventing another harm matters after one harm can no longer be prevented.

Doomerism takes a field of narrowing choices and paints a wall across it.

A promise can close the same door

False reassurance leaves people standing still too. If a machine will certainly clean the atmosphere later, if markets will adjust by themselves, or if one election settles the crisis, everyone else can remain a spectator.

Hope is no forecast that everything works out. It is a relation between knowledge, power, and action.

People need to know what is happening, what causes it, what can still change, which work is necessary, who can block that work, and how organization changes the balance.

Without a path from danger to action, frightening facts can become a rain of stones. More facts alone do not build a shelter.

Grief needs somewhere to go

Climate grief is sorrow for damaged places, lost futures, dead species, uprooted communities, and the fear of what may come. It is a reasonable response to real loss.

A movement that treats grief as weakness asks people to become machines. A movement that lives only inside grief loses the ability to move.

Memorial, art, study, care, and shared work give loss a place inside collective memory. Anger can sharpen responsibility when it refuses an easy victim. Fear can reveal what people love enough to defend.

The point is the ability to act through changing emotional weather.

Hope becomes material through organization

Organized hope is confidence grounded in growing collective capacity. It has meeting places, skills, relationships, records, tools, plans, and institutions that can learn.

A neighborhood with no heat plan can only wish the power stays on. A neighborhood that has mapped vulnerable homes, won a public cooling center, trained health workers, built tenant organization, and gained a voice in grid planning possesses adaptive capacity: the practical ability to prepare, respond, recover, and change.

None of these steps solves climate change. Together they save lives now, teach cooperation, expose larger barriers, and create people more capable of fighting those barriers.

Small victories matter when they widen the next possibility. They become traps when leaders present them as the end.

The flood leaves more than one road

After a flood, residents may form a relief network because official aid arrives late. They deliver food, clean homes, check medication, and record which landlords refuse repairs.

The network may dissolve when the water falls. It may become charity that never asks why the same homes flood. It may become a tenant and neighborhood organization fighting for drainage, public housing, wetland restoration, and power over redevelopment.

The disaster did not automatically radicalize anyone. People interpreted it through relationships and institutions already present or built during the crisis.

This is why preparation is political. When established authority fails, the best-organized force may be a democratic movement, a private contractor, a religious charity, an armed nationalist group, or a state office seeking more control. Crisis creates openings. It does not choose who enters.

No date ends the need to act

Movements often organize around a deadline because deadlines focus attention. Climate systems do contain time limits, and delay closes options.

No date marks the moment after which action becomes meaningless. The work changes as damage grows. Mitigation remains necessary. Adaptation becomes harder. Repair expands. Struggles over migration and scarcity sharpen.

Organized hope therefore lives on several timescales. It fights for immediate protection, builds durable institutions, and keeps a horizon beyond the present crisis. A campaign can measure months. A forest can require generations.

People inherit work they may not finish. That has always been true of anything worth building.

Hope is no test of personal goodness

Rejecting doomerism cannot mean hiding worst-case risks or softening words such as famine, extinction, war, and death. The truth deserves plain language.

A frightened person does not fail politically because hope will not come on command. Organized hope is no self-help burden placed on an exhausted individual. Institutions must carry memory and capacity when a person cannot.

People can organize well and lose. The absence of a guarantee makes action a real choice rather than a role in a script.

Ecological outcomes remain graded and politically shaped, while collective capacity changes what can be protected. The open work is to communicate extreme danger without paralysis, care for people through chronic disaster, and make each immediate defense build power against the cause.

Hope is sometimes pictured as a light at the end of a tunnel. Organized hope is closer to a hand on a beam: people holding weight together while they build the way out.

It is heavy because the danger is real.
