06Socialism or Extinction

Capitalism's Empty Answers

A company changes the color of its package to green, prints a leaf beside its logo, and promises that the purchase will help save the planet. The factory, mine, freight route, and short life of the product may remain untouched.

Capitalism can turn criticism into a market faster than it can repair the damage. Its answer is empty when the leaf changes and the command beneath it does not.

The leaf on the package

Greenwashing is the use of environmental language, images, or selective facts to make an activity appear more sustainable than it is. A claim can be technically true and still mislead. A bottle may contain less plastic while the company sells twice as many bottles. An electric car has no tailpipe emissions, while its minerals, electricity, roads, and production still carry ecological costs.

Less plastic is usually better than more. The deception begins when a small reduction is offered as proof that the whole system can expand safely.

Marketing turns ecology into another product feature. Responsibility becomes something a buyer expresses at checkout.

An offset moves the promise somewhere else. An emitter pays for a project said to reduce or absorb emissions, then claims compensation for pollution here.

Would the project have happened without the payment? Will a protected forest remain standing? Has logging simply moved to another place? Who owned or used the land before the project? Can stored carbon be released by fire later? Is a present emission being balanced against a future removal?

When the answers fail, the offset becomes permission to continue polluting. A forest community can lose land while a firm buys innocence.

Every current claim about offset effectiveness requires project-level and up-to-date verification.

The product gets lighter. The economy gets larger.

Decoupling means separating economic growth from environmental pressure. Relative decoupling occurs when damage grows more slowly than economic output. Absolute decoupling occurs when total damage falls while output rises.

Efficiency and cleaner technology can produce real absolute declines in particular harms, especially when law bans a pollutant or replaces a process. The stronger green-growth claim is that global capitalist production can keep expanding while total material use, emissions, habitat loss, and other pressures fall quickly enough.

One wealthy country’s cleaner account cannot settle the claim. Production may have moved abroad. GDP may rise through activities with very different effects. Emissions can fall while mining, land use, or biodiversity worsens.

The evidence has to follow the thing all the way home.

An efficient engine uses less fuel for one trip. If cheaper travel leads to more trips, larger vehicles, or farther settlements, total fuel use may fall less than expected or even rise.

This is a rebound effect. Jevons paradox names the strong case in which efficiency helps total resource use increase because it lowers cost and opens new uses.

Efficiency is necessary. It also needs a social limit and purpose. Under accumulation, saved energy becomes room for expansion. Under production for use, the saving can remain a saving.

A machine cannot choose what it serves

Renewable electricity, storage, cleaner industrial processes, heat pumps, public transport, material recovery, and carbon removal research can all contribute. No machine abolishes the relations directing its use.

A battery can stabilize a public grid or feed a fashion for heavier private vehicles and another rush for minerals. Carbon removal may address emissions that cannot be avoided, or its future promise can delay the end of fossil fuel use. Innovation answers how something might be done. It cannot decide what should be made, for whom, or at whose expense.

Betting survival on an unproven future technology is an “emit now, clean up later” wager placed by people who may not suffer the first loss.

The circle keeps widening

A circular economy aims to reduce waste, keep products in use, repair them, recover materials, and regenerate living systems. These are valuable goals.

No material economy forms a perfect circle. Energy changes form, materials degrade, recovery takes labour and power, and some substances cannot be safely reused. More importantly, a firm can sell repair as a service, extract rent from shared platforms, and expand total throughput while calling each part circular.

An economy driven to grow is better pictured as a spiral. Recycling can narrow it. Only a decision to reduce unnecessary production can stop it from widening.

The buyer receives the blame

Personal habits affect demand. Capitalist climate politics often tears those acts away from the systems that make them possible.

A worker may be told to drive less while buses arrive once an hour and housing near work is unaffordable. A household is blamed for packaging it had no power to design. The language of an individual “carbon footprint” can shrink a political struggle into a private confession.

Collective action changes the field of choice. Public transport, durable-product rules, social housing, clean power, shorter supply chains, and workplace control make lower-impact living ordinary instead of heroic.

A useful tool can still be too small

Taxes, standards, bans, public investment, better products, and individual changes can reduce harm now. Rejecting them because they do not abolish capitalism would abandon real lives and ecosystems.

The question is whether they are enough. Judge a reform by what it changes, who controls it, who pays, where the damage goes, and what power exists to continue further.

Every failure is not a conspiracy. Limited knowledge, bureaucracy, and honest error exist. A structural critique asks why error so often protects accumulation and sends cost outward.

Open questions include the useful role of particular taxes, standards, transitional markets, removal technologies, and circular systems. Their performance must be tested empirically, not accepted or rejected by label.

The firm conclusion is that tools and market instruments cannot overcome production for exchange, the growth imperative, and unequal power by themselves.

Turn the green package over. Follow the smoke, mine, freight route, wage, waste, and second sale. An answer is empty when all of them stay in place and only the promise moves.

Ecological repair needs useful tools. It also needs the human power to decide what those tools will stop.

Source notes

Primary texts

  • Engels, The Part Played by Labour in the Transition from Ape to Man, on immediate gain and remote consequences.
  • William Stanley Jevons, The Coal Question, as the historical source of the efficiency paradox that bears his name.

Further study

  • John Bellamy Foster, Brett Clark, and Richard York, The Ecological Rift, especially the discussion of rebound and ecological paradoxes.
  • Research on decoupling, material footprints, carbon offsets, life-cycle assessment, and circular economy.
  • Jason Hickel, Less Is More, treated as one degrowth argument rather than final authority.
