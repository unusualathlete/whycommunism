05Socialism or Extinction

Wartime Mobilization Without Permanent Command Rule

In wartime, a government that worshipped the market on Monday may be counting steel, fuel, grain, ships, factory hours, and workers by Friday. An army cannot wait for profitable demand to reveal how many boots it needs.

This is why climate plans so often reach for the language of war. Society needs to transform energy, transport, housing, industry, and agriculture at extraordinary speed.

But war organizes death. The task is to recover the power to move production without treating people as ammunition or making emergency command permanent.

When property suddenly stops being sacred

Wartime mobilization is the rapid reorganization of production around urgent goals. States have converted factories, allocated fuel, directed transport, guaranteed investment, recruited labour, rationed consumption, and forbidden owners from using productive capacity as they pleased.

This is material planning. Instead of asking which investment promises the best return, planners count tons, hours, machines, kilowatt-hours, hospital beds, and trained hands.

War reveals a truth hidden during ordinary business. When a ruling class believes its survival is at stake, private property becomes negotiable. A state can command an industry and finance work too large, slow, or unprofitable for private capital.

The same history carries blood. War governments conscripted workers, crushed strikes, censored opposition, seized colonial resources, intensified racial rule, and sent millions into industrial slaughter. Their productive miracles were built around killing and often forced the occupied, colonized, poor, and imprisoned to carry the worst burdens.

Climate politics cannot borrow that machinery and wash the blood from its gears.

The copper has to go somewhere

Replacing fossil systems means more than installing new generators. Grids, heating, industrial furnaces, freight, buildings, farms, and material production must change while hospitals remain open and people continue eating.

These tasks reach for the same resources. Copper used for luxury vehicles cannot enter a transmission line at the same moment. An electrician teaching new crews cannot repair a failing substation that day. A plan must choose.

Markets make the choice through purchasing power and expected profit. They can fill a showroom while a low-return power line waits. Emergency transformation needs the social power to choose the line.

Rationing means allocating something scarce by a public rule instead of a private wallet. The rule can protect medicine, food, heating, and ordinary mobility while restricting luxury first. It can also shelter the powerful, feed a black market, and make poor families live on patriotic speeches.

Rationing is never just arithmetic. It reveals whose need counts.

State capitalism can move fast and keep the old master

Under state capitalism, firms and wage labour remain, while the state directs major targets and investment. Infrastructure may rise quickly. Capital still owns the house.

Owners can receive guaranteed contracts and public money while workers receive orders. Domestic emissions can fall while mining expands abroad. When the emergency passes, profitable interests can tear down public controls or turn them toward military and imperial expansion.

Rival capitalist states also compete for minerals, fuel, shipping routes, and strategic advantage. Climate preparation can become another arms race. In its most violent form, eco-fascism answers ecological danger by fortifying borders, expelling racialized populations, seizing land, and deciding which human beings are permitted to survive scarcity.

That is an organized response. It is organized barbarism.

A city of cold walls

Suppose winter enters hundreds of thousands of badly insulated homes. Renters feed expensive heat into walls they do not own. Contractors chase wealthy customers. Landlords delay work. The grid strains.

A public mobilization can survey every building, train crews, standardize safe parts, buy materials in bulk, and begin where cold threatens life. It can coordinate insulation, heating, and grid capacity instead of selling them as separate products.

Then the hard questions arrive. Should scarce crews repair the worst buildings first, or complete whole districts efficiently? Where do residents live during construction? Who checks the work? What happens when a tenant reports that the official survey missed black mold behind a wall?

Proletarian self-government makes those questions public. Tenants, maintenance workers, engineers, disabled residents, carers, and neighborhood bodies need channels that can alter the schedule. Decision-making bodies need authority in an emergency, but that authority must remain accountable, recallable, open to audit, and capable of dissolving into ordinary common administration.

Speed grows from trust as well as command. Workers report failure sooner when telling the truth is safe. A target protected from criticism becomes a number everyone learns to fake.

Emergency power must not become a new climate

The wartime analogy does not turn the military into an ecological model. Armed forces consume enormous materials and fuel. War kills people, burns infrastructure, poisons land, and destroys the international cooperation repair requires.

Mobilization cannot justify powers with no end. A command body that cannot be recalled or dissolved can harden into a new ruling layer. Rapid planning also need not assign every person’s occupation from a central desk. Security, training, voluntary movement, and workplace control can release human capacity without blind coercion.

History settles two points at once: markets can be overridden, and concentrated command can unleash terrible violence. The principle that follows is democratic material planning with protection by need. Emergency institutions, allocation rules, technologies, and timetables remain open to conditions and review.

War economies proved that society can move mountains of steel when power decides the task matters. Ecology asks for something harder.

Move the steel. Keep every person human.
