08Socialism or Extinction

The Horizon of Possibility: Less Waste, More Life

A library gives a child more books than any family could buy or fit inside a home. A train gives a whole city more movement with fewer engines, roads, and parking spaces than millions of private cars.

Both make the material world lighter by making the human world larger.

Ecological communism does not ask people to want less life. It asks us to stop mistaking a mountain of goods for the powers those goods were supposed to give us.

Two economies can shrink for opposite reasons

Degrowth is a planned reduction of excessive energy and material use so society can live within ecological limits while human well-being rises.

The word sounds frightening because people know what contraction means under capitalism. Investment collapses. Workers lose wages and homes. Public budgets are cut. Owners protect their wealth while everyone beneath them is told to tighten a belt already against the skin.

That is recession. Production falls through crisis while need goes unmet.

Communist degrowth chooses what should become smaller: fossil dependence, military waste, planned obsolescence, competitive advertising, destructive luxury, speculative construction, needless duplication, and products built to fail. It protects the people whose lives depended on those industries, then moves labour and materials toward something worth keeping.

One economy becomes smaller by throwing people out. The other becomes lighter by throwing waste out.

What must grow

The world does not enter ecological transformation from one starting line. Colonialism concentrated wealth, infrastructure, and technical power in some regions by draining others.

Regrowth means expanding necessary productive capacity where colonial underdevelopment and present inequality have denied people a secure life. Homes must grow. So must sanitation, medicine, cooling, electricity, public transport, education, resilient farming, scientific capacity, and the means to survive disaster.

Poorer regions cannot be ordered to preserve global inequality in the name of nature. Rich countries cannot claim ecological virtue after sending mines, factories, and waste abroad.

The two movements belong together. Imperial centers release materials, technology, and ecological space by contracting excess. Formerly colonized regions develop according to human need without copying every destructive stage of capitalist industrialization.

Useful production can rise across the world while unnecessary throughput falls.

Enough is a floor before it becomes a limit

Sufficiency means enough material security for a person to develop fully. It draws a floor below which no one may fall. It also draws a limit where one person’s excess begins consuming conditions everyone needs.

Enough cannot mean the same pile for every body. A disabled person may need more equipment, care, transport, or electricity. A cold region needs different housing from a tropical one. A hospital uses more power than a billboard and should.

Equity starts from these differences.

Sufficiency also changes as human powers grow. Better medicine, art, knowledge, and tools can make life richer without requiring the same growth in raw material. Ecological limits do not freeze development. They ask development to change its object.

Which abundance do we want?

The city with fewer cars and more movement

Imagine a city beginning to leave car dependence behind. At first it must produce more: rail, buses, stations, electrical capacity, accessible sidewalks, trees, and homes closer to daily life.

Then the movement changes. A train used all day replaces rows of private machines sitting still in parking lots. Land returns from asphalt. Children cross streets more safely. A worker no longer gives two hours of every day to traffic. Fewer vehicles are sold, but more people can go where they need.

GDP may record the missing car purchases as loss. Human life records air, safety, time, and motion.

This is how material contraction can open a social horizon.

The same principle works elsewhere. A tool library gives a neighborhood access to hundreds of tools without putting a rarely used drill in every cupboard. Safe public water removes the need to manufacture, transport, and discard mountains of bottles. Durable public housing can give more security with less speculative construction and vacancy.

Decommodification means access no longer waits for a purchase. Healthcare, housing, transport, education, energy, water, communication, and culture become common powers.

Time is something society produces

Capitalist growth consumes hours as surely as it consumes ore. Advertising produces dissatisfaction. Long commutes eat the morning and evening. Fragile objects demand repair or replacement. Some people work until their bodies fail while others are kept unemployed so every worker knows they can be replaced.

When wasteful activity contracts and necessary labour is shared, the working day can shorten. Productivity no longer has to return as another round of sales. It can return as an afternoon.

That time may become care, study, music, friendship, politics, sport, sleep, science, or walking with no purchase waiting at the end.

No material index can fully count an afternoon returned to a person.

Technology belongs inside this horizon. Renewable power, automation, durable materials, efficient buildings, medicine, and careful biotechnology can widen human possibility. A machine earns its place when it removes dangerous labour, protects ecology, improves a useful thing, or gives time back.

“High tech” and “low tech” tell us very little. The question is what human and living power the technique allows to grow.

There is no ecology in preserved poverty

Degrowth can never mean equal austerity. A private jet and a rural ambulance do not enter the same moral column because both burn fuel. Poverty, unpaid drudgery, and the absence of medicine do not become beautiful when painted green.

Regrowth also cannot excuse a new wave of land seizure. A state cannot sacrifice a mining community today and call it liberation because the mineral will serve development tomorrow. The people living on and working the land need power over that choice.

The course is clear: destructive and wasteful production contracts, universal services and necessary capacity expand, and material access becomes more equal. Regional budgets, measures beyond GDP, transition speeds, and the shifting line between preference and luxury require public argument and revision.

Capital offers abundance as a shop with no closing time while the shelves beneath the world come loose.

Communist abundance is a life secure enough to unfold: enough things for everyone, more time returned to everyone, and human powers no longer trapped behind a price.
