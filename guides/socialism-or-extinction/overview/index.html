Socialism or Extinction: Ecology Is a Social Question

A storm tears the roof from a school in one night. Rain falls through the lights. Teachers carry books and computers out of the water while children wait in a gym across town.

The storm arrived that morning. The disaster began much earlier.

It began in the fuel burned for electricity and transport, in the mangroves cleared for buildings, in the concrete poured across land that once held rain, in budgets that repaired wealthy districts first, and in company accounts that counted all this as growth. Ecosocialism starts from that whole chain. It asks society to take conscious control of how it produces life so the living world can keep producing life in return.

The storm began before the clouds

Climate change is one opening into a wider ecological crisis. Soil loses its structure. Rivers carry poison. Forests thin into timber lots. Ocean water grows warmer and more acidic. Species vanish, sometimes before anyone has learned their names.

These losses pull on one another. Drought dries a forest. Fire releases carbon and kills the plants that would have drawn carbon back down. When rain finally comes, bare ground cannot hold it. The flood carries ash into a river already struggling for oxygen.

Earth systems have ranges within which human societies developed. Scientists use planetary boundary for a threshold beyond which one of those systems enters greater danger and uncertainty. A boundary is not a line painted on the ground, with perfect safety on one side and instant death on the other. It tells us that the farther pressure travels, the more we gamble with processes no government can command back into place.

Current measurements change with the science. Every figure published here will need a date and a checked source. The basic relation is already visible: society is pushing living systems faster than they can renew themselves.

Money keeps one account. Life keeps another.

An economy is never money circling above the ground.

Bread begins with seed, soil, water, sunlight, machinery, knowledge, transport, and hands. A phone begins with mines and chemical plants, then moves through electricity, design, assembly, ships, shops, pockets, and finally a drawer or waste heap.

A company account can record the price of copper down to the cent. It may never record a poisoned river, an exhausted aquifer, a miner’s damaged lungs, or the village forced from the land. Economists call harms like these externalities, as if they happened outside the real business.

The people breathing the dust know exactly where the business is.

Ecology follows the full movement. What enters production? Who works on it? What does it become? Where does the waste go? What must be returned, rested, repaired, or left alone?

The machine has a command

Capital is value sent out to come back larger. A major firm that stops expanding can lose investors, credit, markets, and the fight against its rivals. The pressure works even when an owner feels sincere concern for the planet.

It rewards faster turnover, cheaper inputs, and costs passed to someone else. A refrigerator built to last twenty years meets a need once. A fragile refrigerator creates another sale. A wetland can protect a coast for centuries without earning rent. Drain it, divide it, and sell the lots, and private wealth appears on paper while public wealth disappears under water.

Regulation can stop particular harms. Better technology can use less material or energy. Both are worth fighting for. Yet if every saving becomes room for more production, the pressure returns in a new form. If dirty production moves overseas, one country’s balance sheet grows cleaner while another country receives the wound.

The command remains: grow, sell, return larger, begin again.

A hospital shows what another calculation could do

During a heatwave, a hospital needs steady electricity, clean water, medicine kept cold, staff able to travel safely, and homes nearby that do not cook their residents alive.

A market can sell air conditioners to households with money. Those separate purchases cannot redesign the grid, insulate every home, shade streets, protect outdoor workers, organize emergency transport, and decide that the frailest people come first.

A social plan can hold these needs together. It can count power, materials, labour, health risk, and what residents report from each block. A neighborhood knows which apartment tower traps heat. A region can coordinate water and transport. A country can redirect industry. International institutions can share technology and stop one place from buying safety through another’s ruin.

The plan will make mistakes. Its strength is that people can inspect it, correct it, and judge it by life rather than sales.

This calculation also moves differently across the world. Imperial centers need to contract fossil dependence, military waste, planned obsolescence, destructive luxury, and excessive material throughput. Regions kept poor through colonialism need more housing, sanitation, hospitals, energy, transport, and technical capacity. Useful production can rise while the machinery of waste shrinks.

A common plan can still go blind

Communism does not make a society ecological by magic. Historical socialist states built important public transport, conservation, monitoring, and agroecological systems. They also caused grave ecological damage. War, isolation, rushed industrial targets, bureaucracy, secrecy, and weak ecological knowledge all mattered. Those histories need criticism without caricature.

Planning also does not hand political power to a computer. Models can show constraints and compare possible paths. People decide which needs come first and which risks they will accept.

The center is clear: ecological repair requires production for use, democratic planning, internationalism, and the end of accumulation as society’s ruling purpose. The energy mix, regional material budgets, restoration methods, and institutions remain open to science, struggle, and review.

The roof of the school will still need hands, timber, tools, and time. The larger task is to build a society that sees the storm inside every one of those things, then organizes them so children do not keep learning beneath a leak.
