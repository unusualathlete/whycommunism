07Socialism or Extinction

Communist Social Metabolism: Repair, Return, Renew

A town opens a gate and draws water from a river. Pipes carry it into kitchens, showers, workshops, gardens, and hospital rooms. Farther downstream, another town waits for the same river. So do fish, reeds, farms, wetlands, and people not yet born.

The gate does more than move water. It ties every user to a duty of return.

A communist social metabolism makes that duty part of production from the beginning. Associated producers meet human needs while maintaining the living conditions that let needs be met again.

The river cannot be divided into private pieces

Common stewardship means that land, water, energy, species, and ecological systems are not disposable property simply because a title carries one person’s name.

A river makes the idea obvious. Its water flows across boundaries. Its fish migrate. A factory upstream can change the lungs and crops of people it will never meet. Cutting the river into legal slices does not cut the relation.

The atmosphere has no fence either.

Common stewardship gives everyone secure access to the material conditions of life, then binds that access to responsibility. No group may enrich itself by ruining those conditions for others. Use must respect renewal time. Future generations and nonhuman life must enter a decision even though neither can appear at today’s meeting.

This is possible only when production answers to use and need. A society producing for exchange sees the river mainly as water rights, power, freight, waste capacity, and land value. Common stewardship sees the river as all these human uses joined to a living whole that must continue.

Follow the thing until it comes back

Marx called for associated producers to regulate their metabolism with nature consciously and rationally. Rational regulation means following matter from extraction through production, use, maintenance, recovery, and return.

Take a sack of rice. The plan cannot stop at the number of sacks a farm can sell. It follows seed, soil nutrients, irrigation, fuel, farm labour, milling, storage, transport, cooking, human nourishment, packaging, spoiled grain, wastewater, and whatever can safely return to the soil.

At every step another question appears. What need is being met? Which living system carries the cost? How long does renewal take? Can the tool be repaired? Who lives near the waste? How much labour does restoration require?

Price can contain useful information in limited settings, but it cannot carry all of this. A cheap input may be cheap because another region lost clean water. A species with no price may hold a food web together. A wetland can save a city from floods without ever issuing an invoice.

The plan needs physical measures, ecological knowledge, social need, and public judgment. It also needs the humility to discover that something it counted as waste was doing work no ledger understood.

Repair is part of production

Capitalist accounts often place restoration after “real” production, as cleanup paid from whatever profit remains. Communist accounting recognizes restoration as production of common wealth.

Rebuilding soil is productive. Cleaning a poisoned site is productive. Recovering wetlands, protecting habitat, repairing watersheds, removing dangerous infrastructure, and helping species return all maintain the conditions of life.

Repair is social too. A community exposed to poison needs healthcare, safe housing, and restitution for land and years taken from it. Some losses cannot be reversed. No labour resurrects an extinct species. Money paid after destruction must never be called a cure.

An ecological reserve protects what cannot safely be used now. It can be water left in a river, forest left standing, spare capacity in a grid, seed diversity, stored food, or labour time held for emergency.

Capital sees idle capacity and asks why it is not earning. Life sees room to survive a shock.

A food region learns to see itself whole

Imagine farms, towns, and a city planning food as one region. Farmers report soil, water, pests, labour, and likely yields. Hospitals, kitchens, schools, and households report what people need. Distribution workers record loss without punishment for telling the truth.

The region can shorten some supply chains, protect fertile land, vary crops, reduce packaging, and recover nutrients where science shows it is safe. Researchers, sanitation workers, farmers, cooks, and residents each hold a piece of the cycle.

The goal is not to seal the region off. Different soils grow different foods. Drought and disaster require exchange and reserves. Culture gives people reasons to share food across distance. Reciprocity means that no place feeds another by emptying its own soil, water, and people.

This work changes labour itself. The farmer, engineer, cleaner, nurse, ecologist, and cook no longer meet only in the prices of separate products. They can see their activities as moments of one metabolism.

Repair cannot be dumped onto workers sent into toxic conditions for low pay while everyone else celebrates a green target. Their safety, health, time, knowledge, and control belong inside the ecological account.

A plan needs roots and a horizon

Ecology is local enough to live in a handful of soil. A wetland, monsoon, reef, or mountain cannot be governed by one universal recipe. Indigenous peoples and long-rooted communities may hold knowledge formed through generations of attention and struggle.

Ecology also crosses every local border. Smoke travels. Rivers flow. Animals migrate. Climate is planetary.

Local bodies must shape regional plans. Regions must change national choices. International limits must return as clear and equitable duties. Information must travel back again when the plan meets the world. Audit can expose hidden damage and discover a method worth sharing.

Models can compare paths and reveal combinations that do not fit. They cannot decide which forms of life matter. Computation informs. People and their accountable institutions choose.

Common ownership can still go blind

No material economy becomes a perfect circle. Energy disperses. Materials wear down. Some waste cannot be safely recovered. Thermodynamic limits survive every revolution.

Common ownership also offers no automatic innocence. Socialist societies have built conservation, public transport, monitoring, and agroecology, and have committed severe ecological destruction. War, secrecy, rushed targets, weak science, and the exclusion of workers or local communities can break a plan’s eyes and ears.

The principles are firm: common stewardship, production for use, ecological accounting, restoration, reserves, worker power, and coordination across scales. Institutions, land choices, regional limits, and ways of representing nonhuman needs remain open to science and democratic conflict.

The town will still open the gate. People need water.

Freedom begins when everyone downstream, including the river itself, is present in the hand that turns it.

Primary texts

Further study

  • John Bellamy Foster and Brett Clark, The Robbery of Nature.
  • Research on agroecology, restoration ecology, industrial ecology, Indigenous stewardship, and democratic ecological planning.
  • Historical studies of both ecological achievements and failures in socialist states.
