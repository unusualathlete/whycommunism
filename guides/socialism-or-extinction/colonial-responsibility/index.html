10Socialism or Extinction

Colonial Responsibility: Who Owes Repair?

An electric bus moves through a wealthy city without leaving diesel smoke behind it. Beneath the floor sits a battery. Inside the battery are metals dug from somebody’s land, water used in processing, factory labour, electricity, freight, and a future pile of waste.

The city calls the bus clean. Whether it is truly clean depends on how far the city is willing to look.

Climate justice begins with that full distance. Humanity shares one atmosphere, but every class, company, and country did not use it equally, profit equally, or receive equal protection from the damage.

The clean city and the dirty frontier

European empires seized land, forests, minerals, crops, and human beings. Plantation slavery, forced labour, mining, unequal trade, and colonial taxation fed industrial growth in imperial centers. This violence supplied capitalism with labour, materials, markets, and accumulated wealth.

Fossil industrialization stretched the chain. Colonized regions supplied fuel and raw material, then bought finished goods. Their economies were reshaped around export. Communities received poisoned water, stripped soil, dangerous work, and dependence on prices set elsewhere.

Formal independence ended direct colonial rule across much of the world. It did not dissolve every material relation empire built. Debt, military power, corporate ownership, trade rules, patents, and control of finance still pull value and material wealth toward the old centers.

This movement can hide inside a green transition. If the electric bus depends on a mine that poisons water, crushes workers, or removes an Indigenous people from their land, clean air in one city has been purchased with another place’s wound.

That is green colonialism: climate policy used to justify a new round of seizure, exploitation, debt, or foreign control.

The bus remains worth building. Its whole supply chain must enter the public decision.

The atmosphere was occupied before everyone arrived

The atmospheric commons is the limited capacity of the atmosphere to receive greenhouse gases without dangerous warming. Industrial powers used a vast part of that capacity while building wealth.

The countries that contributed least often face the hardest heat, storms, crop loss, displacement, and debt. The system that carried wealth outward now sends disaster inward.

Climate debt names the obligation created when some societies take more than their share of ecological space and leave others with both the damage and a narrower path to development.

This debt cannot be settled by one payment or a charitable photograph. It demands faster cuts by historically high emitters, resources for adaptation and repair, compensation for irreversible loss and damage, rights for displaced people, and room for poorer regions to build what their people need.

It also demands technology on terms that create independence. A solar panel is not a gift if the recipient must remain permanently dependent on a foreign patent, contractor, spare part, and loan.

Every numerical account of climate debt makes political choices. Where does the clock begin? Which gases count? Are emissions counted where a product is made or where it is consumed? How do population and present capacity enter? The choices must be shown instead of hidden inside a final figure.

Repair must return power

Climate finance can arrive as another loan, adding debt to a country already paying for disasters it did little to cause. Foreign consultants may choose the project. Contractors from the funding state take the work. A sea wall protects an export port while nearby homes remain in the water.

Repair worthy of the name increases self-determination. Resources must be sufficient and governed by the people living with the result. Technology sharing must include knowledge, training, tools, spare parts, and the legal freedom to maintain and develop the system locally.

Indigenous peoples and affected communities need more than consultation after a decision has already been made. Their land rights, refusal, consent, and knowledge must have the power to change or stop the project.

Self-determination still exists inside a shared world. No state gains an unlimited right to poison a river that crosses a border or destroy the path of a migrating species. The goal is cooperation without domination.

Responsibility follows power

The climate convention uses common but differentiated responsibilities for a basic truth: every country has duties, but the duties differ according to contribution and capacity.

Class cuts through the national map. A low-income worker in an imperial center does not wield the power of an oil company, bank, military, or wealthy household. Many workers live beside refineries, mines, highways, and waste themselves.

This does not erase advantages built through empire. It shows where the heaviest responsibility belongs. Owners, high consumers, military systems, and institutions that directed extraction must surrender more resources and power. Ordinary people should gain homes, care, transport, security, and control through the transition.

Climate politics becomes useless when it turns structural debt into personal guilt. Guilt can be confessed without changing a mine, patent, trade rule, or armed border.

Development cannot mean another sacrifice zone

Formerly colonized regions need housing, sanitation, medicine, food security, electricity, transport, education, cooling, and industry. Denying these in the name of a global material limit would preserve colonial inequality.

Copying the imperial pattern would also deepen the crisis: car dependence, planned obsolescence, luxury construction, fossil lock-in, and an export economy that eats its own land.

Common development can take another route. Build public transportation before car dependence buries a city in roads. Build durable homes before speculative sprawl. Join agroecology to food security. Develop regional industry around social need. Fit clean power to local ecosystems and knowledge.

Rich regions owe the resources and ecological space that let these paths remain possible.

Anti-imperialism must reach beneath the flag

Colonial responsibility is no inherited moral stain placed on a child because of nationality. It identifies institutions, accumulated advantages, and living relations that can be transformed.

Governments in formerly colonized countries can also seize land, crush workers, enrich local rulers, and join foreign capital. A national flag cannot turn exploitation into liberation. Anti-imperialism must reach the people beneath every state.

The principles are clear: responsibility follows historical contribution, present power, and capacity; repair returns power rather than offering charity; necessary development receives ecological room. Measurement, institutions, reparations, migration rights, and enforcement without a new empire remain matters for struggle led by those most affected.

The bus reaches the end of its route and returns through the clean city. Its real route is longer. It runs through mine, river, factory, ship, debt, patent, wage, road, and atmosphere.

It becomes part of a just transition only when every person along that route gains the power to breathe.

Source notes

Primary texts

  • United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change, Article 3, “Principles”, on equity and common but differentiated responsibilities and respective capabilities.
  • UNFCCC, Fund for responding to Loss and Damage, which collects the founding, operationalization, board, and guidance decisions in one current record.
  • Karl Marx, Capital, Volume I, Chapter 31, “Genesis of the Industrial Capitalist”, on colonialism, slavery, public debt, and expropriation in the formation of industrial capital.

Further study

  • Jason Hickel, Less Is More: How Degrowth Will Save the World, especially “Growth as Colonisation” and “Colonialism 2.0,” on atmospheric colonization, the North’s excess material use, and the South’s need to expand necessary energy and resource use.
  • John Bellamy Foster, Brett Clark, and Richard York, The Ecological Rift: Capitalism’s War on the Earth, especially the chapters “Carbon Metabolism and Global Capital Accumulation” and “Imperialism and Ecological Metabolism.”
  • John Bellamy Foster and Brett Clark, The Robbery of Nature: Capitalism and the Ecological Rift, on ecological imperialism, racial capitalism, social reproduction, and appropriation without reciprocity.
  • John Bellamy Foster, “Marx’s Theory of Metabolic Rift: Classical Foundations for Environmental Sociology”, on the historical and theoretical basis of metabolic-rift analysis.
  • Paul Burkett, Marxism and Ecological Economics: Toward a Red and Green Political Economy, especially Chapters 9 and 10 on ecological planning, sustainable human development, and international inequality.
