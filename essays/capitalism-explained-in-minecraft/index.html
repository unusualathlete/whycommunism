When Play Becomes Work and the Market Rewrites the World

by eleanor aster

This began when I got a little too excited in a Discord server and started explaining capitalism through Minecraft. I thought I was only talking about a game. Then the game kept getting bigger, so I gathered the messages together and built them a proper home here.

RuneScape, Roblox’s Adopt Me!, and Minecraft have taught millions of players what a market feels like long before they ever encounter economic theory. To understand capitalism, let’s begin with the popular DonutSMP, where we can follow a commodity from labour to exchange, block by block.

Commodities are bought and sold in these in-game markets. A beacon and a stack of iron are both useful, but they are not equal in the market. The market forgets who made either one and ignores how long one particular player took. It reduces both to a common question: using the tools and methods that have become normal across the server, how much player labour is needed to replace it? This is socially necessary labour time, and it determines value. We do not see value floating above an item. We see one beacon exchange for piles of iron, or for a number in the server’s money. That visible number is its price. Price expresses value in money, but it does not copy it perfectly. Netherite and beacons usually command high prices because the server still needs enormous player effort to reproduce them. Build an iron farm and the change becomes visible: ingots that once cost minutes underground now fall into chests while players simply remain online. As farms spread and become the normal way to produce iron, the socially necessary labour time of each ingot falls. Its value falls with it, and competition brings down its price.

The balance beside your username looks like wealth sitting by itself, but it can buy only because other players’ labour has filled the market with things worth buying. Money is a transferable claim on part of the social product their labour created. You can turn that claim into any commodity another player is willing to sell. Production is private: players and factions decide what to produce separately, and their products meet one another afterward through exchange in the market.

money is a claim on other players’ labour$ 128,400

The most important part is alienation: becoming a stranger to our shared humanity and to yourself. Players in DonutSMP become strangers to other players, to their own labour, and to the commodities they themselves produce. They can still form friendships and help one another, but market competition repeatedly makes them confront each other as rivals. What is missing is not every form of community, but a true community in which their shared labour is consciously organized for everyone. Their hard work, like mining for diamonds, no longer fulfils them; it only fills their balance. The first diamond you ever found may have made you call a friend over to the screen. Under the market, the same flash of light blue becomes only a number added to your balance. The things players produce lose their personal and sentimental character. Made purely for exchange, they are already intended for somebody else before they are even finished. Instead of playing for fun, you are playing to get rich.

the same flash of light blue becomes only a number
+1

A person could build a cute house with a friend, explore, make gifts, roleplay, or create beauty. But the market whispers:

[Market] That is wasted time unless it makes money.

The pursuit of profit drives players to feats of engineering no casual server ever sees: gold farms spanning the Nether roof, raid farms filling chests with totems, redstone sorting systems running like factories. Competition makes every innovation compulsory. Once one player automates, everyone must automate or be undersold. You stop asking what you want to build and start asking which farm earns the most money per hour.

once one player automates, everyone must

Iron that was once wealth becomes something every player can own; gear that took days now takes minutes. Castles and cities once displayed what players could build block by block. The market overshadows them with machines that cross dimensions and make the world itself work while their owners simply remain online. In weeks, they pour out more wealth than years of miners could carry from the Earth by hand. Every one of those machines produces, and produces, and produces. This is exactly where the crisis begins.

The second most important part is crisis. When abundance crashes prices, servers respond by creating artificial scarcity. The chests reveal two different movements. Once farms become the normal way to produce an item, every chest spreads the labour of building and running them across more products. Each item now represents less socially necessary labour, so its value falls. When those chests fill the market faster than players can buy their contents, the price falls too, sometimes below the item’s value. Excess supply explains the market crash. Changed labour explains the movement underneath it.

The chests are full, yet the sellers are in trouble. Nothing is missing. There is simply more than can be sold profitably, so prices crash. Under capitalism, abundance itself becomes the catastrophe. This is overproduction.

abundance itself becomes the catastrophe
▼

DonutSMP writes scarcity back into the map. A finite End border prevents players from reaching fresh terrain, limiting the remaining unlooted ships that supply Elytras. The border can keep Elytra prices high even when no new labour is producing more of them. That is scarcity raising price, not labour creating value.

a finite border writes scarcity back into the map

DonutSMP ended its first season with a total reset, erasing the accumulated labour stored in its world and forcing everyone to begin again. The hidden base you spent months building disappeared with the farm beneath it. A real crisis can devalue wealth and destroy infrastructure, but it cannot regenerate the nature accumulation consumed. The reset did both at once. Forests returned. Mountains became whole. The server woke to a beautiful world again, ready to be liquidated once more. Think about what that admits. To restart accumulation, the server erased its result and restored the world it consumed.

to begin accumulation again, it erases the result of accumulation

Scarcity also returns through destruction. Players blow up rival auto-farms with TNT. Whatever the raider intended, the economy reads the explosion in the same way: productive power is gone and future output falls. A machine that could enrich the server becomes useful to destroy because its output threatens another player’s position. The server compresses into a single raid what the real world mediates through firms, states, borders, and armies. This does not mean every war has one simple economic cause. It means competition over markets, resources, territory, and productive power can turn socially useful capacity into a strategic target.

competition turns productive power into a target
TNTTNTTNTTNT

Put two players before the same market. One brings hand tools and whatever fits in an inventory. An established faction arrives with spawners and hidden farms it can defend. That difference is accumulated productive power. The goal is not simply to own good items, but to own what keeps producing more. Yet a machine is not capital merely because it produces. A farm that feeds friends is useful wealth. It becomes capital when one player controls it, hires others to work around it, and sells its output so the original money returns enlarged. New players have almost nothing while established players command the output of entire machines.

The difference in productive power soon becomes a difference of class. The capitalist is the player or faction that owns the spawners and auto-farms and pays other players to grind for profit. The worker is the player who grinds for them. The market is open to every player by the game’s design, but players do not enter it with equal power. One arrives with the contents of a hand-mined inventory. Another arrives with warehouses filled by machines. The market does not invent the initial difference in ownership, but it reproduces and magnifies it, converting control of production into money and power over other players.

capital on one side, a working class on the other

When a faction pays a player to grind for an evening, it buys the use of that player’s ability to labour for the evening and owns everything the work produces. Once the used tools and materials are replaced, whatever new value exceeds the player’s payment remains with the owner. That excess is surplus value. When the commodities are sold, it appears as profit and lets the faction begin again with more than before.

the excess is surplus valueWAGESURPLUS

Auto-farms can push the value represented in each item toward zero as the labour of building, maintaining, supplying, and operating them is spread across immense output. An hour of mining by hand earns almost nothing because buyers do not pay for the time you personally spent. Your iron has the same market price as iron produced by the server’s normal method, which is now the auto-farm. If you spend an hour mining what a farm produces in seconds, most of that hour is not socially necessary and adds no extra value to the ingot. The work that still pays is grinding, fighting, and building for the players who control the farms. For a new player unable to build comparable infrastructure, the viable path becomes selling your ability to labour to established capital.

The same accumulation that floods and crashes the market also concentrates its most productive machinery in fewer hands. Players shut out from that machinery are drawn into working for those who own it. Accumulation produces both poles at once: capital on one side and a working class on the other.

A forest no longer invites you to build a home among the trees. You look at it and count the saleable logs before cutting the first block. A cave becomes ore per hour. The world that once invited exploration now appears as material to liquidate. The forest is already part of the world’s wealth, but the market recognizes it only after it is cut into commodities. What was useful and beautiful while standing becomes money by being destroyed.

money by being destroyed
64 × $12

A game built around infinite creativity, when governed by the law of value, produces scarcity, class division, crisis, and destruction. It keeps the cycle moving by capping its own world and erasing everything its players’ accumulated labour built, burning it all back into nothing. That is the verdict the server delivers on itself. The Minecraft server does not merely simulate capitalism. It simulates the contradictions that make capitalism destructive and unstable. They also make it historically surpassable, which means people can collectively build a way of living beyond it.

But a server is a game. You can log out.

Back to Reality

DonutSMP is not a perfect model of capitalism. Players do not depend on the server to keep their bodies alive, and its rules were consciously written by administrators. Those differences matter. They also reveal what is harsher outside it. Capitalism’s rules confront us as impersonal forces, not settings one administrator can simply switch off. Its crises are not announced resets. People without enough property to live from cannot log out. Most must sell their ability to labour, depend on someone who does, or face deprivation.

Outside the game, crisis comes blindly, without warning or an announced reset. It arrives as crashes and unemployment, while competition for profit, markets, and resources can carry destruction into war. In the real world, the division between capitalists and the working class governs access not merely to equipment in a game, but to the necessities of life and the power to shape it.

And unlike a Minecraft world with infinite resources, real capitalism deals with finite resources on a finite planet. We cannot generate new chunks and biomes. Capitalism's pursuit of profit at the expense of planet and people drives catastrophic climate change and ecological breakdown, threatening human societies and ecosystems on a planetary scale.

we cannot generate new chunks

A capitalist can replace one worker who says no. Replacing an organized workforce all at once is costly, slow, and sometimes impossible. When everyone puts down their tools together, the owner discovers where the power was hiding. People taught to compete for hours and pay begin to see one another differently: not as rivals for a job, but as the people without whom the place cannot run. DonutSMP showed what happens when the market turns play into a grind. Acting together begins the reverse movement. Workers, if united, take back the world that already moves through their hands, not merely to labour under kinder terms, but to win more of life back for fun, creativity and play.

the owner discovers where the power was hiding

Someone wrote the server’s rules. Together, people can rewrite the rules that govern our world.

The working class have nothing to lose but their chains. You have a world to win.

Workers of the world, unite!

What this builds on

The theory did not spawn inside the server. The account of value here is Marx’s, though other economists mean different things by that word. I kept Capital, Volume I, Chapter 1 and Chapter 3 nearby while working through commodities, labour time, value, and price. The ache at the centre of the essay comes from “Estranged Labour”.

When the farms became capital and the grinder became a worker, I went back to Capital, Volume I, Chapter 4, Chapter 7, Chapter 9, and Capital, Volume III, Chapter 2. The machines and crashes led me to Volume I, Chapter 15, Chapter 25, and Chapter 1 of The Communist Manifesto.

The climate ending got its reality check from the IPCC Sixth Assessment Report’s headline findings on impacts and risk.

And then there was DonutSMP itself. Long before I started taking notes, I watched DrDonut’s livestreams and the clip-farming videos cut from them simply because they were fun and made me laugh. That is how the server found its way into the essay. DonutSMP also has its own shard currency and server-set sell prices, but this essay stays with the market players make among themselves. Since servers change while essays sit still, I checked the details about seasons and borders on 23 July 2026 against the community’s Getting Started guide, season history, and World Border reference. Minecraft’s official End City guide confirms where Elytras are found.
